EU Agencies: a literature review
Juan Carlos Trivino and Jacint Jordana
Institut Barcelona d’Estudis Internacionals (IBEI), Barcelona, April 2016

Clean text extract notes

1) Core framing
- EU agencies emerge as part of the EU as a regulatory state.
- Agencies are specialized, non-majoritarian bodies designed to add technical and administrative capacity.
- The literature focuses on creation/design logics, autonomy/independence, and accountability.

2) Main concepts in the review
- Agencification: formalization of stable organizations with specific mandates.
- Networkation: formalization of looser regulatory networks.
- Governancing: decentralization of power through governance systems.
- Single European Regulatory Space (SERS): shaped by these three parallel processes.

3) Why agencies were created
- Functional reasons: expertise, efficiency, harmonization, crisis response.
- Political reasons: institutional bargaining among Commission, Council, Parliament, and member states.
- Agencies can be compromise outcomes between supranational and intergovernmental interests.

4) Networks vs agencies
- Early EU regulation used many networks.
- Over time, agencies often replaced or absorbed networks because agencies were more adaptable and durable.
- Networks still matter for cooperation and diffusion of soft rules.

5) Typologies of agency functions
- Information providers.
- Managerial or executive agencies.
- Operational/cooperative bodies.
- Decision-making or quasi-regulatory agencies.
- Agencies vary by power and by whether they can issue binding decisions.

6) Independence and autonomy debates
- De jure independence (formal legal design) is not equal to de facto autonomy (real behavior).
- Multi-principal control (Commission, Council, Parliament, member states) can weaken practical independence.
- Agencies may show high formal autonomy but low practical room for maneuver, or the reverse.

7) Accountability findings
- Management boards are central to accountability in practice.
- Board composition and information asymmetries can reduce effective oversight.
- Vertical accountability is often weaker than expected.
- Horizontal/peer accountability can be stronger, frequently with national orientation.

8) Commission’s role
- Commission often remains highly influential in agency operation, including via boards and informal rules.
- Agencies can support an executive-center dynamic at EU level, even when formally decentralized.

9) Main conclusion of the review
- Literature is strong on institutional design and principal-agent logic.
- Gaps remain on outcomes: whether agencies actually improve harmonization, efficiency, and policy impact.
- More research is needed on:
  - Coordination performance during EU-wide crises.
  - Informal decision dynamics.
  - Influence of non-institutional stakeholders.
  - Multi-level governance effects beyond formal legal design.

10) Study-use takeaway
- For class/exam analysis, compare:
  - Functional justification vs political bargaining.
  - Formal independence vs practical autonomy.
  - Designed accountability vs real accountability behavior.
