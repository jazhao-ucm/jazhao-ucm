## High-yield study notes

I. EU agencies in the integration model.
   A. Why agencies expanded.
      1. EU shifted toward regulatory governance and technical coordination.
      2. Agencies fill capacity gaps where harmonization needs expertise.
   B. Core creation logics.
      1. Functional, efficiency, credible technical decisions, crisis management.
      2. Political, institutional bargaining among Commission, Council, Parliament, and states.
   C. Agency-network trajectory.
      1. Networks initially central for coordination and soft rule diffusion.
      2. Agencification increases when stable mandates and implementation power are required.

II. Independence, autonomy, accountability.
   A. Independence distinction.
      1. De jure, formal legal design and delegated powers.
      2. De facto, real behavior under political and organizational constraints.
   B. Multi-principal constraint.
      1. Agencies answer to multiple principals, which can reduce operational autonomy.
      2. Commission influence often remains high through staffing, procedure, and board dynamics.
   C. Accountability tension.
      1. Management boards are intended as accountability anchors.
      2. In practice, board size, information asymmetry, and national incentives can weaken strategic oversight.

III. European Council in institutional architecture.
   A. Strategic function.
      1. Sets broad political direction, priorities, and integration momentum.
      2. Works by consensus and high-level bargaining, not ordinary legislation.
   B. Institutional method.
      1. Flexible summit format with strong pre-negotiation and delegated implementation.
      2. Outcomes depend on leadership authority, coalition constraints, and crisis context.

IV. ECB and monetary governance shift.
   A. Baseline model.
      1. ECB created with price-stability mandate and high independence.
      2. Governing Council, Executive Board, General Council structure.
   B. Crisis-era expansion.
      1. Debt-crisis pressures broadened practical intervention role.
      2. Quantitative easing and bank supervision initiatives strengthened central position.
   C. Core debate.
      1. Mandate fidelity versus broader stabilization responsibility.
      2. Independence versus democratic control in macroeconomic steering.

V. Other institutions and agencies from chapter frame.
   A. ECA.
      1. Financial watchdog auditing legality and regularity of EU spending.
      2. Strong agenda pressure power, limited direct enforcement power.
   B. Europol and Eurojust.
      1. Cross-border criminal intelligence and judicial coordination.
      2. Support member-state systems rather than replace national sovereignty.
   C. EESC and CoR.
      1. Advisory representation of sectoral and regional interests.
      2. Consultation relevance with limited binding force.

## Exam answer anchors (quick use)

- **Agency question:** Functional efficiency logic exists, but political design and multi-principal control shape real autonomy.
- **Council question:** European Council gives strategic direction and crisis impetus; legislative and implementation detail sits elsewhere.
- **ECB question:** Built as an inflation-focused independent central bank, then expanded operationally under crisis pressure.
- **Accountability question:** Formal mechanisms are broad, but practical accountability quality varies by board behavior and institutional incentives.
