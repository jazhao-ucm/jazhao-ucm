## Formal outline (Introduction to the EU, Week 11)

I. European Council as strategic center.
   A. Institutional role.
      1. Heads of government set broad political direction and priorities.
      2. Day-to-day detail remains with Commission, Council of the EU, and Parliament.
   B. Core operating style.
      1. Consensus-focused summit logic.
      2. High informality, flexibility, and delegation of technical follow-through.
   C. Political significance.
      1. Launches major integration initiatives and treaty momentum.
      2. Effectiveness depends on preparation, leadership, and coalition dynamics.

II. European Central Bank in EU power balance.
   A. Foundational mandate.
      1. Created in 1998 for eurozone monetary policy.
      2. Single mandate emphasized price stability.
   B. Organizational structure.
      1. Governing Council sets monetary direction.
      2. Executive Board runs daily operations.
      3. General Council links euro and non-euro members.
   C. Crisis-era evolution.
      1. Debt-crisis response expanded practical influence.
      2. Quantitative easing and "whatever it takes" raised policy reach.
      3. Ongoing tension, mandate interpretation versus broader macro stabilization.

III. European Court of Auditors and financial accountability.
   A. Oversight function.
      1. Audits legality and regularity of EU revenue and expenditure.
      2. Reviews budget management and reports to Commission, Council, Parliament.
   B. Political effect.
      1. Findings can pressure institutional spending and accounting reform.
      2. Repeated concerns, waste, mismanagement, and control weaknesses.
   C. Structural limits.
      1. Audit influence depends on follow-through by political institutions.
      2. Oversight capacity does not automatically guarantee compliance.

IV. Specialized agencies and executive expansion.
   A. Why agencies expanded.
      1. Regulatory state logic required technical and sector-specific capacity.
      2. Agencies and networks emerged to coordinate harmonization across member states.
   B. Agency functions in practice.
      1. Information and expertise production.
      2. Monitoring and implementation support.
      3. Operational coordination in cross-border domains.
   C. Representative examples from readings.
      1. Europol and Eurojust in criminal cooperation.
      2. EIB in long-term development finance support.
      3. EESC and CoR as advisory representation channels.

V. Governance debate, intergovernmental versus supranational.
   A. Design logic.
      1. Agencies presented as technical solutions to policy complexity.
      2. Political bargaining among Commission, Council, Parliament, and states shapes design.
   B. Autonomy question.
      1. De jure independence does not guarantee de facto autonomy.
      2. Multi-principal control often keeps agencies institutionally constrained.
   C. Executive-center dynamic.
      1. Agencies can strengthen Commission-centered implementation patterns.
      2. Boards and staffing links create practical dependence despite formal independence claims.

VI. Accountability and legitimacy tensions.
   A. Management board problem.
      1. National representation can increase control but reduce strategic coherence.
      2. Information asymmetries weaken effective oversight quality.
   B. Vertical and horizontal accountability.
      1. Formal accountability channels exist to multiple EU actors.
      2. Real accountability intensity varies by salience, sector, and institutional incentives.
   C. Open governance issue.
      1. Need stronger evidence on outcomes, not only institutional design.
      2. Need more analysis of stakeholder influence and coordination performance in crises.

VII. Integration takeaway from Session 9.
   A. EU institutional architecture is layered, not singular.
      1. European Council sets strategic direction.
      2. ECB, agencies, and auditors execute or constrain within specialized mandates.
   B. Central analytic lens.
      1. Separate legal competence from practical power.
      2. Track who sets direction, who implements, and who is accountable.

## Quick study summary

- The European Council acts as the strategic board, while other bodies operationalize policy.
- The ECB moved from narrow monetary guardian to broader crisis actor, with mandate debates.
- The ECA is the EU financial watchdog, influential through audit pressure rather than direct command.
- EU agencies add technical capacity but sit inside a political control architecture with mixed autonomy.
- Main exam lens, compare formal design with real decision power and accountability outcomes.
