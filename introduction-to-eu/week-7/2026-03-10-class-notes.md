# Section 1: Summary (AP Level / Accessible)

*Use this section first. Plain language. Short sentences.*

## Key Words
- **Text analysis** = Explain context, main idea, argument, key expressions. Do not just retell the story.
- **International law** = Rules that states agree to follow (UN Charter, Geneva Conventions, etc.).
- **Multilateralism** = Many countries deciding together. Opposite of one country acting alone.
- **EU unity** = EU “speaks with one voice” in foreign policy.
- **Discourse** = How something is talked about (words, frames, concepts).

## Main Ideas (Simple Version)

1. **Exam = short text, deep analysis.**
   - You get 2–3 short excerpts.
   - Your job: give context, explain what is being said, and link to the course.
   - Not an essay; not just summary. Show that you understand the *movie* (whole story) and can place a *scene* (the text) inside it.

2. **Ribera vs von der Leyen = example text.**
   - Ribera publicly disagrees with von der Leyen on Middle East policy.
   - She says: “Respect for international law is a basic premise.”
   - She worries that von der Leyen’s words seem to question international law.
   - She links this to *European security* and to the post‑1945 order.

3. **What issues are in this text?**
   - **International law vs. politics:** Is the EU defending the UN‑based order, or undermining it?
   - **Who speaks for Europe?** Tension between Commission President and other commissioners; need for a common line from European Council + High Representative.
   - **EU unity and credibility:** If top leaders contradict each other in public, partners and adversaries may doubt the EU.
   - **Security framing:** International law is not only “moral” but a security interest for Europe.
   - **Communication and legitimacy:** How leaders talk in public can either strengthen or weaken trust in the EU.

4. **Costa + multilateralism (second text).**
   - Costa defends **multilateralism** and criticises one‑sided US policy.
   - He uses a past example (EU united against US threats to Greenland) to show that unity gives the EU weight.
   - Main theme: Europe must act together and not just follow another power.

5. **Why this matters for the course.**
   - Connects **Block 2 (institutions)**: Who is allowed to speak for the EU externally?
   - Connects **Block 4 (foreign & security)**: EU as a multilateral actor defending international law.
   - Connects Duff’s **“twin executive”**: confusion when there are two presidents and many voices.
   - Shows how current news continues the long history after 1945 of building and defending an international order.

# Section 2: Full Notes (College Prep Academic)

## I. Exam Format and Expectations

### A. Structure
1. ~2 hours (class time slot).
2. 2–3 short texts (could be historical extracts, speeches, news like the Reuters piece).
3. Prompts look like:
   - “Provide context for this statement.”
   - “Identify which debate / author / moment is being discussed.”
   - “What is the specific meaning of *X* (e.g. ‘national’, ‘international law’, ‘multilateralism’) in this context?”
   - “What are the main elements of the discourse? What is similar / different between two texts?”

### B. What the professor wants
1. Show that you can:
   - Place the text in **time** (when) and **space** (where / which conflict).
   - Link it to **blocks**: history, institutions, policies, foreign policy.
   - Identify **key concepts** and use course vocabulary.
2. Not required:
   - “Hard‑core” archival history.
   - Very long essay. About 1–1.25 hours of solid writing is enough.
3. Level:
   - Intro course. Show you know **milestones** and main ideas.
   - You can add extra readings if you know them, but it is not mandatory.

### C. How to study
1. Follow the **chronological blocks** (like in the midterm guide):
   - After WWI / WWII.
   - Founding treaties.
   - Enlargements.
   - Post‑1992 crises and reforms.
2. For each block, know:
   - Key treaties, dates, actors.
   - How they connect to later debates (democratic deficit, twin executive, foreign policy).
3. Think of it as a **movie**:
   - First know the whole story.
   - Then identify the main scenes (Schuman, Rome, Maastricht, Lisbon, etc.).
   - The exam texts are *frames* from that movie.

## II. Example Text 1: Ribera vs von der Leyen (Reuters, 10 March 2026)

### A. Basic information
1. Source: **Reuters** news agency.
2. Date: **10 March 2026**.
3. Actors:
   - **Teresa Ribera** – Vice‑President of the European Commission (Clean, Fair and Competitive Transition).
   - **Ursula von der Leyen** – President of the European Commission.
   - **European Council** and **High Representative** (Kaja Kallas) – will define common EU position.
4. Context: Escalation of conflict in the **Middle East**. Debate on how the EU positions itself.

### B. What Ribera says
1. She openly disagrees with von der Leyen’s speech.
2. She says it is “very dangerous” to enter a debate that seems to question **international law**.
3. She softens the criticism (it was a “thought spoken aloud”) but still calls the wording “not appropriate”.
4. She insists:
   - “Respect for international law is a basic premise.”
   - This is not just **moral**, but also about **European security**.
   - The post‑WWII international order “cannot be left hanging” or put “in a drawer”.

### C. Other issues in the text (beyond disagreement)
1. **Role of institutions**
   - She reminds that the **European Council** defines the common line.
   - This reflects the **twin executive problem**: Commission President vs European Council vs High Representative.
   - Question: who has the legitimacy to speak for “Europe” in foreign policy?
2. **Rule‑of‑law identity**
   - EU presents itself as a community of law.
   - If top leaders seem to relativise international law, it undermines that identity.
3. **Security framing**
   - International law is linked to **European security**, not just values.
   - If the legal order collapses, small and middle powers like EU member states are more vulnerable.
4. **Credibility and soft power**
   - The EU often criticises others (Russia, Israel, US, etc.) in the name of law and human rights.
   - Internal contradictions weaken that soft power.
5. **Public discourse and trust**
   - Disagreements aired in public can:
     - Show pluralism and internal debate.
     - But also create confusion about the EU’s stance.

### D. Why it is important (course angle)
1. Shows how **history since 1945** (creation of the UN, human rights system) is present in today’s debates.
2. Links to **institutional complexity**:
   - Twin executive.
   - Council vs Commission vs High Representative.
3. Links to **foreign policy block**:
   - EU as a defender of multilateralism and the UN system.
4. Provides a **contemporary example** you can use in the exam to illustrate:
   - International law.
   - Democratic deficit and accountability.
   - Unity vs fragmentation.

## III. Example Text 2: Costa and Multilateralism (class reference)

*(Based on the short Costa excerpt you summarised.)*

### A. Main points
1. Costa defends **multilateralism**:
   - Many countries cooperate in international organisations.
   - Against unilateral or purely bilateral deals.
2. He criticises:
   - Lack of critical stance toward US foreign policy.
   - Weak EU response in some crises.
3. He recalls:
   - A moment when the EU reacted unitedly to US threats about **Greenland**.
   - Lesson: unity makes the EU stronger.
4. He concludes:
   - The EU must “speak with one voice” to defend its values and interests.

### B. Issues present
1. **Transatlantic relations**
   - How independent should EU foreign policy be from the US?
2. **Strategic autonomy**
   - Debate on whether the EU should rely less on others and act more as a global actor.
3. **Consistency**
   - If EU defends international law and multilateralism, it should do so in *all* crises, not selectively.
4. **Link to institutions**
   - Again: who organises and expresses that “one voice”? (Council, Commission, High Representative).

## IV. How to Use These Texts in the Exam

### A. Step‑by‑step method
1. **Identify**:
   - Who is speaking? When? In which crisis?
2. **Contextualise**:
   - Link to course blocks (history, institutions, policies, foreign policy).
   - Place inside longer story (post‑1945 order, EU integration, crises).
3. **Analyse discourse**:
   - Key words: “international law”, “multilateralism”, “unity”, “security”.
   - What values and interests are invoked?
4. **Connect**:
   - Compare with readings: Duff, Judt, Krawatzek & Pestel, Gilbert, Kenny & Pearce, Bijsmans…
   - Show you see both the **official story** and **critical perspectives**.

### B. Answering your question
> “What other issues are part of this, and why is it important?”

You can answer:
- It is not only about one disagreement between Ribera and von der Leyen.
- It is also about:
  - The **future of the international legal order**.
  - The **clarity of EU executive power** (twin executive).
  - The EU’s **ability to act as a coherent foreign‑policy actor**.
  - The **credibility** of the EU when it talks about law, rights and security.
- It is important because:
  - If the EU sends mixed messages, both **friends and rivals** will doubt it.
  - If international law is weakened, **Europeans are less safe**.
  - It shows why understanding **institutions + history** is essential to read one short news text.
