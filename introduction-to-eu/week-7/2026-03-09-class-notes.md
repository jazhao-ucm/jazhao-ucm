## III. Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein, Switzerland

### A. EEA (European Economic Area)
- Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein: in single market, not EU.
- 1994: EEA established. Economic and monetary cooperation.
- **Norway:** Referendum no. Fishing, oil. High living standards. Small population.
- **Iceland:** Fishing main industry. Geographically isolated.
- **Liechtenstein:** Small. Banking.

### B. Switzerland
- Not in EU. Banks, chocolate, tax paradise.
- High standards of living. Economically connected but politically outside.

### C. Why stay out?
- Sovereignty. Specific interests (fishing, banking).
- Referendums: populations said no.

## IV. Central and Eastern Europe: Copenhagen Criteria

### A. 1997: Copenhagen criteria
- Clear criteria for membership.
- Political: democracy, rule of law, human rights.
- Economic: market economy, ability to compete.
- Adopt *acquis* (EU law).

### B. Different readiness
- **Slovenia, Czech Republic, etc.:** Met criteria earlier. Joined 2004.
- **Romania, Bulgaria:** Ruled out initially. Still had political/economic issues. “Transition” countries. Joined 2007.
- **Russia:** Special case. Not on track.

### C. Why referendums?
- Populations had to approve accession.
- Spain, Yugoslavia: different salary levels. Romania 90s: minimum wage maybe 300 or less (four digits, not four family).
- Economic union assumes similar living standards for fair competition.
- Investment: Americans put money in West Germany after unification. Harder in Romania—uncertainty, instability.
- **Stability:** Instability = benefits decrease. National capitals first to feel it.
- **Investors need:** Transparency, rule of law, guaranties that policies won’t change in five years.

### D. Post‑accession gaps
- Some countries still didn’t fully meet criteria when they joined.
- Bulgaria, Romania: continued issues (e.g. rule of law, corruption).

## V. Smaller Candidates and Cyprus

### A. Western Balkans
- Albania, North Macedonia, Montenegro, Serbia, Bosnia, Kosovo.
- Former Yugoslavia: complicated. Different configurations over time.

### B. Cyprus
- **Obstacle for Turkey’s accession.**
- Island split: Greek Cypriot (south), Turkish Cypriot (north).
- Conflict since 1974. Like India/Pakistan: population exchanges, people moved.
- EU: Cyprus joined (Greek part). Northern Cyprus: Turkish. Not recognized.
- Cyprus conflict = insurmountable obstacle in Turkey negotiations.

## VI. ECB and Economic Governance

### A. European Central Bank (ECB)
- Frankfurt. Like Federal Reserve in US.
- **Power:** Can dictate economic policy to governments. Proved in Greek crisis.
- **Democratic deficit:** Nobody votes for ECB. Technocrats, not elected.
- Central bank of Europe.

### B. Attempt at European army
- Failed. EDC (European Defence Community) failed in 1950s.
- Defence remains national/NATO.

## VII. 1999 Commission Crisis

- Fraud, corruption, deportation scandals.
- Parliament refused to approve budget (financial management).
- Commission resigned as a body.
- 25% of commissioners asked to resign.
- New Commission. Moment of crisis for EU legitimacy.

## VIII. Constitution (2004) Rejected; Lisbon (2007)

### A. Constitutional treaty
- Ambitious: permanent EU president, foreign minister, etc.
- “Super‑state” above national level.
- Rejected by **France** and **Netherlands** (referendums).

### B. Why France said no?
- Fear of “Polish plumber”: cheap labour from new member states.
- “Make France great again” narrative. Sovereignty.
- Economic competition from enlargement.

### C. Why Netherlands said no?
- Similar concerns. Sovereignty, identity.

### D. Lisbon Treaty as Plan B
- More modest. No full constitution.
- Created: **President of European Council** (not “President of Europe”).
- **High Representative** for foreign affairs.
- **Rolling presidency:** Countries rotate. Not one permanent executive president.
- Limited power: President cannot decide many things alone; must work with member states.

---

## IX. Greek Crisis and “Whatever It Takes”

### A. Greece
- Political choice: austerity. Suffered. Still paying today.
- Banks, risk, public money: “When things go well, you profit. When they go bad, we save you.” Moral hazard.

### B. Spain and Draghi
- Mario Draghi (ECB President): “Whatever it takes.”
- July 2012: ECB would do whatever needed to save euro. Intervene in bond markets.
- Calmed investors. Saved Spanish (and Italian) economy from speculation.
- “Super Mario”: one man, one phrase, market confidence.

### C. Spread and risk
- Spread = risk premium. Higher for weaker economies.
- ECB said: we will assume the risk. Bonds backed by ECB = more reliable.
- Moved integration towards federal dimension (European Monetary Fund proposed).

## XI. NATO, 9/11, Balkan Wars

### A. 9/11 and shift
- Shift towards militarism. Defence and security on agenda.
- 1990s: interventions (Balkan wars, etc.). Now: Ukraine.

### B. EU failure in Yugoslavia
- EU did not react effectively. First failure.
- NATO bombed Serbia (1999). One month. Without it, conflict might have continued.
- **Dayton Agreement:** Bosnia. Separated entities.
- **Kosovo:** Separated from Serbia. With international support.

### C. Yugoslavia: complexity
- Multi‑ethnic. Serbs, Croats, Bosnians, etc. Religious mix (Orthodox, Catholic, Muslim).
- Foreign powers exploited divisions.
- Some feel nostalgia for Yugoslav era (Tito). Others celebrate independence.
- Srebrenica: genocide. EU failure to prevent.

### D. Ukraine (2022)
- Pressure on EU for collective military response.
- International dimension. EU as security actor tested.

## XII. Turkey Accession Debate

### A. Atatürk
- Secularized Turkey. Military. Modernized remnants of Ottoman Empire.
- Inspired by European models. “We are the same people.”

### B. Erdogan
- Criticized for authoritarianism: political prisoners, human rights abuses, quelling opposition.
- Islam: different interpretations (like Protestant vs Catholic). Moderate vs conservative.
- 10 years in power. Complicated relationship with EU.

### C. Reasons FOR membership
- Istanbul: great European city. Economic and cultural heart.
- Geography: bridge Europe–Asia. Energy (Caspian, Central Asia).
- Security: second largest army in NATO. Strategic asset.
- Democracy: membership as incentive for reform.
- Historic ties: Ottoman Empire, Byzantium. “Undeniably European.”

### D. Reasons AGAINST membership
- **Cultural/religious:** Turkey missed shared experiences (WW2, Christian legacy). Overwhelmingly Muslim. “Fundamentally different.”
- **Cyprus:** Insurmountable obstacle. Conflict unresolved.
- **Geography:** 97% of territory in Asia. Borders Syria, Iraq.
- **Economy:** GDP per capita lower. Would need huge transfers. Financing unsustainable.
- **Human rights:** Erdogan’s record. Amnesty International, European Court. Political prisoners, free speech.
- **Population:** 80M+. Would change EU demography and voting power.

### E. “Guest workers”
- Germany: Turkish workers since 1960s–70s. *Gastarbeiter*.
- Book: *Alamanya* (Pulitzer). “Terminal.” Two beers on Sunday.
- Integration, identity, second generation.

## XIII. COVID Response

- States led. EU depends on member states.
- EU cannot act alone in health. States are main source of response.
- **Spain:** No natural gas, no oil. Depends on renewables (wind, solar). Steel dependent on imports.
- **Energy:** External dependence. Ukraine crisis highlighted this.
- COVID: some European funds, but states did the work. Nuanced discussion for later.
