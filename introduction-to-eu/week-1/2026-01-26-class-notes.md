### Course structure (blocks)

### I. Block 1 – History and origins

1. Timeline: before and after the Treaty of Rome; World Wars (world-wide, Europe at the centre); how the Union started.
2. Emphasis on economics and money: the first economic union (e.g. coal and steel) to avoid tariffs and promote cooperation (Schuman Plan, etc.).
3. Reference to Schengen: many countries, free movement, economics “outside” (external dimension).
4. Goal: understand the logic and milestones, not just dates.

### II. Block 2 – Institutions

1. Described as “even more complex” and very important.
2. Four parts:
   a. **Executive** (two bodies: European Council and European Commission—“twin executive”).
   b. **Legislative** (European Parliament and Council of the EU).
   c. **Judiciary** (Court of Justice of the EU).
   d. **Other bodies:** e.g. European Central Bank, Court of Auditors, agencies (EUIPO and other agencies spread across Europe).
3. Institutions are a major focus; not the “most fascinating” for everyone but essential.

### III. Block 3 – Policies and current challenges

1. Presented as the “most relevant” for many: challenges, conflicts, tensions between member states and with third countries; power and policy (e.g. euro, identity, enlargement, migration).
2. Euro: e.g. when Spain left the peseta and adopted the euro—narrated as “now we really are European.”
3. Identity and membership: enlargement, European citizenship, migration; recent debates (e.g. visas) that materially affect daily life.
4. Crises (e.g. oil) discussed as affecting not only “structure” but the street level; EU and domestic politics interact.

### IV. Block 4 – Foreign and security policy

1. Guest session with an expert from the department.
2. “Language of power”: foreign policy and security policy (e.g. CFSP, defence, “European security”).
3. Comparison with the USA (e.g. different approaches to Mexico, Ukraine).
4. Discussion of populism, deindustrialization, “blue collar” vs “white collar”, and democratic backsliding—studied in Spain, France, and the USA.

### Assessment

### V. Midterm

1. Format: In-person; short texts (e.g. two texts).
2. Task: Not summarising the text, but analysing—e.g. “What is the main idea? What are they arguing? What is the key expression?”
3. Texts (written or possibly audiovisual, e.g. a speech) are strictly related to themes from the first units.
4. Focus on understanding and critical reading, not memorisation of dates.

### VI. Final exam

1. In-person test.
2. “Not playing with language” (like a driving test)—you must pay attention to both language and content.
3. Not trivial recall (e.g. “Who was X?”) but questions that require reflection and argument.
4. Balanced across the units; possible to pass without having studied every chapter in depth, but coverage helps.

### VII. Presentations (readings)

1. Each session: two readings; one or two students present per text (e.g. 20–25 minutes each).
2. Goal: Present key ideas and open discussion; you don’t have to memorise—emphasis on debate and “bringing ideas to the table.”
3. Can use PowerPoint or any other format.
4. Participation is a competency and is valued; the more people engage, the better the session.
5. Sign-up: a table (or list) is used to decide who presents which text and when.

### VIII. Essay

1. Length: 3,500 words.
2. Topic: Any topic related to the course; must be agreed with the professor in advance (e.g. “I was thinking of studying X”).
3. Recommendations: Choose something you are interested in; start early; propose the topic as soon as possible.
4. Short presentation: 5 minutes on your essay (question, approach, progress) so the professor can give feedback.
5. Structure: Clear structure (e.g. introduction, development, conclusion); use of secondary sources and proper citation (academic standards, EU websites, etc.). EU official website is a very useful source.
6. Tone: Not to “prove” a single view but to develop an informed, balanced argument (e.g. including different perspectives, e.g. trade unionist vs employer views).
7. Critical discourse analysis is acceptable if done with balance and rigour.
