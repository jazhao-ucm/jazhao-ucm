## Understanding Europe's complexity

### I. Multiple overlapping unions

A. The EU is **not a union of countries** but a **union of previous unions**—this adds complexity

1. Council of Europe (not the same as EU Council):
   a. Includes countries like Turkey, Russia (historically), etc.
   b. Focus: human rights, democracy, rule of law.
   c. Not defined by free movement (Schengen).

2. European Union (proper):
   a. 27 member states.
   b. Includes customs union, single market, Schengen (for most), euro (for most).

3. Customs union:
   a. Includes more countries than EU membership (e.g. Turkey has customs union but not full membership).

4. Schengen area:
   a. Free movement zone (no border checks).
   b. Includes some non-EU countries: Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway, Switzerland.
   c. Excludes some EU countries: Ireland, Cyprus, Bulgaria, Romania (partial).

5. Eurozone:
   a. Countries using the euro.
   b. One country outside: Denmark (opt-out).

Example: Denmark is EU member, part of Schengen, but NOT part of eurozone.

B. Challenge
1. Harmonizing interests when countries are members of some but not all of these systems.

## Four freedoms of the EU

A. According to the Treaty text, the EU guarantees

1. Free movement of people:
   a. Citizens can travel, work, study, live anywhere in the EU.
   b. Applies to EU citizens and their families.

2. Free movement of goods:
   a. No tariffs or customs duties between member states.
   b. Common standards and regulations.

3. Free movement of services:
   a. Businesses can operate across borders.
   b. Professionals can provide services in any member state.

4. Free movement of capital:
   a. Money can move freely.
   b. Investment across borders.

Additional: Cooperation in justice and home affairs (security, police cooperation).

## Economic and social cohesion

1. Purpose: Reduce economic disparities between regions/countries.
2. Mechanism: Funds (e.g. structural funds, cohesion funds) to support:
   a. Infrastructure (e.g. railways, transport).
   b. Regional development.
   c. Social development.
   d. Environmental projects.

Example: Upgrading railway systems in less developed regions to match standards elsewhere.

A. Reality
1. Two-speed Europe—some regions/countries are more developed than others. Funds aim to reduce these gaps, but disparities remain.

---

## Membership requirements (Copenhagen Criteria)

A. To join the EU, countries must meet

1. Democracy:
   a. Stable democratic institutions.
   b. Rule of law.
   c. Respect for human rights, including minority rights.

2. Market economy:
   a. Functioning market economy.
   b. Capacity to cope with competitive pressure within the EU.

3. Acquis communautaire:
   a. Ability to adopt and implement all EU laws and regulations.

Note: These are "elusive" criteria—interpretation can vary. Recent emphasis: rule of law (e.g. Poland, Hungary cases).

## Historical timeline

### VII. 1951 – Treaty of Paris

1. Purpose: Secure peace between France and Germany (and others).
2. Mechanism: Coal and Steel Community—putting war industries under common control.
3. Logic: If countries control each other's war resources together, war becomes harder.
4. Founding six: France, Germany, Italy, Belgium, Netherlands, Luxembourg.

### VIII. 1957 – Treaty of Rome

1. Created European Economic Community (EEC).
2. Goal: Common market—wider market for goods and services, no tariffs.
3. Example: French agriculture products can be sold in Germany without tariffs; fair competition.

### IX. 1970s – Economic challenges

1. Oil crisis: Price shocks (Middle East conflicts).
2. Stagflation: Economic stagnation + inflation.
3. Euroscepticism: Some skepticism about EU's future (similar to post-2008).

### X. 1985 – Single European Act

1. Goal: Create a single market (not just common market).
2. Result: Positive impact on European economy—GDP growth, increased trade.
3. Sectors: Some sectors benefited more than others.

### XI. 1995 – Schengen Agreement enters into force

1. Signed: June 14, 1985 (Schengen, Luxembourg) by 5 countries.
2. Effect: Open internal borders, stronger external borders.
3. Today: 29 members (all EU except Ireland, Cyprus, plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway, Switzerland).
4. Impact: ~3.5 million people cross borders daily; ~7 million live in one country, work in another.
5. Personal example: Professor's experience—as a child, border checks (like US-Mexico border); now seamless travel.

A. **Reality check:** Free movement works well for some (e.g. white Europeans) but not for all
1. External borders: not always democratic (e.g. cooperation with non-democratic countries).
2. Internal discrimination: Roma people, minorities face barriers despite legal rights.
3. Roma: Nomadic people, in Europe since ~1300s (600+ years), affected by restrictions.

### XII. 1999/2002 – Euro currency

1. Purpose: Make life easier for consumers and travelers; facilitate business.
2. Reality: Now mostly digital payments; cash less common.
3. Motto on coins: "United in diversity" (but originally only in some languages, not all).

### XIII. 2004–2013 – Eastern enlargement

1. Context: After Cold War, collapse of Soviet Union.
2. Challenge: Integrating former communist countries (market economy transition, democratic transition).
3. Process: Step-by-step (e.g. Romania, Bulgaria had restrictions that were gradually lifted).
4. Requirements: Countries had to adopt EU laws, align with EU standards.
5. Result: 27 member states today; people can embrace more open, democratic systems.

### XIV. 2007 – Lisbon Treaty

1. Purpose: Replace all previous treaties; streamline institutions.
2. Key changes:
   a. More power to European Parliament (directly elected).
   b. More transparency (easier access to documents, meetings).
   c. Clarified roles of institutions.
   d. Citizens' initiative (petition mechanism).
3. Context: World became more complicated (climate change, energy, digital issues); EU needed to adapt.

## Democracy requirements – discussion

Question: Can countries with monarchies be EU members?

A. **Answer:** Yes—monarchies are compatible with democracy if
1. Parliament is elected regularly.
2. Monarch is constitutional (ceremonial role).
3. No hereditary succession to executive power.

B. Examples of EU monarchies
1. UK (left EU, but was member).
2. Netherlands, Sweden, Denmark, Spain, Belgium, Luxembourg.

Question: Can countries with no free press join?

Answer: No—free press is part of democracy requirement.

Question: Can countries with one-party systems join?

Answer: No—democracy requires pluralism, multiple parties, free elections.

C. Recent cases
1. Poland, Hungary: Rule of law concerns; EU using mechanisms to pressure compliance.
2. Romania, Bulgaria: Had restrictions that were gradually lifted; now full members.

## Key Takeaways

1. EU is complex—multiple overlapping systems (EU, Schengen, eurozone, customs union).
2. History matters—understanding pre-1945 Europe is essential to understand integration.
3. Four freedoms are central but have limitations in practice.
4. Membership requires democracy, market economy, ability to adopt EU law.
5. Enlargement has been gradual, with step-by-step integration for newer members.
6. Lisbon Treaty (2007) modernized EU institutions and increased transparency.
