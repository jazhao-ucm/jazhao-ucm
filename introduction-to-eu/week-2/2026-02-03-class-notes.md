## Post-WWII context (1945–1949)

1. Europe in 1945: Devastated; millions displaced; the “world of yesterday” (Zweig) was gone; people were looking for ways to cooperate.
2. Two main goals of early cooperation:
3. Secure peace after two world wars (no repeat of nationalism and war).
4. Rebuild the economy (reconstruction, stability).
5. First five years (1945–1949): A mix of political and economic initiatives; no single “European Union” yet—rather intergovernmental cooperation and the first supranational experiment (ECSC would come in 1951–1952).
6. Key tension: France and Germany had been at war repeatedly (e.g. over Alsace-Lorraine); the challenge was to embed cooperation in economic and institutional arrangements so that war became unthinkable.

---

## Three strands of cooperation

Early European cooperation developed along three lines—economic, political, and military—that can be identified separately even though they overlapped in time and purpose.

| Strand    | Focus                 | Examples                                        |
| --------- | --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Economic  | Trade, aid, recovery  | Marshall Plan, OEEC, then ECSC (coal & steel)    |
| Political | Assembly, human rights | Council of Europe (Strasbourg)                 |
| Military  | Defense, deterrence   | Western Union (1948), NATO (1949), EDC (failed) |

## 1. Council of Europe (1949)

1. Where: Strasbourg (France)—on the border with Germany; symbolically important.
2. Ideas behind it: Federalist ideas; the “European idea”; more than a simple intergovernmental forum—an assembly where representatives of member states could discuss issues.
3. Key features (from the statute):
   a. **Assembly** for discussion between states.
   b. **Committee of Ministers** (executive dimension).
   c. **Call for human rights**—one of the first postwar efforts to codify human rights at European level (later led to the European Convention on Human Rights, ECHR).
4. Historic clip shown in class: Signing of the Statute of the Council of Europe; rhetoric of “united Europe,” “age of hope and reason,” “foundations of something new in European life”; “the ideal cherished by all well-known men is still a long way off, but … groundwork has been done.”
5. Note: Council of Europe is not the same as the EU Council or the European Council; it is a separate organisation (47 members today), focused on human rights, democracy, and rule of law.

---

## 2. Economic strand: Marshall Plan and OEEC

1. Marshall Plan (1947–1948): US aid for European reconstruction; huge sums of money arriving in Europe.
2. Problem of distribution: Who gets what? Countries had to agree on how to share the aid—so they had to cooperate (reduce barriers, coordinate) to receive it.
3. OEEC (1948): Organization for European Economic Co-operation; created in Paris with 16 European countries to manage Marshall Plan aid and coordinate reconstruction.
   a. **Germany:** Not included at first (1948); later joined (West Germany). Context: distrust after the war; only when the Schuman Plan proposed pooling coal and steel did France accept integrating Germany.
4. Result: Helped double the output of Western Europe (not all of Europe—Spain, Portugal, etc. were outside or different); mainly West-Central Europe (France, Benelux, Italy, later West Germany).
5. Aim (from US perspective): Rebuild stable, capitalist economies; contain Soviet influence (“helping to cover Soviet expansion”).

---

## 3. From OEEC to Schuman Plan: customs union and “supranational” step

1. Ideas in the air: (1) Customs union / free trade area; (2) Supranational body to manage key sectors.
2. Difference from after WWI: After the First World War, Germany was forced to pay reparations alone (“you lost, you pay”). After WWII, the idea was multilateralisation—shared responsibility and shared institutions (e.g. common management of coal and steel), not one country singled out.
3. Schuman Declaration (9 May 1950):
   a. **Who:** Robert Schuman (French foreign minister); inspired by Jean Monnet.
   b. **Proposal:** Place **coal and steel** (war industries) under a **common High Authority**—not under any single country, so that no country could rearm in secret. “If we control coal and steel together, we can prevent war.”
   c. **Countries:** France, West Germany, Italy, Belgium, Netherlands, Luxembourg (the “Six”).
   d. **Date:** **9 May 1950**—celebrated today as **Europe Day**.
4. ECSC (European Coal and Steel Community): Established by the Treaty of Paris (1951).
   a. **Why it matters:** First time states **handed over** part of their sovereignty to a **supranational** body (the High Authority).
   b. **Structure:** High Authority (supranational); **Council of Ministers** (representing states)—this model became the basis for the future **EEC** (European Economic Community).
   c. **UK:** Did **not** join; did not believe the Community would work. When the ECSC proved successful in the 1950s, it seemed natural to extend the principle to the whole economy (Treaty of Rome, 1957)—but the UK stayed out for years.

---

## 4. US attitude toward European integration

1. Question in class: Was the US supportive or opposed to the Coal and Steel Union (more competitive Europe)?
2. Answer: The US supported it. Reasons: (1) Cold War—stronger, united Western Europe against the USSR; (2) Market—prosperous Europe was good for US trade; (3) Europeans were not seen primarily as rivals but as allies. Later the picture becomes more “two superpowers” (US and USSR), but in this period US support was important.

---

## 5. Military strand: NATO (1949)

1. Context: Berlin blockade (1948); communist takeovers in Eastern Europe; communist victory in China (1949); fear of Soviet expansion.
2. NATO (North Atlantic Treaty Organization): Founded April 1949, Washington DC.
   a. **Members (original 12):** USA, Canada, UK, France, Belgium, Netherlands, Luxembourg, Norway, Iceland, Denmark, Italy, Portugal.
   b. **Principle:** **Collective defense**—“one for all, all for one”; an attack on one is an attack on all.
   c. **Aim:** Deter Soviet aggression; prevent a return of nationalist militarism in Europe; “keep the Russians out, the Americans in.”
3. Later enlargements: Greece and Turkey (1952), West Germany (1955), Spain (1982); more after the end of the Cold War.
4. France: Withdrew from the integrated military command in 1966 but remained in the Alliance.
5. USSR: In 1954 proposed joining NATO to “preserve peace”; NATO rejected—members feared the USSR would try to weaken the alliance. Warsaw Pact (1955) was the Soviet response (Eastern bloc military alliance).
6. Today: NATO remains the main Western military alliance; with the war in Ukraine, debate continues on enlargement and “NATO expansion to the East.”

---

## 6. European Defense Community (EDC) and its failure

1. US pressure: USA pushed for European rearmament (e.g. in the Korean War context)—Europe had to contribute to its own defense.
2. French dilemma: How to rearm (West) Germany without allowing a national German army? Solution proposed: a European army under supranational control—so German soldiers would be part of a European force, not a German one.
3. Pleven Plan (1950): Named after French PM René Pleven; proposed a European Defense Community (EDC)—a single European army, common institutions, no national German army.
   a. **Federalist** idea: like ECSC but for defense.
   b. **Opposition in France:** Nationalists argued “we cannot put our army under supranational control”; sovereignty and national identity (France had a large colonial empire and a strong sense of military independence). UK was not fully committed either (“we don’t want to be part of a European army”).
   c. **Vote in French National Assembly (1954):** EDC was **rejected** (e.g. 264 in favour, 319 against—or similar margin). The treaty was never ratified.
4. Consequence: Political/military integration stopped at that point. Integration continued only on the economic track—“step by step,” via the ECSC and later the EEC. No European army to this day (though there are EU defense initiatives).

## 7. Cartoon discussion (EDC / European army)

1. Cartoon shown: Soldiers in uniform from different countries; multiple languages; dictionaries; “Tower of Babel” feel—everyone speaking different languages, possible confusion.
2. Interpretations in class:
   a. **Satire of integration:** Military would be ineffective because of language barriers (marching but reading dictionaries to translate).
   b. **Germans “taking orders from everyone”:** Germans following American, French, British orders—no national German command; Germans rearming but under foreign control.
   c. **Celebration with paper/treaty, but “Germany rearming behind”:** Surface: signing treaties, peace; underneath: fear that Germany was rearming anyway (ghost of WWII).
3. Professor’s point: The cartoon reflects fear and scepticism—especially in France—about (1) a European army that might not work (Babel), and (2) the risk of German military power returning. “Bury the hatchet” (reconcile with Germany) to make people safer, but “the wolf” (strong Germany) was still a worry. The EDC was meant to control German rearmament, but the idea was not convincing enough to pass in France.

## Key Takeaways

1. Post-1945 cooperation had three strands: economic (Marshall, OEEC, ECSC), political (Council of Europe, human rights), and military (NATO, failed EDC).
2. ECSC was the first supranational transfer of power (coal and steel); it became the model for the EEC (Council of Ministers + supranational body).
3. 9 May 1950 (Schuman Declaration) is Europe Day—symbol of peace through economic integration.
4. NATO provided military security; EDC (European army) failed due to sovereignty fears and distrust, so integration continued only economically “step by step.”
5. UK stayed out of ECSC; US supported European integration for Cold War and economic reasons.

- **Ethos:** Founding fathers; NATO; Council of Europe; "no return to war" as collective commitment.
- **Logos:** Three-strand structure; timeline; ECSC as model; cause-and-effect of EDC failure.
