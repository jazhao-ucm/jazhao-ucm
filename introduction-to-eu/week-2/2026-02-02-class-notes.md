## Context: migration and regularization (Spain)

1. Recent development: Spain (and other EU countries) have implemented regularization of migrants—more or less large-scale, depending on the period.
2. Spain has had three major regularizations (e.g. 2005, one in the 1990s, one in the 1980s).
3. Personal note (professor): As a child, in a large public school with hundreds of kids, there were very few non-Spanish pupils; now in some neighbourhoods more than half of students are not “white”—Spanish society has changed a lot.
4. Political dimension: The current regularization was negotiated between the government and a small party; “iron fist” rhetoric had been used by politicians for years; now a left-wing government managed to agree with the movement on ambitious terms.
5. Equality in the debate: Different actors invoke “equality” in different ways:
   a. **Pro-regularization:** Equal treatment for people who work and contribute.
   b. **Far right:** “What about nationals who work hard? This is not equal treatment”; “close the borders.”
6. Example (France): One of the leaders of the far right (Rassemblement National) said “please don’t call us the National Front anymore” (old name from the 1920s); they present themselves as “the people,” not only “white and blond”—shows how the discourse is more complex than simple labels.

## Stefan Zweig, *The World of Yesterday* (1942)

### I. Why this text matters

1. Testimony of a Europe that disappeared as a result of war, nationalism, and persecution.
2. Connects personal experience with history—the atmosphere in which the idea of “Europe” started to be rethought after the catastrophes of the first half of the 20th century.
3. Relevant for understanding minorities (e.g. Roma, Jewish communities) and colonial dimensions that will be discussed later in the course.

### II. Key quotes and themes

A. Uprooting and loss (1945 preface)

1. “I have indeed been torn from all of my roots, even from the earth that nourished them more entirely than most in our times.”
2. Born 1881 in the Habsburg monarchy (which no longer exists on the map).
3. Grew up in Vienna (international metropolis for 2,000 years); had to leave “like a thief in the night” when it became a “national German” city.
4. His literary work in German was burned in the country where his books had millions of readers.
5. “So I belong nowhere, now. I am a stranger or at the most a guest everywhere.”
6. “Even the true home of my heart’s desire, Europe, is lost to me after twice tearing itself suicidally to pieces in fratricidal wars.”
7. “Never … has a generation fallen from such intellectual heights as ours to such moral depths.”

B. Xenophobia and bureaucracy after the First World War

1. Before the war: You could get on a means of transport and get off without being asked anything—no permits, no visas.
2. After the war: “Pathological distrust”; “offensive red tape”; borders became real.
3. “Humiliations previously devised for criminals were now inflicted upon every traveler”:
   a. Photographs (front, left profile, full face).
   b. Hair cropped so ears were visible.
   c. Fingerprints (thumbs, then all ten digits).
   d. Health certificates, police certificates of good standing.
   e. Letters of recommendation, invitations, addresses of relatives, moral and financial guarantees.
   f. Forms in triplicate and quadruplicate.
4. Interpretation: Borders and strict controls are not a natural given; they were a historical product of nationalism and fear after WWI (and later Nazism).

C. Nostalgia and the “golden age of security”

1. Zweig describes a period (roughly 30–40 years before WWI) of “private belief that the world is a good place.”
2. Belief in “a German, a European, an international conscience”; “a certain degree of progress” and “human standards.”
3. Critical note: “The past is a foreign country”—nostalgia can idealize; the “golden age” was not golden for everyone (e.g. colonies, workers, women).
4. In the 1930s (1933–1934) he had contemplated the possibility of a “hundred per cent” [catastrophe] in Germany and Austria—the book is also a warning about how quickly democracy can be undermined by fear and authoritarian movements.

D. June 28, 1914

1. The assassination (Sarajevo) that “in a single second … destroyed the world security” in which people had lived.
2. Contrast between “the world of yesterday” (the title of the book) and the world after that event—displacement, loss, tragedy.
3. Temporal dimension: The EU project is in part a “future of the past”—a response to that destruction; “the opposite of what was [Europe] in 1945.”

### III. Discussion points from class

1. Democracy and equality: Can you have democracy without equality? (e.g. India: large democracy but caste system; formal vs substantive equality.)
2. Formal vs substantive: Romania in the EU—Romanians did not have full free-movement rights until recently; “Romanian” and “Spanish” citizens were not treated the same in practice. So “democracy” and “equality” are not just formal labels; they have to be implemented.
3. Illiberal democracy: Some scholars speak of “illiberal” variants—e.g. restrictions on minorities, LGBTQ+ rights, in Poland or elsewhere—where you still have elections but not full liberal rights.

## EU values: universal or European?

### IV. The question

1. Are dignity, freedom, equality, democracy, rule of law, human rights universal values, or are they European / Western / historically specific?

### V. Student responses (summary)

1. Universal: They should be given and respected for all people; we have come to recognize their importance through history, but they apply to everyone—not “invented” by Europe.
2. European construction with bias: What we label “human rights” is limited and skewed; very European in nature; “human rights” has been stripped to a minimum to suit the “machine of states”; Europe has a lot of responsibility for the current framing (not necessarily credit).
3. Western narrative: The idea that “the West” or “Western civilization” created these rights is a historical construct; many countries in “the West” are or have been undemocratic or authoritarian; the rhetoric of “the West saving others” is problematic.
4. Historical contingency: Hierarchy, class, religion—people came together and set standards; what we call “human rights” or “democracy” today is one historical outcome, not the only possible one.
5. Cold War: The moment when “human” and “human rights” were defined in a global way was heavily shaped by the Cold War and the need to present a “universal” front.

### VI. Professor’s points

1. Ancient Athens: Often cited as birthplace of democracy, but only male citizens had political rights (maybe ~15% of population); not women, not slaves. So “democracy” from the start was not full equality.
2. Greek ideas and the “East”: A lot of what we attribute to “Greek” or “European” thought has roots or parallels elsewhere (e.g. Taoism, Buddhism, other traditions). We tend to tell the story as if Europe “invented” democracy and rights.
3. Rights of “Man”: The French Revolution spoke of “rights of man”—the name itself excludes women. Napoleon spread a legal code that was a step toward equality in some ways but also consolidated inequality (e.g. property, gender).
4. Conclusion: The meanings we give to “democracy,” “human rights,” “equality” are contested and historically shaped; they are not self-evidently universal, but that doesn’t mean other societies don’t have their own concepts of justice or dignity.

## Freedom vs security

1. Current trend: Strong discourse that we must sacrifice some freedom for security (e.g. airports, surveillance, data collection).
2. EU: No clear, single rule yet on data collection—technology moves faster than regulation.
3. Reflection: From a certain perspective, society is “free” (e.g. consumer choice, delivery, convenience), but “at what price?”—surveillance, control, less privacy.
4. The relationship between freedom and security is problematic and must be constantly negotiated; there is no fixed “right” balance.

## Key Takeaways

1. Zweig illustrates the collapse of a certain “European” world and the rise of borders, xenophobia, and bureaucracy—backdrop for why “Europe” was reimagined after 1945.
2. Borders and free movement are not natural; they are political and historical choices (compare pre-WWI, interwar, and today’s Schengen).
3. EU values (dignity, freedom, equality, democracy, rule of law, human rights) are debated as universal vs European; their implementation (e.g. equality for Roma, for newer member states) is often incomplete.
4. Freedom and security are in tension; how they are balanced is a central political and legal question for the EU and member states.
