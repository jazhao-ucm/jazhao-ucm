# Introduction to the EU — Class Notes
## February 2, 2026 — Week 2

**Topic:** Zweig; borders; theory vs. practice; migration and regularization; EU values.

---

I. Theory vs. Practice
   A. Key principle
      1. Theory never equals practice
      2. Reality is organic, messy, collective
      3. Course focuses more on practice
   B. Political movements
      1. Impregnated with meaning
      2. Theory cannot fully capture

II. Migration and Regularization — Spain Context
   A. Background
      1. Spain has implemented regularization of migrants
      2. Three major regularizations (1980s, 1990s, 2005)
      3. Society changed dramatically
   B. Political dimension
      1. Negotiated between government and small party
      2. "Iron fist" rhetoric used for years
      3. Left-wing government achieved agreement
   C. "Equality" invoked differently
      1. Pro-regularization: equal treatment for workers
      2. Far right: "what about nationals?"
      3. Same word, opposite meanings

III. Stefan Zweig — *The World of Yesterday* (1942)
   A. Why text matters
      1. Testimony of Europe that disappeared
      2. War, nationalism, persecution destroyed his world
      3. Connects personal experience with history
   B. Relevant for understanding
      1. Minorities (Roma, Jewish communities)
      2. Colonial dimensions
      3. How catastrophes shaped "Europe" idea

IV. Zweig — Uprooting and Loss
   A. Personal experience
      1. Born 1881 in Habsburg monarchy (now gone)
      2. Grew up in Vienna (2,000-year metropolis)
      3. Left "like a thief in the night"
   B. Key quotes
      1. "Torn from all of my roots"
      2. "I belong nowhere now"
      3. "Europe lost to me after fratricidal wars"
   C. Moral dimension
      1. "Never has a generation fallen from such intellectual heights to such moral depths"

V. Zweig — Xenophobia and Bureaucracy After WWI
   A. Before the war
      1. Travel without permits or visas
      2. No questions asked
      3. Freedom of movement
   B. After the war
      1. "Pathological distrust"
      2. "Offensive red tape"
      3. Borders became real barriers
   C. New requirements
      1. Photographs (front, left profile, full face)
      2. Fingerprints (all ten digits)
      3. Health certificates
      4. Police certificates
      5. Letters of recommendation
      6. Forms in triplicate and quadruplicate

VI. Zweig — Freedom vs. Security
   A. His position
      1. Freedom matters more than security
      2. Willing to risk danger for freedom
      3. Human dignity above bureaucratic control
   B. Key quote
      1. "I always felt as if the shadow of this ignominy rested on the creature asking them"
      2. Bureaucracy dehumanizes
   C. Historical lesson
      1. Strict controls = product of nationalism
      2. Fear after WWI created borders
      3. Not natural or inevitable

VII. Far Right Discourse — Complexity
   A. France example
      1. Rassemblement National (formerly National Front)
      2. Rebranding to seem mainstream
      3. Present as "the people"
   B. Key insight
      1. Discourse more complex than labels
      2. Same terms used for opposite goals
      3. "Equality" can mean different things

VIII. Implications for EU Study
   A. Theory vs. practice
      1. EU ideals vs. EU reality
      2. Four freedoms on paper vs. in practice
   B. Historical context matters
      1. Pre-1945 shapes post-1945
      2. Zweig's experience shows stakes
   C. Migration as ongoing issue
      1. Regularization debates continue
      2. Values contested across spectrum
