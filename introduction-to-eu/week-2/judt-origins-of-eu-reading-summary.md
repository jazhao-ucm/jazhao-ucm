**Key insight:** Judt emphasizes *logos* (pragmatic causes, institutional logic) while building *ethos* through careful historical argument. *Pathos* appears in the backdrop—war, reconciliation, fear of repetition—rather than in overt emotional language.

## Summary & Key Points

### I. Pre-WWII Context

- The idea of European union predates WWII, with customs unions and pan-European visions emerging in the 19th and early 20th centuries.
- WWI intensified calls for European unity, with leaders like Aristide Briand and Charles Gide advocating for overcoming rivalries and economic collaboration.
- The interwar period saw attempts at economic cooperation (Steel Pact, customs unions), but these were limited by crises and national interests.

**Rhetorical note:** Judt establishes *logos*—a causal chain (ideas → WWI → interwar attempts → failure) that sets up why postwar integration would differ.

---

### II. Post-WWII Developments

- WWII and Nazi occupation paradoxically unified much of Europe technically, but under authoritarian rule. Postwar, the desire for a peaceful, collaborative Europe grew stronger.
- Churchill, Monnet, and Schuman were key advocates for European unity, emphasizing partnership between France and Germany as foundational.
- The Council of Europe (1949) was an early forum for European conversation, but lacked real power.
- The Benelux Agreement (1948) was a successful customs union among Belgium, Luxembourg, and the Netherlands.
- The Marshall Plan and postwar recovery highlighted the need to integrate Germany into the European economy, balancing security and prosperity.

**Rhetorical note:**
- **Ethos:** Named figures (Churchill, Monnet, Schuman) lend authority.
- **Pathos:** "Desire for peaceful, collaborative Europe" taps into postwar trauma and hope.
- **Logos:** Institutional sequence (Council of Europe → Benelux → Marshall Plan) shows step-by-step logic.

---

### III. The Schuman Plan and ECSC

- The Schuman Plan (1950) proposed joint management of French and German coal and steel, leading to the European Coal and Steel Community (ECSC, 1951) with six founding states.
- The ECSC was a political solution to Franco-German hostility, not just an economic project.
- Christian Democratic leaders from border regions played a key role.
- Britain declined to join the ECSC and later the EEC, preferring national control and global trade links over supranational European integration.

**Rhetorical note:**
- **Logos:** Economic instrument (coal/steel) as political tool—cause-and-effect argument.
- **Ethos:** "Political solution" frames Judt as interpreter of motives, not just chronicler.
- **Pathos:** Franco-German hostility and its resolution carry emotional weight beneath the institutional story.

---

### IV. Treaty of Rome and EEC

- The Treaty of Rome (1957) established the European Economic Community (EEC) and Euratom, aiming for a common market and economic integration among the ECSC six.
- The EEC was grounded in weakness, not strength, but mutual self-interest and the lessons of war drove cooperation.
- The creation of the EEC marked a shift toward economic integration as the practical path for European unity, with free movement of goods, currencies, and labor as core goals.

**Rhetorical note:**
- **Pathos:** "Grounded in weakness"—appeals to humility, shared need, postwar vulnerability.
- **Logos:** "Practical path" vs. political union—strategic choice with clear reasoning.
- **Ethos:** Judt's critical distance ("grounded in weakness") signals scholarly judgment.

## Context & Analysis

### Judt's Approach

**Analysis:** Judt's account emphasizes the pragmatic, sometimes reluctant, steps toward European integration, shaped by security concerns, economic interests, and the need to overcome historical antagonisms.

**Rhetorical profile:** Heavy on *logos* (pragmatism, institutions, causes); *ethos* through scholarly tone and evidence; *pathos* implicit in the stakes (peace, prosperity, avoiding another war).

---

### Key Insights (Rhetorical Framing)

| Insight | Ethos | Logos | Pathos |
|---------|-------|-------|--------|
| Founding treaties were symbolic and political before fully effective | Judt as interpreter of motives | Institutional development over time | Hope vs. immediate results |
| Britain's exclusion; Christian Democratic role | Diversity of perspectives | Different interests, different choices | National identity, sovereignty |
| Integration driven by practical necessity | Not idealistic alone | Mutual self-interest as engine | Fear of war, desire for stability |
| Economic integration as pragmatic choice | Avoids political difficulties | Practical path to unity | Deferring harder questions |

---

### Key Themes

1. **Pragmatism over Idealism**
   - **Logos:** European integration was driven by practical concerns, not just visionary ideals.
   - **Pathos:** Ideals mattered, but necessity and fear drove action.

2. **Franco-German Reconciliation**
   - **Ethos:** Partnership as moral and political foundation.
   - **Logos:** Structural precondition for wider integration.

3. **Economic Integration as Path**
   - **Logos:** Economic cooperation chosen as practical route to European unity.
   - **Pathos:** Economics as safer, less divisive than politics.

4. **British Reluctance**
   - **Ethos:** Britain's different priorities and perspective.
   - **Logos:** National control and global trade vs. supranational commitment.

5. **Weakness as Foundation**
   - **Pathos:** Mutual need, postwar vulnerability.
   - **Logos:** Weakness as driver of cooperation, not strength.
