## Rhetorical Framework (Ethos, Logos, Pathos)

| Appeal | Meaning | Seidel's Use |
|--------|---------|--------------|
| **Ethos** | Credibility, authority, trust | Institutional history; scholarly synthesis; balanced account of crises |
| **Logos** | Logic, structure, cause-and-effect | Chronological narrative; institutional development; economic vs. political outcomes |
| **Pathos** | Emotion, stakes, human dimension | Crises, stalemate, compromise; tension between federalism and intergovernmentalism |

**Key insight:** Seidel emphasizes *logos* (institutions, timelines, outcomes) and *ethos* (authoritative institutional history). *Pathos* appears in the drama of crises—Empty Chair, de Gaulle's resistance, Luxembourg Compromise—and in the contrast between economic success and political deadlock.

## Summary & Key Points

- The period 1958–1968 saw the early development of the Common Market and the establishment of key EEC institutions
- Customs union was achieved ahead of schedule in 1968, marking a major success for the European Community
- The 1960s were marked by political crises, particularly the Empty Chair Crisis (1965–1966) and the Luxembourg Compromise
- De Gaulle's leadership shaped this period, with his resignation in 1969 marking a turning point
- The Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) was established as a key policy, though controversial
- This period established the institutional framework that would guide European integration for decades

**Rhetorical note:** Seidel sets up a *logos* contrast—economic success vs. political crisis—that structures the chapter. *Pathos* in the "crises" and "compromise"; *ethos* in the institutional focus.

---

## Main Arguments

### I. Institutional Development

The early years of the Common Market saw the establishment and consolidation of the four main EEC institutions: the Commission, Council of Ministers, Parliament, and Court of Justice. Each institution developed its role and powers during this period.

**Rhetorical profile:**
- **Logos:** Institutional structure, roles, powers—cause-and-effect of institutional design.
- **Ethos:** Seidel as synthesizer of institutional history; top-down perspective.
- **Pathos:** Implicit—institutions as sites of conflict and compromise.

---

### II. Economic Integration Success

Despite political challenges, the economic integration project was largely successful. The customs union was achieved ahead of schedule, trade doubled, and living standards improved faster than in the US and UK.

**Rhetorical profile:**
- **Logos:** Evidence (schedule, trade, living standards); comparative argument.
- **Pathos:** "Despite political challenges"—resilience, success against odds.
- **Ethos:** Quantitative claims lend authority.

---

### III. Political Crises and Compromises

The period was marked by significant political crises, particularly the Empty Chair Crisis when France withdrew from Council meetings for six months. The Luxembourg Compromise that resolved it slowed decision-making but kept France in the Community.

**Rhetorical profile:**
- **Pathos:** High stakes—France's withdrawal, six-month deadlock, compromise.
- **Logos:** Crisis → compromise → institutional change (slower but preserved).
- **Ethos:** Seidel narrates without taking sides; balanced account of de Gaulle vs. federalists.

---

### IV. De Gaulle's Impact

Charles de Gaulle's leadership fundamentally shaped this period. His vision of intergovernmental cooperation clashed with more federalist approaches, leading to tensions but also ensuring French participation in the Community.

**Rhetorical profile:**
- **Ethos:** De Gaulle as key actor; his vision vs. others'.
- **Logos:** Intergovernmentalism vs. federalism as structural tension.
- **Pathos:** Clash of visions; national sovereignty vs. supranationalism.

## Critical Analysis

### Strengths

- Provides detailed account of institutional development and economic achievements during a crucial period

**Rhetorical note:** Strengths align with *logos* (institutional detail) and *ethos* (scholarly thoroughness).

---

### Weaknesses or Limitations

- May focus more on elite actors and institutions than on social or popular perspectives

**Rhetorical note:** Connects to Krawatzek & Pestel's critique—top-down bias; *pathos* of ordinary Europeans underrepresented.

---

### Questions Raised

- How did ordinary Europeans experience this period of integration?
- What alternative paths were possible?

**Rhetorical framing:**
- **Ethos:** Whose experience counts?
- **Pathos:** Whose story is told?
- **Logos:** What counterfactuals exist?
