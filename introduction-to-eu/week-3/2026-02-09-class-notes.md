# Introduction to the EU — Class Notes
## February 9, 2026 — Week 3

**Topic:** EEC; four institutions; empty chair crisis; Luxembourg Compromise; UK reluctance; CAP.

---

I. Recap — EDC and Cold War Context
   A. USSR expanding west
      1. Real fear in West (e.g. Czechoslovakia)
      2. Military response: NATO
      3. EDC attempted (1952) but failed
   B. French objection to EDC
      1. Loss of sovereignty
      2. Army under supranational body
   C. Key figure — De Gaulle
      1. French president 1958–1969
      2. Symbol of French independence

II. UK and France — Reluctant but Engaged
   A. Most reluctant to join EEC
      1. Both wanted to collaborate with Six
      2. Both feared loss of control
   B. France's double worry
      1. US influence over Europe
      2. UK influence (Commonwealth, US ties)
   C. US attitude
      1. Not worried about united Europe
      2. In US interest: contain communism
      3. Free market aligned with US ideas

III. Spaak Report (1956)
   A. Paul-Henri Spaak (Belgium)
      1. Pioneer of European unity
      2. 1951 speech in Council of Europe
   B. Report recommendations
      1. Continue unity through economic integration
      2. Extend ECSC model to other sectors
      3. Create customs union and common market
   C. UK response
      1. Refused to participate
      2. British reluctance (more tomorrow)

IV. Treaty of Rome (1957) — EEC Objectives
   A. Three main objectives
      1. Raise standard of living
      2. Closer union among peoples
      3. Economic expansion, balanced trade
   B. Difference from post-WWI
      1. Not "Germany pays" approach
      2. Shared growth and cooperation
      3. Negotiations for fair competition

V. Four Institutions (Same Structure Today)
   A. Commission
      1. Runs and implements treaty
      2. Like supervisor — checks compliance
   B. Council of Ministers
      1. Represents member states
      2. Decision-making body
   C. European Parliament
      1. Originally advisory role
      2. Gained power over time
   D. Court of Justice
      1. Interprets EU law
      2. Settles disputes

VI. Empty Chair Crisis (1965)
   A. What happened
      1. De Gaulle pulled France out
      2. France boycotted Council meetings
      3. Six months of crisis
   B. Why
      1. Commission proposed more supranational power
      2. Qualified majority voting (QMV) planned
      3. De Gaulle wanted national veto retained
   C. De Gaulle's position
      1. Intergovernmentalism over supranationalism
      2. France should not be outvoted
      3. Sovereignty paramount

VII. Luxembourg Compromise (1966)
   A. Resolution
      1. "Agree to disagree"
      2. When vital national interest at stake
      3. Discussion continues until consensus
   B. Effect
      1. De facto national veto
      2. Slowed integration
      3. Intergovernmental victory
   C. Long-term impact
      1. Pattern persisted for decades
      2. Qualified majority voting delayed
      3. Sovereignty concerns always present

VIII. Common Agricultural Policy (CAP)
   A. Purpose
      1. Support European farmers
      2. Ensure food security
      3. Stabilize agricultural markets
   B. Mechanisms
      1. Price supports
      2. Subsidies to farmers
      3. Import tariffs on food
   C. Issues
      1. Very expensive (large % of EU budget)
      2. Benefits some countries more (e.g. France)
      3. Reform debates ongoing

IX. Creating Europeans
   A. The logic
      1. First create economic union
      2. Then "Europe" emerges
      3. Then European identity
   B. Reality at the time
      1. Apart from elites, few felt "European"
      2. Identity preliminary
      3. Born out of process, not preexisting

X. Historical Thinking
   A. Key insight
      1. Actors did not know outcome
      2. Possibilities were open
      3. Could have collapsed or failed
   B. Today's perspective
      1. We know it succeeded
      2. But success was not inevitable
      3. Contingency matters
