## Recap: EDC and Cold War context

1. USSR expanding west: Real fear in the West (e.g. Czechoslovakia); military response was NATO; European Defense Community (EDC) had been attempted (1952) but failed.
2. Main objector to EDC: France—because of loss of sovereignty (putting the army under a supranational body).
3. Key figure: Charles de Gaulle—president of France (1958–1969); one of the most important French presidents of the 20th century; symbol of French independence in international affairs.
4. Other opposition in France: French Communists—a strong force in that period; similarly, Italian Communists were the second (later first) political force in Italy for a long time.
5. This tension (supranational vs national sovereignty) is very related to what was studied today: the EEC, the empty chair crisis, and French insistence on intergovernmentalism.

---

## UK and France: reluctant but engaged

1. France and the UK were the most reluctant to join the “new Europe” (the EEC) in the first years, but both wanted to collaborate with the Six.
2. 19 September (Messina / Brussels): Historic meeting where the Six discussed next steps; double worry for France: (1) US influence over European populations; (2) UK influence (UK had Commonwealth ties, “special relationship” with the US).
3. US attitude: The US was not worried about a stronger, united Europe—it was in its interest: (1) Contain communism; (2) Free market and liberalism aligned with US ideas.

---

## Spaak Report (1956) and the path to the EEC

1. Paul-Henri Spaak (Belgium)—one of the “pioneers” of Europe; in 1951 had already made a famous speech in the Council of Europe advocating European unity.
2. Spaak Report: Recommended that European states continue unity by focusing on economic integration—extend the ECSC model to other economic sectors (not only coal and steel): a customs union and common market.
3. UK: Refused to participate in this process; reasons will be elaborated (e.g. tomorrow with Natalie on Tony Judt and British reluctance).

## Treaty of Rome (1957) – EEC objectives

A. The **European Economic Community (EEC)** had three main objectives

1. Raise the standard of living of people (living standards were low in Europe in the 1950s).
2. Closer union among the peoples of Europe (not only economics—political and social dimension).
3. Economic expansion, balanced trade, fair competition—unlike after WWI (reparations: “Germany lost, Germany pays”), the idea was shared growth and cooperation. Many tensions along the way (e.g. “my industry cannot compete with the other country’s”—constant negotiations and changes).

---

## The four institutions (same structure as today)

### I. Commission

1. Role: Runs and implements the treaty; like a supervisor—checks that everyone does what they are supposed to do.
2. Composition: Nominees from member states, but independent of member states once in office; they do not represent their country.
3. Logic: A college—they work together as a whole, discuss, and decide; supranational institution.

### II. Council (of Ministers)

1. Role: Balance—defend national interests; each minister speaks for his or her country.
2. Logic: Intergovernmental; “we need to pay attention to our national interest”; decisions often require agreement of (all or key) states.

### III. European Parliament

1. Then: Called Assembly; consultative only—could give opinions but could not adopt laws or block decisions.
2. Today: “Rebellion of the European Parliament”—MEPs want more power (e.g. block agreements, co-decide). One of the characteristics of the EU: people want democratic representation, but for a long time the Parliament did not propose or block; that has changed with later treaties.

### IV. Court of Justice

1. Role: If one country says “this is not in the treaty” or “this violates the treaty,” the Court interprets the treaty and ensures it is respected.
2. Logic: Rule of law above the states; states have given part of their sovereignty to supranational bodies (Commission, Court).

---

## Which institution is most powerful?

1. Formally: The Council has the most power (votes, budget, key decisions).
2. In practice: The Commission can “make a difference” because it proposes—without its proposal, often nothing moves; but it needs the support of the member states (Council). So the relationship is: Commission proposes, Council decides.
3. Not in the treaty but in reality: When France and Germany agree, things move; when they don’t, they block. “The Union has equal members, but some are more equal than others.” Germany is often described as the locomotive of Europe (economy, money, power, army).

---

## EEC key policies

1. Customs union: Abolition of tariffs between the Six; common external tariff.
2. Common market: Free movement of goods (and later services, people, capital) among the Six.
3. Common Agricultural Policy (CAP): Very important; origin and content discussed below.
4. European Investment Bank (EIB): Redistribution of wealth within the Union—richer states contribute more, poorer states or regions receive more (investment, cohesion); “economy of solidarity” (similar in principle to federal transfers in the USA between states).
5. 1967 – Merger: The three Communities (ECSC, EEC, Euratom) were merged into one (single Commission, single Council, etc.)—the European Community (EC), “mother” of today’s EU.

## The “empty chair” crisis (1965–1966)

### I. Context: de Gaulle’s vision

1. Charles de Gaulle (1958–1969): WWII hero; strong nation-state vision: states more important than supranational institutions.
2. Algeria: War of independence (Algeria was considered part of France, like Lorraine); deeply traumatic for France.
3. De Gaulle’s priorities: (1) French interests first—e.g. agriculture (CAP); (2) Block UK membership twice (1963 and 1967)—UK seen as a US “Trojan horse”, too much American influence; (3) No transfer of sovereignty—“we go to the common market, but we decide”; “we are in the driver’s seat.”

### II. What triggered the crisis

1. The Commission (e.g. President Hallstein) proposed:
2. Own resources for the Community (budget from customs, etc., not only national contributions).
3. Qualified majority voting (QMV) in the Council for some areas—so that one country could not block everything.
4. De Gaulle refused: “I want consensus; we all have to agree.” He did not want majority voting to override French (or any big country’s) veto.

### III. France’s reaction

1. France withdrew from the Council of Ministers for six months—“empty chair”: French seat was empty, no French minister at the table.
2. Until there was a compromise satisfactory to France, they did not return.

1. Two elements of the compromise:
2. Own resources: Not introduced immediately (came later).
3. Luxembourg Compromise (accord): When a vital national interest is at stake, the Council would not outvote that country—discussion would continue until unanimous agreement. In practice, this was used well beyond “vital” interests: many states invoked it to block decisions. So the veto became the norm in sensitive areas; decision-making slowed down for years.
4. Result: A long period of paralysis (roughly 15 years)—but also “lost opportunities”: e.g. in 1968 (Czechoslovakia, etc.) some argue the Community could have developed faster without this blockage. Historians debate: was the empty chair a “disaster” (paralysis) or the “only way” to get French acceptance and keep the Community together? Both interpretations exist.

## UK and EFTA: “in or out?”

1. UK’s dilemma: Wanted the benefits of a large market but without being part of the supranational EEC (no transfer of sovereignty, no common policies imposed).
2. EFTA (European Free Trade Association), 1960: UK created EFTA with Austria, Portugal, Sweden, Switzerland, etc.—free trade among themselves, but not a customs union or common market with the EEC, and no supranational institutions. Idea: “We have a free market among us, but we are not part of the EEC.”
3. Problem: The EEC said: if you want full access to our market, you must join the EEC and accept its rules (customs union, common policies, institutions). The UK could not have both: (1) full EEC market access and (2) set its own external tariffs and keep Commonwealth preferences. So: “In or out?”—no special half-in half-out deal.
4. UK applied to join the EEC but was refused twice by France (1963 and 1967). The British had hoped the USA would pressure the EEC to let them in; the US did not support as much as expected. The UK feared political isolation (e.g. “if we don’t join, we’re alone”).
5. Reasons for British reluctance (and for refusal by France):
6. Commonwealth—UK felt closer to India, Australia, English-speaking countries; joining EEC would affect Commonwealth trade (e.g. New Zealand butter).
7. Special relationship with the USA—EEC members (especially France) feared UK would be a “Trojan horse” for US influence.
8. Sovereignty—UK wanted to keep control of its own trade policy; EEC required a common external tariff and common policies.
9. 1973: After de Gaulle left (1969) and a new French president (Georges Pompidou), the UK (with Ireland and Denmark) joined the EEC. “The French had to accept.”

## Success of the common market (by end of 1960s)

1. Customs union: Tariffs and quotas between the Six abolished by 1967; a single market of about 170 million people.
2. Economic growth: Contributed to strong growth in Western Europe; living standards rose faster than in the US and UK from the late 1950s onward (in the early 50s the US was ahead).
3. Trade: Doubled—more trade, more goods, more investment; economies became more integrated.
4. Spain: Was not in the EEC yet; in 1959 Spain signed an agreement with the USA (aid, investment); tourism was promoted (e.g. “Spain: different”); massive investment in the 1960s (e.g. 50% growth in certain sectors). So Spain’s take-off was partly outside the EEC club.

## Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) – 1962

1. Why agriculture? French (and Italian, etc.) demand; France had (and has) a large agricultural sector and a powerful farming lobby—French farmers can block Paris (France is very centralized: “if you block Paris, you block the country”). De Gaulle made CAP a condition for French engagement in the EEC.
2. Mechanism (simplified):
3. Common prices for key products (e.g. cereals, milk)—prices fixed at European level (“your tomatoes will be bought at this price”).
4. Subsidies and support for farmers (so they earn a stable income).
5. Higher prices for consumers—people paid more for food inside the EEC to fund this system.
6. Why not fisheries or industry? Fisheries came later; industry was not included in the same way at first. CAP was very detailed—thousands of pages of rules; “maybe you want to eat a tomato—this time it’s French.”
7. Today: Still controversial—e.g. “false competition” (can’t compete with cheap imports from China); debate on workers’ conditions, pesticides, environment. But in the 1960s it was a success in the eyes of the Six: it secured French (and others’) support and integrated agriculture into the Community.
8. Cartoon: France and Germany (recent enemies) now “fighting” over crops and quotas—the “toughest parallel” of the whole project, but they put it together (CAP as a compromise).

## Key Takeaways

1. EDC failed because of France (sovereignty, de Gaulle, communists); military integration stopped; economic integration continued.
2. Spaak Report pointed to economic integration (customs union, common market); UK refused to participate.
3. EEC (1957) had four institutions: Commission (supranational), Council (intergovernmental), Parliament (then consultative), Court of Justice (rule of law). Council is the most powerful in formal terms; Commission proposes; France and Germany are the “engine.”
4. Empty chair crisis (1965–1966): France (de Gaulle) opposed own resources and qualified majority voting; withdrew for six months; Luxembourg Compromise = veto when “vital national interest” (in practice used a lot) → paralysis but also French stay in the Community.
5. UK: Created EFTA (free trade without supranationality); applied to EEC but refused twice by France; joined 1973 after de Gaulle left.
6. CAP: French priority; common prices, subsidies, consumer prices; still controversial today; at the time, key to French support and integration.
