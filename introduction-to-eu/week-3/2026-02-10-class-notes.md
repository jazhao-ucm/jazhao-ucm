# Introduction to the EU Class Notes
## February 10, 2026 Week 3

**Topic:** Judt on British reluctance; historiography "of and for" Europe; UK identity.

---

I. Judt's Argument European Integration
   A. Key thesis
      1. Integration was slow, uneven, reluctant
      2. Not single unified vision
      3. Step-by-step, driven by national interests
   B. Process
      1. Political compromise
      2. Messy and pragmatic
      3. Common conception built, not given

II. Timeline of Early Integration
   A. International Steel Pact (1926)
      1. Germany and France manage steel
      2. Did not survive 1929 crisis
      3. Early step toward cooperation
   B. Council of Europe (1949)
      1. Churchill early advocate
      2. Founded in Strasbourg
      3. European Convention on Human Rights
   C. Schuman Plan (1950)
      1. "Founding software" of EU
      2. Led to ECSC
      3. High Authority supranational executive
   D. Treaties of Rome (1957)
      1. EEC and Euratom created
      2. Six countries proceeded
      3. UK helped set up but did not join

III. Why Was Britain Reluctant?
   A. Identity factors
      1. "We won" WWII nationalism
      2. "Never surrendered" myth
      3. Island identity, not invaded
   B. Empire/Commonwealth
      1. Overseas territories
      2. Imperial and global identity
      3. Not primarily "European"
   C. Great power status
      1. Yalta, Potsdam equal with US, USSR
      2. Not "one of small European countries"
      3. Nuclear weapons reinforced rank
   D. Institutional differences
      1. Labour in power (not Christian Democrats)
      2. Keynesian policies
      3. Bank of England, Treasury autonomy

IV. UK-US Relationship
   A. Dependencies
      1. Specific agreements
      2. English-speaking bloc
   B. Competition
      1. Two big international powers
      2. Economic ties complex
   C. Atlantic alignment
      1. Western camp
      2. But UK saw itself as equal partner

V. Historiography "Of and For" Europe
   A. Key distinction
      1. "Of" = describing what happened
      2. "For" = prescriptive, normative
      3. Implies how Europe should be
   B. Context
      1. Recent crises (Brexit, Ukraine, pandemic)
      2. Demand for historical explanations
      3. Different books, different angles

VI. Five Books Reviewed
   A. Hopkins Intellectual project
      1. Enlightenment to today
      2. Elite bias great thinkers
      3. Ordinary Europeans absent
   B. Yourgos Peace settlements
      1. Vienna, Versailles, postwar
      2. Unity from negotiations
      3. Periphery downplayed
   C. Gilbert Institutional history
      1. Treaty politics, executive decisions
      2. Pragmatic, less ideological
      3. Citizens as passive
   D. Patel Critical approach
      1. Dissent and agency included
      2. Contested history
      3. More balanced
   E. Other approaches
      1. Teleological vs. contingent
      2. Success story vs. critical
      3. Colonialism as "blank spaces"

VII. "History For Europe" vs. Critical History
   A. "For Europe" approach
      1. EU as success story
      2. Unity emphasized
      3. Progress narrative
   B. Critical approach
      1. Contingency matters
      2. Dissent and conflict included
      3. Colonialism acknowledged
   C. Implications
      1. How we write history shapes understanding
      2. No neutral narrative
      3. Political stakes in historiography

VIII. Key Takeaway
   A. British reluctance
      1. Identity, power, institutions combined
      2. Not just economic reasons
      3. External (Cold War, Commonwealth) + internal (nationalism)
   B. Integration process
      1. Not inevitable
      2. National interests drove it
      3. Compromise and pragmatism

X. 30-Second Cold Call Version.
A. 3-Line Speaking Frame.
   1. TOPIC: STATE THE CORE QUESTION IN ONE LINE.
   2. LOGIC: GIVE 2 DRIVERS/FACTORS THAT EXPLAIN THE ANSWER.
   3. ACTION: END WITH ONE DECISION OR IMPLICATION.
B. Delivery Rule.
   1. 25-30 SECONDS MAX.
   2. NO BACKGROUND STORY; GO STRAIGHT TO CLAIM + EVIDENCE + CLOSE.

DI. Oral Answer Template (60-90 Seconds, Class Use).
A. Opening.
   1. STATE THE CORE QUESTION OR TOPIC IN ONE LINE.
   2. STATE YOUR MAIN CLAIM/THESIS IN ONE LINE.
B. Logic.
   1. GIVE 2-3 KEY FACTORS OR DRIVERS.
   2. LINK EACH FACTOR TO THE CLAIM (CAUSE -> EFFECT).
C. Action Or Implication.
   1. END WITH ONE DECISION, RECOMMENDATION, OR CONSEQUENCE.
   2. CLOSE CLEARLY WITHOUT ADDING NEW IDEAS.
D. Delivery Rule.
   1. 60-90 SECONDS.
   2. CLAIM -> EVIDENCE -> CLOSE.
