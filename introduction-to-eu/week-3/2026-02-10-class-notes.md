## Presentation 1: Tony Judt – British reluctance to join European integration

### I. Timeline covered (student summary)

1. International Steel Pact (1926): One of the first pushes for a “unified” Europe; signed at Luxembourg by Germany and France (later Belgium, etc.); aimed to manage steel between former enemies; did not survive the 1929 crisis and Depression—so not a direct blueprint for the EU but a step toward transnational cooperation.
2. Churchill and the Council of Europe: Churchill was an early advocate for a “sub-language” or structure that became the Council of Europe; with postwar tensions it was harder than presumed; the Council was founded in Strasbourg, May 1949 (UK, Ireland, France, others). It had no real power but issued the European Convention on Human Rights and recognised that reconciling with Germany was essential.
3. Marshall Plan / German economy: US (e.g. Marshall) determined that German economic recovery was needed to raise living standards in Western Europe; Western countries still had few links to each other and could still see Germany as a threat; the Plan took shape in 1949 and incorporated West Germany into Western European affairs.
4. Schuman Plan (1950): “The original plan for Europe” / “European Union’s founding software”; led to the European Coal and Steel Community (ECSC). The French proposed that direction be based on a High Authority—a clear supranational executive: competition, pricing policy, buying/selling on behalf of the countries. Germany was the first to ratify; then the other countries; six Western European states signed—the future “Six” of the Community.
5. ECSC signing: All six foreign ministers signed on behalf of their countries but also beyond party politics—sense of cultural and economic unity, Christian Democratic concerns, collective responsibility, “no return to war.”
6. UK and Scandinavia: Remained sceptical; UK in particular did not join the ECSC. Despite the ECSC being an economic organisation, it was seen as a single Western European interest and a supranational body—the idea of a supranational executive did not sit well with Britain. Also: Labour was in power (different from Christian Democratic dominance on the continent); strikes, economic and social model differed (e.g. more liberal, less “social market”). British identity was strongly tied to the Commonwealth and victory in WWII—“we won”; nationalism (e.g. 1945 elections) and a sense of pride influenced reluctance to “merge” into a more unified European setting.
7. 1950s – Messina and Treaties of Rome: The Benelux countries pushed for further economic integration. Messina Conference (June 1955): ECSC members (the Six) met; proposals for customs union, common market, and related economic agreements; France was not very enthusiastic; UK was doubtful and dropped out of negotiations. The Six went ahead and established the European Economic Community (EEC) and Euratom (atomic energy) by the Treaties of Rome (1957)—same six countries. The Treaties outlined future cooperation and laid the foundation for the Court of Justice, etc. UK had helped set up the process but did not join; over the 1950s Western European countries that did join recognised they had more in common with each other than with a UK that stayed outside.

### II. Judt’s argument (as summarised in class)

1. European integration was a slow, uneven, and often very reluctant process—not a single unified vision that everyone wanted. It was step-by-step, driven by national interests and political compromise.
2. First “round” of pioneers (e.g. Churchill, Monnet, Schuman) had visions, but international cooperation was damaging (or at least difficult) in the postwar period; a common conception of Europe was not a given—it was built through compromise and pragmatism.

## Discussion: Why was Britain so reluctant?

1. Thesis of the text: Critical of the idea that the EU was “one unified vision” all countries wanted; more realistic: separate countries pursuing self-interest, messy and uneven.
2. Britain’s role: Britain’s reluctance contributed to that messy process—very nationalist, did not want to associate with a supranational entity; saw itself as different from the continent.
3. Economic vs political/social? Judt stresses economic and resource-based arguments (trade, coal, steel). But the professor added: resources and decisions were also political—e.g. domestic politics (Labour in the UK, Keynesian policies, different from Christian Democratic “social market” on the continent); external factors (Cold War, US, Commonwealth) and internal (Bank of England, Treasury, “we are masters of our own house”). So: combination of political and economic factors, both external (international position) and internal (identity, institutions).
4. Why did Britain feel so powerful?
   a. **WWII:** Britain felt it had **won triumphantly**; **nationalism**, “we never surrendered”; **myth** that “we were never invaded” (island; war fought on the continent and elsewhere)—used by politicians: “we defended our island and soul.”
   b. **Empire / Commonwealth:** Britain still had **overseas territories**, **metropole** and colonies; identity was **imperial** and **global**, not primarily “European.”
   c. **Seat at the table:** At **Yalta**, **Potsdam**, etc., Britain was treated as a **great power** alongside the **USA** and **USSR**—so “even footing with superpowers,” not “one of the small European countries.”
   d. **Military:** British **naval** power was still major (e.g. **U-boat** campaigns to cut supply lines); in the **nuclear** age, Britain was one of the **few** countries with access to **nuclear weapons**—very important for its sense of rank.
5. UK–US relationship: “Complicated”—they depended on each other (e.g. specific agreements, English-speaking bloc) but were also two big international powers → competition. Economic ties; also a question of aligning in blocs in the new postwar world (Atlantic, Western camp).

## Presentation 2: “New histories of and for Europe” (historiographical review)

### III. Title and theme

1. “Histories of and for Europe”—the idea that these are not only descriptions of the past but also prescriptive: they narrate Europe in a way that implies how it should be (or should remain), not just “how it was.”
2. Context: Recent crises (e.g. Brexit, Ukraine, pandemic, institutional strain) have increased demand for historically grounded explanations of “how we got here”; the review compares how different recent books define Europe and present its history, revealing political or normative angles.

### IV. Five books reviewed (summary from presentation)

1. Anthony Hopkins, *The Pursuit of Power* (2022)
   a. Europe as **intellectual project** (Enlightenment, Kant, etc.); **Europeanism** as **ideology**; strong **continuity** from Enlightenment to today.
   b. **Critique:** **Elite bias**—“intellectual gallery” of great thinkers (Kant, Napoleon, etc.); **ordinary Europeans** and **experience** are absent; **prescriptive** (what Europe is and why it’s good) rather than strictly analytical.

2. Yourgos, *Confry in Peace* (peace settlements)
   a. European history organised around **peace settlements** (Vienna, Versailles, Geneva, postwar); Marshall Plan as **institutional peace**; unity as **product of negotiations**, not just absence of war.
   b. **Critique:** **Periphery** (e.g. Eastern Europe, former Soviet space) downplayed; **oversimplification** that peace/unity come mainly from institutions and elites; **ordinary citizens’** perspectives underplayed.

3. Gilbert, *European Integration* (2021)
   a. **Institutional** history; **treaty politics**, **executive** decision-making; **pragmatic**, less ideological.
   b. **Critique:** **Citizens as passive**; democratic and **social** dimensions **minimised** (e.g. **austerity protests** barely engaged); **elite bias**—treaty negotiations, not popular agency.

4. Luuk van Middelaar, *The Passage to Europe* (2020)
   a. Integration across **nation-state**, **intergovernmental**, and **supranational** levels; **legitimacy** from identity, benefits, participation.
   b. **Critique:** **Institutional** focus; **political actors outside** institutional circles are **peripheral**; history as “how institutions function” rather than “how societies engage with Europe.”

5. Conrad / Jarausch, *Germany in the Battle for Europe* (2021)
   a. Europe as **progressive alternative** to **American** political dysfunction; **explicitly prescriptive**—Europe as “the right way” to do cooperation.
   b. **Critique:** **Strong normative** stance; Western/Northern European focus; **simplifies** some crises; uses history to make a **political argument**.

### V. Reviewers’ main criticisms

1. Elite bias: All five privilege leaders, thinkers, institutions—not ordinary people, protest, or dissent.
2. Top-down narrative: “Main actors” drive the story; citizens as spectators, not participants; voices outside elite or institutional circles absent.
3. Risk: Readers whose experience is not in the story may feel alienated; history becomes a justification for integration rather than a critical account.
4. Blank spaces: Little on popular attitudes, opposition to Europe, colonialism, non-European perspectives—Eurocentric; “Europe told by Europeans.”

## Professor’s points: teleology, contingency, and how to read European history

### VI. Historians are not unbiased

1. Historians always write from a position—their perception, experience, lens. “Unbiased” or “purely factual” history is not possible; the story we tell projects a certain coherence onto the past.
2. If we choose a certain narrative, we obscure other actors, events, and logics that were also at play.

### VII. Teleology – “history as destiny”

1. Teleology = the idea that history unfolds toward a specific end; events happen in order to achieve that goal.
2. Example: “The French Revolution took place in order to create modern democracy.” That suggests the Revolution had to lead to democracy—necessity. But the Revolution could be interpreted very differently (e.g. different ideas of “freedom” and “equality” at the time); other outcomes were possible.
3. In European history: The biggest temptation and danger is teleological storytelling: “European history is aiming at peace, unity, integration.” Many historiographies do this—pro-integration stance, affirmative narrative; dissent and failure are underplayed.
4. Contingency: At each moment, actors did not know what would happen; failure was always possible; resistance and alternatives existed. History is full of failures—not a smooth path to the present.

### VIII. Two ways to do history

1. Top-down: Institutions, summits, treaties, elites—what we often get in “European integration” history.
2. Bottom-up: Experience of ordinary people—how they lived, contested, or supported integration; protest, dissent, everyday construction of “Europe.”

### IX. How to avoid teleological traps

1. Do not rely on “historical necessity” (“it had to happen”).
2. Context: What did words mean at the time? (e.g. freedom, equality—different in different places and periods.)
3. Actors: Who decided? Who protested? Who was excluded?
4. Concrete moments: Focus on specific situations, choices, conflicts.
5. Possibility of failure: Remember that integration could have failed or taken other paths.

### X. “Long durée” explanations – pros and cons

1. Long durée = large-scale, long-term explanations (e.g. “Europe as idea since the Enlightenment”).
2. Pros: Large scale; can connect institutions and ideas over time (e.g. Congress of Vienna 1815 as a precedent for peace settlements and balance of power).
3. Cons: Can obscure small actors, ordinary people, contingency; can suggest cycles (“history repeats itself”—e.g. Marx: “first as tragedy, then as farce”) and downplay ruptures and specificity.

### XI. Blank spaces in European historiography

1. Eurocentric: “Strikingly” no non-European perspective—history of Europe told by Europeans, for Europeans; colonialism and empire in the construction of Europe (and of “European” identity) are often missing.
2. Dissent and opposition: “Europe” as idea is discussed, but opposition to Europe, scepticism, and everyday contestation are absent in many accounts—reinforcing a unified, top-down narrative.

## Student comments: US history and context

1. US history teaching: Often top-down—federalism came because “Federalists won”; Revolution framed as liberty from tyranny; imperialism and expansion are left out or reframed positively (“spreading democracy”). Very teleological—“America was always going to succeed.”
2. Constitution and context: The context in which the Constitution was written is different from today (e.g. right to bear arms—written when technology was different); interpretation depends on historical context.
3. History written by the winners: Whoever triumphs tends to rewrite the story so that victory seems inevitable—legitimising the new order.
4. Translation and sources: In the past, much was written and copied; things can be lost or changed in transmission and translation (e.g. Bible)—not necessarily “malicious,” but interpretation and context matter.

## Key Takeaways

1. Judt: British reluctance was due to identity (Commonwealth, “we won the war”), power (great power, nuclear, naval), institutions (Labour, Treasury), and scepticism toward supranationality—economic and political factors, internal and external.
2. Historiography: Recent books on “Europe” often offer histories “for” Europe—prescriptive, pro-integration, elite-centred; ordinary people, dissent, colonialism, and non-European perspectives are blank spaces.
3. Teleology is a risk: telling history as if it had to lead to peace/unity; contingency and failure must be part of the story.
4. Context, actors, and concrete moments help avoid necessity and open the door to bottom-up and critical readings of European integration.

## Key Takeaways (Rhetorical)

- **Ethos:** Judt's critical stance; "histories for Europe" as prescriptive; ordinary people, dissent as missing voices.
- **Logos:** British reluctance (identity, power, institutions); teleology as analytical flaw; contingency as corrective.
- **Pathos:** "We won," Commonwealth; emotional stakes of whose story gets told.
