## The European Community (EC) and recession

### I. Merger of the Communities (1967)

1. 1 July 1967: ECSC, EEC, and EURATOM were amalgamated into the European Community (EC).
2. Customs union: Achieved ahead of schedule in 1968.
3. De Gaulle resigned as French president in 1969.
4. After de Gaulle: Following his resignation and the Luxembourg Compromise, the "European bandwagon started to roll again."

### II. The Hague Summit (December 1969)

1. Initiator: George Pompidou (de Gaulle's successor as French President).
2. Issues considered:
3. Enlargement of Community membership.
4. Economic and monetary union.
5. Cooperation on technological and social policies.
6. More regular discussions between Foreign Ministers.
7. Agreement: Enlargement was finally reached in June 1971, to take effect from 1 January 1973—the first enlargement of the Community.

---

## First enlargement: 1973 (Six → Nine)

### III. New members

1. United Kingdom, Ireland, Denmark joined the Community (from six to nine members).

### IV. Special arrangements

1. Commonwealth: Various arrangements made to ease difficulties for the British Commonwealth, which exported food to Britain and would now be subject to Community tariffs.
2. Fisheries: New members granted special fisheries arrangements—necessary because a fisheries policy initiated at the same time gave all Community members equal and free access to each other's waters. The new members all had extensive fishing fleets and would suffer most from competition.

### V. Referendum results

1. Norway: 53% voted against joining—mainly due to concerns over Norway's fishing industry and its relationship with Scandinavian neighbours.
2. Denmark: 63% voted in favour—expected that Danish agriculture would benefit from the Common Agricultural Policy (CAP); Denmark's chief export markets were Britain and Germany.
3. Ireland: 83% voted in favour—logical outcome given that 70% of Irish exports went to Britain.

### VI. Timing

1. The expansion from six to nine members was expected to give the Community a new boost.
2. Unfortunately, the enlargement took place just as the Community became embroiled in problems and economic recession.
3. The 1973 Arab-Israeli War led to an Arab embargo on oil supplies and a four-fold increase in oil prices.

---

## The 1973 oil crisis

### VII. Background: Europe's dependence on cheap oil

1. Post-1945 prosperity: Europe's prosperity and industrial expansion after 1945 depended on cheap oil supplies.
2. Price: Between 1950 and 1973, the average price of a barrel of oil was two dollars.
3. Usage: By 1972, oil provided 65% of Western Europe's electricity, while coal accounted for only 22%.
4. Supply: Plentiful supply of oil before 1973 kept prices low, as new entrants into the oil-producing business charged low prices.
5. African suppliers: African countries supplied 6% of Europe's oil in 1960, but over 40% in 1970.

### VIII. OPEC and decolonisation

1. Third World producers: Some of the world's most important oil-producing states were "Third World" states that had recently become independent as a result of decolonisation.
2. Radical Arab leadership: The emergence of radical Arab leadership during the 1950s and 1960s upset traditional arrangements with oil companies.
3. OPEC (1960): In September 1960, Saudi Arabia, Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, and Venezuela set up the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) in Baghdad.
4. OPEC's aim: Get control of oil fields away from oil companies in order to increase OPEC's financial return from the sale of oil.
5. By 1973: Most oil-producing countries had taken control of their oil resources, either through nationalisation (as in Iran, Libya, and Iraq) or through new contracts with oil companies.
6. October 1973: OPEC decided to set and control the price of oil on its own without negotiating with oil companies.

### IX. Colonel Muammar Gaddafi (1942–2011)

1. Libyan soldier who overthrew King Idris in 1969.
2. Promoted himself to colonel (highest rank in Libyan army).
3. Supported violent revolutionaries in other parts of the world, including the IRA.
4. In 1986, the US bombed Libya in an attempt to kill him.
5. Died in the civil war that followed the Arab Spring of 2011.

### X. The oil weapon (October 1973)

1. 6 October 1973: The Arab-Israeli War started when President Sadat of Egypt invaded Israeli territory.
2. Ceasefire: Agreed in November after an Israeli victory.
3. OPEC's decision: Use oil as a weapon against countries that supported Israel.
   a. **No oil** to the **USA** or the **Netherlands**.
   b. Countries **friendly to the Arab cause** would receive their usual amount of oil.
4. Result: The oil trade was thrown into chaos.
5. Outcome: The immediate shock did not produce the anticipated change in American attitude towards Israel.

## Short-term consequences

### XI. Revealed weaknesses

1. Over-dependency: Revealed the over-dependency of Europeans on cheap oil.
2. Weakness of the EEC: Showed the weakness of the EEC—EEC leaders failed to agree in December 1973 on the "pooling" of oil among countries.
3. No common response: It was left to individual countries to decide on measures to save energy.

### XII. National measures

1. Restrictions: Countries began to restrict industrial and consumer use of oil.
2. Consumer impact: Individual consumers found it hard to obtain oil for home heating or car use.
3. Price escalation: Prices escalated; oil producers saw their revenues soar.
4. Auction market: Consumers were prepared to pay any price; auction market saw a tripling of prices in some cases.
5. Mild winter (1973–1974): A mild winter and fall in demand lessened the impact during that winter.

### XIII. Government responses

1. Essential services: Governments gave any available oil supplies to essential services (electricity stations, hospitals).
2. Publicity campaigns: Governments ran intensive publicity campaigns showing ways consumers could save energy.
3. Specific measures:
   a. **Belgium and Netherlands:** Banned **Sunday driving**.
   b. **Belgium:** Cancelled its national car rally.
   c. **Luxembourg:** Closed petrol stations on **Saturdays and Sundays**.
   d. **Denmark:** Lowered **speed limits**.
   e. **France:** Asked offices to **lower central heating by two degrees**.

---

## Long-term consequences

### XIV. Stagflation

1. Definition: When the flow of cheap, imported oil dried up, the swift increase in oil prices led to stagflation.
2. Recession: Industrialised economies learned the meaning of recession: high inflation and high unemployment.
3. Cost pass-through: Oil companies passed increased costs on to consumers.
4. Economic impact: Countries produced less, exported less, and ran up high trade deficits.
5. End of "Golden Age": The oil crisis brought an end to the "Golden Age" of economic growth in Western Europe.

### XV. Diversification of oil sources

1. New oil fields: After 1973, main oil companies started to invest in new oil fields (e.g. North Sea).
2. Non-OPEC sources: Oil-consuming countries looked elsewhere—used non-OPEC countries such as Mexico and the USSR to meet their oil needs.

### XVI. Alternative energy sources

1. France: Turned to nuclear power.
2. UK: Turned to natural gas.
3. Iceland: Used its geothermal resources to replace oil.
4. Taxes: High local taxes on oil in most European countries also encouraged consumers to cut down on consumption.

## Seminar / presentations – Seidel and CAP

### XVII. Seidel on the 1960s – from honeymoon to crisis

1. Context: After the Treaty of Rome (1957), the EEC aimed to build a common market among the Six (France, West Germany, Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands, Luxembourg) with free movement of goods, services, capital, and people.
2. Supranational vs intergovernmental:
   a. **Supranationalism:** Independent European institutions (the **Commission**, Court, etc.) with authority above states.
   b. **Intergovernmentalism / “Europe of states”:** Cooperation between **sovereign governments** that keep a veto on key decisions.
3. Dreams and models:
   a. **United States of Europe / federalist dream:** EEC as a first step towards a single European government.
   b. **Europe of nation-states:** Partnership of states that **retain sovereignty** and can **block** decisions against their “vital interests.”

### XVIII. “Honeymoon” years (1958–1962)

1. Implementation phase: Early progress in removing tariffs and moving toward the customs union.
2. French leadership and the Fouchet Plan:
   a. De Gaulle’s France promoted **intergovernmental cooperation** (Fouchet Plan) to keep **decision-making with national leaders**.
   b. Plan was **rejected**, showing early resistance to a purely intergovernmental political union.
3. Common Agricultural Policy (CAP), 1962:
   a. Designed to **support farmers**, stabilise markets, and ensure **food self‑sufficiency**.
   b. Created a **shared budget**: Germany and others paid in, France and other major agricultural producers received substantial support.
   c. Deepened **financial and economic interdependence** among the Six.

### XIX. Crises of the 1960s – Empty Chair and Luxembourg Compromise (1965–1966)

1. Hallstein’s proposals: Give the EEC “own resources” (direct revenues) and more power to the European Parliament, pushing in a supranational direction.
2. De Gaulle’s reaction: Feared a “superstate” in Brussels; saw Hallstein as turning the EEC into something that would undercut French sovereignty.
3. Empty Chair Crisis (1965–1966):
   a. France **withdrew its representatives** from Brussels for about six months.
   b. Decision‑making in the EEC **froze**; no major decisions could be taken without France.
4. Luxembourg Compromise (1966):
   a. Agreement among the Six that if a member state invoked a **“very important national interest,”** it could **block decisions**, even where majority voting was foreseen.
   b. In practice, this pushed the Community toward **consensus and veto politics**, limiting the Commission’s political initiative.
5. Aftermath:
   a. The Commission became more of a **technical administrator** than a political driver.
   b. The **federalist dream** of a “United States of Europe” stalled; economic integration continued, but **political integration slowed**.

### XX. Recovery and irreversibility (1966–1968)

1. Completion of the customs union: All internal tariffs on industrial goods were removed by 1 July 1968, ahead of schedule.
2. Trade over politics: Even when political visions clashed, the economic benefits and numbers (growth, trade, employment) were hard to ignore.
3. British applications: The UK’s second application to join (1967) was again vetoed by de Gaulle, reinforcing the tension between widening and deepening.
4. Irreversibility: By the late 1960s, the Six were so economically intertwined that breaking up the EEC would have been extremely costly; integration became “necessary,” not just voluntary.

### XXI. CAP discussion – benefits and problems

1. Why CAP?
   a. Protect **farm incomes** (especially in France and Italy).
   b. Ensure **stable, secure food supplies** for Europe.
   c. Symbol of **solidarity** and **shared policy** in the Community.
2. Benefits:
   a. Helped modernise farming and kept many **rural regions** viable.
   b. Gave Europe a degree of **food self‑sufficiency**.
   c. Created a **core common policy** that bound member states together.
3. Problems and costs:
   a. **Overproduction:** Famous **“butter mountains”** and **“wine lakes”**—surpluses that had to be stored or dumped.
   b. **High budget share:** CAP absorbed a **very large share** of the Community budget (around **two‑thirds to three‑quarters** before the 1970s).
   c. **Higher consumer prices:** Food prices were **higher** for European consumers than on world markets.
   d. **Distributional tensions:** Different countries and sectors benefited unevenly (e.g. **French and Italian farmers** vs urban consumers, North vs South).

### XXII. Warlouzet – From The Hague summit to Maastricht (1969–1992)

1. Question of the chapter: How did a limited economic community (removing trade barriers) become the more ambitious EU of Maastricht?
2. The Hague summit (1969) – “completion, deepening, enlargement”:
   a. **Completion:** Stabilise the **common market** and policies like CAP.
   b. **Deepening:** Extend integration into new areas (**monetary, social, foreign policy**).
   c. **Enlargement:** Bring in new members, especially the **UK**.
3. 1970s crises and “Eurosclerosis”:
   a. **Oil shocks, stagflation, unemployment, UK budget disputes** made the 1970s look like a **stagnant period**.
   b. Warlouzet argues integration **continued in different forms**:
   c. **Court of Justice** advanced mutual recognition (e.g. **Cassis de Dijon**).
   d. **European Parliament** became **directly elected** in 1979.
   e. **European Monetary System (EMS)** tied currencies and prepared the ground for **EMU**.
   f. Shows a **hybrid system**: stronger **European Council** and intergovernmental leadership, but also **supranational law and institutions** gaining influence.
4. Breakthrough in the 1980s – toward the Single European Act:
   a. By mid‑1980s, three blocks: **UK budget**, **CAP reform**, and **Spanish/Portuguese enlargement**.
   b. **UK rebate** and **Franco‑German cooperation** unlocked compromises.
   c. **Delors Commission** reframed integration around finishing the **internal market by 1992**.
   d. **Single European Act (1986):** Extended **qualified majority voting**, made it easier to legislate for the internal market; marked a **relaunch of integration**.
5. From internal market to monetary union:
   a. Single market created pressure for a **single currency** (efficiency, stability).
   b. **German reunification** increased anxiety about German power; EMU helped **anchor Germany** in Europe.
   c. **Delors Report (1989)** outlined three stages toward EMU.
6. Maastricht Treaty (1992):
   a. Created the **European Union**, set path to the **euro**, and introduced the **three‑pillar structure**:
   b. **European Communities** (mostly supranational).
   c. **Common Foreign and Security Policy** (intergovernmental).
   d. **Justice and Home Affairs** (intergovernmental, at this stage).
   e. Introduced **EU citizenship** (movement and voting rights) and strengthened the **European Parliament** via **co‑decision** in some areas.
   f. Still **not a federal state**: key areas like **foreign policy and taxation** remain largely national; important **opt‑outs** (e.g. UK and the euro, social policy).
7. Limits and backlash:
   a. **Yugoslav wars** exposed the weakness of the new common foreign policy; the EU still relied on the **US** for hard security.
   b. **Referendums**: Danish “no” (first vote) and a very narrow French “yes” revealed **public resistance** and rising **Euroscepticism**.
   c. Economic recession in the early 1990s made citizens **associate integration with austerity and hardship**.
8. Overall theme: Integration is crisis‑driven and adaptive—leaders used shocks and conflicts to reinvent Europe, but each step increased complexity and made legitimacy and democracy more fragile.

## Readings / memo – Vinen and Anderson

### XXIII. Vinen – *A History in Fragments* (Part V: "Europe in the New World Order")

1. Method: Fragmented, non-teleological history—rejects the idea that Europe was always moving inevitably toward the EU.
2. Europe in the "new world order": Post-1989 Europe is uneven and contested (East–West gaps, Balkan wars, NATO vs EU, US power, global capitalism).
3. Critical view of integration: Integration is one project among many, with winners and losers, not automatically progressive.
4. Bottom-up angle: Focus on how ordinary people experienced Schengen, the euro, enlargement, austerity, rather than just treaties and elites.
5. For this course: Warns against telling a simple success story of “from Schuman Plan to Maastricht” and ignoring alternatives and failures.

### XXIV. Anderson – *Ever Closer Union?* (2021)

1. Title with a question mark: Directly questions the EU motto “ever closer union among the peoples of Europe.”
2. Critical analysis: Treats the EU as a political regime with democratic deficits and economic asymmetries (North/South, core/periphery, creditor/debtor).
3. Contemporary crises: Uses euro crisis, migration, Brexit, rule-of-law disputes to argue that integration can stall or reverse, not only deepen.
4. Normative point: “More Europe” is not automatically better; the form and content of integration, and its social consequences, matter.
5. Link to Vinen: Both resist teleology; Vinen shows a fragmented past, Anderson shows a fragile, contested present—integration is not guaranteed.

## Key Takeaways

1. 1967: Merger of ECSC, EEC, EURATOM into European Community (EC); customs union achieved in 1968.
2. 1969: De Gaulle resigned; Hague Summit set agenda for enlargement and EMU.
3. 1973: First enlargement (UK, Ireland, Denmark); Norway rejected by referendum (fishing concerns).
4. 1973 oil crisis: OPEC used oil as weapon; prices quadrupled; revealed EEC weakness (no common energy policy).
5. Short-term: National measures (speed limits, heating restrictions, Sunday driving bans); stagflation.
6. Long-term: End of "Golden Age"; diversification (North Sea, Mexico, USSR); alternatives (nuclear, gas, geothermal).
