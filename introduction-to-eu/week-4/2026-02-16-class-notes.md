1. French opposition (1950s): Two main reasons:
2. Loss of sovereignty—military under supranational control, not national.
3. De Gaulle—did not want American influence in European defense: “The French didn’t want the Americans in European [affairs].” The European army was proposed as an alternative to a national German army but also to limit US control.

   c. **President Macron:** Said it is **almost time** to promote the idea of a **European army** / **true European defense** (so Europe can act without depending only on the USA).

## How united was Western Europe by 1957?

### I. Economic dimension

1. A lot of progress since the start of integration (ECSC, then Treaties of Rome).
2. But not all countries were in favour—Spaak, Monnet, and others had to push. From a purely economic view, EEC and EFTA were two organisations competing for the same space (Europe).

### II. Political dimension

1. Besides Spain and Portugal (exceptions—dictatorships), most Western European countries had some form of democracy (parliamentary system, rule of law).
2. But not nearly as united as today; the Council of Europe existed but was not a powerful political body; the EC was mainly economic, not a full political union.
3. UK (and others outside the Six) were fearful of the power that the Six had together—economic integration was creating political weight and straining relations with those left outside.

### III. Conclusion

1. “The ingredients for the soup were there”—framework, institutions, treaties—but there was still a lot of tension. The war had ended only 12 years before (1945 → 1957); nobody had forgotten; many still thought “each country for itself.”
2. France, especially under de Gaulle, wanted to do things its own way (sovereignty, no supranational army, empty chair crisis)—leading to a period of paralysis. When things don’t work, people often stick to their positions (vicious circle). After the Treaty of Rome, the complexity and tensions of the process became clear; we then “jump” to 1992 (Maastricht, single market, etc.) as the next major milestone.

## First enlargement: 1973 (UK, Ireland, Denmark)

1. 1973: The Community went from six to nine members: United Kingdom, Ireland, Denmark.
2. Norway had also applied but did not join—in a referendum, 53% voted no. Main reasons: fishing industry (very powerful in Norway; feared EEC rules would harm it) and relationship with the EEC (sovereignty, economy).
3. Denmark: 63% voted in favour; expected agriculture to benefit from the Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) (e.g. dairy, products).
4. Ireland: 83% in favour of joining Europe.
5. UK: Joined after de Gaulle left (1969); French had to accept. CAP and access to the common market were important for British (and Irish, Danish) calculations.
6. Fishing: For new members (e.g. Denmark, UK, Ireland), fishing was a big issue—they had large fishing industries; they wanted equal rights in Community waters and protection so that overfishing by others would not destroy stocks. Norway stayed out partly because of fishing and oil (North Sea).
7. CAP: Already in place (1962); France had been the main driver; new members wanted fair access and benefits (e.g. Danish agriculture).

## 1970s context: oil dependence and OPEC

1. By the early 1970s, Western Europe (and the USA) depended heavily on oil for transport, industry, and electricity.
2. 1950s–1960s: Oil was cheap (e.g. around $2 per barrel); 1960 – only 6% of Europe’s oil from African producers; one decade later – 14%; growth in imports from Middle East and North Africa.
3. OPEC (Organisation of Petroleum Exporting Countries): Created 1960 (e.g. Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, Venezuela). Goal: coordinate production and prices—resource nationalism (producers controlling their own oil). Decolonisation had created many new states; some had huge oil reserves and wanted to use them as leverage.
4. Libya / Gaddafi (1969): Coup; nationalisation of oil; example of a radical producer. 1973 – Yom Kippur War (Egypt, Syria vs Israel); Arab oil producers used oil as a weapon.

## 1973 oil crisis (first oil shock)

### IV. What happened (October 1973)

1. Yom Kippur War: Arab states attacked Israel; USA and some European countries (e.g. Netherlands) supported Israel.
2. OPEC decided to raise the price of oil unilaterally and to embargo countries that supported Israel (USA, Netherlands, etc.)—“turn off the tap.”
3. Result: Oil price quadrupled in a short time; shortages, queues, panic.

### V. Short-term effects

1. Revealed the structural dependency of Europe (and the West) on cheap oil.
2. Weakness of the EEC: No common energy policy. The Community failed to agree on a common response at the December 1973 meeting—each country developed its own energy-saving measures (speed limits, thermostats, rationing). No solidarity—when there is a crisis, “who wants to share? No one.”
3. Stagflation: Stagnation (growth stops, unemployment rises) plus inflation (prices rise)—“the worst thing for an economist.” You don’t want to use more (economy slows) but prices go up anyway (e.g. food, transport).
4. Consumers: Psychologically unprepared; panic buying; in winter 1973 many had no heating (e.g. no fuel for heating).
5. National measures: Speed limits (e.g. USA 55 mph); daylight saving; thermostats lowered (e.g. France: public offices 20°C → 18°C); rationing in some places; advertising to “save energy” (e.g. “only heat one room,” “close the door”).
6. USA – President Carter: Famous speech—“We are in the middle of the moral equivalent of war”; “the government is not going to do anything—save yourself.” This did not help the atmosphere in Europe (USA not seen as sharing the burden).
7. Companies passed cost increases to consumers; unemployment rose; production fell (tractors, machines, transport all needed oil).
8. End of the “spirit of 45”: Postwar optimism (growth, cheap cars, consumer society) was replaced by a 1970s mentality of “save yourself” and austerity.
9. Western countries looked for other sources: Mexico, Soviet Union (could buy oil from USSR); nuclear (France pushed nuclear power; UK, Germany too); renewables in the long term (e.g. wind—Spain later invested heavily in wind in Galicia and elsewhere).

### VI. Long-term consequences

1. Energy-saving campaigns became permanent in Europe—efficiency, insulation, public awareness. In the USA the culture was sometimes different (e.g. heating with windows open—professor’s experience).
2. Diversification of energy sources; nuclear in France; renewables (wind, etc.) developed over decades.
3. Stagflation and unemployment lasted through the 1970s and 1980s—“hard” decades in Europe and the USA (e.g. punk as expression of social discontent).
4. EEC had failed to create a common energy policy—each country acted alone; a lesson for later (e.g. today’s debates on energy solidarity, Russian gas, green transition).
