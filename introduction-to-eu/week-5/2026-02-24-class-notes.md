## Key Concepts

### I. Review of EU Enlargements (Class Discussion)

A. Waves of enlargement
1. 1973: UK, Ireland, Denmark join (first enlargement – Six → Nine)
   a. UK application repeatedly vetoed by France (de Gaulle)  
   b. Concerns: US influence in Europe; UK focus on Commonwealth rather than Europe
2. 1981: Greece
3. 1986: Spain and Portugal
4. 1995: Austria, Finland, Sweden
5. 2004: \"Big Bang\" enlargement – Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Poland, Slovenia, Slovakia, Czech Republic, Hungary, Malta, Cyprus
6. 2007: Bulgaria and Romania
7. 2013: Croatia

B. Key point
1. Enlargements followed both political and economic logics (democracy, market reforms, geopolitics).

---

### II. Economic vs Political Integration – Why Economics First?

A. Priorities after WWII
1. Early proposal for European Defence Community (EDC) failed in 1950s  
   a. Fear of rearming Germany  
   b. Political union too sensitive
2. European Economic Community (EEC) was the first strong, successful project

B. Why economic integration came first
1. Economic benefits are easier to show – growth, trade, jobs  
2. Political union involves sovereignty, identity, national interests – more conflictual  
3. Political integration often postponed: \"we will deal with this later\"

C. When political union returned
1. Single European Act (mid‑1980s): first serious step toward a more political project  
2. Maastricht Treaty (1992): Economic and Monetary Union, political union, foreign and security policy

---

### III. Vinen – *Europe in the Twentieth Century* (Presentation)

A. Main question
1. Did the fall of the Berlin Wall (1989) really \"unite\" Europe?

B. Argument
1. Europe remained divided after 1989, but along new lines:
   a. East vs West  
   b. Urban vs rural  
   c. Young vs old  
   d. Rich vs poor; center vs periphery
2. National communist states (e.g. Yugoslavia, Czechoslovakia) broke into smaller nation-states  
   a. New physical borders appeared where there had been internal borders before  
   b. Example: break‑up of Yugoslavia

C. Social and economic divisions
1. Within countries, big cities integrated quickly into global and European markets  
   a. Western companies invested in urban areas (e.g. Prague, Warsaw)  
   b. Very low unemployment in some city centers vs much higher national averages
2. Rural and deindustrialized regions often left behind  
   a. Fewer jobs, less foreign investment  
   b. Stronger nostalgia for communist‑era stability

D. Generational divide
1. Younger people
   a. More enthusiastic about integration and globalization  
   b. Learned Western languages, travelled, consumed Western culture  
   c. Identified with a \"European\" or global identity
2. Older people
   a. Remembered economic security of the planned economy  
   b. Felt they lost status and protection  
   c. More attached to national identity; more skeptical of rapid change

E. New European elites
1. Emergence of a transnational professional class  
   a. Well‑educated, well‑dressed, mobile  
   b. Worked for multinational companies and EU‑related institutions  
   c. Often lived in global cities like London, Brussels, Berlin
2. Example: London as a European/global financial hub  
   a. Average salaries very high  
   b. Attracted workers from across Europe; \"Anglo‑Saxon capitalism\"

F. Borders after 1989
1. Physical borders between East and West came down (e.g. Berlin Wall)  
2. But **social** and **economic** borders grew:
   a. Class, race, age, and geography separated people  
   b. Example: Roma communities denied effective citizenship within new states

G. Key insight
1. The end of communism did not simply unite Europe – it produced \"many Europes\" divided by class, region, age, and privilege.

### IV. Anderson – *The European Union and its Critics* (Presentation)

A. Central question
1. How was the EU’s power built, and what does that mean for democracy?

B. Main argument
1. European integration was not primarily driven by democratic will  
2. Unelected institutions (Commission, Court of Justice, central bankers) expanded their authority gradually  
3. Results in a **democratic deficit**: citizens are governed by bodies they did not directly choose and cannot easily change

C. Language and narrative of integration
1. Term \"integration\" sounds technical and neutral  
2. More political terms (\"federation\", \"union\") were avoided in early decades  
3. Economic cooperation language hid a deeper political project

D. Role of the European Court of Justice (ECJ)
1. ECJ described as one of the most powerful and least visible EU institutions  
2. Closed proceedings; no published dissenting opinions; judges appointed, not elected  
3. 1960s–1970s: ECJ established **supremacy of EU law** over national law  
   a. This was not explicitly written in the treaties  
   b. Created a constitutional revolution \"through the back door\"

E. Member states vs nation states
1. Integration transforms classic nation states into **member states**  
2. Governments become accountable \"upwards\" to Brussels and other governments, not only \"downwards\" to their own citizens  
3. Domestic policy choices increasingly constrained by EU law and fiscal rules

F. Constitutional constraints and austerity
1. \"Constitutionalized\" economic rules limit democratic choice  
2. Leaders use Brussels as justification for unpopular decisions  
   a. \"Our hands are tied; Europe requires this\"  
   b. Similar to how US leaders sometimes blame Washington or international agreements

G. Democratic deficit
1. Important decisions shifted from parliaments to courts, central banks, and councils  
2. Elections have limited impact on core economic policies  
3. Politics moves from public debate to legal/technical arenas

H. Author’s background and tone
1. Perry Anderson: long‑time editor of *New Left Review*; Marxist‑inspired historian  
2. Tone: critical and analytical, skeptical of official pro‑integration narratives

I. Key insight
1. EU integration without strong democratic control creates resentment and fuels Euroscepticism – the challenge is how to reconnect integration with democracy.

## Key Takeaways

1. EU enlargements came in waves (1973, 1981, 1986, 1995, 2004, 2007, 2013), each with its own political and economic logic.
2. Economic integration was prioritized before political union because economic benefits were easier to demonstrate and less sensitive than sovereignty questions.
3. Vinen: the fall of the Berlin Wall did not simply unite Europe; it produced multiple Europes divided by age, class, region, and privilege.
4. New transnational elites emerged, centered in global cities, while rural and deindustrialized regions often lagged behind.
5. Anderson: EU integration expanded the power of unelected institutions (especially the ECJ) and created a democratic deficit.
6. Supremacy of EU law, member‑state logic, and constitutional constraints limit what national governments and voters can decide.
7. Both texts help explain rising Euroscepticism and tensions between integration and democracy.
