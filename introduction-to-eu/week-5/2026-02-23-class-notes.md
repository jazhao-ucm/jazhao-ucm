## Key Concepts

### I. Luxembourg Compromise - National Interest Prevailed

1. After the Luxembourg agreement, countries could exert the veto when national interest was at stake.
2. National interest prevailed over supranational decision-making.
3. US played a role; context of the times.

---

### II. Southern Enlargement - Greece (1981)

A. Greece before accession

1. Military dictatorship (colonels) 1967–1974
2. 1967 coup by group of colonels; community was initially hesitant
3. Cyprus conflict 1974: war with Turkey over Cyprus; Cyprus partitioned (north under Turkey, south Greek)
4. Fall of dictatorship; transition to democracy
5. Greece joined EC in 1981

B. Key point
1. Joining the European Community was central to consolidating democracy.

---

### III. Southern Enlargement - Spain and Portugal (1986)

A. Portugal

1. Carnation Revolution (1974): peaceful revolution; flowers in the streets
2. Last European empire (colonies in Africa); decolonisation movements
3. Different from Spain: revolution, not negotiated transition

B. Spain

1. Transition after Franco's death (1975)
2. No revolution; negotiated transition
3. Mandatory for EU membership: democratic consolidation
4. Process: 1977–1986 (negotiations)

C. Why Southern enlargement mattered

1. Consolidating democracy in these countries
2. Fear of communism; anchoring them in the West
3. External factors (US) and internal factors both important

D. Challenges during negotiations

1. France worried about CAP: Spain and Portugal had large farming sectors; would receive substantial CAP funds
2. Spain's fishing industry: concern from other countries
3. Steel and textile industries: long-standing, many women workers; restructuring needed
4. Specialization: in common market, cannot produce everything; must specialize
5. Winners and losers between and within countries

---

### IV. Cohesion Policy - Helping Poorer Members

A. Concept

1. Spain and Portugal were poorer than existing members (France, Germany, Italy)
2. Cohesion: share resources with poorer members to strengthen the whole
3. Committee of Regions, structural funds, European Investment Bank

B. Mechanisms

1. Grants and subsidies for development projects
2. Infrastructure, transport, environmental projects in less developed regions
3. Richer states contribute; poorer receive
4. Step towards federalism (federal-style redistribution)

C. Key point
1. Cohesion policy was created to help Spain, Portugal, and other poorer members catch up.

---

### V. Jacques Delors (1985–1995)

A. Significance

1. One of the most productive political figures in the history of the European Community
2. President of the European Commission for 10 years

B. Achievements

1. Presided over accessions of Spain and Portugal (1986)
2. Managed new situation after fall of Soviet Union (Eastern European countries)
3. German reunification
4. European Monetary System, Single European Act, Maastricht Treaty
5. Schengen Agreement
6. Cohesion policy and structural funds
7. Group of reforms that changed the Community

C. Key point
1. Delors was central to the integration process during this period.

---

### VI. Northern Enlargement - Sweden, Finland, Austria (1995)

A. Timeline

1. Sweden, Finland, Austria joined in 1995
2. Norway applied but referendum rejected (again)
3. Switzerland: traditional reservations about foreign policy and welfare system

B. Sweden

1. High taxes but comprehensive social services (health, education, transport)
2. Different model from US; proud of their system
3. Applied in 1991; joined 1995

C. "Sandwich" metaphor for enlargement

1. First bread: Southern enlargement (Greece, Spain, Portugal)
2. Filling: Northern countries (Sweden, Finland, Austria) in between
3. Second bread: Eastern enlargement (later)

---

### VII. European Monetary System (EMS)

A. Purpose

1. Means for member states to settle transactions between themselves
2. Stable exchange rates; precursor to single currency
3. Similar systems exist in Southeast Asia (e.g. Singapore, Indonesia)

B. Key point
1. First step towards Economic and Monetary Union; not yet single currency.

---

### VIII. Single European Act (1986)

A. Context

1. Defeat of federalist/supranational proposals (e.g. Spinelli draft treaty)
2. Luxembourg pattern: national interest prevailed again
3. But objectives were achieved "through the back door"

B. Objectives (Delors)

1. Single market without frontiers (goods, services, people, capital)
2. Economic and social cohesion
3. Research and technology
4. Monetary union
5. Social dimension

C. Key point
1. "The market with no frontiers" – merchandise, people, capital; economic union.

---

### IX. Maastricht Treaty (1992)

A. Context

1. Fall of Soviet Union; Eastern European countries seeking to join
2. German reunification
3. New challenges: more countries, different levels of development

B. Structure

1. Built on existing treaties (Rome, etc.); not a replacement
2. New objectives: Economic and Monetary Union, political union
3. Political union now among main objectives (not just economic)

C. UK opt-outs

1. Social policy: UK opted out (did not want EU social regulations)
2. Economic and Monetary Union: UK opted out of euro
3. Negotiation leverage: UK had power; got opt-outs others did not

D. Institutional changes

1. Qualified majority voting extended in Council
2. More power to European Parliament (co-decision)
3. Committee of Regions to participate in decision-making
4. Structural funds for environmental and transport projects

E. Ratification

1. Signed February 1992; ratified November 1993 (almost two years later)
2. Referendums in some countries; increasing talk of "multi-speed Europe"
3. Different parts of Europe growing at different speeds

---

### X. Schengen Agreement

A. Achievement

1. Abolition of internal border controls
2. Free movement of persons across participating states
3. Single visa: apply in one country, travel to all Schengen countries

B. Scale

1. 26 countries (most of EU plus some non-EU)
2. Population over 400 million
3. No internal border checks for residents and travellers

C. Key point
1. Central to the idea of "Europe without frontiers."

---

### XI. Federalism vs. Intergovernmentalism - Tensions

A. Italian position

1. Italy favoured strong supranational institutions
2. More power to Parliament and Commission

B. UK position

1. UK (Thatcher) opposed federalism
2. Wanted intergovernmental cooperation
3. Flexibility, competitive markets, less regulation

C. Key point
1. Tension between federalist and intergovernmental visions continued throughout this period.

## Key Takeaways

1. Luxembourg Compromise: national interest and veto prevailed.
2. Southern enlargement (1981, 1986): Greece, Spain, Portugal; democracy consolidation central.
3. Cohesion policy: help poorer members; structural funds, Committee of Regions.
4. Jacques Delors: central figure; Single European Act, Maastricht, Schengen.
5. Single European Act: single market, no frontiers.
6. Maastricht Treaty: EMU, political union, UK opt-outs.
7. Schengen: free movement, 26 countries, 400+ million.
8. Northern enlargement (1995): Sweden, Finland, Austria.
