## Rhetorical Framework (Ethos, Logos, Pathos)

| Appeal | Meaning | Gilbert's Use |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| **Ethos** | Credibility, authority, trust | Historiographical critic; challenges dominant narratives; advocates for methodological reform |
| **Logos** | Logic, structure, argument | Critique of teleology; contingency, dissent, losers as analytical categories |
| **Pathos** | Emotion, stakes, persuasion | Appeal to readers who distrust "success story" histories; urgency of honest engagement |

**Key insight:** Gilbert uses *logos* (teleology as problem, contingency as alternative) to build *ethos* (as critical historian). His *pathos* targets readers who feel the official EU story is too smooth—crisis, power, exclusion deserve more space.

## Summary & Key Points

- Gilbert criticises the dominant "progressive" narrative that portrays European integration as a linear story of peace, prosperity and democracy.
- He argues that such teleological accounts downplay conflict, failure and alternative paths that were possible at each moment.
- The article calls for more critical histories that treat the EU as one contested political project among many, rather than as the inevitable end-point of European history.

**Rhetorical note:** "Questioning" in the title = *logos* (interrogation as argument) + *ethos* (Gilbert as critic). "Progressive story" = target of critique; *pathos* in what gets suppressed (conflict, failure, alternatives).

---

## Main Arguments

### I. Normative, Teleological Histories

Much writing about European integration is implicitly normative: it is a history *for* Europe, justifying the EU by projecting a coherent, upward trajectory from war to peace. Gilbert argues that this obscures the messy, contingent nature of the process.

**Rhetorical profile:**
- **Logos:** Teleology as analytical flaw; "history for" vs. "history of"; messy, contingent as corrective.
- **Ethos:** Gilbert as critic of conventional historiography; identifies what dominant narratives hide.
- **Pathos:** "Messy, contingent"—appeal to readers who resist neat success stories.

---

### II. Foregrounding Contingency, Dissent, and Losers

He proposes that historians should foreground contingency, dissent and losers of integration, giving more space to actors and ideas that do not fit the official success story. This would make our understanding of the EU more realistic and less celebratory.

**Rhetorical profile:**
- **Logos:** Contingency, dissent, losers as analytical categories; "more realistic" as standard.
- **Ethos:** Gilbert as advocate for methodological reform; alternative historiography.
- **Pathos:** "Losers" = whose story gets told; appeal for inclusion, honesty.

## Critical Analysis

### Strengths

- Offers a sharp, well-argued critique of conventional EU histories; encourages more honest engagement with crisis, power and exclusion

**Rhetorical note:** Strengths align with *logos* (argumentative clarity) and *ethos* (critical voice). Directly foundational for Krawatzek & Pestel's "new histories" discussion—Gilbert is a key reference.

---

### Weaknesses or Limitations

- Less concrete in showing how to write such alternative histories in practice; focuses more on historiographical debate than on detailed case studies

**Rhetorical note:** *Logos* (prescription) weaker than *logos* (critique). *Ethos* as critic may overshadow *ethos* as practitioner of alternative history.

---

### Questions Raised

- How can teaching and public communication about the EU balance the need for a motivating story with the need for critical accuracy?

**Rhetorical framing:**
- **Ethos:** Who tells the story? Politicians vs. historians?
- **Logos:** What trade-offs? Motivation vs. accuracy?
- **Pathos:** Stakes of legitimacy—if success story is questioned, how do we justify (or criticise) the EU?

---

### Connections to Other Readings

- Directly linked to Krawatzek & Pestel's "new histories of and for Europe"
- Connects to Anderson and Vinen's sceptical perspectives on integration

**Rhetorical note:** Gilbert is a methodological anchor for the critical strand of the course. His *ethos* (historiographical critic) underpins Krawatzek & Pestel's review and Anderson/Vinen's scepticism.

## Relevance to Course

- **Ethos:** Meta-level framework for evaluating how EU history is told in textbooks, speeches, official narratives.
- **Logos:** Avoid teleology in your own essays—discuss crises, failures, alternatives without assuming inevitability.
- **Pathos:** Connects to legitimacy debates—if the success story is questioned, new justifications (or criticisms) are needed.
