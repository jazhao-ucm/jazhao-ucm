\section*{\color{eublue}Optional recap (from transcript) ... democratic deficit}
\begin{itemize}
\item Democratic deficit (plain words): the EU can make big decisions, but citizens don't always feel they can directly influence those decisions.
\item Early integration: decisions were shaped mostly by national politics and big institutions (especially the Council/Commission), while the EP initially had less direct impact on what citizens experienced.
\item EP at the start: because it wasn't directly elected and wasn't very visible, it could feel more like a discussion space than a real decision-maker.
\item Over time: reforms and treaty changes gradually increased the EP's legitimacy and influence (not all at once).
\item How debates get organized: political groups in the EP are built around left--right ideology across countries, so the link between what you vote for nationally and what you hear in EU debates can feel confusing.
\item 1979 turning point: the first direct elections boosted the EP's democratic mandate and awareness in public life.
\item Today: the EP shares legislative power and has budget influence, so it is more than symbolic; still, people can feel unsure about what their vote actually changes.
\item Citizen engagement (from the discussion): focus on clearer explanations of what each EU body does, and how to understand the EU political groups in real life.
\item Workshop connection: the groups that can push their language and proposals through the parliamentary process shape what legitimacy looks like to citizens.
\end{itemize}
