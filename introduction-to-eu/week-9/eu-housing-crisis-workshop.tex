\textit{Overview of major political groups' positions on the EU housing crisis, as reflected in the 2026 Special Committee report and plenary debates.}\\
\textit{Context note: committee rapporteur was from EPP; committee chair was from S\&D.}

\small
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.12}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{>{\bfseries\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{3.2cm} >{\raggedright\arraybackslash}X}
\toprule
Party & Position summary \\
\midrule
European People's Party (EPP) & {\raggedright\textbf{Core argument:} shortage is mainly a supply problem: too few homes built due to slow permits, complex planning, and high construction costs.\par
\textbf{Tools:} permit/planning simplification (incl. 60-day permit target), buyer/developer tax incentives, construction support, legal certainty for property owners.\par
\textbf{EU stance:} cautious-supportive (EU as facilitator, not heavy regulator).\par
\textbf{Pass/block:} pass supply-side measures; block rent caps and strong anti-speculation intervention (argues caps reduce investment and worsen shortages).} \\
\midrule
Progressive Alliance of Socialists and Democrats (S\&D) & {\raggedright\textbf{Core argument:} housing is a social emergency and basic right; market-only approaches have failed renters.\par
\textbf{Tools:} EU Affordable Housing Plan (high-grant public funding), tenant protections, short-term rental regulation.\par
\textbf{EU stance:} pro-EU intervention.\par
\textbf{Pass/block:} pass earmarked social/affordable measures; block market-only approach (framed as favoring landlords over tenants). Fiscal line: push ``golden rule'' treatment for social housing investment.} \\
\midrule
Renew Europe (Renew) & {\raggedright\textbf{Core argument:} affordability crisis is real, but heavy mandates risk backfiring; prefer practical, incentive-based fixes.\par
\textbf{Tools:} investment tools, tax barrier removal for first-time buyers, incentive-based measures, internal-market harmonization for construction materials/standards.\par
\textbf{EU stance:} pro-EU coordination.\par
\textbf{Pass/block:} pass investment-led measures; block strict price/rent controls. Coalition line: bridge EPP supply agenda with S\&D social concerns.} \\
\midrule
Greens/European Free Alliance (Greens/EFA) & {\raggedright\textbf{Core argument:} right to housing requires stronger public intervention against speculation and homelessness.\par
\textbf{Tools:} public/social housing investment, anti-speculation rules, Housing First, retrofit/energy standards, Social Climate Fund linkage.\par
\textbf{EU stance:} strong pro-EU intervention.\par
\textbf{Pass/block:} pass stronger public-housing and anti-speculation rules; block weak compromise outcomes.} \\
\midrule
The Left in the European Parliament (The Left) & {\raggedright\textbf{Core argument:} housing has been financialised into an asset class; decommodification is required.\par
\textbf{Tools:} rent caps, large public/cooperative housing, stronger anti-eviction protections, no-eviction-without-alternative-housing line.\par
\textbf{EU stance:} pro-EU, but critical of EU fiscal constraints on public investment.\par
\textbf{Pass/block:} pass tenant/public-housing protections; block diluted or market-dominant packages.} \\
\midrule
European Conservatives and Reformists (ECR) & {\raggedright\textbf{Core argument:} subsidiarity first; housing policy is mainly national/local, not an EU competence.\par
\textbf{Tools:} deregulation, planning simplification, national/local decision control, property-rights emphasis.\par
\textbf{EU stance:} skeptical of binding EU housing rules.\par
\textbf{Pass/block:} pass simplification and national-control measures; block EU-wide rent/social mandates.} \\
\midrule
Patriots for Europe (PfE) & {\raggedright\textbf{Core argument:} affordability reflects bad national choices, high energy costs, and migration pressure, not lack of EU legislation.\par
\textbf{Tools:} national-level policy, lower family burden, reduced cost pressure framing, anti-``green over-regulation'' line.\par
\textbf{EU stance:} very skeptical of EU mandates.\par
\textbf{Pass/block:} pass sovereignty-focused and cost-relief measures; block binding EU housing intervention.} \\
\midrule
Europe of Sovereign Nations (ESN) & {\raggedright\textbf{Core argument:} hardest line against EU involvement; EU housing directives are framed as bureaucratic overreach.\par
\textbf{Tools:} redirect funding to national/regional priorities, minimal EU market intervention.\par
\textbf{EU stance:} very skeptical/hostile to EU-level housing regulation.\par
\textbf{Pass/block:} pass national-priority framing; block supranational regulatory expansion.} \\
\midrule
Non-Inscrits (NI) & {\raggedright\textbf{Lexicon:} no single line; depends on individual MEP.\par
\textbf{Tools:} case-by-case (individual MEP position).\par
\textbf{EU stance:} mixed/variable.\par
\textbf{Pass/block:} mixed/variable by member.} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}

\section*{\color{eublue}Key}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Pass/block}: what each party tends to support moving forward vs what it tends to oppose.
\item \textbf{EU stance}: overall position on how strong the EU-level role should be in housing policy.
\item \textbf{Source frame}: based on positions reflected in the 2026 HOUS committee report and group statements/debates.
\item \textbf{Abbreviations}: EPP, S\&D, Renew, Greens/EFA, The Left, ECR, PfE, ESN, NI (Non-Inscrits).
\end{itemize}

\section*{\color{eublue}Debate Fault Lines and Negotiation Map}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Fault line 1}: supply-side fixes vs demand-side protections.
\item \textbf{Fault line 2}: market-led adjustment vs public intervention.
\item \textbf{Fault line 3}: EU competence vs national sovereignty/subsidiarity.
\item \textbf{Likely negotiation axis}: EPP + Renew can align on investment/supply tools; S\&D + Greens can align on public housing and anti-speculation.
\item \textbf{Blocking dynamic}: ECR, PfE, and ESN tend to resist or dilute binding EU-level outcomes.
\end{itemize}
