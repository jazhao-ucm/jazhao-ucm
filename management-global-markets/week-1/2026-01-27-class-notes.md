## Chapter 2: The Silent Language in Business – Cross-Cultural Competence

---

## Key Concepts

### I. National vs. International Operations

A. National Operations
1. Working with people from the same culture
2. Makes things less complicated
3. Easier communication and understanding
4. Shared language and cultural norms

B. International Operations
1. Working with people from different cultures
2. More complex and challenging
3. Requires cross-cultural competence
4. Need for empathy and understanding

C. Key insight
1. When working internationally, we can't assume everyone shares the same culture, language, or business practices.

---

### II. Cross-Cultural Competence & Empathy

A. What You Need to Work Internationally
1. Language - Communication is essential
2. Empathy - Understanding different perspectives
3. Cultural awareness - Recognizing differences

Without empathy: "Inside company, and you go nowhere."

Example: If you're 25 years old managing people in different countries, you need empathy to understand their perspectives and work effectively.

---

### III. Value Chain Analysis (Michael Porter)

Definition: A tool to analyze company activities and identify which create value vs. which are costs.

A. Purpose
1. Recognize which activities are valuable or sources of cost
2. Identify differentiation advantages
3. Determine competitive advantages
4. Find areas for improvement within the company

B. Key question
1. What can we improve inside the company to increase profitability?

---

### IV. Primary Activities vs. Support Activities

A. **Primary Activities** (Everything related to the product itself)
1. Inbound Logistics - Receiving, storing raw materials
2. Operations - Production/transformation of goods
3. Outbound Logistics - Moving finished products to customers
4. Marketing & Sales - Promoting and selling products
5. Service - Customer support and after-sales service

B. **Support Activities** (Departments that support primary activities)
1. Procurement - Purchasing raw materials, supplies
2. Technology Development - R&D, innovation
3. Human Resources - Hiring, training, supporting employees
4. Infrastructure - Management, finance, administration

C. Key Difference
1. Primary activities = Directly create value and generate revenue
2. Support activities = Enable primary activities but don't directly generate revenue

Important: All departments work together and are connected. Managers must communicate across departments.

---

### V. Marketing vs. Sales

A. Marketing
1. What you show to get customers
2. Creates awareness and interest
3. Comes first

B. Sales
1. What gets the money
2. Converts interest into purchases
3. Comes after marketing

Process: Marketing → Sales → Profit

---

### VI. Internationalization Process

A. The Order of Internationalization

B. When moving a company to a new country, departments move in this order

1. Sales (First)
   a. Need to explore new markets
   b. Find new customers
   c. Test the market

2. Marketing (Second)
   a. Support sales efforts
   b. Build brand presence
   c. Understand local market

3. Operations/Production (Later)
   a. Only after establishing market presence
   b. If it becomes cheaper to produce locally
   c. After many years of successful sales

C. Why This Order?
1. You need to check the market first (sales)
2. Marketing supports sales efforts
3. Production is expensive and risky - only move when it makes economic sense

Timeline: This process takes many years, not just one year.

Example: Instructor's experience - took 2 years just to move sales department from France to Chile.

---

### VII. Globalization vs. Internationalization

A. Globalization
1. Moving departments to different locations
2. Isolating departments in different countries
3. Example: Production in China, Sales in USA, R&D in Europe
4. "We're not moving the whole world, we're isolating departments"

B. Internationalization
1. Moving entire company operations to a new country
2. Complete relocation of business

C. Key Difference
1. Globalization involves fragmenting operations across countries, while internationalization involves moving everything together.

---

### VIII. Logistics & Supply Chain

A. What You Need for Logistics
1. Good and strong infrastructure
2. Warehouses - Storage facilities
3. Transportation - How to move goods
4. Distribution networks - How to sell and deliver

B. Four Types of Transportation
1. Truck - For continental/land transport
2. Train - For land transport
3. Ship - 95% of world trade (most important)
4. Plane - For urgent/time-sensitive goods

C. Key point
1. Ships handle 95% of all world trade - most goods move by sea.

---

### IX. Procurement & Purchasing

A. Why Look for Different Suppliers?
1. Different needs
2. Price comparison
3. Quality comparison
4. Delivery time options
5. Always negotiate prices

B. Key principle
1. "You buy, you don't want to buy, you need to buy something. So price goes up."

C. Location Considerations
1. Simple products - Can produce anywhere (e.g., bread)
2. Complex products - Need to stay in certain locations (e.g., advanced technology)
3. Example: Can't send complex products to China for production if they require specific expertise

D. Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)
1. When foreign companies invest in your country
2. Creates competition
3. Can benefit local economy

---

### X. Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions

Geert Hofstede - Psychologist who studied IBM employees across different countries

A. Key Cultural Dimensions

1. Masculinity vs. Femininity
   a. Different ways men and women relate
   b. Varies by culture

2. Collectivism vs. Individualism
   a. **Individualism:** People in rich countries tend to be more individualistic
   b. **Collectivism:** People in poorer countries tend to be more collective/group-oriented

3. Power Distance (Most Important for Management)
   a. **High Power Distance:**
   b. Strong hierarchy
   c. Boss is clearly superior
   d. Employees show great respect/fear to boss
   e. Examples: Spain, Mexico, North Korea
   f. Employees do everything to please boss, avoid being fired

   a. **Low Power Distance:**
   b. Flat hierarchy
   c. Boss and employees work together as equals
   d. Examples: Northern Europe, USA
   e. More casual relationships

B. Examples
1. Spain: High power distance - "If you talk back to manager, you get fired"
2. Mexico: Very high power distance - Instructor was 27 years old making much more than older employees
3. Northern Europe: Low power distance - Everyone works together, no hierarchy

---

### XI. Power Distance & Management

A. High Power Distance Countries
1. Boss has absolute authority
2. Employees fear boss
3. Employees won't challenge or question
4. Example: If instructor asks student to leave, student leaves immediately because instructor is "the boss"

B. Low Power Distance Countries
1. More collaborative
2. Employees can question managers
3. More equal relationships

C. Management Insight
1. "A good manager always blames yourself"
2. If you have to fire someone, it's because you're doing something wrong
3. Don't blame employees - take responsibility
4. "We don't control people, we manage them"

Warning: Power distance can lead to abuse of power. Some professors/managers think they're great just because of their position.

---

### XII. Hierarchy Differences Across Cultures

A. Spain
1. Strong hierarchy
2. Can't talk back to manager
3. High power distance

B. Mexico
1. Very high power distance
2. Extreme hierarchy
3. Salary differences reflect power distance

C. USA
1. More individualistic
2. Lower power distance
3. More equal relationships

D. Key point
1. "Hierarchy is never the same" across countries. What works in one country doesn't work in another.

---

### XIII. Finance Department

A. Finance (Inside Company)
1. Budget management
2. Travel expenses
3. Sales tracking
4. Profit calculation
5. Cash flow management
6. Not about stock market - about internal company finances

Purpose: Manage money flow within the company to ensure profitability.

---

### XIV. Business Goals

Primary Goal: Profit/Profitability

A. Key principle
1. "Any business, tell me what they're looking for, if it's not profit."

2. If you make money, you're successful
3. If you don't make money, you're not successful
4. Profit drives all business decisions

5. Power distance varies dramatically across cultures and affects management style
6. High power distance = strict hierarchy, fear-based management
7. Low power distance = collaborative, equal relationships
8. All departments are connected - managers must communicate across functions
9. Logistics is critical - 95% of trade moves by ship
