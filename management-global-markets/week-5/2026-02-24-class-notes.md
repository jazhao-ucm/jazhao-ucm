## Key Concepts

### I. Three Scopes of Activities

A. Three scopes
1. Product scope – what you produce (specialization vs. diversification)
2. Geographic scope – where you operate (domestic → international → global)
3. Activity scope – which departments/activities (Marketing, Finance, HR, Operations)

B. Departments
1. Marketing – find customers, introduce improvements
2. Finance – money, taxes, investment
3. HR – human resources, qualified personnel
4. All must integrate. If departments don't work together, you lose information and money when internationalizing.

---

### II. Product Scope – Specialization vs. Diversification

A. Specialization
1. One single product. Low variety. High volume.
2. Same competencies. Same technology. Same production.
3. Cannot produce very different products with same capabilities.

B. Diversification
1. Different products. Different packaging. Different markets.
2. Example: Fanta – orange in one country, different color/flavor in another (Mexico, Europe).
3. Depends on company strategy.

C. Single business vs. dominant business
1. **Single business**: One product only. That's all you sell.
2. **Dominant business**: One product sells best (e.g. 50%+ of revenue). Others are secondary. You focus on the dominant one.
3. If single business is not selling, you may add products → move toward dominant business.

---

### III. Geographic Scope – Internationalization

A. Why leave domestic market?
1. Domestic market collapsing. Too many competitors. Price pressure. Can't survive.
2. Opportunities outside. Many possibilities. Attractive markets.
3. Need to grow. Need to internationalize.

B. The graph: local → middle → international
1. Most companies stay in the middle (domestic/regional).
2. Few move outside. Requires money, competencies, capabilities.
3. Every time you go out, you look at different markets. More kilometers = more complexity. Different regulations. Different features.

---

### IV. Export vs. Internationalization

A. Export
1. Sell product to another country. Product moves. Company stays.
2. Lower risk. Fewer barriers. No physical relocation.
3. Direct export: company sells directly to buyer.
4. Indirect export: through intermediaries (retailers, distributors in other countries).

B. Internationalization
1. **Physically move** departments, production, facilities to another country.
2. Marketing in another country. Production in another country.
3. Much more complex. Transport. Facilities. People. Communication. Language.
4. Higher risk. Higher cost. Especially at the beginning.

C. Key distinction
1. "When you move physically, when you detach one department physically – marketing, production – outside, then it is internationalization."
2. Export = product crosses border. Internationalization = company crosses border.

---

### V. Reasons for Internationalization

A. Push factors (domestic)
1. Market collapsing. Too many competitors.
2. Not enough customers. Need to grow.

B. Pull factors (abroad)
1. **Attractive market** – customers, growth, opportunity.
2. **Lower costs** – taxes (e.g. France, Belgium high; relocate to lower-tax countries), labor.
3. **Natural resources** – raw materials where they are (e.g. Ukraine, Russia for certain resources).
4. **Qualified personnel** – skilled workers. R&D. Technology.
5. **Reputation/position** – "international" looks better than "local."
6. **Economic integration** – invest in another country. Create jobs. Participate in local economy.

C. Relocation of production
1. Why move production site to another country?
2. Market – closer to customers.
3. Cost – lower taxes, lower labor.
4. Scale – bigger space, produce more (e.g. China, extended territory).
5. Resources – access to raw materials.

---

### VI. Risk and Integration

A. Risk levels
1. Export: lower risk. Fewer barriers.
2. Internationalization: higher risk. More costs. More complexity. Regulations. Currency.

B. Technology and information
1. Must integrate departments. Marketing, Finance, HR, Technology.
2. Information flows. Technology belongs to company.
3. If you don't integrate when going international, you lose: money, information, control.

C. Finance risk
1. Moving money between countries. Repatriation of investment.
2. Government can block. Regulations. Currency controls.
3. Chile case: company lost money. Could not repatriate. Different rules in different countries.

---

### VII. Levels of Internationalization

A. Multi-domestic
1. Different production sites in different countries.
2. Each country has its own operations.
3. Adapted to local market.

B. Global
1. Integrated across countries. Centralized. Standardized.
2. Different logistics. Different departments in different places.
3. More complex. More coordinated.

---

## Takeaways

1. Three scopes: product, geographic, activity. All matter.
2. Specialization = one product. Diversification = many. Single vs. dominant business.
3. Export ≠ Internationalization. Export = product moves. Internationalization = company moves.
4. Reasons: market, cost, resources, personnel, reputation.
5. Integration between departments is critical. Otherwise you lose money and information.
6. Finance risk: repatriation. Government. Regulations.
