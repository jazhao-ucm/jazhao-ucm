## Management in Global Markets – Midterm Cheat Sheet

**How to use:** Use Section 1 (Summary) for quick recall; Section 2 (Full notes) for definitions and application. In the exam, always **define terms**, **give one example**, and **tie to internationalization success/failure** when possible.

---

### Unit 0 – Course & Big Ideas

- **International business** = Operating across borders. Goods, services, capital, people move.
- **Key question**: Why go abroad? Market size, resources, efficiency, learning.
- **Reality**: Most companies fail at internationalization. Wrong location, weak CA, poor execution.
- **"Location is the masterpiece"** – Choosing the right location is more important than skills, timing, or hard work.

---

### Unit 1 – Globalization vs. Slowbalization

- **Globalization** = Free movement of goods, services, capital across borders. High production + high export. Interconnected economies.
- **Slowbalization** = Globalization slowing. Countries more protectionist. Reduced trade. Focus on localization.
- **Winners**: China (biggest exporter), India, Indonesia. **USA** = biggest importer. Strong in software, tech.
- **Economic complexity** = Ability to transform raw materials into complex products (computers, iPhones) using technology, innovation, R&D. Higher complexity = higher selling prices.
- **Top in economic complexity**: USA, UK, Netherlands. Innovation = difference between complexity and mere distribution.
- **Demographics**: Europe ageing (60% over 60 by 2032). Africa growing. Young workers = production capacity.
- **Protectionism**: Tariffs = taxes on imports. Countries protect domestic industries. "If we can't stop China, we slow them down."
- **G20**: ~20 countries, highest GDP, most important for trade. Represents ~50% global economy. Spain is part.
- **Localization** = Adapting products/services for specific local markets. Necessary before internationalization.

---

### Unit 2 – Supply Chain & Products by Country

- **Supply chain** = How parts move from one place to another before a product is made. Global = many countries involved.
- **Quality vs. cost trade-off**: Japan = high quality, higher price ("when you buy Japan, you buy quality"). China = lower cost, volume, repetition; quality varies.
- **Products from many countries**: Shoes (Vietnam), shirt (India), watch (China), jewelry (Vietnam, Botswana). Rarely 100% from one country.
- **Aircraft assembly**: Parts from multiple countries, assembled in one location (e.g. Chicago). Different countries specialize in different parts.
- **Risks when going global**:
  - **Cultural**: Different norms, values, beliefs. "You cannot escape communication and language."
  - **Legal**: Different systems, regulations, compliance.
  - **Logistical**: Transportation, infrastructure.
  - **Financial**: Currencies, exchange rates, tax.
  - **Communication**: Language barriers. Must adapt; cannot avoid.
- **Market attractiveness**: GDP growth, population growth, political risk (stability). Low risk: USA, UK, France, Germany.
- **Tax systems**: High taxes on what you produce domestically; low on what you need but don't produce. Protection.
- **Competition strategy**: **Go where competitors are** (Option B). Competitors = market validation, infrastructure exists, you can learn, customers exist. Don't create market alone (Option C). "You cannot escape competition."
- **Culture shock**: Honeymoon (excitement) → reality (hard) → adapt. Inevitable. Must face it and adapt.

---

### Unit 3 – Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions

- **Culture** = "Shared beliefs, values, norms that distinguish one group from another."
- **Power distance**: High = strong hierarchy (Spain, Mexico). Low = flat (Northern Europe, USA).
- **Individualism vs. collectivism**: Rich countries tend individualistic (USA); poorer tend collective (many Asian).
- **Uncertainty avoidance**: How comfortable with ambiguity. Some prefer rules; others tolerate it.
- **Long-term vs. short-term orientation**: Long-term = plan for future (USA, West) when you have security. Short-term = survive today (developing) when uncertain.
- **Key insight**: Culture is one of the biggest challenges. You cannot escape it—you must adapt.

---

### Unit 4 – Internationalization: Four Phases & Location

- **Phase 1 – Conceive (Planning)**: See market, analyze opportunities. 2–3 years. Research.
- **Phase 2 – Execute (Implementation)**: Move, plant, associate. Establish operations. 2 years. Highest investment. **Critical phase** – if you leave too early or wrong place, you fail.
- **Phase 3 – Establish (Stabilization)**: Company running, making money. **Most dangerous phase** – most companies fail here. If not done correctly, everything fails after.
- **Phase 4 – Expand (Growth)**: Successful. Scale operations.
- **Total**: 4–5 years typically.
- **Two types of risk**: **Artificial** (laws, rules)—you can manage. **Natural** (language, distance, culture)—you cannot escape; must adapt.
- **Why companies fail**: Wrong location, not enough information, poor communication, no backup plans, left alone.

---

### Unit 5 – Strategy Levels & Internationalization Logic

- **Corporate strategy** = Which businesses? (Cars vs. trucks). Portfolio of divisions.
- **Competitive (business) strategy** = How to compete in each business? (Price, quality, differentiation, niche).
- **Functional strategy** = HR, marketing, finance, operations, R&D. Who does what inside each division.
- **Keep strategy simple. Long-term.** Strategy for 15 years, not 15 days. "Slow is fast."
- **SME** = Small/medium. Under 250 employees, <€50M revenue. Spain: ~99% are SMEs. Few internationalize.
- **Location choice** = Critical. Wrong location can ruin good company. Politics, economy, market, legal. If environment is bad, internationalization fails regardless of internal strengths.
- **Resources** = What you have (tangible, intangible, human). **Capabilities** = What you do with them (processes, routines).
- **Competitive advantage** = Better/different than competitors. **Absolute advantage** = Almost no one has it. Rare. For internationalization, strong CA necessary; absolute helps more.
- **Before going abroad**: Do we have strong competitive advantage? If "just like" existing players in mature market, very risky.
- **Entry modes**: Acquisition (buy local—needs money), joint venture (share ownership with local partner), strategic alliances (licensing, distribution). Partner brings local knowledge, contacts, legitimacy.

---

### Unit 6 – Chile Case & Technology Transfer

- **Chile case**: French company moved capital, people, technology from France to Chile. Shared advanced tech with local partners.
- **What went wrong**: Project failed. Lost money, jobs, technology. Local partners used technology independently. No legal or practical protection. Technology "stolen" in practice. Communication broke down.
- **Lessons**: Be careful transferring core technology abroad. IP protection, contracts, partner selection. Culture and trust differences increase risk.

---

### Unit 7 – Export vs. Internationalization & FDI

- **Export** = Company produces at home. Products shipped abroad. Lower risk. Limited commitment.
- **Internationalization** = Beyond export: set up plants abroad, open subsidiaries, move HQ functions. Requires capital (FDI), strategic decisions, people ready to implement.
- **FDI (Foreign Direct Investment)** = Long-term, heavy commitment in host country. "Next step" after simple trade. Creates need for international HRM.
- **Domestic** = All activities in one country.
- **International/multinational** = Activities in several countries. Often few key locations.
- **Transnational/global** = Think global, act local. Global brands, adapt to each market (cosmetics, food, fashion).

---

### Unit 8 – International HRM & Motivation

- **International HRM** = Managing people for international expansion. Multiple countries, cultures. Cross-border mobility (expats, short assignments). Coordinate HR policies across locations.
- **First questions before expanding**: Which functions move? How many people? Do we have them or hire? How support them and families?
- **Who goes abroad?** Typically younger (flexible, fewer family constraints). Comfortable with tech, languages, different cultures. **Internal candidates** preferred for sensitive roles (known, trusted). **External** = fresh ideas but riskier.
- **Talent** = Skills + availability to move. At some stage you may no longer want to move, even for more money.
- **Motivation**: Salary + ambition, recognition, quality of life. As important as technical skills. "Price to pay" for career: long hours, stress, time away from family.
- **Turnover**: Young workers often leave quickly. Negotiate salary and benefits. Know your market value.
- **Trust**: Firms prefer to send people they know. International roles control large budgets; giving "keys to the kingdom" to outsiders is risky.

---

### Unit 9 – Cash Generation & Competition

- **Cash comes from customers.** If you don't sell, you don't get cash. Business is about profit.
- **Two options**: Minimize expenses OR push sales (more customers).
- **Repeat business** = Most important. Stable cash. Communication prevents abandonment. Deliver on promises.

### Common Question Angles

- **"Why might internationalization fail?"** Wrong location, weak competitive advantage, technology transfer risk (Chile), culture, partner issues, poor execution in Phase 2–3, no backup plans.
- **"Corporate vs. competitive vs. functional strategy?"** Which business / how to compete / who does what.
- **"Export vs. internationalization?"** Export = product moves, company home; internationalization = company moves operations (plants, subsidiaries, HQ roles).
- **"What matters for success abroad?"** Strong CA, right location ("masterpiece"), motivated people, careful tech transfer, good partners, go where competitors are.
- **"Why go where competitors exist?"** Market validation, infrastructure, customers, learn from them, differentiate.
- **"Apply four phases to [case]."** Conceive → Execute (critical) → Establish (most dangerous) → Expand.
