# Section 1: Summary (AP Level / Accessible)

*Use this section first. Plain language. Short sentences.*

## Key Words
- **Export** = Sell abroad. Product moves. Company stays at home.
- **Internationalization** = Company itself moves activities abroad (plants, marketing, HQ roles).
- **FDI (Foreign Direct Investment)** = Long‑term investment in another country (factories, offices).
- **Global strategy** = Think worldwide, adapt locally when needed.
- **International HRM** = Managing people for international expansion.
- **Turnover** = How often employees leave a company.

## Main Ideas (Simple Version)

1. **Export is only the first step.**
   - Export: send products to other countries.
   - Internationalization: build operations abroad and manage them.

2. **Firms choose different international strategies.**
   - Some focus on a few key countries.
   - Some try to be present “everywhere” (global brands).
   - Some adapt a lot to each market, others less.

3. **To internationalize, you need people, not only money.**
   - Competencies and capabilities.
   - Young, mobile, motivated workers are key.

4. **People management becomes international too.**
   - Attract, select, train and move talent to new locations.
   - Trust and internal promotion matter for sensitive positions.

5. **Motivation is complex.**
   - Salary is important, but not the only thing.
   - Ambition, recognition and quality of life also matter.

6. **Turnover and negotiation.**
   - Young workers often leave after a short time.
   - You must learn to negotiate your conditions.

## Key Concepts

### I. From Export to Internationalization

Purpose: Connect earlier strategy content to the human side of going international.

A. Export (review)
1. Company produces in the home country.  
2. Products are shipped abroad.  
3. Lower risk, limited commitment to the foreign market.

B. Internationalization
1. Beyond export:  
   a. Set up production plants abroad.  
   b. Open marketing and sales subsidiaries.  
   c. Move parts of HQ functions to other countries.  
2. Requires:
   a. Capital (FDI).  
   b. Strategic decisions about which markets matter most.  
   c. People ready to implement and run foreign operations.

C. FDI (Foreign Direct Investment)
1. “Next step” after simple trade.  
2. Long‑term, heavy commitment in host country.  
3. Creates a need for international HRM (who will work there? who will lead?).

---

### II. International Strategies and “Thinking Global”

Purpose: Show how different strategy types create different people needs.

A. Domestic vs. international vs. transnational
1. Domestic:  
   a. All activities inside one country.  
   b. Only local customers and regulations.
2. International / multinational:
   a. Activities in several countries.  
   b. Often a few key locations rather than “everywhere.”
3. Transnational / global:
   a. Think global, act local.  
   b. Use global brands and systems.  
   c. Adapt marketing mix and service to each market.

B. Global brands and local adaptation
1. Some products are truly global (same almost everywhere).  
2. Others require strong local adaptation:
   a. Examples from class: cosmetics, food, fashion.  
   b. Same brand, different flavours, packaging, or price positioning.
3. Prices can vary across countries:
   a. Same product cheaper or more expensive depending on local conditions.  
   b. Students recognise this when travelling.

C. Strategic implication
1. Strategy determines:
   a. How many foreign locations you have.  
   b. How different they are from each other.  
2. This, in turn, determines:
   a. How complex your international people management must be.  

---

### III. Competencies, Capabilities and International HRM

Purpose: Introduce why HRM becomes central when firms expand abroad.

A. Need for competencies and capabilities
1. Competencies = skills of individuals.  
2. Capabilities = systems and routines of the firm.  
3. To move corporate activities abroad you need both:
   a. People who can perform the tasks.  
   b. An organisation that supports them.

B. From HRM to International HRM
1. Domestic HRM:
   a. Focus on local recruitment, training, evaluation.  
   b. One legal system and one culture.
2. International HRM:
   a. Must deal with multiple countries and cultures.  
   b. Includes cross‑border mobility (expats, short assignments).  
   c. Must coordinate HR policies across locations.

C. First questions before expanding
1. Which functions will we move abroad?  
2. How many people do we need?  
3. Do we already have them inside the company, or must we hire outside?  
4. How will we support them and their families?

### IV. Talent: Who Goes Abroad?

Purpose: Discuss what “talent” means in an international context.

A. Profile of international talent
1. Typically younger workers:
   a. More flexible.  
   b. Fewer family constraints.  
2. Comfortable with:
   a. Technology and innovation.  
   b. Working in English and/or other languages.  
   c. Living in different cultures.

B. Internal vs. external candidates
1. Internal candidates:
   a. Known performance; higher trust.  
   b. Already socialised into the company’s culture.  
   c. Often preferred for sensitive positions (e.g. factory director).
2. External candidates:
   a. Bring fresh ideas and skills.  
   b. But riskier to give them immediate control over large assets.

C. Availability and willingness
1. Talent is not only about skills, but also about being available to move.  
2. Professor’s point:
   a. At some stage of life you may no longer want to move, even for more money.  
   b. Then you are no longer the right profile for certain international roles.

---

### V. Motivation, Ambition and Quality of Life

Purpose: Begin linking HRM to motivation (developed further in the March 3 lecture).

A. What motivates people?
1. Salary and financial security.  
2. Work conditions (hours, safety, work–life balance).  
3. Recognition and growth.  
4. Personal ambition and desire for responsibility.

B. Ambition and the “price to pay”
1. Not everyone is willing to pay the same price for career success.  
2. International roles can mean:
   a. Long hours.  
   b. High stress and responsibility.  
   c. Long time away from home country and family.
3. Students are encouraged to think about:
   a. How far they want to go.  
   b. What they are, and are not, willing to sacrifice.

C. Negotiation and turnover
1. Young employees today often change jobs quickly.  
2. Companies expect high turnover and may offer minimum conditions.  
3. Advice from professor:
   a. Do not be afraid to negotiate salary and benefits.  
   b. Know your market value; life is expensive.  
   c. Compensation should reflect your contribution and the cost of living.

---

### VI. Teams, Trust and Working with Others

Purpose: Emphasise that international management is never a solo activity.

A. Need for teams
1. No one manages international operations alone.  
2. Baseball analogy from class:
   a. The “star player” still depends on the rest of the team.  
   b. Success is collective, even if recognition is individual.

B. Trust and internal promotion
1. Firms prefer to send abroad:
   a. People they already know and trust.  
   b. People with a history inside the firm.
2. Reason:
   a. International roles often control large budgets and sensitive operations.  
   b. Giving “keys to the kingdom” to complete outsiders is risky.

C. Learning to work with diversity
1. Group exercise announced for the next week:
   a. Students will work with classmates from different countries.  
   b. Aim: experience first‑hand the challenges of international teamwork.  
2. Message:
   a. Diversity is enriching but also demanding.  
   b. You must develop tolerance, patience and communication skills.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Export is only the first step; true internationalization means moving activities and decisions abroad.  
2. Strategic choices (global, multi‑domestic, transnational) determine how complex people management will be.  
3. International HRM links FDI and strategy to concrete questions about who will work where, and under what conditions.  
4. International talent combines skills, mobility, and motivation; availability to move is part of the profile.  
5. Motivation goes beyond salary: ambition, recognition and quality of life all matter, and you must decide what trade‑offs you accept in a global career.
