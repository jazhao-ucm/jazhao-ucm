# Management in Global Markets - Class Notes
## March 3, 2026 - Week 6

**Focus:** Internationalization and international HRM - talent, motivation, and managing diversity in global firms.

---

I. Recap: Internationalization vs. Export
   A. Export (Review from Week 5)
      1. Product crosses border; company stays home
      2. Lower risk, limited commitment
      3. Often first step in going abroad
   B. Internationalization
      1. Physical move of functions abroad:
         a. Production plants
         b. Marketing and sales units
         c. Shared service centers, sometimes HQ roles
      2. Requires:
         a. Capital (FDI)
         b. Organizational capabilities
         c. People with right skills and mindset
   C. FDI (Foreign Direct Investment)
      1. Long-term commitment: factories, offices, logistics
      2. Higher risk and higher potential return
      3. Triggers need for international HRM

II. Why International HRM Matters
   A. From Strategy to People
      1. Strategy (global, multi-domestic, transnational) decides where to operate
      2. HR decides who will run operations and how they'll be supported
      3. Without right people, FDI and internationalization fail
   B. Definition of International HRM
      1. Management tasks related to people in global context:
         a. Attracting and selecting talented workers
         b. Training and developing them
         c. Designing evaluation and reward systems
         d. Managing international assignments (expats, inpatriates, short missions)
      2. Goes beyond domestic HR: cross-border mobility, legal and cultural differences
   C. Competencies and Capabilities
      1. Competencies (people level)
         a. Functional skills (finance, marketing, operations)
         b. Language skills
         c. Cultural openness and tolerance for ambiguity
         d. Willingness to relocate and work in diverse teams
      2. Capabilities (firm level)
         a. Systems for selection and development
         b. Support for expats (housing, schools, safety)
         c. Knowledge transfer between units

III. Attracting, Selecting and Developing Talent
   A. Attraction and Selection
      1. Talent profile for international roles
         a. Typically younger workers - more flexible and mobile
         b. Comfortable with technology and change
         c. Open to living abroad and accepting hardship
      2. Internal vs. external hiring
         a. Internal: higher trust, known performance, fits culture, lower risk
         b. External: new ideas and skills, higher risk if given immediate access
   B. Training and Development
      1. Technical training: products, systems, procedures
      2. Cross-cultural training: local norms, communication styles, negotiation
      3. Leadership development: learning to use power responsibly
   C. Career Paths and Mobility
      1. Typical international career:
         a. Start in home country
         b. Several years abroad (Chile, Mexico, Spain)
         c. Higher responsibility in regional or global HQ
      2. Trade-offs:
         a. Fast learning, high responsibility
         b. Personal cost: distance, stress, safety, burnout

IV. Motivation and Rewards in International Contexts
   A. Classic Theories (Briefly)
      1. Taylor (scientific management, 1911) - pay for performance
      2. Maslow (1943) - hierarchy of needs
      3. Herzberg - hygiene vs. motivator factors
      4. Vroom - expectancy theory (effort → performance → reward)
      5. Theories old, but questions remain similar
   B. What Employees Say They Want
      1. Salary and financial security
      2. Working conditions (work-life balance, safety, hours)
      3. Recognition and respect
      4. Opportunities to learn and grow
      5. For some: leadership and power; for others: stability
   C. Reality in Many Companies
      1. Strong focus on financial targets and profit
      2. Annual performance evaluations tied to bonuses
      3. Bonuses sometimes only to managers, not teams
         a. Manager receives large bonus, team receives nothing
         b. Raises questions about fairness
      4. Managers may face pressure to:
         a. Fire people to meet cost targets
         b. Hit short-term objectives at expense of long-term
   D. Burnout and Life Choices
      1. Professor's experience:
         a. Very high pay and responsibility young
         b. 12-hour days, constant pressure, safety risks
         c. Feeling job was "killing" him → decision to leave
      2. Message to students:
         a. Ambition is good, but know the price
         b. Quality of life matters as much as career success

V. Diversity and International Teams
   A. Sources of Diversity
      1. Demographic changes:
         a. People move across borders for work
         b. Age, gender, ethnicity, educational background
      2. Globalisation:
         a. Multinationals hire in many countries
         b. Project teams mix people from Europe, Latin America, Asia
   B. Effects of Diversity
      1. Potential advantages
         a. More creativity and richer ideas
         b. Better problem-solving (different perspectives)
         c. Easier access to different markets and customers
      2. Challenges
         a. Communication problems (language, style)
         b. Misunderstandings about hierarchy, feedback, deadlines
         c. Risk of conflict and exclusion if not managed
   C. Managing Diversity
      1. Need inclusive company culture:
         a. Clear values and norms everyone knows
         b. Respect for differences; zero tolerance for discrimination
      2. Role of manager:
         a. Build trust within diverse teams
         b. Mediate conflicts; encourage participation
         c. Use diversity as strength, not paralysis
   D. Turnover and Retention
      1. High turnover common among young employees
         a. Companies expect many will leave after few years
         b. Can reduce willingness to invest in people
      2. For employees:
         a. Important to negotiate fair conditions from start
         b. Do not be afraid to discuss salary and benefits

VI. Power, Responsibility and Ethics in Global Management
   A. Power of Managerial Roles
      1. Ability to:
         a. Approve or deny projects
         b. Hire, promote or fire people
         c. Allocate budgets and bonuses
      2. These decisions shape:
         a. Company performance
         b. Many people's lives and careers
   B. Using Power Well vs. Badly
      1. Well:
         a. Transparent criteria
         b. Link rewards to real contribution
         c. Protect people's dignity, even in difficult decisions
      2. Badly:
         a. Favouritism and politics
         b. Taking all credit and rewards for oneself
         c. Abusing hierarchy rather than leading
   C. Personal Fit and Self-Knowledge
      1. Not everyone suited to high-pressure international leadership
      2. Important questions:
         a. How much stress and travel can I accept?
         b. Do I want to manage large teams, or contribute as specialist?
         c. What kind of life do I want, not only title and salary?

VII. Key Takeaways
   A. Exporting products ≠ building international organisation
   B. Global strategies depend on people with specific competencies
   C. Motivation combines salary, conditions, recognition, fair treatment
   D. Diversity in global teams can be asset but needs careful management
   E. Power in international roles comes with responsibility
   F. Must decide trade-offs between money, ambition, and quality of life

X. 30-Second Cold Call Version.
A. 3-Line Speaking Frame.
   1. TOPIC: STATE THE CORE QUESTION IN ONE LINE.
   2. LOGIC: GIVE 2 DRIVERS/FACTORS THAT EXPLAIN THE ANSWER.
   3. ACTION: END WITH ONE DECISION OR IMPLICATION.
B. Delivery Rule.
   1. 25-30 SECONDS MAX.
   2. NO BACKGROUND STORY; GO STRAIGHT TO CLAIM + EVIDENCE + CLOSE.

DI. Oral Answer Template (60-90 Seconds, Class Use).
A. Opening.
   1. STATE THE CORE QUESTION OR TOPIC IN ONE LINE.
   2. STATE YOUR MAIN CLAIM/THESIS IN ONE LINE.
B. Logic.
   1. GIVE 2-3 KEY FACTORS OR DRIVERS.
   2. LINK EACH FACTOR TO THE CLAIM (CAUSE -> EFFECT).
C. Action Or Implication.
   1. END WITH ONE DECISION, RECOMMENDATION, OR CONSEQUENCE.
   2. CLOSE CLEARLY WITHOUT ADDING NEW IDEAS.
D. Delivery Rule.
   1. 60-90 SECONDS.
   2. CLAIM -> EVIDENCE -> CLOSE.
