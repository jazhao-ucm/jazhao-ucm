# Section 1: Summary (AP Level / Accessible)

*Use this section first. Plain language. Short sentences.*

## Key Words
- **Export** = Sell abroad. Product moves. Company stays at home.
- **Internationalization** = Move parts of the company abroad (production, marketing, HQ units).
- **FDI (Foreign Direct Investment)** = Long‑term investment in another country (plants, offices).
- **International HRM** = Managing people for global operations (recruit, select, train, pay, move).
- **Turnover** = How often people leave the company.
- **Diversity** = Different nationalities, cultures, ages and backgrounds working together.

## Main Ideas (Simple Version)

1. **Export and internationalization are not the same.**
   - Export: only products travel.
   - Internationalization: people, capital, decisions also move.

2. **To internationalize, you need the right people.**
   - Young, mobile, open to other cultures.
   - Talent = skills + willingness to move + motivation.

3. **HR has key tasks in global firms.**
   - Attract and select talent.
   - Train, evaluate and keep good people.
   - Decide who goes abroad and for how long.

4. **Motivation is not only about money.**
   - Salary matters.
   - But also recognition, learning, good work conditions, fair treatment.

5. **Diversity brings chances and problems.**
   - Positive: more ideas, more creativity, better problem‑solving.
   - Difficult: misunderstandings, different expectations, more conflict.

6. **Power in management is real.**
   - Managers take decisions that affect careers and jobs.
   - They must balance company goals (profit) with fairness to people.

## Key Concepts

### I. Recap: Internationalization vs. Export

Purpose: Connect previous week’s strategy discussion to people‑related issues.

A. Export (review from Week 5)
1. Product crosses the border; company stays in the home country.
2. Lower risk, limited commitment.
3. Often first step in going abroad.

B. Internationalization
1. Physical move of functions abroad:
   a. Production plants.
   b. Marketing and sales units.
   c. Shared service centers and sometimes HQ roles.
2. Requires:
   a. Capital (FDI).
   b. Organizational capabilities.
   c. People with the right skills and mindset.

C. FDI (Foreign Direct Investment)
1. Long‑term commitment: factories, offices, logistics hubs.
2. Higher risk and higher potential return.
3. Triggers the need for **international HRM**.

---

### II. Why International HRM Matters

Purpose: Show that you cannot implement an international strategy without international people management.

A. From strategy to people
1. Strategy (global, multi‑domestic, transnational) decides **where** to operate.
2. HR decides **who** will run these operations and **how** they will be supported.
3. Without the right people, FDI and internationalization fail.

B. Definition of International HRM
1. Set of management tasks related to people in a global context:
   a. Attracting and selecting talented workers.
   b. Training and developing them.
   c. Designing evaluation and reward systems.
   d. Managing international assignments (expatriates, inpatriates, short‑term missions).
2. Goes beyond domestic HR because of cross‑border mobility, legal and cultural differences.

C. Competencies and capabilities
1. Competencies (people level)
   a. Functional skills (finance, marketing, operations, etc.).
   b. Language skills.
   c. Cultural openness and tolerance for ambiguity.
   d. Willingness to relocate and work in diverse teams.
2. Capabilities (firm level)
   a. Systems for selection and development.
   b. Support for expats (housing, schools, safety).
   c. Knowledge transfer between units.

---

### III. Attracting, Selecting and Developing Talent

Purpose: Understand the HR processes behind building an international workforce.

A. Attraction and selection
1. Talent profile for international roles
   a. Typically younger workers – more flexible and mobile.
   b. Comfortable with technology and change.
   c. Open to living abroad and accepting hardship.
2. Internal vs. external hiring
   a. Internal:
      - Higher trust; known performance.
      - Fits company culture; lower risk with strategic positions.
   b. External:
      - New ideas and skills.
      - Higher risk if given immediate access to “keys to the kingdom” (e.g. big P&L, large budgets).

B. Training and development
1. Technical training: products, systems, procedures.
2. Cross‑cultural training: local norms, communication styles, negotiation habits.
3. Leadership development: learning to use power responsibly in another context.

C. Career paths and mobility
1. Typical international career described in class:
   a. Start in home country.
   b. Several years abroad (e.g. Chile, Mexico, Spain).
   c. Higher responsibility roles in regional or global HQ.
2. Trade‑offs:
   a. Fast learning, high responsibility.
   b. Personal cost: distance from home, stress, safety issues, burnout risk.

---

### IV. Motivation and Rewards in International Contexts

Purpose: Link classic motivation theories to real HR decisions in global firms.

A. Classic theories (briefly mentioned)
1. Taylor (scientific management, 1911) – pay for performance, efficiency.
2. Maslow (1943) – hierarchy of needs (physiological, safety, belonging, esteem, self‑actualisation).
3. Herzberg – hygiene vs. motivator factors.
4. Vroom – expectancy theory (effort → performance → reward).
5. Professor’s comment: theories are old, but questions remain similar today.

B. What employees say they want
1. Salary and financial security.
2. Working conditions (work–life balance, safety, hours).
3. Recognition and respect.
4. Opportunities to learn and grow.
5. For some: leadership positions and power; for others: stability.

C. Reality in many companies
1. Strong focus on financial targets and profit.
2. Annual performance evaluations tied to bonuses and promotions.
3. Bonuses sometimes allocated mainly to managers, not teams:
   a. Example: manager receives large bonus for team results; team members receive nothing.
   b. Raises questions about fairness and collective motivation.
4. Managers may face pressure to:
   a. Fire people to meet cost targets.
   b. Hit short‑term objectives at the expense of long‑term health (for themselves and for the organisation).

D. Burnout and life choices
1. Professor’s personal experience:
   a. Very high pay and responsibility at a young age.
   b. 12‑hour days, constant pressure, safety risks in some countries.
   c. Feeling that the job was “killing” him → decision to leave corporate world.
2. Message to students:
   a. Ambition is good, but you must know the price you are willing to pay.
   b. Quality of life matters as much as career success.

### V. Diversity and International Teams

Purpose: Show how globalisation and demographics change the workforce, and what that means for management.

A. Sources of diversity
1. Demographic changes:
   a. People move across borders for work (migration, expats).
   b. Age, gender, ethnicity, educational background.
2. Globalisation:
   a. Multinationals hire in many countries.
   b. Project teams mix people from Europe, Latin America, Asia, etc.

B. Effects of diversity
1. Potential advantages
   a. More creativity and richer ideas.
   b. Better problem‑solving (different perspectives).
   c. Easier access to different markets and customers.
2. Challenges
   a. Communication problems (language, style).
   b. Misunderstandings about hierarchy, feedback, deadlines.
   c. Risk of conflict and exclusion if not managed well.

C. Managing diversity
1. Need for inclusive company culture:
   a. Clear values and norms everyone knows.
   b. Respect for differences; zero tolerance for discrimination.
2. Role of the manager:
   a. Build trust within diverse teams.
   b. Mediate conflicts; encourage participation from all members.
   c. Use diversity as a strength, not let it paralyse the team.

D. Turnover and retention
1. High turnover is common among young employees:
   a. Companies expect that many will leave after a few years.
   b. This can reduce willingness to invest in people.
2. For employees:
   a. Important to negotiate fair conditions from the start.
   b. Do not be afraid to discuss salary and benefits.

---

### VI. Power, Responsibility and Ethics in Global Management

Purpose: Reflect on what it means to hold power in international roles.

A. Power of managerial roles
1. Ability to:
   a. Approve or deny projects.
   b. Hire, promote or fire people.
   c. Allocate budgets and bonuses.
2. These decisions shape:
   a. Company performance.
   b. Many people’s lives and careers.

B. Using power well vs. badly
1. Well:
   a. Transparent criteria.
   b. Link rewards to real contribution (individual and team).
   c. Protect people’s dignity, even in difficult decisions (e.g. layoffs).
2. Badly:
   a. Favouritism and politics.
   b. Taking all the credit and rewards for oneself.
   c. Abusing hierarchy rather than leading.

C. Personal fit and self‑knowledge
1. Not everyone is suited to high‑pressure international leadership roles.
2. Important questions:
   a. How much stress and travel can I accept?
   b. Do I want to manage large teams, or contribute as a specialist?
   c. What kind of life do I want, not only what title and salary?

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Exporting products and building an international organisation are very different; the latter requires strong international HRM.
2. Global strategies depend on people with specific competencies: mobility, cultural openness, technical and leadership skills.
3. Motivation in international firms combines salary, working conditions, recognition and fair treatment; classic theories still help us think about this.
4. Diversity in global teams can be a major asset but creates challenges that require careful management and inclusive culture.
5. Power in international management roles comes with responsibility; you must decide what trade‑offs you are willing to accept between money, ambition and quality of life.
