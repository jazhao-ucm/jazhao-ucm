## Formal outline (Management in Global Markets, Week 11)

I. Internationalization as planned execution, not ad hoc export.
   A. Core definition and logic.
      1. Internationalization expands activity portfolio abroad.
      2. Growth abroad should be progressive and capability-based.
   B. Project management framing.
      1. PM cycle, define, organize, plan, execute, monitor, close.
      2. Three levers, scope, resources, and schedule.

II. Market selection and strategic sequencing.
   A. Country attractiveness filters.
      1. GDP and population growth indicate market potential.
      2. Country risk, trade barriers, and industry rivalry shape entry viability.
   B. Required analysis order for internationalization schedule.
      1. Internal SWOT first, capabilities and gaps.
      2. External SWOT second, opportunities and threats.
      3. Target market selection tool and concentration versus diversification choice.
      4. Company changes and action plan after market choice.

III. Organizational setup and execution governance.
   A. Project organization design.
      1. Define roles, responsibilities, and authority boundaries.
      2. Confirm sponsor level and PM authority sufficiency.
   B. Work breakdown structure discipline.
      1. Identify all tasks and subtasks.
      2. Assign explicit owners and estimate duration.
   C. Resource load balancing risk.
      1. Brooks law, adding people increases coordination overhead.
      2. Avoid over-allocation and under-allocation across teams.

IV. Action plan architecture for market entry.
   A. Plan quality standard.
      1. Goals must be defined, realistic, measurable, and time-bound.
      2. Each action needs owner, deadline, and required resources.
   B. Cross-functional structure.
      1. CEO coordinates resource commitment and departmental alignment.
      2. Finance handles international account, invoicing, taxation setup.
      3. Marketing and commercial teams handle habits, competitors, fairs, and client lists.
   C. Execution artifact.
      1. Use one shared action-plan diagram visible to all departments.
      2. Monitor progress with reporting and adjustment loops.

V. Global versus local operating model choices.
   A. Global-local grid logic.
      1. High globalization force plus low localization force implies global dominance.
      2. Low globalization force plus high localization force implies local dominance.
      3. Mixed force context implies hybrid global-local activity design.
   B. Export development phases.
      1. Pre-engagement.
      2. Initial exporting.
      3. Advanced exporting with higher distance and complexity.
   C. Centralization versus decentralization tradeoff.
      1. Centralization favors HQ authority consistency.
      2. Decentralization favors local responsiveness and retention of local authority.

VI. Economic integration and geopolitical context.
   A. Macro shift and governance forums.
      1. Long-horizon shift from G7 concentration toward broader G20 relevance.
      2. G20 combines major trade and population weight in global governance.
   B. Why integration matters.
      1. Reduces economic border frictions and transaction barriers.
      2. Addresses currency diversity costs and factor mobility restrictions.
   C. Integration ladder forms.
      1. Preferential agreements, FTA, customs union.
      2. Common market, economic union, monetary union.
   D. Applied regional case.
      1. MERCOSUR as South American integration framework.
      2. Four adaptation axes for managers, geography, communication, law, and culture.

VII. B2B commercialization and revenue strategy (LC0036).
   A. Sales process pipeline.
      1. Find leads, qualify, assess needs, present solution, propose, negotiate, close, and expand by referrals.
      2. Qualification quality controls time cost and opportunity cost.
   B. Go-to-market architecture.
      1. Product-market fit, target customers, buyers/users, systems/process, and funnel.
      2. ICP and persona definitions anchor prospecting quality.
   C. Value-based selling logic.
      1. Move from feature pitch to customer problem and ideal-state framing.
      2. Tie solution to measurable outcomes, revenue, cost, risk, CAC, and LTV-CAC.
   D. Negotiation and closing.
      1. Trust is central; negotiation is not only about price.
      2. Use gives-and-gets structure and clear next-step commitments.

VIII. Leadership, change, and conflict in international projects.
   A. Change mode distinctions.
      1. Reactive change closes performance gap.
      2. Proactive change closes opportunity gap.
      3. Passive change is late and less effective.
   B. Authority and urgency fit.
      1. Intervention style depends on urgency and decision authority level.
      2. Misalignment creates delays, credibility loss, and poor rollout timing.
   C. Team conflict management.
      1. Typical conflict types, disagreement, consequences, personality.
      2. Leadership task is to manage conflict productively, not eliminate all tension.

## Quick study summary

- Build internationalization as a staged project with SWOT order, target selection, and action-plan ownership.
- Use clear authority design, WBS discipline, and cross-functional coordination to avoid execution failure.
- Choose global, local, or hybrid operating model based on force balance, not ideology.
- Connect market entry choices to economic integration structures and regional institutional context.
- In B2B execution, qualify hard, sell on value, and negotiate through trust plus concrete reciprocal commitments.
