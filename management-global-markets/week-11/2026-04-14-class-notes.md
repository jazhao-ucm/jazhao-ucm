## Formal outline (Management in Global Markets, Week 11)

I. International business framing beyond simple trade.
   A. Scope of international business operations.
      1. Not only moving goods, also moving people, capital, services, and decisions.
      2. Core managerial aim, right thing, right place, right time.
   B. Execution requirement.
      1. Internationalization fails without operational control.
      2. Expansion requires capability fit, not only market desire.

II. Team composition for internationalization execution.
   A. Role-bundle requirement.
      1. Technical capability.
      2. Country and context understanding.
      3. Management and coordination capability.
   B. Practical staffing logic.
      1. Do not search for one perfect profile.
      2. Build a small complementary team with distinct lead responsibilities.

III. Managerial focus, urgent versus important.
   A. Priority discipline in early expansion.
      1. Managers must not stay trapped in low-value urgent noise.
      2. Focus should remain on activities that create commercial traction.
   B. Operational delegation.
      1. Assign execution tasks clearly to team members.
      2. Preserve manager attention for strategic and cash-critical decisions.

IV. First-stage internationalization priorities.
   A. Commercial activation tasks.
      1. Build pipeline, prepare tenders, negotiate, and close sales.
      2. Issue invoices and convert activity into cash.
   B. Market-entry practicality.
      1. Choose location and entry path based on expected cash generation.
      2. Avoid expansion moves without contracts, partners, or demand signal.

V. Cash generation as survival logic.
   A. Instructor emphasis.
      1. Revenue movement matters more than perfect form in initial phase.
      2. No invoicing means no cash, no cash means strategic collapse.
   B. Bankruptcy risk framing.
      1. Persistent no-income periods destroy firm continuity.
      2. Cost control must pair with revenue acceleration.
   C. Financial statement distinction for managers.
      1. Income statement tracks period performance, revenues versus expenses.
      2. Balance sheet tracks position, assets, liabilities, and equity.

VI. Cash optimization levers in international operations.
   A. Expense-side actions.
      1. Renegotiate supplier terms and reduce unnecessary cost.
      2. Improve labor productivity and process efficiency before extreme cuts.
   B. Revenue-side actions.
      1. Increase commercial conversion speed and invoicing discipline.
      2. Use negotiation quality to improve payment terms and certainty.
   C. Process-side actions.
      1. Automation and standardization for faster throughput.
      2. Continuous monitoring of cash cycle, buy, produce, sell, collect.

VII. Currency strategy in cross-border business.
   A. Multi-currency reality.
      1. International transactions create currency-choice decisions.
      2. Currency selection affects realized cash value.
   B. Practical arbitrage logic.
      1. Align expense currency and income currency strategically where possible.
      2. Exchange-rate awareness can materially improve net cash outcome.

VIII. Market attractiveness over time, static versus dynamic view.
   A. Present versus future indicators.
      1. Some markets look weak now but recover over multi-year horizons.
      2. Market selection should include projection, not only current snapshot.
   B. Growth and risk interpretation.
      1. Negative short-term indicators do not always imply strategic exit.
      2. Long-term participation can outperform short-term reaction.

IX. Managerial identity in international execution.
   A. Cost-killer concept.
      1. Manager role includes continuous control of outflows.
      2. Spending decisions must protect strategic viability.
   B. Cash-builder concept.
      1. Manager role also includes building inflows and commercial momentum.
      2. Sustainable internationalization requires both disciplines at once.

## Quick study summary

- International business is broader than trade, it is coordinated movement of resources across borders.
- Early internationalization success depends on complementary teams, clear priorities, and strong delegation.
- Cash generation is the key control variable, sell, invoice, collect, and watch survival risk continuously.
- Managers need dual focus, cost discipline and revenue acceleration, supported by statement literacy.
- Market choice should include forward indicators and recovery potential, not only current-year metrics.
