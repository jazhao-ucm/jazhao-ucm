\section{Internationalization of Firms --- Foundations}
\label{sec:part2}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{What is Internationalization?}

Internationalization consists of developing a company's portfolio of activities abroad. Critically, it is rarely a planned decision from the outset --- it typically emerges as a progressive consequence of searching for new markets. It is a strategy with multiple facets that leads to both cultural and structural changes within the firm.

\subsection{The Internationalization Schedule}

A structured plan should be built around four pillars:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Internal SWOT} --- assess the company's current situation and international potential
  \item \textbf{External SWOT} --- assess the target market's economy, politics, and competitive landscape
  \item \textbf{Target market selection} --- use quantitative tools to rank candidate markets
  \item \textbf{Action plan} --- define changes needed internally, set SMART goals, assign responsibility
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Target Market Selection}

Two primary strategic orientations exist:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Concentration} --- deep focus on one or a few markets
  \item \textbf{Diversification} --- spread risk across many markets simultaneously
\end{itemize}

When selecting using GDP and population data: higher GDP per capita and higher forecast GDP growth indicate greater market attractiveness, while lower GDP per capita implies lower price levels and costs.

Additional factors include country risk, trade barriers, and industry rivalry, all of which affect profitability and price sensitivity.

\subsection{Location Choice: Decision Pattern}

In internationalization, \textbf{location choice} is strategic and must be tied to business goals: cost, security, market access, political/regulatory stability, and operational feasibility.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Language and region assumptions can mislead; market analysis should use country-level data, not broad stereotypes.
  \item Geographic chokepoints and border regions can matter disproportionately for trade flows and logistics.
  \item Political risk and instability are practical constraints, even when market potential is high.
  \item Country complexity (institutions, regulation, conflict exposure, infrastructure) should be screened before entry.
\end{itemize}

Pattern from class discussion:
\begin{itemize}
  \item If risk and uncertainty are high, firms usually start with \textbf{partial relocation} (selected functions), not full-company relocation.
  \item Department sequencing depends on the objective (market access, cost efficiency, or risk reduction), not on a fixed order.
  \item Core operational question: \textit{which function should move first to create value with manageable risk?}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Global / Local Grid}

The G/L grid helps identify which company activities are candidates for internationalization versus those that must remain local.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Global dominance} --- globalization forces are strong, localization forces are weak (e.g., logistics)
  \item \textbf{Local dominance} --- localization forces are strong, globalization forces are limited
  \item \textbf{Mixed / Glocal} --- both sets of forces are active; activity requires a hybrid approach
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Three Phases of Export Development}

Firms typically progress through three stages:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Pre-engagement} --- focus on international project efficiency
  \item \textbf{Initial exporting} --- focus on impact on the international customer
  \item \textbf{Advanced exporting} --- focus on international business success
\end{enumerate}

As firms advance, they export to more countries, extend to more distant markets, face increasingly different cultural environments, and expect exports to grow as a percentage of total sales.

\subsection{Centralization vs.\ Decentralization}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{p{6cm}p{6cm}}
  \toprule
  \textbf{Centralization} & \textbf{Decentralization} \\
  \midrule
  Decisions made by senior managers & Decisions made by lower-level employees \\
  Value chain effectiveness depends on headquarters authority & Value chain effectiveness depends on local retained authority \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{Project Management Applied to Internationalization}

The three classic levers of project management apply directly to international expansion:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Scope} --- adjust what is included in the international project
  \item \textbf{Resources} --- define who does what; assign roles, responsibilities, and commitment levels
  \item \textbf{Schedule} --- set realistic target timelines backed by SWOT analysis and action plans
\end{itemize}

\textit{Brooks's Law}: adding more people to a project increases coordination overhead, reducing value-adding work. Some resources become over-allocated while others are underused.

\subsection{The Urgency / Authority Matrix}

A core framework for understanding change dynamics in international organizations:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{p{2cm}p{5.5cm}p{5.5cm}}
  \toprule
  & \textbf{Low Urgency} & \textbf{High Urgency} \\
  \midrule
  \textbf{Low Power} &
  Scenario 1: Low/mid-level employees, proactive, non-urgent results. Cheap. &
  Scenario 3: Low-level employees, passive, urgent results required. \\[1em]
  \textbf{High Power} &
  Scenario 2: CEO/Director, reactive, non-urgent results. Expensive. &
  Scenario 4: Mid-level / CEO, passive, urgent results required. \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{Change Strategy}

\subsubsection{Three Types of Change}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Reactive Change} --- closing a performance gap (what is vs.\ what should be)
  \item \textbf{Proactive Change} --- closing an opportunity gap (what is vs.\ what could be)
  \item \textbf{Passive Change} --- attempting a last-resort solution (what could have been changed)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Two Process Orientations}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{p{6cm}p{6cm}}
  \toprule
  \textbf{High Urgency / Crisis} & \textbf{Low Urgency / Stable} \\
  \midrule
  Dissatisfaction with leadership & Satisfaction with leadership \\
  Organization seeking new direction & Organization focused on standard routines \\
  Change agent has relevant, urgent information & Change agent adopts an educator role \\
  Need to show results quickly & Slow and steady approach \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{CEO vs.\ Manager Responsibilities}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{p{6cm}p{6cm}}
  \toprule
  \textbf{CEO / General Manager} & \textbf{Mid Manager / Low-level Employees} \\
  \midrule
  Investment of new capital & Hiring employees \\
  Define contribution margin & Customer service and preserving relationships \\
  Fix and determine salaries & Management of lower-level employees \\
  Designation of responsibilities & Supply chain management \\
  Control of taxes and government policy & Improve technique or quality \\
  Growing sales and building relationships & Preparation of tenders \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsubsection{What CEOs Should NOT Do}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Interfere with lower-level employee decisions
  \item Position themselves between lower and middle management
  \item Disrespect the autonomy of middle managers
  \item Make decisions without involving middle management
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{What Middle Managers Should NOT Do}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Manipulate capital without CEO consent
  \item Influence lower employees against the CEO
  \item Take on purely administrative tasks that belong to lower levels
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Financial Performance Fundamentals}

Three financial statements are central to business decision-making:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Income Statement} --- revenues, costs, and profit/loss for a period
  \item \textbf{Balance Sheet} --- assets, liabilities, and equity at a point in time
  \item \textbf{Cash Flow Statement} --- movement of cash in and out of the business
\end{itemize}

Key investment areas and their effects:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{R\&D} --- improves product attractiveness and reduces manufacturing costs; has both an immediate and a cumulative effect
  \item \textbf{HR \& Quality} --- improves employee morale and effectiveness of marketing, R\&D, and production; takes one period to become effective
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Market Size Drivers}

The potential size of a market depends on:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Expected market growth rate in the industry
  \item General economy (business index)
  \item Prices offered by competing companies
  \item Quantities of products available for sale
  \item Administrative \& selling expenses (marketing effort)
  \item R\&D spend to create product appeal
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Team Conflict in International Contexts}

Three types of team conflict emerge in cross-cultural or change-driven environments:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Disagreement} --- differences of opinion over what course of action to take
  \item \textbf{Consequences} --- tensions arising from the results of choices made
  \item \textbf{Personality} --- clashes between leadership styles or personality types
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Takeaways on Leadership}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Leadership in an adaptive challenge means experimenting with interventions that keep a system focused on the problem at hand
  \item Failure often teaches significant lessons
  \item Conflicts are neither good nor bad --- the task is managing them productively
  \item Loss management (of standing, results, or direction) is a central leadership task during upheaval
\end{itemize}

\section{Economic Integration \& Mercosur}
\label{sec:mercosur}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Global Economic Outlook}

China is projected to overtake the US as the world's largest economy around \textbf{2035}. India is projected to overtake the US around \textbf{2075}. The shift from G7 to G20 reflects the expansion of intergovernmental coordination in pursuit of global economic stability.

The G20 is composed of 19 countries plus the European Union, collectively accounting for:
\begin{itemize}
  \item 90\% of gross national product
  \item 80\% of world trade
  \item 75\% of the world population
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Why Economic Integration?}

International transactions face barriers that domestic ones do not:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Economic borders} --- protectionist policies that discriminate against foreign products
  \item \textbf{Currency diversity} --- exchange costs and uncertainties
  \item \textbf{Factor mobility restrictions} --- limits on movement of labor and capital
  \item \textbf{Divergent macro/microeconomic policies} --- different national industrial strategies
\end{itemize}

Economic integration aims to eliminate these differential characteristics.

\subsection{Forms of Economic Integration}

Listed from weakest to deepest integration:

\begin{center}
\begin{longtable}{p{4cm}p{9cm}}
  \toprule
  \textbf{Form} & \textbf{Description} \\
  \midrule
  Preferential Agreements & Countries grant each other reduced tariffs or other preferences \\[0.5em]
  Free Trade Area (FTA) & Member countries eliminate tariffs among themselves, while maintaining independent trade policy toward third parties \\[0.5em]
  Customs Union & Eliminates internal tariffs and establishes a common external tariff \\[0.5em]
  Common Market & Adds free movement of labor and capital to the customs union \\[0.5em]
  Economic Union & Coordinates fiscal, monetary, and other economic policies among members \\[0.5em]
  Monetary Union & Adopts a single shared currency \\
  \bottomrule
\end{longtable}
\end{center}

\subsection{Motivations for Integration}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Economic} --- expand markets, increase competition, accelerate growth
  \item \textbf{Political} --- reduce tensions between countries, carry more weight internationally, find joint solutions to global problems (environmental, migratory, etc.)
  \item \textbf{Security} --- reduce risk of conflict through economic interdependence
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Mercosur}

The Southern Common Market (\textbf{MERCOSUR}) is a regional integration process initially established by Argentina, Brazil, Paraguay, and Uruguay. Official working languages are Spanish and Portuguese. South America is the fourth largest continent, covering approximately 6.89 million square miles and comprising 12 sovereign countries.

\subsection{The International Manager --- Four Axes}

When operating across integrated economic areas, the international manager must navigate four key dimensions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Geography} --- physical distance, logistics, time zones
  \item \textbf{Communication} --- language, channels, cultural communication norms
  \item \textbf{Law} --- local regulations, trade law, labor law, IP protection
  \item \textbf{Culture} --- values, business norms, interpersonal expectations
\end{enumerate}

\section{B2B Sales Process --- iSense LiveCase}
\label{sec:sales}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\textit{Source: Harvard Business Publishing LiveCase \#LC0036. Focuses on how post-revenue businesses can effectively sell products and services to other established businesses.}

\subsection{The Sales Process}

The sales cycle is not linear --- it is a continuous loop:

\begin{center}
\textbf{Find Leads} $\to$ \textbf{Qualify} $\to$ \textbf{Assess Needs} $\to$ \textbf{Present Solution} $\to$ \textbf{Proposal} $\to$ \textbf{Negotiate \& Close} $\to$ \textbf{Repeat Business \& Referrals}
\end{center}

The B2B buying journey is not linear or organized. Buyers are well-informed, organizations are layered, budget approval is complex, and buyers prefer to be consulted rather than sold to.

\subsection{Building a Revenue Strategy}

Before finding leads, define:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Product-market fit} --- confirmed by existing revenue
  \item \textbf{Target customers} --- the organizations most likely to buy
  \item \textbf{Buyers \& users} --- the individuals within those organizations
  \item \textbf{Processes \& systems} --- what they currently use that you can improve
  \item \textbf{Sales funnel shape} --- determines go-to-market strategy and where effort is needed
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Funnel Shape by Segment}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Niche} --- small top of funnel; few buyers; awareness building needed
  \item \textbf{Large Enterprise} --- high awareness and interest, but long decision processes; procurement and budgeting delays
  \item \textbf{SMB} --- high awareness, but limited purchasing power; deals often stall
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Key Financial Metrics}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{LTV} (Lifetime Value) --- total revenue expected from a customer
  \item \textbf{CAC} (Customer Acquisition Cost) --- total cost to acquire a customer
  \item \textbf{Profit Margin} --- what remains after all costs
\end{itemize}

If it costs \$3 of spend to generate \$1 of revenue, the business model needs reconsideration.

\subsection{Finding Leads}

\subsubsection{Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)}

The ICP describes the \textit{type of organization} most likely to purchase. Key attributes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Industry verticals, company size (\$ and headcount), geography, timing
  \item Executive priorities --- what leaders care about in annual reports and earnings calls
  \item Business initiatives --- specific projects stemming from executive priorities
  \item Challenges --- current blockers preventing success
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Buyer Personas}

The persona describes the \textit{type of individual} within the ICP who will engage with you:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Function/department, title, professional background, KPIs
  \item Goals, responsibilities, needs, and current challenges
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Research Tools}
\begin{itemize}
  \item LinkedIn Sales Navigator --- lead search, tracking, recommendations
  \item ZoomInfo, LeadIQ, Seamless.ai --- titles, emails, department data
  \item Google --- 10-Ks, annual reports, press releases for organizational context
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Inbound vs.\ Outbound Prospecting}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{p{6cm}p{6cm}}
  \toprule
  \textbf{Inbound} & \textbf{Outbound} \\
  \midrule
  Passive engagement; customer initiates & Active engagement; seller initiates \\
  Medium-to-high awareness & Low-to-high awareness \\
  Personalized, 1:1 communication & Account-based, segmented communication \\
  Shorter time horizon & Longer sales cycle \\
  Content placed online for prospects to find (pull) & Content pushed via email, LinkedIn, phone \\
  Best when: small team, high search volume, quick decisions & Best when: large enterprise, complex problem, high commissions \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{Qualifying}

The purpose of qualification is to channel effort toward opportunities most likely to close --- including disqualifying those that are not ready.

\subsubsection{Qualification Frameworks}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
  \toprule
  \textbf{BANT} & \textbf{CHAMP} & \textbf{MEDDPICC} \\
  \midrule
  Budget & Challenge & Metrics \\
  Authority & Authority & Economic Buyer \\
  Need & Money & Decision Process \\
  Time & Priority & Decision Criteria \\
  & & Paper Process \\
  & & Identify Pain \\
  & & Champion \\
  & & Competition \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

These are methodologies, not linear checklists. Information is gathered over multiple conversations. Always approach from a position of curiosity, not interrogation.

\subsubsection{How to Ask Good Qualifying Questions}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Avoid yes/no questions --- keep them open-ended
  \item Use expansion questions: \textit{``Tell me about\ldots''} or \textit{``Explain to me\ldots''}
  \item Ask questions that compel action: \textit{``What would happen if you did nothing?''}
  \item Keep the focus on the prospect, not on your product
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{The Cost of Bad Qualification}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lr}
  \toprule
  \textbf{Item} & \textbf{Value} \\
  \midrule
  AE base salary & \$150,000 USD = \$72/hr \\
  AE quota & \$1,200,000 USD = \$577/hr \\
  Wasted hours/month (4 bad leads $\times$ 2 calls) & 8 hours \\

  \midrule
  \textbf{Team of 20 sellers --- annual salary cost} & \$138,240 \\

\end{center}

\subsection{Assessing Needs}

This is an extension of qualifying --- still asking questions, still not pitching. Examples of effective questions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textit{``Tell me about your current challenges.''}
  \item \textit{``What are the bad things happening related to those challenges?''}
  \item \textit{``What does your ideal state look like?''}
  \item \textit{``What could your business accomplish if you solved those pain points?''}
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Tying to Value}

Conversations should operate on two customer-side levels:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Organization level} --- executive priorities, business initiatives, what appears in annual reports
  \item \textbf{Project level} --- current pain points, ideal state, specific requirements to get from A to B
\end{itemize}

Tie your solution to metrics the prospect cares about: revenue increase, cost reduction, risk mitigation (e.g., \% churn, \$ CAC, \$ ROI, \# incidents identified).

\subsection{Presenting a Solution}

Present only after fully understanding the customer. A presentation should include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item How your solution addresses each stated requirement
  \item Customer success stories in the format: \textbf{Problem} $\to$ \textbf{Solution} $\to$ \textbf{Results}
  \item Quantifiable outcomes wherever possible
  \item Stories from the same industry as the prospect whenever available
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Proposal}

The proposal is the culmination of all prior conversations. Packaging matters:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Include a written summary of pain points, desired outcomes, and scope
  \item Make it visually clear and easy to digest
  \item Deliver it live and/or with a short summary video
  \item Consider presenting \textbf{multiple options} tailored to the prospect's expressed concerns (e.g., budget flexibility, implementation support)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Pricing Strategies}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Anchoring bias} --- showing a higher original price makes the final price feel like a deal
  \item \textbf{Banded pricing} --- price per seat/license decreases at higher volumes
  \item \textbf{Deadline discounts} --- reduced price if signed by end of quarter
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Negotiate \& Close}

\subsubsection{Understanding the Customer's Process}
Validate continuously:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Who needs to sign off on budget?
  \item What is the procurement timeline?
  \item Who needs to be involved in legal review?
\end{itemize}

Build a shared timeline from \textit{desired go-live date} backwards through implementation, signature, and vendor selection.

\subsubsection{Negotiations Are Not Always About Price}

They are always about trust. Approach every negotiation with reciprocity --- the goal is mutual benefit. Key areas to negotiate (\textbf{Gives \& Gets}):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
  \toprule
  \textbf{You Might Give} & \textbf{You Might Get} \\
  \midrule
  Price discount & Testimonial / case study \\
  Extended payment terms & Signed by a specific date \\
  Additional training or support & Access to senior stakeholders \\
  Flexible contract length & Defined next steps / commitment \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Two essential principles:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Be willing to walk away} --- not every deal is worth closing
  \item \textbf{Control your emotions} --- especially under pressure or objection
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Hard vs.\ Soft Close}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{p{6cm}p{6cm}}
  \toprule
  \textbf{Hard Close} & \textbf{Soft Close} \\
  \midrule
  Direct and clear & Open-ended, more ambiguous \\
  Asks for an immediate response & Keeps ``maybe'' deals alive \\
  Seller maintains control & Non-confrontational \\
  Clearly defined power dynamic & More organic approach \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsubsection{Closing Is Incremental}

You do not close a deal only in the final stage. Closing happens across every touchpoint:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Building trust and rapport from first contact
  \item Creating shared agendas and writing summary emails
  \item Recapping each meeting before starting the next
  \item Handling objections well throughout
  \item Building relationships across departments and levels
\end{itemize}

\vspace{2em}
\begin{center}
\textit{``Negotiations are not always about price. But they are always about trust.''}
\end{center}
