I. Power Distance - Detailed Characteristics
   A. High Power Distance Countries
      1. More powerful people expect more privileges
         a. Bosses get special treatment
         b. Hierarchy determines benefits
         c. Examples: Russia, Japan, Mexico, Spain, France
      2. Subordinates considered socially/legally inferior
         a. Employees have lower status
         b. Clear social separation
         c. Not considered equals
      3. Members accept power distance as part of social order
         a. Determines what is right and wrong
         b. Hierarchy seen as natural
         c. Examples: Spain, Mexico, Latin American countries
      4. Low trust among coworkers
         a. People don't share information easily
         b. Selfish behavior common
         c. Not helpful for collaboration
   B. Low Power Distance Countries
      1. High trust among coworkers
      2. Information sharing is common
      3. Collaborative environment
      4. Examples: Sweden, Iceland, Northern Europe, USA
   C. Key Insight
      1. Power distance not black/white - scale from 0 to 100
      2. Countries fall somewhere on spectrum

II. Power Distance in Practice
   A. Instructor's Experience in Mexico
      1. Was 27 years old, "baby boss"
      2. Had older people working for him
      3. Extreme hierarchy and respect for position
      4. "Nothing worse than working with people who take advantage"
   B. Micromanagement
      1. Common in high power distance cultures
      2. Bosses closely control employees
      3. Less autonomy for workers
   C. Key Point
      1. In high power distance cultures
      2. Boss's position matters more than age or experience

III. Mistakes and Blame Culture
   A. High Power Distance
      1. Employees try to hide mistakes
      2. Fear of punishment
      3. Blame culture - "It's your fault"
   B. Low Power Distance (USA Example)
      1. "If you make a mistake, it's fine"
      2. Boss's job is to correct, not punish
      3. "I'm here to help you"
      4. System tries to fix problems together
      5. "We don't fire people we need"
   C. Example
      1. 300 workers in Mexico factory
      2. Can't fire all of them - you need them
      3. Must work together to solve problems
   D. Key Insight
      1. Low power distance: mistakes = learning opportunities
      2. High power distance: mistakes = failures to hide

IV. Experience vs. Education
   A. High Power Distance
      1. More credit given to experience
      2. Older, experienced people valued more
      3. Age = wisdom and respect
   B. Low Power Distance
      1. More credit given to education
      2. Young educated people can advance
      3. Merit-based advancement
   C. Key Point
      1. Different cultures value experience vs. education differently

V. Culture Shock - The 4 Phases
   A. Phase 1: Euphoria (Honeymoon)
      1. Everything exciting and new
      2. "I love it here!"
      3. High positive feelings
      4. Duration: days to weeks
   B. Phase 2: Culture Shock (Crisis)
      1. Everything feels wrong and annoying
      2. "Why do they do things this way?!"
      3. Food tastes weird, people act strange
      4. Low negative feelings
      5. Duration: weeks to months (WORST PHASE)
      6. People often leave and go back home
   C. Phase 3: Acculturation (Recovery)
      1. Starting to understand why they do things
      2. Learning step by step
      3. Beginning to adapt
      4. Requires empathy and cultural intelligence
      5. "Don't wait, don't be a wimp - stick with it"
   D. Phase 4: Stable State (Adaptation)
      1. Feels normal now
      2. Might even prefer their way
      3. Fully adapted
      4. High positive feelings again
   E. Key Insight
      1. "You need time. Take your time."
      2. "Slow process, slow business"
      3. "Need time and energy"

VI. Cultural Distance
   A. Definition
      1. Difference between home country and target country
      2. Across cultural dimensions
   B. Why It Matters
      1. Smaller cultural distance = easier to do business
      2. Larger cultural distance = harder to adapt
      3. Affects time to reach "stable state"
   C. Examples
      1. USA to UK: small cultural distance (same language, similar culture)
      2. USA to Middle East: large cultural distance (different language, religion, customs)
      3. Mexico to Spain: medium cultural distance (same language, different customs)
   D. Key Point
      1. Similar cultures (USA/UK) reach stable state faster
      2. Very different cultures take longer

VII. Integration Process - The Journey
   A. Steps to Cultural Integration
      1. Step 1: Academic Knowledge (High School)
         a. Low level of cultural knowledge
         b. Books and lectures
         c. Theoretical understanding
      2. Step 2: Training Method (College)
         a. Case studies
         b. Cultural awareness begins
         c. Still learning
      3. Step 3: Cultural Intelligence
         a. Gained through experience
         b. Living in different culture
         c. Understanding how to work with different people
         d. "You're lucky to be here" - studying abroad
      4. Step 4: Integration
         a. Working in different countries
         b. Full adaptation
         c. Professional competence in cross-cultural environment
   B. Key Insight
      1. Step-by-step process
      2. Don't start integrated - grow into it
      3. Through education, experience, and time

VIII. Communication - The 35/65 Rule
   A. The Rule
      1. 35% = what you say (actual words)
      2. 65% = how you say it (non-verbal)
   B. Non-Verbal Communication Includes
      1. Facial expressions
      2. Tone of voice
      3. Gestures
      4. Eye contact
      5. Posture
      6. Movement
      7. Body language
   C. Key Insight
      1. "How you say it matters way more than what you say"
      2. Non-verbal expressions 65-93% more influential than words
   D. For Business
      1. In negotiations, how you act is crucial
      2. Shows motivation, preparation, experience
      3. Sometimes what you're NOT saying is more important
   E. Example
      1. Don't just say "I'm motivated" - SHOW it:
         a. Enthusiasm
         b. Eye contact
         c. Energy
         d. Positive body language

IX. Verbal vs. Non-Verbal Language
   A. Verbal Language
      1. What we say
      2. Actual words
      3. Can be translated
   B. Non-Verbal Language
      1. What we don't say
      2. Body language, gestures, expressions
      3. Often more important than words
      4. Harder to translate across cultures
   C. Key Point
      1. In negotiations/business, non-verbal is crucial
      2. What we're not saying often more important

X. Cultural Similarities & Differences
   A. What Creates Similarity
      1. National boundaries - same country
      2. Shared experiences - same background
      3. Language - same communication
      4. Values - similar beliefs
   B. What Creates Differences
      1. Gender - different credit to men vs. women
      2. Age - different value on youth vs. experience
      3. Family - different structures and values
      4. Language - different communication
      5. Religion - different beliefs and practices
      6. Sports/Interests - different cultural activities
   C. Example from Class
      1. Students naturally grouped by nationality
      2. French students sat together
      3. Spanish students sat together
      4. "You go where you feel better"
   D. Key Insight
      1. "Basic similarity amongst people within countries"
      2. "Both a cause and effect of national boundaries"

XI. Market Entry Simulation Game
   A. The Game Concept
      1. Managing restaurant chain going international
      2. Pick home country
      3. Decide which countries to open in over 6 years
      4. Like Monopoly, but with countries
   B. Key Factors - Part 1: Economic
      1. GDP Per Capita
         a. How much average person makes
         b. Rich countries = people afford to eat out more
      2. GDP Growth
         a. Is country getting richer or poorer?
         b. Fast-growing = future customers
      3. Population
         a. How many people
         b. More people = more potential customers
      4. Bottom Line: countries with money NOW + getting richer LATER
   C. Key Factors - Part 2: Risk & Competition
      1. Country Risk
         a. How unstable?
         b. Government change, economy crash, wars?
      2. Trade Barriers (Tariffs)
         a. Taxes on imported stuff
         b. High tariffs = higher costs
      3. Industry Rivalry
         a. How many competitors already there?
         b. Too many = hard to succeed
   D. Key Factors - Part 3: Culture (BIGGEST)
      1. Cultural Distance
         a. Compare home to target on Hofstede dimensions
         b. Bigger difference = harder business
         c. Small distance = customers understand better

XII. Hofstede's 6 Dimensions (Review)
   A. Power Distance (PDI)
      1. High = boss is boss, don't question
      2. Low = everyone equal-ish
      3. Restaurants: High = formal service, Low = casual
   B. Individualism (INV)
      1. Individualistic = "I want what I want" (USA, Australia)
      2. Collectivistic = "What's good for group?" (China, India)
      3. Restaurants: Individualistic = flexible menus, Collectivistic = family meals
   C. Masculinity vs. Femininity (MAS)
      1. Masculine = success = money and power (Japan, USA)
      2. Feminine = success = good relationships (Nordic)
      3. Restaurants: Masculine = fancy status, Feminine = cozy family
   D. Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI)
      1. High = "We've always done it this way" (Japan, France)
      2. Low = "Let's try something new!" (USA, Australia)
      3. Restaurants: High = traditional menu, Low = fusion food
   E. Long-Term vs. Short-Term (LTO)
      1. Long-term = think about future (China)
      2. Short-term = think about right now (USA)
      3. Restaurants: Long-term = stability, Short-term = trendy
   F. Indulgence
      1. High = "Life is for enjoying!" (Latin America)
      2. Low = "Discipline first" (East Asia)
      3. Restaurants: High indulgence = MORE CUSTOMERS

XIII. Customs & Traditions
   A. Starting a Meeting - Different Countries
      1. Germany: formal, straight to business (0 min)
      2. Finland: formal, coffee, then business (5 min)
      3. USA: informal, coffee, chit-chat, then business (3 min)
      4. UK: formal, tea and weather small talk (10 min)
      5. France: formal, 15 min chatting about politics
      6. Japan: super formal, tea, protocol, big guy starts (20 min)
      7. Spain/Italy: very informal, small talk while waiting (30 min)
   B. For Restaurants
      1. Understand how people like to eat and socialize
      2. Different customs in different countries

XIV. Religion
   A. Major Religions
      1. Christianity: ~2.4 billion people
      2. Islam: ~1.9 billion people
      3. Hinduism: ~1.2 billion people
      4. Buddhism: ~530 million people
      5. Judaism: ~16 million people
   B. Why It Matters
      1. Some religions have food rules (Halal, Kosher, Vegetarian)
      2. Holy days affect when people eat out
      3. Values in religion affect customer wants
   C. For Restaurants
      1. Respect food rules
      2. Know holiday calendars
      3. Understand values

XV. Cultural Empathy
   A. Definition
      1. Understanding and appreciating how others' culture is different
      2. Not because it's weird
      3. Because it makes sense in THEIR context
   B. Why It Matters
      1. More tolerant of differences
      2. Better at business across cultures
      3. Happier life overall
      4. Better employee/manager
   C. Simple Version
      1. Different doesn't mean wrong
      2. Try to understand WHY they do it

XVI. How to Succeed in New Markets
   A. DO
      1. Study GDP, growth, population (who can afford you?)
      2. Check country risk (is it stable?)
      3. Understand cultural dimensions (how do they think?)
      4. Learn customs and traditions (how do they do things?)
      5. Watch communication (show enthusiasm, not just tell)
      6. Respect religions and food rules
      7. Be prepared for culture shock
      8. Develop cultural empathy
      9. Take your time - integration is slow
   B. DON'T
      1. Assume your home country way is the only way
      2. Ignore high tariffs and trade barriers
      3. Enter markets with too much competition without a plan
      4. Try to change a country's culture to match yours
      5. Give up during culture shock phase
      6. Hide mistakes - learn from them
   C. Real Talk
      1. Host countries don't always want you to change completely
      2. They might accept that you're different
      3. Smart companies learn from other cultures

XVII. Key Takeaways
   A. Power distance varies dramatically - affects how people work
   B. Culture shock is normal - everyone goes through 4 phases
   C. Cultural distance matters - smaller = easier adaptation
   D. Communication is 65% non-verbal - how you say it matters
   E. Integration takes time - slow process, education and experience
   F. Cultural empathy is crucial - understand WHY cultures differ
   G. Market entry requires multiple factors - economics, risk, competition, culture
   H. Hofstede's 6 dimensions help understand differences
   I. Customs vary by country - understand local ways
   J. Religion affects business - respect food rules and values
   K. Mistakes = learning opportunities in low power distance cultures
   L. Experience vs. education valued differently across cultures

XVIII. Final Thought - Glocalization
   A. Think Global, Act Local
      1. Keep your core brand identity
      2. Adapt to local culture, tastes, traditions
      3. Not trying to be like everyone else
      4. Being SMART about serving different people
   B. Key Quote
      1. "You need time. Take your time."
