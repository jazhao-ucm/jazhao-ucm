## Key Concepts

### I. Power Distance - Detailed Characteristics

A. High Power Distance Countries

B. Characteristics
1. More powerful people expect to receive more privileges
   a. Bosses get special treatment
   b. Hierarchy determines benefits
   c. Examples: Russia, Japan, Mexico, Spain, France

2. Subordinates are considered socially and legally inferior
   a. Employees have lower status
   b. Clear social separation
   c. Not considered equals

3. Members accept power distance as part of social order
   a. Determines what is right and wrong
   b. Hierarchy is seen as natural
   c. Examples: Spain, Mexico, many Latin American countries

4. Low trust among coworkers
   a. People don't share information easily
   b. Information is kept to oneself
   c. Selfish behavior is common
   d. Not helpful for collaboration

C. Low Power Distance Countries
1. High trust among coworkers
2. Information sharing is common
3. Collaborative environment
4. Examples: Sweden, Iceland, Northern Europe, USA

D. Key insight
1. Power distance is not black and white - it's a scale from 0 to 100. Countries fall somewhere on this spectrum.

E. Examples
1. USA: Low power distance
2. Russia: High power distance
3. Japan: High power distance
4. Spain: Very high power distance
5. France: High power distance
6. Sweden/Iceland: Very low power distance

### II. Power Distance in Practice

A. Instructor's Experience in Mexico
1. Was 27 years old, "baby boss"
2. Had people older than him working for him
3. They had to wait in line, couldn't do job wrong
4. Extreme hierarchy and respect for position
5. "There is nothing worse in this world than working with people who will take advantage"

B. Micromanagement
1. Common in high power distance cultures
2. Bosses closely control employees
3. Less autonomy for workers

C. Key point
1. In high power distance cultures, the boss's position matters more than age or experience.

---

### III. Mistakes and Blame Culture

A. High Power Distance
1. Employees try to hide mistakes
2. Fear of punishment
3. Blame culture - "It's your fault"

B. Low Power Distance (USA example)
1. "If you make a mistake, it's fine"
2. Boss's job is to correct, not punish
3. "I'm here to help you"
4. System tries to fix problems together
5. "We don't fire people we need"

C. Example
1. Instructor had 300 workers in Mexico factory
2. Can't fire all of them - you need them
3. Must work together to solve problems

D. Key insight
1. In low power distance cultures, mistakes are learning opportunities. In high power distance, mistakes are failures to be hidden.

---

### IV. Experience vs. Education

A. High Power Distance
1. More credit given to experience
2. Older, experienced people valued more
3. Age = wisdom and respect

B. Low Power Distance
1. More credit given to education
2. Young educated people can advance
3. Merit-based advancement

C. Key point
1. Different cultures value experience vs. education differently.

---

### V. Culture Shock - The 4 Phases

Phase 1: Euphoria (Honeymoon Phase)
1. Everything is exciting and new
2. "I love it here!"
3. High positive feelings
4. Duration: Days to weeks
5. Example: "Do you remember the day you left the USA? Excitement?"

Phase 2: Culture Shock (Crisis Phase)
6. Everything feels wrong and annoying
7. "Why do they do things this way?!"
8. Food tastes weird, people act strange
9. Low negative feelings
10. Duration: Weeks to months (WORST PHASE)
11. What happens: People often leave and go back home
12. Example: Instructor in Chile - didn't understand language, culture, had no communication, felt alone

Phase 3: Acculturation (Recovery Phase)
13. Starting to understand why they do things
14. Learning step by step
15. Beginning to adapt
16. Feelings improving
17. Requires: Empathy and Cultural Intelligence
18. Key: Don't wait, don't be a "wimp" - stick with it

Phase 4: Stable State (Adaptation Phase)
19. Feels normal now
20. Might even prefer their way
21. Fully adapted
22. High positive feelings again

A. Key insight
1. "You need time. Take your time. It's a slow process, slow business, and you need time and energy."

---

### VI. Cultural Distance

Definition: The difference between your home country and target country across cultural dimensions.

A. Why It Matters
1. Smaller cultural distance = easier to do business
2. Larger cultural distance = harder to adapt
3. Affects how long it takes to reach "stable state"

B. Example
1. USA to UK: Small cultural distance (same language, similar culture)
2. USA to Middle East: Large cultural distance (different language, religion, customs)
3. Mexico to Spain: Medium cultural distance (same language, different customs)

C. Key point
1. People from similar cultures (like USA/UK) reach the "middle" (stable state) faster than people from very different cultures.

### IX. Communication - The 35/65 Rule

A. The Rule
1. 35% = What you say (actual words)
2. 65% = How you say it (non-verbal communication)

B. Non-Verbal Communication Includes
1. Facial expressions
2. Tone of voice
3. Gestures
4. Eye contact
5. Posture
6. Movement
7. Body language

C. Key insight
1. "How you say it matters way more than what you say."

D. Studies Show
1. In communication, non-verbal expressions are 65% to 93% more influential than words
2. 93% - That's a lot!

E. For Business
1. In negotiations, how you act is crucial
2. Shows your motivation, preparation, and experience
3. Sometimes what you're NOT saying is more important than what you ARE saying

F. Example
1. Don't just say "I'm motivated" - SHOW it with:
   a. Enthusiasm
   b. Eye contact
   c. Energy
   d. Positive body language

### X. Verbal vs. Non-Verbal Language

A. Verbal Language
1. What we say
2. The actual words
3. Can be translated

B. Non-Verbal Language
1. What we don't say
2. Body language, gestures, expressions
3. Often more important than words
4. Harder to translate across cultures

C. Key point
1. In negotiations and business, non-verbal communication is crucial. Sometimes what we're not saying is more important than what we are saying.

### XI. Cultural Similarities & Differences

A. What Creates Similarity
1. National boundaries - People from same country
2. Shared experiences - Same background
3. Language - Same communication
4. Values - Similar beliefs

B. What Creates Differences
1. Gender - Different cultures give different credit to men vs. women
2. Age - Different value placed on youth vs. experience
3. Family - Different family structures and values
4. Language - Different ways of communicating
5. Religion - Different beliefs and practices
6. Sports/Interests - Different cultural activities

C. Example from Class
1. Students naturally grouped by nationality
2. French students sat together
3. Spanish students sat together
4. "You go where you feel better" - natural grouping by culture

D. Key insight
1. "The basic similarity amongst people within countries is both a cause and an effect of national boundaries."

### XII. Market Entry Simulation Game

A. The Game Concept
1. Managing a restaurant chain going international
2. Pick a home country
3. Decide which other countries to open restaurants in over 6 years
4. Like Monopoly, but with countries instead of streets

B. Key Factors to Consider

#### Part 1: Economic Factors

C. GDP Per Capita
1. How much money the average person makes
2. Rich countries = people can afford to eat out more
3. Example: Australia has high GDP per capita

D. GDP Growth
1. Is the country getting richer or poorer?
2. Fast-growing countries = future customers
3. Good place to open restaurant

E. Population
1. How many people live there
2. More people = more potential customers

Bottom Line: Look for countries where people have money NOW and the country is getting richer LATER.

#### Part 2: Risk & Competition

F. Country Risk
1. How unstable is the country?
2. Could government change, economy crash, or wars happen?
3. Risky countries = restaurant might fail due to things you can't control

G. Trade Barriers (Tariffs)
1. Taxes on imported stuff
2. "You want to bring ingredients from home? That'll cost extra!"
3. High tariffs = ingredients cost more = food costs more = fewer customers

H. Industry Rivalry
1. How many other restaurants are already there?
2. Too many competitors = hard to succeed

#### Part 3: Culture (The BIGGEST Part)

I. Cultural Distance
1. Compare your home country to target country on all 6 Hofstede dimensions
2. Bigger difference = harder to do business
3. Small cultural distance = customers understand you better = easier to succeed

### XIII. Hofstede's 6 Dimensions (Review)

A. Power Distance (PDI)
1. High = Boss is boss, don't question
2. Low = Everyone equal-ish
3. For restaurants: High = formal service, Low = casual service

B. Individualism (INV)
1. Individualistic = "I want what I want" (USA, Australia)
2. Collectivistic = "What's good for the group?" (China, India)
3. For restaurants: Individualistic = flexible menus, Collectivistic = family meals

C. Masculinity vs. Femininity (MAS)
1. Masculine = Success = money and power (Japan, USA)
2. Feminine = Success = good relationships (Nordic countries)
3. For restaurants: Masculine = fancy status symbol, Feminine = cozy family place

D. Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI)
1. High = "We've always done it this way" (Japan, France)
2. Low = "Let's try something new!" (USA, Australia)
3. For restaurants: High = traditional menu, Low = fusion food

E. Long-Term vs. Short-Term Orientation (LTO)
1. Long-term = Think about future (China)
2. Short-term = Think about right now (USA)
3. For restaurants: Long-term = stability, Short-term = trendy

F. Indulgence
1. High = "Life is for enjoying!" (Latin America)
2. Low = "Discipline first" (East Asia)
3. For restaurants: High indulgence = MORE CUSTOMERS (they love eating out)!

### XIV. Customs & Traditions

A. Different countries have different ways of doing things

Example: Starting a Meeting
1. Germany: Formal, get straight to business (0 mins)
2. Finland: Formal, coffee, then business (5 mins)
3. USA: Informal, coffee, chit-chat, then business (3 mins)
4. UK: Formal, tea and small talk about weather (10 mins)
5. France: Formal, 15 mins chatting about politics (15 mins)
6. Japan: Super formal, tea, protocol, big guy starts (20 mins)
7. Spain/Italy: Very informal, small talk while waiting (30 mins)

For Restaurants: You need to understand how people like to eat and socialize in that country.

### XV. Religion

A. Major Religions
1. Christianity: ~2.4 billion people
2. Islam: ~1.9 billion people
3. Hinduism: ~1.2 billion people
4. Buddhism: ~530 million people
5. Judaism: ~16 million people

B. Why It Matters

3. Values in religion affect what customers want

For Restaurants: Respect food rules, know holiday calendars, understand values.

### XVI. Cultural Empathy

A. Definition
Understanding and appreciating how other people's culture is different from yours - NOT because it's weird, but because it makes sense in THEIR context.

B. Why It Matters
1. More tolerant of differences
2. Better at business across cultures
3. Happier life overall
4. Better employee/manager

Simple Version: Just because something is different doesn't mean it's wrong. Try to understand WHY they do it.

C. Key point
1. "People are more tolerant of differences in others" when you have cultural empathy.

### XVIII. The Bottom Line: How to Succeed in New Markets

A. ✅ DO
1. Study GDP, growth, and population (who can afford you?)
2. Check country risk (is it stable?)
3. Understand cultural dimensions (how do they think?)
4. Learn customs and traditions (how do they do things?)
5. Watch your communication (show enthusiasm, not just tell)
6. Respect religions and food rules
7. Be prepared for culture shock in yourself and employees
8. Develop cultural empathy
9. Take your time - integration is a slow process

B. ❌ DON'T
1. Assume your home country way is the only way
2. Ignore high tariffs and trade barriers
3. Enter markets with too much competition without a plan
4. Try to change a country's culture to match yours
5. Give up during culture shock phase
6. Hide mistakes - learn from them

C. 🎯 REAL TALK
1. Host countries don't always want you to change completely to their way
2. They might accept that you're different
3. BUT smart international companies look at what works in other cultures and bring those ideas back home
