## KEY EU INSTITUTIONS & FRAMEWORKS (Summary)

### **Core Institutions**
1. **European Commission** – Executive; exclusive legislative initiative right; "guardian of treaties"; 27 Commissioners (one per member state); President leads
2. **Council of the EU** – Ministerial body; co-legislator with EP; rotating presidency (6-month terms)
3. **European Council** – Heads of state/government; strategic direction; some rule-of-law enforcement roles
4. **European Parliament (EP)** – Directly elected since 1979; 705 MEPs (2026); organized by transnational political groups; co-legislator in most areas via Ordinary Legislative Procedure (OLP)
5. **Court of Justice of the EU (CJEU/ECJ)** – Ensures uniform EU law interpretation; preliminary rulings (Art. 267); landmark primacy & direct effect doctrines
6. **Court of Auditors** – Audits legality and efficiency of EU spending

### **Foundational Frameworks**

#### **Historical Stages (Exam Focus)**
- **1945–1958**: Post-WWII foundations (Marshall Plan, Council of Europe, NATO, ECSC 1951)
- **1958–1973**: EEC to first oil crisis (Treaty of Rome 1957, customs union, CAP, Luxembourg Compromise 1965)
- **1973–1992**: Crises & Southern enlargement (stagflation, 1986 Iberian accession, SEA 1987, path to Maastricht)
- **1992–present**: Maastricht framework (euro, Schengen, Lisbon reforms, crisis management)

#### **Core Six (ECSC 1951) & Later Waves**
- **Founding:** France, W. Germany, Italy, Belgium, Netherlands, Luxembourg
- **1973 Enlargement:** UK, Ireland, Denmark
- **1986 Enlargement:** Spain, Portugal, Greece (Iberian & Southern wave)
- **1995 Enlargement:** Austria, Finland, Sweden
- **2004 Big Bang:** Poland, Czech Republic, Hungary, Slovakia, Slovenia, Baltic states, Cyprus, Malta
- **2007:** Bulgaria, Romania | **2013:** Croatia

#### **Integration Ladder (Economic Depth)**
Free Trade Area → **Customs Union** (common external tariff) → **Common Market** (four freedoms: goods, services, capital, people) → **Single Market** (SEA 1987, regulatory harmonization) → **EMU/Euro** (single currency, 20 member states in 2026)

#### **Copenhagen Criteria (Accession Tests)**
- **Political Pillar:** Stable democracy, rule of law, human rights
- **Economic Pillar:** Functioning market economy
- **Legal Pillar:** Ability to adopt **acquis communautaire** (EU legal body)

#### **Rule of Law & Values Framework**
- **Article 2 TEU:** Foundational values (democracy, rule of law, human dignity, pluralism, non-discrimination, tolerance)
- **Article 7 TEU:** "Nuclear" enforcement procedure for systematic breaches (Hungary, Poland case studies); requires high political consensus
- **Conditionality (Regulation):** EU budget suspension when rule-of-law breaches threaten; faster than Article 7
- **Backsliding Indicators:** Judicial independence erosion, media pluralism weakening, electoral manipulation (Orbán/Fidesz, PiS Poland)

## HIGH-YIELD STUDY CONCEPTS (50–100 words each)

### **1. Supranationalism vs. Intergovernmentalism**
**Definition:** Supranationalism delegates authority to independent EU institutions (Commission, CJEU) that can override national states. Intergovernmentalism keeps states as masters, with decisions by bargaining in the Council and European Council. EU is hybrid: supranational in trade/single market (Commission initiative, QMV voting), intergovernmental in CFSP, budgets. Course emphasizes that "integration through law" and CJEU jurisprudence deepened supranational authority despite national-interest friction (Luxembourg Compromise 1965, Empty Chair Crisis 1965 de Gaulle).

### **2. Primacy & Direct Effect (CJEU Jurisprudence)**
**Definition:** *Costa v. ENEL (1964)*: EU law has **primacy** over conflicting national statutes in areas of EU competence; member states cannot override it. *Van Gend en Loos (1963)*: **Direct effect** means some EU norms give private parties enforceable rights in national courts without national implementation acts. Combined, these doctrines subordinate national law to EU law, making CJEU the authoritative interpreter. Enables uniform single-market enforcement but sparks "constitutional identity" friction (national courts claiming exceptions for core constitutional matters).

### **3. Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) & Budget Politics**
**Definition:** Largest EU budget item (~35% of budget in 1990s, declining to ~30% by 2020s). Supports farm incomes via price supports, direct payments, market interventions. Generated "butter mountains" and surpluses, sparking consumer and fiscal critiques. Originally French political anchor in 1960s deal (France accepts common market, others accept CAP). Symbols EU's paradox: integration creates winners (agribusiness) and losers (consumers, taxpayers); CAP reform contentious. Environmental concerns (pesticides, land use) now link CAP to Green Deal.

### **4. Accession Waves & Enlargement Logic**
**Definition:** EU expanded from Core Six (1951) to 27 members (2026). Waves: 1973 (UK, Ireland, Denmark), 1986 (Spain, Portugal, Greece), 1995 (Austria, Finland, Sweden), 2004 Big Bang (10 Central/Eastern European states + Cyprus, Malta), 2007 (Bulgaria, Romania), 2013 (Croatia). **Copenhagen Criteria (1993)** standardized accession tests: stable democracy, rule of law, functioning market economy, ability to adopt acquis. Post-2004 wave raised concerns: rule-of-law backsliding (Hungary, Poland), sovereignty concerns ("Anglosphere" post-Brexit imaginaries), and integration depth limits.

### **5. Euro Crisis, EMU Governance & Fiscal Rules**
**Definition:** Economic & Monetary Union (Maastricht 1992, euro launch 1999, banknotes 2002) unifies monetary policy (ECB, Frankfurt; price-stability mandate) but leaves national fiscal sovereignty. **Stability & Growth Pact** constrains deficits (3% GDP) and debt (60% GDP). **Asymmetric shocks** (e.g., 2008 financial crisis hitting Ireland, Greece harder than Germany) exposed rigidity: one monetary policy, fragmented fiscal response. Greece crisis (2010+) required **Troika** (IMF, ECB, EU Commission) conditionality; sparked "eurozone" vs "core periphery" divides. **ESM (European Stability Mechanism)** now provides crisis financing with rule-of-law conditionality strings.
