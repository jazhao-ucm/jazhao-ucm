Four Phases of Internationalization

Companies expand abroad in stages. Each phase is riskier than the last.

PhaseCharacteristicsExamplesRisk Level
1. Conceive Research, planning, feasibility study. No major capital commitment. Minimize exposure. Hiring local consultants, trade missions, market research Low
2. Execute Entry: exporting, licensing, partnerships. Test the market with limited commitment. Initial shipments, trial deals with distributors Medium
3. Establish ❌ MOST DANGEROUS PHASE — Greenfield investment, JVs, hiring. High capex, local infrastructure. Most failures occur here. Building factories, setting up operations, hiring staff HIGH
4. Expand Scale operations, increase market share, optimize costs. If you survived Phase 3, this is easier. Expanding capacity, new product lines, regional HQ Medium–High

Key insight: Phase 3 is where most ventures fail. Companies often overestimate success in Phases 1–2, then stumble when they must establish operations locally. Why? Cultural mismatches, underestimated costs, weak partnerships.

Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions

Six dimensions explain how culture affects business behavior and negotiations. Each country has a profile.

DimensionLow ScoreHigh ScoreBusiness Impact
Power Distance (PD) Equality, flat hierarchies (USA, Denmark, Australia) Hierarchy, authority respected (Mexico, Spain, France) High PD: defer to boss, top-down decisions. Low PD: challenge authority, consensus. Affects negotiations, org structure.
Individualism (IDV) Collective, group harmony (Japan, Mexico, China) Individual achievement, self-reliance (USA, Australia, UK) High IDV: personal incentives, performance-based. Low IDV: group loyalty, family business. Affects motivation, teamwork.
Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI) Risk-takers, flexible, ambiguous OK (Singapore, Denmark) Risk-averse, need rules, formal processes (Greece, Portugal, Mexico) High UAI: demand contracts, detail-oriented, resist change. Low UAI: adapt quickly, fewer rules. Affects negotiation pace, compliance.
Masculinity (MAS) Consensus, quality of life, caring (Nordic countries, Netherlands) Achievement, competition, ambition (USA, Germany, Japan) High MAS: competitive, performance-driven, work-life imbalance. Low MAS: collaborative, values balance. Affects work culture, gender roles.
Long-Term Orientation (LTO) Short-term thinking, tradition (USA, Middle East) Long-term planning, future focus, patience (China, Japan, Singapore) High LTO: invest for decades, relationships first. Low LTO: quarterly results, transactions. Affects business relationships, investment horizon.
Indulgence (IND) Restraint, control, discipline (East Asia, Russia) Indulgence, pleasure-seeking, leisure valued (Western Europe, Americas) High IND: work-life balance, consumer spending. Low IND: frugality, delayed gratification. Affects consumer markets, employee retention.

Practice: Hofstede scores: USA (PD=40, IDV=91) vs. Mexico (PD=81, IDV=30). How do these differences affect a US company trying to set up operations in Mexico? Management style? Negotiations?

Entry Mode Spectrum (Risk ↔ Control Trade-Off)

Choose your entry mode based on capital available, market uncertainty, and strategic importance.

ModeCapital RequiredControlRiskBest For
Exporting Low Low (indirect) Low Testing markets, limited resources
Licensing Very Low Low (licensee controls) Low–Medium (IP risks) IP-intensive businesses, risk-averse
Joint Venture (JV) Medium Medium (shared) Medium (partner risk, cultural clashes) Regulatory barriers, local knowledge required, capital sharing
Foreign Direct Investment (Greenfield) Very High Very High (full control) Very High (Phase 3 trap) Strategic importance, proven market, long-term commitment
Acquisition Very High Very High (100% ownership) High (integration, overpay risk) Speed, established brand/operations, market share

Decision framework: (1) Can we test with low risk? → Export or license. (2) Must we be in-market? → JV or Greenfield. (3) Regulatory barriers? → JV. (4) Strategic core business? → Greenfield or Acquisition.

Porter's Value Chain (Global Perspective)

When sourcing globally, decide which activities to keep in-house and which to outsource.

Primary Activities (direct value creation)

  • Inbound Logistics: Sourcing raw materials globally (e.g., Vietnamese factories → shipping)
  • Operations: Manufacturing (e.g., shoes in Vietnam, electronics in China)
  • Outbound Logistics: Distribution from factories to markets
  • Sales & Marketing: Adapt to local markets, local sales teams
  • Service: After-sales support, warranty (often needs local presence)

Support Activities

  • Procurement: Sourcing decisions (global suppliers vs. local)
  • Technology: R&D location (centralized HQ vs. distributed innovation centers)
  • HR: Talent management, expatriate vs. local hire strategy
  • Infrastructure: Finance, planning, legal (often centralized, then cascaded)

Case: Apple. Where is each value chain activity performed? Why? What are the risks?

Risk: Artificial vs. Natural

Two types of risk when going global. One is manageable; one is not.

Artificial Risk (Manageable)

  • Definition: Caused by poor planning, weak partnerships, or wrong entry mode.
  • Examples: Choosing wrong distributor, underestimating capex, poor contract terms, weak market research.
  • Mitigation: Better planning, due diligence, pilot phase, flexible contracts.

Natural Risk (Cannot Escape)

  • Definition: Rooted in fundamental cultural, political, or macro differences.
  • Examples: High Power Distance culture = hierarchy, corruption norms, different trust patterns. High Uncertainty Avoidance = slow decision-making, bureaucracy.
  • Mitigation: Long-term investment, deep cultural learning (CQ training), patient capital, local leadership.

Culture Shock: The U-Curve Model

  1. Honeymoon (Week 1–2): Everything is exciting and novel. High morale.
  2. Shock/Low Point (Week 3–8): Reality sets in. Frustration, homesickness, culture clashes. Many expatriates fail here.
  3. Adjustment (Week 9–16): Learning, adaptation, cultural understanding increases. Performance improves.
  4. Mastery (Month 6+): Comfort, effectiveness, full engagement.

For exam: When discussing international ventures that fail, distinguish between artificial risk (preventable) and natural risk (inherent). Show you understand the difference.

Competitive Strategy Across Borders

Two key strategic choices: Global standardization vs. Local customization (Glocalization trade-off).

StrategyGlobal StandardizationLocal Customization
Product/Service Same worldwide (e.g., Apple iPhone, Coca-Cola) Tailored to local tastes (e.g., McDonald's menu by country)
Advantages Economies of scale, lower cost, consistent brand Better market fit, stronger local loyalty, easier entry
Disadvantages May not fit local needs, cultural insensitivity, less competitive locally Higher costs, complexity, slower innovation transfer
Best For Commodity products, strong global brand, universal needs (e.g., tech) Food, retail, services, culturally sensitive products

Best practice: "Glocalization" — standardize where it adds value (R&D, core technology, brand), customize locally (product features, marketing, distribution).

Practice Cases & Exercises

Case 1: France → Chile Tech Transfer (Real UCM Case)

A French IT firm tried to establish operations in Chile. They failed due to: weak IP protection, cultural mismatch (high PD France vs. egalitarian Chile), partners didn't communicate, trust broke down. Analyze using frameworks: Entry mode? Risk type? Cultural dimensions? What should they have done differently?

Hint: Was this artificial risk or natural risk? Which phases did they fail?

Exercise 2: Hofstede + Entry Mode

You want to expand your restaurant business to: (a) Germany, (b) Japan, (c) Saudi Arabia. For each, recommend an entry mode and explain using Hofstede dimensions.

Hint: Germany: low PD, high IDV → exporting or licensing might work. Japan: high UAI, low IDV → JV or local hire. Saudi Arabia: high PD → JV with local partner crucial.

Exercise 3: Value Chain Optimization

You manufacture sporting goods. Sketch your global value chain: where do you source materials? Where do you manufacture? Where do you market/sell? Why? What are the risks?

Hint: Think: labor costs, quality, logistics, tariffs, IP protection, tax efficiency.
