Exam Tips & Master Pattern (High-Yield)

The Master Pattern (Use in Every Answer)

"From a [framework] perspective, [force/concept] is [high/low] because [reason], which makes the industry [attractive/unattractive] for [who]; therefore [strategic implication]."

Emergency Checklist (When Your Brain Freezes)

  1. Opener: Write one line naming the topic (e.g., "This question is essentially about industry attractiveness").
  2. Framework: Pick ONE and say it (e.g., "From a Porter Five Forces perspective..." OR "Using the VRIO framework...").
  3. 2–3 Strong Points: Apply the Master Pattern to each point.
  4. Close: One line with recommendation (e.g., "In summary, these forces reduce profit potential; therefore the firm should differentiate or focus").

Stock Openers

  • "This question is essentially about [industry attractiveness / internal vs external analysis / CA in an unattractive industry]."
  • "From a Porter Five Forces perspective..."
  • "Using the resource-based view (VRIO)..."
  • "At the macro level (PESTEL)..." / "At the firm level (VRIO)..."

Exam secret: Write clearly. Make your framework visible. Judges want to see structured thinking, not perfect answers.

Porter Five Forces Analysis

Diagnose industry attractiveness (profit potential). Five competitive forces determine margins.

ForceKey QuestionHigh Force = Bad for ProfitExample
Threat of New Entrants How easy is entry? Low barriers → easy entry → competitive pressure ↑ Airlines (low barriers, many entrants)
Bargaining Power of Suppliers How much can suppliers charge? Few suppliers, differentiated inputs → prices ↑ Pharmaceutical APIs (concentrated suppliers)
Bargaining Power of Buyers How much can customers push price down? Many buyers, low switching costs → prices ↓ Retail (powerful big-box chains)
Threat of Substitutes Do alternatives exist? Many substitutes → customer loyalty ↓ Railroads vs. trucking (substitutes limit prices)
Rivalry Among Competitors How intense is competition? High rivalry, slow growth, exit barriers high → price wars Airlines (commoditized, high fixed costs, cutthroat)

Quick Diagnosis

If ALL five forces are strong: Industry is unattractive; expect low profit margins. Example: Airlines.

If some forces are strong, others weak: Opportunities exist. Find the protected niches.

Practice: Analyze the smartphone industry. Which forces are strongest? Why are profit margins concentrated in Apple (high) vs. Android manufacturers (low)?

VRIO: Building & Sustaining Competitive Advantage

Porter Five Forces tells you industry profit potential. VRIO explains firm performance differences.

VRIO Checklist (Ask in Order)

LetterQuestionIf YESIf NO
V: Valuable? Does it help exploit opportunities or neutralize threats? Continue to R Not a source of advantage; stop
R: Rare? Do competitors have it? Continue to I Valuable but common; no sustained advantage
I: Inimitable? Is it hard to copy? (patents, culture, relationships, complexity) Continue to O Can be copied; temporary advantage only
O: Organized to Capture Rents? Does the company structure allow exploitation? ✅ Sustained CA Resource not fully exploited

Examples

  • Apple brand + design: Valuable ✓, Rare ✓, Inimitable ✓ (culture-based), Organized ✓ → Sustained CA.
  • Database technology (generic): Valuable ✓, Rare ✗ (everyone uses similar) → No CA.

Practice: Analyze a competitor. Pick 2–3 resources. For each, run through VRIO. Which resources drive advantage?

Ansoff Matrix & Growth Strategies

Two strategic choices: Products (current vs. new) × Markets (current vs. new).

Current ProductsNew Products
Current Markets Market Penetration
Sell more to existing customers. Increase share of wallet, usage, loyalty.

Risk: Low
Example: Coca-Cola selling more to same store		 Product Development
New products for existing customers. Leverage existing customer base & distribution.

Risk: Medium
Example: Apple Watch (new product, existing Apple customers)
New Markets Market Development
Existing products in new geographies/segments.

Risk: Medium
Example: Coca-Cola expanding to new countries		 Diversification
New products, new markets. Highest risk; lowest synergy.

Risk: Very High

Exam tip: When a case asks "What should firm X do?" Use Ansoff to structure your answer. Market penetration = safe. Diversification = risky.

Grant's 4-Step Case Template (Universal)

Use this for any case or strategic analysis:

  1. Goals (Long-term vision): What are the firm's economic, social, reputational objectives?
  2. Environment (External analysis): Industry structure (Five Forces), macro trends (PESTEL), competitive positioning.
  3. Resources & Capabilities (VRIO): What does the firm own that matters? Is it valuable, rare, inimitable, organized?
  4. Implementation (Coherence check): Are goals, environment, and resources aligned? If diverge, strategy is weak.

Golden rule: Strategy is coherent when these four elements align. Where they diverge, performance suffers.

Practice Exercises (with Model Answer Hints)

Exercise 1: Industry Attractiveness (Five Forces)

Analyze the airline industry. Rate each of the five forces (high/low). Conclude: is the industry attractive? Why are airline margins so thin?

Hint: High rivalry (many competitors, commoditized), high threat of substitutes (train, car, video conf), high supplier power (fuel, aircraft), low buyer power (diverse buyers). Result: low-margin, competitive industry.

Exercise 2: VRIO Diagnosis

A small cafe has: (a) unique location, (b) loyal regular customers, (c) excellent coffee (replicable recipe), (d) skilled barista. For each, run VRIO. Which are sources of sustained CA? What's the biggest vulnerability?

Hint: Location & culture (loyal customers) are hard to replicate. Coffee recipe is easy to copy. Barista: if person-dependent, risky.

Exercise 3: Strategic Case (TechCore Pivot)

Use Grant's 4-step template. A 15-year-old software firm (built on data management) sees AI disrupting its market. Revenue stalled. Should they pivot to AI, stick with legacy, or acquire an AI startup?

Hint: (1) Goals: maintain revenue growth & market position. (2) Environment: AI is high-growth, disrupting legacy software. (3) Resources: brand, customer relationships, but outdated tech. (4) Implementation: pivot = risky but necessary; acquisition = faster but integration risk; stick = slow decline. Which aligns best?
