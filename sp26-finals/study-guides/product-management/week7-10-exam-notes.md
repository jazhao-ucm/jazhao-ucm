Week 7 — Manufacturing, Process Design & Memos (Mar 9–10, 2026)
- **Goal:** understand process-design trade-offs and write a concise memo comparing alternatives.
- **High-yield frameworks:** **Six Dimensions of Process Design** — Lot Size, Equipment, Skills, Scheduling, Product Range, Inventory.
- **Core idea:** trade-offs; a firm cannot optimize all six simultaneously. Choose alignment with **strategy** (cost vs differentiation).

- Quick definitions to handwrite and memorize:
  - **Lot Size:** batch size (small = flexibility, large = economies of scale).
  - **Equipment:** general-purpose (flexible) vs specific-purpose (efficient at scale).
  - **Skills:** artisan/high-skill vs standardized/low-skill labor.
  - **Scheduling:** make-to-order (MTO) vs make-to-stock (MTS).
  - **Product Range:** variety increases complexity and cost.
  - **Inventory:** buffer vs flow; high inventory masks variability.

- Exam tip (write & practice): Given two options (artisan vs factory), list **3 evidence points** for each (cost drivers, lead time, quality variance). Always tie to strategy: **differentiation** vs **cost leadership**.

- Memo structure (pencil in headings when handwriting):
  1) **Executive summary (1–2 lines)** — recommended option + 1 reason.
  2) **Options compared** — Option A (short bullets), Option B (short bullets).
  3) **Trade-offs** — apply six dimensions (one-liners).
  4) **Recommendation & next steps** — pilot plan + metric to track.

- Key metric examples to mention in memo: **Unit Cost**, **Lead Time**, **Defect Rate**, **Throughput**. Bold whichever is primary.

Week 8 — Customer Interviews & Midterm Prep (Mar 17–18, 2026)
- **Goal:** run discovery interviews properly; synthesize findings into testable hypotheses. Use midterm materials for core frameworks.
- **High-yield method (write and memorize):** **Interview rules** — ask about past behavior, avoid hypotheticals, probe for context, get specifics (time, place, cost), and seek emotional cues.
- **Synthesis steps (4):** 1) Capture raw quotes; 2) Code themes; 3) Identify JTBD; 4) Convert to assumptions/hypotheses.

- Template to handwrite when summarizing interviews:
  - Interviewee: ____ (age/role)
  - Problem described: ____
  - Frequency/Context: ____
  - Pain (emotion): ____
  - Hypothesis derived: "If we provide ____, then ____ will improve because ____"

- Midterm study anchors (high-yield items from `midterm-print-all.tex`):
  - **BMC** — know the 9 blocks and short examples.
  - **CSDA** — Customer-focused, Strategic, Data-driven, Action-oriented.
  - **Design thinking steps** — empathize, define, ideate, prototype, test.
  - **MVP definition** — smallest test for one hypothesis.

- Exam checklist (handwrite): For any short-answer, do these 4 steps on paper: Identify the **problem**, name the **framework**, apply 2–3 points with evidence, give a **one-line recommendation**.

Practice prompt: "Synthesize 4 interview notes into one JTBD and propose a 1-week MVP test." (Answer box: _______)

Week 9 — MVP, Prioritization & Product Choices (Mar 23–24, 2026)
- **Goal:** pick the smallest change that validates the riskiest assumption; prioritize features that reduce hypothesis risk.
- **High-yield checklist to handwrite:**
  - **Is this an MVP or a feature?** MVP tests one riskiest assumption.
  - **Primary metric for MVP:** choose one (e.g., activation, retention, engagement).
  - **Loveable MVP heuristics** (from `loveable-mvp-helper`): reduce friction, preserve core quality, enable social proof/buffering, avoid premature scaling.

- Prioritization (handwrite short formulas):
  - **RICE = Reach × Impact × Confidence / Effort** (briefly note each term).
  - **ICE = Impact × Confidence / Effort** — quick estimate for class.

- Practical example to write: For a dating app MVP (unfiltered Q&A + social buffering):
  - Hypothesis: "Structured icebreakers increase quality conversations by X%"
  - Metric: **% conversations that last > 10 messages** (write this down bold)
  - Experiment: Launch to 200 users, measure metric for 2 weeks.

- Exam-style short answer template to handwrite:
  1) State the **hypothesis**.
  2) Define the **metric** and how to measure it.
  3) State **sample size & duration**.
  4) Expected outcome & decision rule.

Practice prompt: "Design an MVP experiment for improving activation. Give hypothesis, metric, sample, and decision rule." (Answer box: _______)

Week 10 — AI Prototyping, Launch & Iteration (Apr 6–7, 2026)
- **Goal:** apply fast AI prototyping patterns and plan iterative launches with clear feedback loops.
- **AI prototyping notes (handwrite):**
  - Use AI to simulate user flows (chatbot prototypes), generate content, or synthesize interview notes quickly.
  - Keep experiments focused: small dataset, clear prompt, validation metric.
  - Ethical check: data privacy, avoid exposing PII in demos.

- **Launch checklist (one-line items to handwrite):**
  - Prelaunch: beta invites, messaging, analytics setup, support plan.
  - Launch day: owners, channels, monitoring (error, engagement), PR/messaging.
  - Postlaunch: collect feedback, tag by severity, run top 3 experiments.

- **Prioritization for fixes:** use **Impact / Effort** or **RICE** and list owners and timelines.
- **Communication cadence:** weekly 1‑page update: Top metric, 3 wins, 3 blockers, next actions.

- Exam cue: If asked for a post-launch plan, handwrite: 1) Monitoring metric; 2) Feedback channels; 3) Top 3 fixes with owners; 4) Rollback criteria.

Practice prompt: "You launched an AI prototype and users report hallucinations. How do you triage and fix?" (Answer box: _______)
