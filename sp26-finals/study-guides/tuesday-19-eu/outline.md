## Complete 14-Session Breakdown

### **Session 1: Introduction to the European Union**
**Focus:** What is the EU today?
- 27 member states, ~450M citizens, €16.6T GDP
- Supranational vs intergovernmental hybrid structure
- Founding principles: democracy, rule of law, human rights, social market economy
- EU as peace project and political experiment

### **Session 2: Origins of European Integration (1945-1958)**
**Focus:** Post-WWII context and early integration steps
- Marshall Plan & NATO
- Monnet-Schuman Plan: functionalist integration approach
- ECSC (1951): Coal & Steel Community - first supranational authority
- Treaty of Rome (1957): European Economic Community (EEC) founding
- Key figures: Jean Monnet, Robert Schuman, Konrad Adenauer
- Reference: Zweig excerpts, Judt "Origins of EU", Karawtzek/Pestel "New Histories of Europe"

### **Session 3: Building the Common Market (1958-1992)**
**Focus:** Integration expansion, crises, and achievements
- 1958-1968: Rapid customs union, Common Agricultural Policy (CAP), tariff elimination
- 1965-66: Empty Chair Crisis (de Gaulle/France) → Luxembourg Compromise
- 1973-79: Oil crises, stagnation, enlargement (UK, Ireland, Denmark)
- 1981-86: Southward enlargement (Greece, Spain, Portugal)
- 1986: Single European Act → 1992 single market deadline
- 1979: European Monetary System (EMS)
- Reference: Seidel "Early Common Market", Warlouzet "Hague to Maastricht", Vinen "Europe in Fragments"

### **Session 4: From Maastricht to Lisbon (1993-2007)**
**Focus:** Political union, monetary union, eastern enlargement, constitutional reform
- Maastricht Treaty (1992): Three Pillars structure (EC, CFSP, JHA) + European Citizenship
- EMU Convergence Criteria & euro launch (1999 electronic, 2002 physical)
- 2004 Historic Enlargement: 10 countries (Poland, Czech Republic, Hungary, etc.) + Cyprus, Malta
- 2007 Eastern expansion: Romania, Bulgaria
- Constitutional Treaty failure (2004-05)
- Lisbon Treaty (2007): Institutional reforms, Parliament power-building
- Reference: Anderson "Ever Closer Union", readings on EMU

### **Session 5: A Turbulent Time (2008-2020) - Crises & Euroskepticism**
**Focus:** Financial crisis, debt crisis, Brexit, migration, populism
- 2008 Financial Crisis: Global recession, bank failures
- 2010-15 Eurozone Debt Crisis: Greece bailout + austerity, contagion risk (Ireland, Portugal, Spain)
- Mario Draghi: "Whatever it takes" speech (2012)
- Rise of euroskepticism: Left (anti-austerity) & Right (sovereignty/migration concerns)
- Euroskeptic parties: UKIP, Front National, Five Star, AfD
- 2015-16 Migration Crisis: 1M+ asylum seekers, Schengen strain, populism fueled
- 2016 Brexit: Referendum vote to leave; first major integration reversal
- 2020 COVID-19: Initial discoordination, then €750B recovery fund (historic fiscal cooperation)
- Reference: Bijsmans "Euroskepticism multifaceted phenomenon", Kenny/Pearce "Brexit & Anglosphere", ongoing news

### **Session 6: The Twin Executive - European Council & Commission**
**Focus:** Political direction vs. executive authority, tensions
- **European Council:** 27 heads of state/government + Commission President; strategic political guidance; 4x yearly; rotating presidency (6 months)
- **Council of Ministers (Sectoral):** Ministers from member states; coordinates policies; co-legislates; uses Qualified Majority Voting (QMV: 55% states, 65% population)
- **European Commission:** 27 Commissioners + President; monopoly on legislative initiative; guardian of EU law; manages budget; external representation
- The "Twin Executive" competition: intergovernmental (Council) vs. supranational (Commission) interests
- Reference: Duff "Twin Executive", "Beginner's Guide to EU"

### **Session 7: The Legislative - Parliament & Council of Ministers**
**Focus:** Legislative power evolution, decision-making process
- **European Parliament evolution:** 1979 direct elections (advisory only) → 1986 co-operation (some power) → 1992 co-decision (true co-legislator) → 2009+ ordinary legislative procedure (full legislator)
- **Today:** 705 MEPs directly elected (5-year terms); co-legislates ~95% of laws with Council; approves budget; elects Commission President; can censure Commission
- **Ordinary Legislative Procedure:** Commission proposes → Parliament 1st reading → Council 1st reading → Parliament 2nd reading → Council 2nd reading → tri-logue negotiation if needed → final vote both chambers
- **Tri-logue:** Behind-the-scenes negotiation (Parliament rapporteur, Council presidency, Commission) to reach compromise
- Reference: Duff "Parliamentary Europe", Olsen "European Parliament", Salm "The EP, Empowering the Talking Shop"

### **Session 8: The Judiciary - Court of Justice of the European Union**
**Focus:** EU law supremacy, integration through law, rule of law
- **CJEU Structure:** 27 judges (one per member state) + 11 Advocates General
- **Jurisdiction:** Cases against member states (Commission enforcement), review of EU institution legality, preliminary rulings from national courts (Article 267 - most powerful tool)
- **Landmark Cases - Integration Through Law:**
  - *Van Gend & Loos* (1963): EU law has "direct effect" → creates individual rights → transformative
  - *Costa v. ENEL* (1964): EU law supremacy over national law → established constitutional hierarchy
  - *Rewe-Zentralfinanz* (1979): Free movement of goods principle (non-tariff barriers struck down)
- **Contemporary Issue: Rule of Law Conflicts**
  - Poland & Hungary judicial independence erosion; EU uses infringement proceedings & budget conditionality
  - Fundamental question: Who enforces EU standards when member states defy?
- Reference: Davies "Court of Justice & Integration", Duff "Judiciary", Reich "Rule of Law in Poland & Hungary"

### **Session 9: Other Institutions & Bodies**
**Focus:** Decentralized authority, monetary policy, audit, agencies
- **European Central Bank (ECB):** Manages euro for 20 eurozone members; sets interest rates; conducts monetary policy; regulates eurozone banks; independence protected from political pressure; President: Christine Lagarde (2019-); sometimes controversial (bond purchases, political neutrality questioned)
- **Court of Auditors:** 27 members; external auditor of EU accounts; investigates fraud; limited enforcement (relies on other bodies)
- **EU Agencies (100+):** Specialized implementation bodies (EMA, EFSA, Frontex, etc.); decentralized but coordinated approach; expertise & efficiency
- **Economic & Social Committee:** Advisory role; represents employer, worker, consumer interests
- **Committee of Regions:** Advisory; represents regional & local governments; balances centralization
- Reference: Triviño/Jordana "EU Agencies Literature Review", Olsen chapter on ECB

### **Session 10: A Currency for a Single Market - Economic & Monetary Union**
**Focus:** EMU design, challenges, eurozone governance
- **EMU Phases:**
  - Stage 1 (1990-93): Capital liberalization
  - Stage 2 (1994-98): ECU conversions fixed; convergence criteria assessed
  - Stage 3 (1999-2002): Euro launch (electronic 1999, physical 2002); 12 initial members, now 20
- **Convergence Criteria (for euro membership):** Inflation ≤1.5% above 3 best performers; deficit <3% GDP; debt <60% GDP; EMS participation 2+ years stabilit; interest rates within 2% of 3 best
- **EMU Challenges:**
  - Asymmetric shocks (one policy doesn't fit all economies)
  - No fiscal union for redistribution
  - Labor immobility (language barriers)
  - Sovereign-bank doom loop (banks hold national debt; crises cascade)
- **Governance: Stability & Growth Pact**
  - Deficit max 3% GDP; public debt max 60% GDP
  - Often violated; enforcement weak (political resistance)
  - COVID-19 suspension (2020-22)
- **Recent Reforms:**
  - Banking Union (2014): Unified regulation, bail-in regime
  - European Stability Mechanism: Lender of last resort
  - Recovery Fund (2020): €750B for post-COVID distressed economies (breakthrough!)
  - Capital Markets Union: Diversify eurozone financing
- Reference: Schelkle "Economic & Monetary Union", Drach "EMU from 1957 to eurocrisis", O'Dwyer "Gendered history of EMU"

### **Session 11: Identity & Membership - Enlargements, Citizenship, Migration**
**Focus:** Who is European? Enlargement waves, free movement, third-country immigrants
- **European Identity Construction:**
  - Geographic (ill-defined; Turkey controversy)
  - Cultural/Religious (Judeo-Christian heritage debated)
  - Political/Values (democracy, rule of law, human rights)
  - Historical (shared traumas; different national narratives)
- **Enlargement Waves:**
  - 1973: UK, Ireland, Denmark (Western consolidation)
  - 1981-86: Greece, Spain, Portugal (post-authoritarian transitions)
  - 1995: Austria, Finland, Sweden (wealthy neutrals)
  - 2004-07: Historic Eastern enlargement (8 ex-communist + Cyprus, Malta; then Romania, Bulgaria 2007)
- **Copenhagen Criteria (for membership):** Democracy/rule of law/human rights; functioning market economy; implementing EU law capacity
- **European Citizenship (Maastricht 1992):**
  - Automatic for member state nationals
  - Rights: free movement, voting in EU elections, diplomatic protection
  - Unprecedented layer between national & international identity
- **Free Movement Rights:**
  - Workers: free movement for employment (Schengen)
  - Services: providers cross borders
  - Capital: money flows freely
  - Residence: EU citizens can live anywhere
  - Exceptions: public policy, security, health grounds
- **Migration & Refugee Policy:**
  - Common Asylum System: EU-wide standards
  - Dublin Regulation: First country processes asylum (controversial; overwhelms frontline states like Greece, Italy)
  - Schengen Area: 27 countries passport-free travel; managed external border
  - Tension: humanitarian obligations vs. sovereignty vs. security
- **Colonial Legacies (Eurafrica):**
  - Historical: Colonial territories had special EU relationship
  - Evolution: ACP (African, Caribbean, Pacific) partnership
  - Critique: Neocolonial dynamics persist in EU-Africa relations
- Reference: Hansen "European Citizenship", Jonson "Eurafrica", readings on migration/identity

### **Session 12: The Language of Power - Foreign & Security Policy**
**Focus:** EU's international role, military constraints, strategic autonomy
- **CFSP (Common Foreign & Security Policy):**
  - High Representative (EU Foreign Minister): Josep Borrell (2019-)
  - European External Action Service (EEAS): EU's diplomatic corps
  - Problem: Unanimity requirement = any member can veto (fragmentation)
- **EU as Global Actor:**
  - Strengths: Trade power (27-member bloc), sanctions coordination (Russia, Iran), largest aid donor, norm-setting (GDPR exported)
  - Constraints: No army (relies on NATO/US); national interests diverge; soft power limited vs. hard power competitors
- **European Defense Policy:**
  - PESCO (Permanent Structured Cooperation): Military cooperation framework
  - European Defense Fund (EDF): Joint research & development
  - Goal: "Strategic autonomy" from US
  - Reality: NATO remains central to European defense
- **Contemporary Challenges:**
  - Russia: Ukraine invasion (2022) fundamentally shifted EU militarization thinking; NATO Article 5 invoked; EU military aid to Ukraine (historic)
  - China: Economic dependency (rare earth, tech) vs. security concerns (Taiwan, Xinjiang human rights)
  - US: NATO cornerstone, trade tensions, democratic values alignment (Trump era questioned commitment)
- Reference: Bindi "European Union Foreign Policy", readings on defense/Ukraine conflict

### **Session 13: Other Policies - From CAP to European Green Deal**
**Focus:** Major policy domains, budget distribution, contemporary transitions
- **Common Agricultural Policy (CAP):**
  - ~38% of EU budget (~€60B/year)
  - Original design: Price supports for farmers (guarantee income) → oversupply, protectionism, high consumer prices
  - Controversy: Favors large agribusiness; harms developing countries (export competition); environmental damage (monoculture, chemical inputs)
  - Reforms: 1992 (MacSharry) shift to direct payments; 1999 (Agenda 2000) decoupling & environmental conditions; 2013 green payments
  - Ongoing: Pressure to prioritize sustainability over production
- **Environmental Policy Evolution:**
  - 1970s-80s: First directives (water, waste, air)
  - 1987: Single European Act elevated environment to key policy
  - 1993: Maastricht made environment core
  - 2005: Emissions Trading Scheme (ETS) launched (cap-and-trade carbon market)
  - Key: Waste Directive (circular economy), Nature Directives (biodiversity), Renewable Energy Directive
- **European Green Deal (2019):**
  - Ambition: Climate neutrality by 2050; 55% emissions reduction by 2030
  - Fit for 55 Package: Legislative suite
  - Just Transition Fund: Support coal-dependent regions
  - REPowerEU: Energy independence post-Russia sanctions
  - Circular Economy Plan: Reduce extraction, maximize recycling
- **Green Deal Challenges:**
  - Cost: Trillions needed for infrastructure transition
  - Social impact: Worker displacement (fossil fuel regions); inequality risks (green jobs training)
  - Member divergence: Coal-dependent Poland resists acceleration; national energy independence tensions
  - Global coordination: Unilateral EU policy risks "carbon leakage" (production moves to non-EU countries with lower standards)
- Reference: Bayerlein et al. "Industrial Employment & Populism in Germany", readings on CAP reform, Green Deal documents

### **Session 14: Course Recap & Exam Preparation**
**Focus:** Synthesis, theory application, exam strategy
- **Periodization:** Recovery → Building → Deepening → Crisis Management
- **Key Frameworks:**
  - **Functionalism (Monnet):** Sector-by-sector integration; spill-over logic; technical experts lead
  - **Intergovernmentalism:** Member states remain dominant; national interest drives; veto power critical
  - **Neofunctionalism:** Adds interest groups, supranational actors, conflict/compromise dynamics
- **Apply these to cases:** When does functionalism work? When does state sovereignty reassert? How do crises reset the balance?
- **Memorize Timelines:**
  - 1951: ECSC; 1957: Rome; 1992: Maastricht; 1999-2002: Euro; 2004: Eastern enlargement; 2008-15: Crises; 2016: Brexit; 2020: COVID
- **Exam Question Types:**
  - Historical causation (Why did X happen then?)
  - Institutional analysis (How does this mechanism work? What are its limits?)
  - Policy evaluation (Is this policy justified? What alternatives?)
  - Theoretical application (Which theory best explains this phase?)
  - Current events synthesis (Connect history to today's challenges)
