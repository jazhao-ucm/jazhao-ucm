What is the EU? (Core Definition)

The European Union is a political and economic union of 27 member states. It combines elements of supranationalism (shared sovereignty) and intergovernmentalism (state cooperation).

Three Core Tensions

TensionSupranational PoleIntergovernmental Pole
Authority EU institutions (Commission, EP, Court) hold power Member states retain sovereignty; EU = forum for bargaining
Decision-Making Majority voting (majority wins; minority bound) Unanimity (all states must agree; veto power)
Law EU law is supreme; direct effect (applies to citizens) International treaty; states implement as they choose

Exam tip: When describing any EU policy, ask: "Is this mainly supranational or intergovernmental?" and explain why.

Key EU Institutions (The Big Three + Others)

The "Big Three" Decision-Makers

InstitutionCompositionRoleCharacter
Commission 27 Commissioners (one per state), led by President Executive: proposes laws, implements policy, enforces treaties Supranational (independent of member states)
European Parliament (EP) 705 MEPs (elected every 5 years by citizens) Co-legislates (with Council), approves Commission Supranational (represents citizens, not states)
Council (of Ministers) Ministers from each member state (rotating presidency) Co-legislates, coordinates policy, represents national interests Intergovernmental (states bargain directly)

Other Key Bodies

  • European Court of Justice (CJEU): Highest court; interprets EU law, ensures member state compliance. Key cases establish supranationalism (Costa v. ENEL, Van Gend en Loos).
  • European Council: Heads of state/government; sets strategic direction, resolves deadlocks.
  • Court of Auditors: Ensures EU budget is properly spent.

Practice: A new environmental law is proposed. Describe its path through the Commission → Council → EP. Who must agree? Where can a veto happen?

EU Law & Supranationalism: Primacy and Direct Effect

Two landmark CJEU doctrines establish EU law as supreme over national law:

1. Primacy (Costa v. ENEL, 1964)

  • EU law overrides national law (even if national law comes later).
  • Member states cannot use national law as excuse for non-compliance.
  • Establishes EU as a legal order, not a loose treaty.

2. Direct Effect (Van Gend en Loos, 1963)

  • EU law can create rights and obligations for citizens directly (not just states).
  • Citizens can sue in national courts to enforce EU law rights.
  • Makes EU law "real" at the citizen level, not just diplomatic.

Key insight: These doctrines are controversial because they shift power from national parliaments to EU institutions. Ask: "Why did states accept this? What are the trade-offs?"

Copenhagen Criteria (Requirements for Membership)

  1. Political: Stable democracy, rule of law, human rights protection.
  2. Economic: Functioning market economy.
  3. Institutional: Ability to adopt and implement EU law.

Question: Why did Turkey not join in 2004? What are the barriers for a new applicant today (e.g., Ukraine, Moldova)?

Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) & Budget Politics

CAP is the EU's most expensive policy (~30% of budget). It shows how EU members bargain over money.

CAP Structure

  • Price supports & subsidies: EU guarantees agricultural prices; farmers get direct payments per hectare.
  • Market intervention: EU buys surplus produce to prop up prices.
  • Rural development: Funding for modernization and sustainability.

Winners & Losers

  • Winners: Large-scale farmers (France, Spain, Poland), agricultural lobbies.
  • Losers: Consumers (higher food prices), non-agricultural industries (less EU budget for them), developing countries (EU agricultural exports compete with theirs).

Budget Tensions

The EU budget is negotiated every 7 years (Multiannual Financial Framework). Large net contributors (Germany, Netherlands) want to cut CAP and overall spending. France, Spain protect CAP. Result: deals and trade-offs.

Exam pattern: CAP symbolizes the tension between efficiency (reform agriculture) and politics (appease farmers, protect rural economies).

Euro Crisis & EMU Governance (2008–2015)

The financial crisis exposed a fundamental design flaw in the eurozone: one monetary policy, fragmented fiscal policy.

The Problem

  • ECB controls interest rates for all eurozone members.
  • Member states control their own budgets (no EU fiscal union).
  • Asymmetric shocks: If Spain enters recession, ECB may not cut rates enough (because Germany is fine). Spain cannot devalue to help exports.
  • Debt spirals: If a country's sovereign debt rises, its borrowing costs spike (bond yields ↑). Banks hold the debt, so bank health declines too.

Crisis Highlights (Greece 2010, Ireland 2010, Portugal 2011)

Countries needed emergency loans. The Troika (ECB, IMF, European Commission) imposed conditionality: austerity, privatizations, labor market reforms. Politically contentious (seen as loss of sovereignty).

Outcomes & Reforms

  • ESM (European Stability Mechanism): firewall for future crises.
  • Banking Union: centralized bank supervision and resolution.
  • Fiscal Compact: limits on budget deficits and debt.
  • Unresolved: No EU-level fiscal transfer mechanism (like US federal spending); debt/deficit rules still tight.

Key takeaway: The euro crisis shows the limits of supranationalism. States gave up monetary sovereignty but not fiscal power—creating contradictions.

Practice Questions (Prepare for Exams)

Question 1: EU Law Supremacy

A member state passes a law that contradicts an EU directive. Can a citizen sue in national court to enforce the EU rule? Explain using Costa v. ENEL and Van Gend en Loos.

Model answer hint: Yes. Cite primacy (EU law overrides) + direct effect (citizens have rights). Explain why this is supranational.

Question 2: CAP & Budget Politics

France and Poland want to protect CAP funding; Germany and Netherlands want to cut it. Why? What are the trade-offs?

Model answer hint: France & Poland = large agricultural interests (winners). Germany & NL = net contributors (want lower EU budget or more spending on tech/research). Discuss efficiency vs. political stability.

Question 3: Euro Crisis

Why did countries like Greece need Troika bailouts? What are the alternatives to austerity? Why are they politically difficult?

Model answer hint: Debt spiral + no currency devaluation. Alternatives: fiscal transfers, debt forgiveness, ECB large-scale QE. Political barriers: Northern countries refuse to "bail out" profligacy; moral hazard fears.
