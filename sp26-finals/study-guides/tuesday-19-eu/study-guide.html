🇪🇺 Introduction to the European Union

The European Union represents an unprecedented experiment in supranational governance - 27 member states pooling sovereignty to achieve peace, prosperity, and political integration.

🎯 Course Overview

  • Session 1: What is the EU today? Core principles and statistics
  • Sessions 2-5: Historical evolution from ECSC to modern crises
  • Sessions 6-9: Institutional architecture (executive, legislative, judicial, other bodies)
  • Session 10: Economic & Monetary Union complexity
  • Session 11: Identity, enlargement, citizenship, migration
  • Session 12: Foreign & security policy role
  • Session 13: Major policy areas (agriculture, environment, social)
  • Session 14: Exam preparation & synthesis

Key Themes Across All Sessions

  • Supranationalism: EU's unique power above member states
  • Democratic Deficit: Legitimacy challenges in EU governance
  • Integration vs. Sovereignty: Eternal tension driving change
  • Crisis as Catalyst: Major reforms follow crises (monetary union, recovery funds)

Session 1: What is the EU Today?

Basic Facts

  • 27 member states after Brexit (2020)
  • ~450 million citizens
  • €16.6 trillion GDP (world's largest economy)
  • 20 eurozone countries use common euro currency

Supranational vs. Intergovernmental

EU is hybrid: partly supranational (Commission, Parliament, Court), partly intergovernmental (Council of Ministers, European Council veto powers).

Founding Principles (Maastricht Treaty, Article 6)

  • Democracy and rule of law
  • Fundamental human rights
  • Social market economy
  • Sustainable development

Session 2: Origins 1945-1958

Post-WWII Context

Europe devastated; Cold War division; need for reconciliation (especially France-Germany), and Western alliance against Soviet threat.

Key Steps

  • 1948: Marshall Plan funds European recovery
  • 1949: NATO founded for collective defense
  • 1951: ECSC (Coal & Steel Community) - Monnet and Schuman's functional integration approach
  • 1957: Treaty of Rome creates EEC (European Economic Community)

Monnet-Schuman Plan (The "Functionalist" Approach)

Pool coal and steel production in supranational authority → makes war materially impossible → spill-over into other areas.

Session 3: Building the Common Market (1958-1992)

Major Phases

  • 1958-1968: Rapid customs union; Common Agricultural Policy; internal tariffs eliminated
  • 1965-66: Empty Chair Crisis: France's de Gaulle rejected supranational authority → Luxembourg Compromise restored national veto
  • 1973-1979: Oil crises, stagnation, skepticism about integration; enlargement (UK, Ireland, Denmark)
  • 1986: Single European Act revives integration → 1992 deadline for single market

Key Achievements

  • Customs union
  • Common Agricultural Policy (controversial budget drain)
  • Free movement of goods
  • European Monetary System (1979)

Session 4: Maastricht to Lisbon (1993-2007)

Maastricht Treaty (1992) - The Big Leap

  • Created actual "European Union" (not just economic community)
  • Three Pillars: EC (supranational) + CFSP (intergovernmental foreign policy) + JHA (justice/home affairs)
  • European citizenship created
  • Launched path to Economic & Monetary Union

Euro Launch Timeline

  • 1999: Electronic ECU conversions fixed
  • 2002: Physical euro coins/bills circulation
  • Today: 20 countries in eurozone

Historic Eastern Enlargement (2004)

10 countries join: Poland, Czech Republic, Hungary, Slovakia, Slovenia, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Cyprus, Malta. Ended Cold War division.

Lisbon Treaty (2007)

  • Reformed institutional balance
  • Strengthened Parliament's co-decision power
  • European Council President, EU Foreign Minister positions created

Session 5: Turbulent Times (2008-2020)

Financial Crisis (2008) & Eurozone Debt Crisis (2010-15)

  • Greece: Hidden debt; bailout + harsh austerity → political upheaval
  • Contagion: Ireland, Portugal, Spain at risk. Euro survival questioned.
  • Draghi (ECB): "Whatever it takes" speech (2012) stabilized markets

Rise of Euroskepticism

  • Left: sees EU as neoliberal, imposing austerity
  • Right: sees EU threatening sovereignty, immigration concerns
  • Examples: UKIP, Front National (France), Five-Star (Italy), AfD (Germany)

Brexit (2016)

UK referendum votes to leave. First major reversal of integration. Negotiated departure completed January 2020.

Migration Crisis (2015-16)

Syrian war + Afghan/Iraqi conflicts. 1+ million asylum seekers. Schengen border controls reimposed. Fueled populism.

COVID-19 (2020)

Initial discoordination but landmark €750 billion recovery fund → historic fiscal cooperation at EU level.

Session 6: The Twin Executive

European Council (Political Direction)

  • 27 heads of state/government + Commission President
  • 4x yearly meetings; presidency rotates 6 months
  • Strategic guidance, but limited legislative role

Council of the European Union (Coordination)

  • National ministers (composition varies by subject)
  • Co-legislates with Parliament
  • Uses Qualified Majority Voting (55% states, 65% population)

European Commission (Executive)

  • 27 Commissioners + President (elected by Parliament)
  • Monopoly on legislative proposals
  • Guardian of EU law; enforces against member states
  • Manages budget; represents EU internationally

"Twin Executive" Tension

Council (intergovernmental interests) vs. Commission (supranational, EU interest) constantly negotiate power balance.

Session 7: The Legislative - Parliament & Council

European Parliament Evolution

  • 1979: Direct elections began (advisory role only)
  • 1986: Co-operation procedure (gained some power)
  • 1992: Co-decision (true co-legislator with Council)
  • 2009+: Ordinary legislative procedure (Parliament = full legislator)

Parliament Today

  • 705 MEPs (directly elected 5-year terms)
  • Co-legislates ~95% of laws with Council
  • Approves EU budget
  • Elects Commission President; can censure Commission

Legislative Process (Ordinary Procedure)

  1. Commission proposes
  2. Parliament & Council read and debate
  3. Tri-logue negotiation if disagreement
  4. Final vote in both chambers

Session 8: The Judiciary - Court of Justice

CJEU Structure

  • 27 judges (one per member state) + 11 Advocates General
  • Ensures EU law supremacy and consistent interpretation

Landmark Cases: "Integration Through Law"

  • Van Gend & Loos (1963): EU law has "direct effect" - creates individual rights
  • Costa v. ENEL (1964): EU law supremacy over national law established

Contemporary Issue: Rule of Law

Poland & Hungary: Court challenges on judicial independence. EU uses budget conditionality as enforcement mechanism.

Session 9: Other Institutions & Bodies

European Central Bank

  • Manages euro, monetary policy for 20 eurozone members
  • Independent (protected from politics)
  • President: Christine Lagarde (2019-)

Court of Auditors

  • External auditor of EU budget
  • Investigates fraud; limited enforcement

EU Agencies (100+)

Specialized bodies: EMA (medicines), EFSA (food safety), Frontex (border guard), etc. Decentralized implementation.

Committee of Regions & Economic-Social Committee

Consultative bodies representing local/regional governments and stakeholder interests.

Session 10: Economic & Monetary Union

EMU Challenges

  • Single interest rate serves diverse economies (Germany ≠ Greece)
  • No federal fiscal union for redistribution
  • Labor immobility across language barriers
  • Debt crises cascade due to sovereign-bank doom loop

Eurozone Governance: Stability & Growth Pact

  • Deficit max 3% GDP; public debt max 60% GDP
  • Often violated; enforcement weak (political resistance)

Recent Reforms

  • Banking Union (2014): Unified regulation & supervision
  • European Stability Mechanism: Lender of last resort
  • Recovery Fund (2020): €750b for post-COVID common investment

Session 11: Identity, Enlargement & Migration

European Citizenship (Created Maastricht 1992)

  • Layer of identity above national citizenship
  • Rights: free movement, voting in EU elections, diplomatic protection

Enlargement Waves

  • 1973: UK, Ireland, Denmark (Western)
  • 1981-86: Greece, Spain, Portugal (post-authoritarian)
  • 2004-07: 12 Central/Eastern European + Cyprus/Malta (historic)

Copenhagen Criteria (for membership)

  • Democracy, rule of law, human rights
  • Functioning market economy
  • Capacity to implement EU law

Migration & Schengen

  • Schengen: Passport-free travel across 27 countries
  • Dublin Regulation: First country processes asylum (controversial)
  • Common Asylum System: EU-wide standards (tension: burden-sharing vs. national control)

Session 12: Foreign & Security Policy

Challenge: EU as Civilian Power

  • No EU army; relies on NATO (US-led) for military defense
  • Foreign policy fragmented (27 national interests)
  • Strength: Trade power, sanctions, development aid, norm-setting (GDPR exported globally)

CFSP Mechanism

  • High Representative (EU Foreign Minister): Federica Mogherini → Josep Borrell (2019-)
  • European External Action Service (EEAS): EU's diplomatic corps
  • Problem: Unanimity required = any member can veto

Contemporary Challenges

  • Russia: Ukraine invasion (2022) shifted EU militarization thinking fundamentally
  • China: Trade dependency vs. security concerns (Taiwan, Xinjiang)
  • US: NATO cornerstone; trade tensions; values alignment questions

Session 13: Major Policies

Common Agricultural Policy (CAP)

  • ~38% of EU budget (~€60b/year)
  • Original design: Guaranteed farmer prices → oversupply, protectionism, high food costs
  • Controversial: Favors large agribusiness; harms developing countries; environmental damage
  • Reforms: Gradual shift to direct payments + environmental conditions

Environmental Policy

  • ETS (Emissions Trading System): Carbon market; cap-and-trade
  • Waste/Nature Directives: Circular economy, biodiversity
  • European Green Deal (2019): Climate neutrality by 2050; 55% emissions cut by 2030

Green Deal Challenges

  • Massive transition cost (trillions)
  • Worker displacement (fossil fuel regions)
  • National divergence (Poland resists coal phase-out)
  • Global coordination needed (carbon leakage risk)

📝 Exam Preparation

Key Timelines (MEMORIZE)

  • 1951: ECSC founded (6 members)
  • 1957: Treaty of Rome (EEC created)
  • 1992: Maastricht Treaty (EU formed, three pillars)
  • 1999/2002: Euro introduced
  • 2004: Historic Eastern enlargement (10 countries)
  • 2008-15: Financial & debt crises
  • 2016: Brexit referendum
  • 2020: COVID-19; recovery fund

Institutions: Know Cold

  • Commission: Executive; 27 commissioners; proposes laws
  • Parliament: 705 MEPs; co-legislates; elects Commission President
  • Council: National ministers; co-legislates; uses QMV
  • European Council: Heads of state/government; political direction
  • Court of Justice: EU law supremacy; interprets treaties

Integration Theories (Apply in Exam)

  • Functionalism (Monnet): Sector-by-sector; spill-over logic
  • Intergovernmentalism: Member states remain dominant; national interest drives
  • Neofunctionalism: Includes interest groups, supranational actors, conflict/compromise

Sample Essay Questions

  1. How did the "Empty Chair Crisis" shape EU decision-making for decades?
  2. Evaluate the "twin executive" model of Commission + Council. Does it work?
  3. Why did Parliament gain so much power from 1979-2007? Was it justified?
  4. Compare the 2008 financial crisis to the 2015 migration crisis. Which was more damaging to the EU project?
  5. Is the eurozone sustainable without fiscal union? Use economic theory.
  6. Trace the rise of euroskepticism. Is it a temporary backlash or structural problem?
  7. Can EU foreign policy ever be effective when unanimity is required on major issues?
  8. Assess the Common Agricultural Policy: Should it be eliminated or reformed?
  9. How does the European Green Deal balance ambitious climate goals with member state divergence?
  10. Will the EU emerge stronger or weaker from the Ukraine crisis (2022+)?

Study Tips

  • ✅ Understand historical phases and their contexts (recovery → building → deepening → crisis)
  • ✅ Know institutions intimately: Powers, limits, interactions
  • ✅ Apply theories: When is functionalism evident? When does intergovernmentalism triumph?
  • ✅ Current events think: Link historical patterns to Brexit, COVID, Ukraine, climate
  • ✅ Balance arguments: EU as success vs. democratic deficit vs. sovereignty concerns
