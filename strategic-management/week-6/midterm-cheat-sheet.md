## Strategic Management – Midterm Cheat Sheet

**How to use:** Use Section 1 (Summary) for quick recall; Section 2 (Full notes) for definitions and application. In the exam, always **define terms**, **give one example**, and **tie back to competitive advantage or performance** when possible.

---

### Unit 0 – Course & Big Ideas

- **Strategy**: long‑term, hard‑to‑reverse decisions about how to compete for **scarce resources** (customers, talent, capital). Limited “spots” in the market → we compete for them.
- **Strategic vs tactical**:
  - **Strategic**: long‑term, shapes future, costly to undo (e.g. what to study, where to live, career change, mergers, entering a country). “No second chance” for many.
  - **Tactical**: short‑term, day‑to‑day operations (scheduling, small price changes).
- **Limits of forecasting**: future is uncertain (economy, politics, climate, health), so we imagine **scenarios**, we don’t know exact outcomes. Theory is a **tool**, not the answer.
- **Key rules**:
  - **Know yourself**: strengths, weaknesses, goals, constraints.
  - **Know your environment**: opportunities and threats (macro + industry).
- **No single “right” answer**: strategy is a **social science**; politics, power, culture and stakeholders matter. Rational analysis has limits.
- **Stakeholders** (not just shareholders): workers, community, society, environment, state/family owners → long‑term success needs more than profit. Debate: **shareholder primacy** vs **stakeholder/CSR**.

### Unit 1 – Strategy & The Art of War

- **Strategy origin**: from Greek *strategos* – the general; the art of commanding the army. Linked to **war and competition** for limited positions and resources.
- **Why “war”?**: On a battlefield, armies compete for territory and survival. In business, firms compete for **customers, talent, capital** – scarce resources. Same logic: **limited spots**, someone wins, someone loses.
- **Strategic decisions in life** (long‑term, hard to reverse):
  - What to study, career choice, where to live, partner, children → big effects, **no easy second chance**. Think 10+ years ahead.
- **Sun Tzu / “The Art of War” analogies**:
  - **Battlefield** → **market** (where competition happens).
  - **Enemy** → **competitors / obstacles** (who you are up against).
  - **General** → **leader / strategist** (who decides and executes).
  - **Terrain** → **industry structure, rules, geography** (where you compete).
- **Core lesson** (apply to any case):
  - If you **know yourself** (resources, capabilities, goals), **know the enemy** (competitors, their moves), and **know the terrain** (market, industry, regulation), your chances of success are much higher.
- **Exam tip:** In a case, ask: What is the “terrain”? Who are the “enemies”? What does the firm “know” or not know?

### Unit 2 – Objectives, Values, Performance, Competitive Advantage, SWOT

#### A. Strategy as intention

- Strategy = **where we want to go** + **how we plan to get there**, knowing reality will change.
- It is like answering “Where do you see yourself in 10 years?” but for a firm. **Long‑term** in strategy usually means **more than 10 years**.

#### B. Mission, vision, objectives, values, performance

- **Mission** = Who am I? What do I do? **Current state.** Define the business: products/services + target market(s). One simple sentence (e.g. “I produce and sell cars”).
- **Vision** = What do I want to become? **Future state**, long‑term (e.g. “Leader in electric vehicles in Europe by 2035”).
- **Strategic objectives** = Concrete **steps** to get from mission to vision (milestones, targets).
- **Objectives** (general): what the firm wants to achieve (profit, growth, survival, reputation).
- **Values**: what the firm **will / won’t** do to reach objectives (ethics, CSR, stakeholder focus). Constrain *how* you compete.
- **Performance**: always ask “**compared to what?**”
  - **Referent group**: similar firms in same industry (benchmark).
  - **History**: firm’s own past (trend).
  - **Intention**: chosen constraints (e.g. closing on Sundays; then “success” is relative to that choice).

#### C. Measuring performance – accounting vs. market

- **Accounting profitability** (mainly for non‑listed / family firms):
  - Use **ratios** instead of raw profit:
    - **ROA** = profit / total assets → **efficiency** using assets.
    - **ROE** = profit / equity → **return for shareholders**.
  - A number (e.g. 10%) only makes sense **vs industry average, main rival, or own past**. No absolute “good” number.
- **Market valuation / shareholder value** (for listed firms):
  - **Shareholder return** = (share price end − share price start) + dividends (over the period).
  - **Opportunity cost**: compare with **next best alternative** (other firms, index, or risk‑free assets). Shareholders are “satisfied” when return ≥ opportunity cost.
  - **Cost of equity** = minimum return shareholders require. Formula:
    - \( \text{Cost of Equity} = R_f + \beta \times (\text{Market Return} - R_f) \)
    - **Rf** = risk‑free rate (e.g. government bonds).
    - **β (beta)** = company’s sensitivity to market (how much the stock moves with the market).
    - **Market Return** = expected return of the market (e.g. index).
  - **Satisfaction**: shareholders are satisfied when their **return > cost of equity** (or better than industry average).

#### D. Competitive advantage – transaction view

- **Competitive advantage** appears **at each transaction** when a customer **chooses you** instead of:
  - A rival, or
  - Not buying at all.
- Built **sale by sale**; over many transactions it looks “sustained”.

#### E. Types of competitive advantage (Porter)

- **Low‑cost**: win mainly on **price**; customers are **price‑sensitive** (demand is elastic). Example: budget airlines, generic products.
- **Differentiation**: win because customers **strongly prefer** your offer (brand, quality, design, service); less price‑sensitive (inelastic). Example: luxury brands, Apple.
- **Focus**: serve a **narrow segment** (niche) instead of the whole market. Can combine with low‑cost or differentiation (e.g. low‑cost focus = budget segment only).
- **Stuck in the middle?** Porter warned: if you are neither low‑cost nor clearly differentiated, you risk the worst of both (low margins, no loyalty).

#### F. Companies as social realities, governance, and CSR

- Companies are **social organizations** with many stakeholders:
  - Shareholders (return on investment),
  - Managers (power, status, pay),
  - Workers (wages, conditions),
  - Customers (quality, low price),
  - Society (jobs, taxes, low pollution).
- **Agency problem** when managers ≠ owners:
  - Need **carrots** (stock options, performance bonuses) and **sticks** (board oversight) to align interests.
- **Externalities & CSR**:
  - **Negative externalities** (e.g. pollution) and **positive** (jobs, taxes) often not fully priced.
  - Debate: pure **shareholder primacy** vs **stakeholder/CSR/sustainability** in the face of climate change and social expectations.

#### G. SWOT “done right”

- **S/W** = **internal** (resources, capabilities). **O/T** = **external** (environment, industry, PESTEL).
- **Bad SWOT**: vague statements like “our people are our strength” or “the market is growing.” No link to **why customers choose you** or to concrete opportunities/threats.
- **Good SWOT**: **specific** strengths/weaknesses tied to **competitive advantage** (e.g. “logistics system allows 24h delivery,” “brand recognition in segment X,” “location next to university”). Opportunities and threats **concrete** (e.g. “new regulation favours renewables,” “two new entrants in 2024”).
- **Exam tip:** For any case, ask: What do we have (S/W)? What’s happening outside (O/T)? How do S and O combine to support strategy?

### Unit 3 – Environment Analysis (PESTEL, Five Forces, Clusters)

#### A. Environment of the firm

- **Environment**: external factors the firm **cannot control** but that **influence** its behaviour and results.
- Managers’ job: **detect opportunities and threats**.
- Two levels:
  - **Macro‑environment**: broad context (country/world).
  - **Industry environment**: specific business arena.

#### B. Macro‑environment – PESTEL

- **P – Political**: governments, stability, policy, taxes, trade, war/peace. *Example: change of government → new subsidies or tariffs.*
- **E – Economic**: growth, inflation, interest rates, unemployment, exchange rates. *Example: recession → less demand; strong euro → export harder.*
- **S – Social**: demographics, culture, lifestyles, education, inequality, values. *Example: ageing population → demand for health services; remote work → demand for tech.*
- **T – Technological**: R&D, new tech, digitalization, innovation speed. *Example: AI, automation → new products and displaced jobs.*
- **E – Ecological / Environmental**: climate change, resources, pollution, extreme weather. *Example: regulation on emissions → cost increase or new opportunities (green tech).*
- **L – Legal**: laws and regulations (labour, consumer, competition, environment, IP). *Example: GDPR, minimum wage, product safety.*
- PESTEL changes → new **opportunities** and **threats** → firms must **adapt**. Two levels: **macro** (country/world) and **industry** (your specific arena).

#### C. Industry environment – Porter’s Five Forces

- **Aim:** assess **industry attractiveness** (profit potential). **Strong forces** → less attractive; **weak forces** → more attractive. Always from the **firm’s perspective** (what’s good for buyers/suppliers is bad for the firm).
- **1. Rivalry among existing firms**:
  - **Higher when:** many competitors, low growth, commoditised products (low differentiation), high exit barriers, high fixed costs. Result: price wars, lower margins.
- **2. Threat of new entrants**:
  - If entry is **easy**, more firms enter → more rivalry → lower profits.
  - **Barriers to entry** (what keeps entrants out):
    - **Natural:** economies of scale, know‑how, technology, learning curve.
    - **Artificial:** licenses, regulation, tariffs, patents.
    - Capital requirements; access to distribution; strong brands; expected retaliation.
- **3. Threat of substitute products**:
  - Products/technologies that satisfy the **same need** (e.g. train vs plane; streaming vs cinema). **Disruptive technology** or **disruptive consumer behaviour** can increase this force.
- **4. Bargaining power of buyers** (customers):
  - **Higher when:** few, large buyers (concentration); low switching cost; they have full info on your costs; they can backward integrate (make it themselves). **Result:** they push down price, demand more → less attractive for you.
- **5. Bargaining power of suppliers**:
  - **Higher when:** few suppliers (concentration); you depend on their specific input (differentiation); they have info on your business; they can forward integrate (become your competitor). **Result:** they push up input prices, change terms → less attractive for you.
- **Goal (strategic):** ideally **no organising power** for either buyers or suppliers (so you keep margin and control).

#### D. Importance & limits of Five Forces

- **Performance** = **industry effect** (attractiveness) + **firm effect** (own strategy and resources). A firm can do well in an unattractive industry (strong resources) or badly in an attractive one (weak strategy).
- Not all five forces are equally relevant in every industry; focus on the **strongest** two or three.
- **Also consider:**
  - **Complements**: products/services that go with yours (e.g. software + hardware). Strong complements can increase demand for your product.
  - **Sixth force – public administration / regulators**: especially in Europe; regulation, subsidies, permits can shape industry (e.g. energy, telecoms, health).
  - **Segments and strategic groups** inside the industry (not all players face the same forces in the same way).
- **Five Forces = simplification** of reality; use it to get a **picture**, not the full truth.

#### E. Clusters (industrial districts)

- **Cluster**: **geographical agglomeration** of firms in a particular activity + suppliers + related industries + universities / research centres + support organisations (e.g. Silicon Valley, Toyota city, Italian textile districts).
- **Benefits** for firms in clusters: **talent attraction**, **entrepreneurship and start‑ups**, **technological innovation**, **access to finance and networks**. Proximity reduces transaction costs and speeds learning.

#### F. Industry definition & attractiveness

- **Define industry from the customer’s point of view**:
  - Not just “all car makers” or “all banks”, but all offers that **meet the same need/wish** in the customer’s mind (including substitutes and luxury experiences).
- **Industry attractiveness**:
  - Attractive when **opportunities > threats** → easier profit, growth, survival.
  - Unattractive when **threats > opportunities** → difficult to survive and earn good returns.
  - Manager’s job: **enter attractive industries** and avoid/exit bad ones when possible.

#### G. Climate change & Arctic/Greenland (illustrative PESTEL case)

- Climate change is **economic and strategic**, not only environmental.
- Melting Arctic / Greenland:
  - Opens **new sea routes**, changes **trade patterns**.
  - Makes **resources** (oil, gas, rare earths) more accessible.
  - Triggers **geopolitical competition** and legal disputes.
- PESTEL helps map:
  - Political (sovereignty, military), Economic (routes, costs, new opportunities), Social (local communities), Technological (ice‑breaking, extraction), Environmental (fragile ecosystem), Legal (UNCLOS, maritime law).

### Unit 4 – Resource-Based View (RBV) & Internal Resources

- **RBV** (Barney and others): strategy starts from **what the firm has inside** (resources and capabilities), not only from the external environment (PESTEL, Five Forces). **Internal view** complements **external view**.
- **Main idea**: a large share of success or failure (e.g. **70–80%**) depends on **internal** factors (management, people, resources, capabilities), not just the industry or macro environment.
- **You don’t need to be “the best”**: you need to be **better than competitors** and **sustain** that advantage over time. **Sustainable competitive advantage** = advantage that lasts because rivals cannot easily copy or substitute it.
- **Resources** = what you *have* (assets, people, brand, patents). **Capabilities** = what you *do* with them (processes, routines, coordination). Both matter for RBV.

#### VRIN (or VRIO) – when do resources sustain advantage?

- **V**aluable → helps exploit opportunities or neutralise threats (creates value for the firm).
- **R**are → few (or no) competitors have it.
- **I**nimitable → difficult or costly to copy (e.g. unique culture, tacit knowledge, long‑term relationships).
- **N**on‑substitutable → cannot be replaced by something else that does the same job.  
  **O** (VRIO) = **O**rganization: structure and processes to **exploit** the resource (otherwise V+R+I+N is wasted).
- **Only resources that meet all four (V+R+I+N)** – and are well organised (O) – support **sustainable** competitive advantage. If a resource is valuable but not rare, or rare but easy to copy, advantage is temporary.

#### Tangible vs. intangible resources

| **Aspect** | **Tangible** | **Intangible** |
| ---------- | ------------ | -------------- |
| **What** | Physical (buildings, machines, inventory) and financial (cash, investments). | Human (knowledge, skills, motivation, loyalty), technology (patents, know‑how), reputation, brand, culture. |
| **Where** | Usually in **financial statements**. | Often **not** in the books; “encapsulated in the person” or in routines. |
| **Control** | Easier to measure and control. | Harder: you don’t “own” people; when they leave, **knowledge leaves** (double loss). |

- A large part of firm value (e.g. **~70%**) can sit in **intangibles**; financial statements show only part of the story.

#### Human-based resources

- **Knowledge, motivation, behaviour, loyalty** – all “in the person”; firm does **not own** the person. They can leave.
- **Most valuable but hardest to control**: when key people leave, you lose **both** the person **and** the knowledge (**double loss**). What was in their heads is gone.
- **Retention**: culture, identity, belonging, incentives (e.g. “we are champions”), not only pay. Best workers often stay when they feel part of something (e.g. top football clubs).
- If a resource is **complementary** to others and hard to imitate, competitors cannot easily “buy” the same advantage (e.g. star player + team + culture + system = unique combination).
- **Example – football:** Real Madrid, Barcelona, Man City: players are **human resources**; rare talents (e.g. Ronaldo, Messi) are **VRIO**. Reputation (club brand) goes up or down with results and behaviour. Most of the value is **intangible** (not on the balance sheet).

#### Reputation / brand

- Use of the **brand name** can **increase or decrease** value over time (quality, scandals, consistency).
- Intangible; **not fully reflected in financial statements**. Same for human capital and many capabilities.

### Case 1 – Madonna & Lola Flores (Strategy + RBV)

#### Economic vs. social “success” (March 4)

- **Madonna**: one of the **most profitable** artists; **economic** success (revenue, profitability, global reach).
- **Lola Flores**: huge **social and cultural** contribution – working class, radio, fashion, identity; “did more than profit” for society.
- **Both** are human-based strategic resources; we can assess value and rareness in **economic and social** terms.

#### A. Four key elements of a successful strategy (Grant, 2015)

1. **Simple, consistent, long‑term goals**.  
2. **Profound understanding of the environment** (industry, culture).  
3. **Objective appraisal of resources and capabilities**.  
4. **Effective implementation** (organization, choices, actions).

#### B. Madonna

- **Goals**:
  - Clear, long‑term aim: become **“Queen of Pop”** and a global cultural icon.
- **Environment understanding**:
  - Learned in New York that talent is not enough; industry is **intensely competitive** and **volatile**.
  - Saw need for **differentiation and reinvention**.
- **Resources & capabilities**:
  - Discipline, charisma, strong work ethic.
  - Ability to **reinvent personas and styles**.
  - Strong, controlled **personal brand**.
  - Network of top **choreographers, designers, producers**.
  - Instinct for **cultural trends**.
- **Implementation**:
  - Negotiated **autonomy** over artistic direction.
  - Used **controversy and media** to stay visible.
  - Built teams to execute each new phase.

#### C. Lola Flores

- **Goals**:
  - Determined to succeed from modest beginnings; long career as a **central Spanish cultural figure**.
- **Environment understanding**:
  - Aware of economic hardship and limited roles for women.
  - Differentiated in a crowded entertainment market by **blending flamenco with copla and popular theatre**.
- **Resources & capabilities**:
  - Extraordinary **charisma and stage presence**.
  - Distinct identity as **“La Faraona”** rooted in gipsy culture.
  - Hybrid artistic style appealing to a broad public.
  - **Family artistic dynasty** – “The Flores Clan”.
- **Implementation**:
  - Expanded into **film, radio, television**.
  - Built a strong **media presence** and used humour and authenticity.
  - Integrated family into performances to reinforce the brand.

#### D. RBV / VRIO – examples

- **Madonna – examples of VRIO resources**:
  - Strategic **reinvention ability** (valuable, rare, hard to copy, supported by her team and contracts).
  - Strong, global **brand** and image control.
  - High‑level **creative network** (choreographers, producers, designers).
  - **Instinct for trends** + disciplined work ethic.

- **Lola Flores – examples of VRIO resources**:
  - **Charisma + emotional connection** with audiences.
  - Cultural identity **“La Faraona”**, linked to gipsy roots.
  - **Fusion style** (flamenco + copla + popular music + theatre).
  - **Family clan** continuing and amplifying her brand.

**Exam tip – Apply Grant’s four elements to any case:** (1) What are the **goals**? (2) How well does the firm **understand the environment**? (3) What **resources and capabilities** does it have (and are they VRIO)? (4) How **effective** is **implementation** (organisation, execution)?

### Common question angles

- **“Compare internal and external analysis.”** External = PESTEL + Five Forces (opportunities, threats, industry attractiveness). Internal = RBV, resources, capabilities, VRIO (strengths, weaknesses). Both needed for strategy.
- **“Why might a firm have competitive advantage in an unattractive industry?”** Strong **internal** resources and capabilities (VRIO) can outweigh a bad industry. Example: niche player with unique know‑how.
- **“Why do financial statements underestimate firm value?”** Many key resources are **intangible** (human capital, reputation, culture) and not fully reflected; ~70% of value can sit in intangibles.
- **“Apply Grant’s four elements to [case].”** Goals → environment understanding → resources/capabilities (VRIO?) → implementation.
