## Formal outline (Strategic Management, Week 10)

I. Guiding class question
   A. Public interest and corporate growth.
      1. Why does society care when firms grow, merge, or concentrate market power?
      2. Why do public administrations review or intervene in those processes?
   B. Three-basis framing used in discussion.
      1. Economic effects.
      2. Social and employment effects.

II. Why society reacts to concentration and scale
   A. Potential positive effects.
      1. More production and broader market reach.
      2. Possible efficiency gains and lower operating costs.
      3. Tax base and supplier demand can rise if growth is healthy.
   B. Potential negative effects.
      1. Fewer competitors can reduce consumer choice.
      2. Pricing power can move against customers and smaller suppliers.
      3. Post-merger restructuring can destroy jobs in overlapping units.

III. Why administrations intervene
   A. Competition and market structure.
      1. Authorities evaluate concentration risk before approval.
      2. Deals can be limited, conditioned, or blocked.
   B. Broader public-value logic.
      1. Employment stability and regional impact matter politically.
      2. Strategic sectors receive stricter review.
   C. Regulatory geography.
      1. Rules differ by country and region.
      2. Tax and competition regimes shape how expansion is executed.

IV. Domestic champions versus open competition
   A. Strategic-policy tension.
      1. Protect domestic control in key sectors.
      2. Avoid overprotection that weakens innovation and productivity.
   B. International pressure.
      1. Firms need scale to compete in globalized markets.
      2. Europe-level and cross-border dynamics push consolidation logic.
   C. Long-run risk if balance fails.
      1. Too much concentration can hurt employment and taxes over time.
      2. Too little scale can leave domestic firms uncompetitive globally.

V. Case-thread in class discussion, supermarket strategy in Spain
   A. Market power proxy.
      1. Market share is a useful first indicator of competitive strength.
      2. Profitability remains the deeper performance test.
   B. Cost leadership clarification.
      1. Everyday low price is not the same as temporary promotions.
      2. Temporary discounts can drive volume without defining strategic position.
   C. Resource and capability basis.
      1. Integrated logistics and purchasing systems.
      2. Private-label quality consistency.
      3. Operational discipline and customer trust routines.

VI. Assignment method emphasized in class
   A. Select key resources.
      1. Choose two most relevant resources or capabilities for the case.
      2. Justify relevance to industry profitability.
   B. Evaluate with strategic criteria.
      1. Apply Unit 4 strategic analysis criteria systematically.
      2. Conclude whether each selected resource is truly strategic.
   C. Argument quality over memorization.
      1. No single universal answer in strategy.
      2. Strong economic and strategic reasoning is the grading core.

VII. Practical study reminders from discussion
   A. Build explanations with examples.
      1. Link each strategy label to observable business behavior.
      2. Distinguish resources from strategy outcomes.
   B. Keep concepts separate.
      1. Strategy, resources, and performance indicators are different levels.
      2. Use clear logic chain from resources to advantage to results.
