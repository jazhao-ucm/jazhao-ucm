## Formal outline (Strategic Management, Week 10)

I. Corporate strategy refresher
   A. Growth directions
      1. Market penetration in current business domain
      2. Diversification into new products or markets
   B. Diversification logic
      1. Enter new business spaces with new customers and competitive rules
      2. Raise complexity and execution burden versus staying focused

II. Types of diversification
   A. Related diversification
      1. Shared resources across businesses (technology, brand, channels, know-how)
      2. Potential synergies in marketing, operations, and R and D
   B. Unrelated diversification
      1. Portfolio spread without deep operational overlap
      2. Financial and risk-balancing rationale, but weaker operating synergies

III. Portfolio analysis frame
   A. BCG-style logic discussed in class
      1. Stars: growth and investment focus
      2. Cash cows: stable cash generation
      3. Question marks: uncertain future, selective investment
      4. Dogs: low attractiveness, consider harvest, divest, or exit
   B. Strategic objective
      1. Use cash from mature units to fund growth options
      2. Avoid emotional attachment to structurally weak businesses

IV. Why firms diversify
   A. Scale and scope motives
      1. Economies of scale and economies of scope
      2. Shared capabilities can lower unit cost or improve performance
   B. Market power and bargaining
      1. Better position with suppliers, channels, and customers
      2. Broader portfolio can increase resilience
   C. Risk management
      1. Reduce dependence on one business cycle
      2. Buffer shocks across sectors and geographies

V. Vertical integration versus externalization
   A. Integration rationale
      1. Control quality, timing, and supply reliability
      2. Protect critical know-how and reduce dependency risk
   B. Limits and trade-offs
      1. High capital needs and managerial complexity
      2. In some contexts, outsourcing is faster or cheaper
   C. Sector note from class
      1. Manufacturing and automotive examples used to compare control versus flexibility

VI. Innovation and protection of value
   A. Innovation pressure
      1. New technologies and customer expectations reshape value chains
      2. Firms must adapt capabilities continuously
   B. Protection concern
      1. Knowledge leakage risk when activities are externalized
      2. Integration or governance choices can protect strategic assets

VII. Restructuring decisions
   A. Trigger conditions
      1. Persistent decline, weak fit, or capital drain in a business unit
      2. Portfolio mismatch with future strategy
   B. Options
      1. Divest, close, merge, or reconfigure units
      2. Reallocate resources to higher-potential activities
   C. Discipline principle
      1. Prioritize strategic fit and long-term competitiveness over sunk-cost bias

VIII. International growth pathways (closing reminder)
   A. Internal development
      1. Build capabilities organically over time
   B. Partnerships and acquisitions
      1. Faster access to technology, markets, or scale
      2. Higher integration and execution risk
   C. No universal best route
      1. Choice depends on resources, urgency, and strategic fit

## Quick study summary

- Core theme: corporate growth choices through diversification, portfolio management, and restructuring.
- Decision lens: related versus unrelated diversification, and integration versus outsourcing.
- Managerial message: move capital toward strategic fit and future advantage, not legacy attachment.

## Reconstructed class questions and answers

I. Why do society and public opinion care about mergers and acquisitions?
   A. Employment and local impact
      1. M and A can change jobs, wages, and regional activity quickly.
      2. Plant closures or relocations create concentrated social costs.
   B. Competition and consumer welfare
      1. High concentration can reduce choice and raise prices.
      2. Market power shifts can affect suppliers and smaller firms.
   C. Strategic sectors
      1. In health, energy, defense, or infrastructure, ownership changes carry public-interest risk.

II. Why do public administrations intervene, approve, or condition M and A?
   A. Competition policy duty
      1. Authorities review deals to prevent monopoly abuse.
      2. Remedies may be required, such as divestitures or conduct limits.
   B. Protection of taxpayers and public value
      1. When subsidies, grants, or public contracts are involved, governments impose conditions.
      2. They seek accountability for jobs, investment, and continuity.
   C. National and strategic resilience
      1. States may screen foreign control in critical assets.
      2. Goal is stability, security, and long-run capability preservation.

III. Why can administrations support private restructuring at all?
   A. Spillover effects justify action
      1. Large restructurings affect unemployment, supply chains, and local demand.
      2. Public cost of collapse can exceed targeted intervention cost.
   B. Support is usually conditional
      1. Typical conditions include governance reform, investment plans, and compliance milestones.
      2. Objective is viability, not unconditional rescue.
   C. Strategic management link
      1. Firms still must choose coherent portfolio logic, resource fit, and execution discipline.
      2. Public support cannot replace sound strategy.
