\section*{\color{eublue}Unit 5 -- Competitive Strategy (Slides 1–18)}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 1/18: Unit 5. Competitive Strategy (Learning Objectives)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item To understand the key concepts of competitive advantage and strategy
  \item To analyze how the two generic competitive strategies (cost and differentiation) work
  \item To critically analyze them and explore the ``strategy clock''
  \item To understand industry evolution and explore the most suitable strategies to compete in
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Focus on: \hlpink{competitive advantage} + \hlpink{how to compete} + \hlpink{industry evolution}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 2/18: 5.1 Competitive Advantage \& Strategy (Competitive Advantage)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item Firm’s key features that distinguishes it from rivals
  \item Competitive Disadvantage; better position for competing $\rightarrow$ market success
  \item Happy end $\rightarrow$ better performance
  \item Competitive Advantage vs Competitive Parity
  \item Usually related to industry key success factors
  \item Should be substantial and sustainable over time
  \item Cost added value; customer value $\rightarrow$ margin; cost/price/perceived value
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Competitive advantage is \hlpink{substantial} and \hlpink{sustainable over time}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 3/18: 5.1 Competitive Advantage \& Strategy (Competitive Strategy)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item Offensive-defensive actions to create a defendable position in the industry and achieve better performance
  \item Competitive Strategy $\rightarrow$ Competitive Advantage $\rightarrow$ Market Success
  \item Focus: \hlpink{PRODUCT DIFF} / \hlpink{COST LEADER}
  \item Source: Porter (1980:60); Stuck in the middle!
  \item Strategic Target $\rightarrow$ Industry wide vs Single Segment
  \item Low cost / Uniqueness perception
  \item Cost Leader vs Product Diff; stuck in the middle
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{To compete: choose \hlpink{one clear strategy target} (avoid \hlpink{stuck in the middle}).}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 4/18: 5.1 Competitive Advantage \& Strategy (Diagram labels)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item Competitive Strategy $\rightarrow$ Competitive Advantage $\rightarrow$ Market Success
  \item Focus: PRODUCT DIFF / COST LEADER
  \item Industry wide / Single Segment
  \item Low cost / Uniqueness perception
  \item Stuck in the middle!
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{The diagram is explicitly \hlpink{Porter-style positioning}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 5/18: 5.2 Cost Leadership Strategy (Cost Leadership Advantage)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item COST LEADERSHIP ADVANTAGE: When the firm costs are lower than its competitors / industry average for a product/service of similar features/quality
  \item Source: Navas \& Guerras (2018:153)
  \item Competitive Advantage: cost ($<$ competitor) $\rightarrow$ margin
  \item Firms: Firm A vs Firm B (industry average)
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{If your \hlpink{costs} are below competitors for similar quality, you get the margin structure.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 6/18: 5.2 Cost Leadership Strategy (Drivers of Cost Leadership)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item Drivers of COST LEADERSHIP ADVANTAGE:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Economies of scale
    \item Learning economies: increase skills, improved organizational routines
    \item Production techniques: process innovation; re-engineering
    \item Product design: standardization, design for manufacture
    \item Input costs: access raw materials, location, bargaining power
    \item Capacity utilization: ration fixed-variable costs, lean/JIT production
    \item Residual effiency: X-inefficiency; employee’s motivation
  \end{itemize}
  \item Source: Grant (2022:154)
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Cost leadership comes from multiple levers: \hlpink{scale, learning, process, design, inputs, utilization}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 7/18: 5.2 Cost Leadership Strategy (Adequacy Conditions)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item Price-sensitive customers
  \item Price competition as a key industry factor
  \item Product standardization - commodity
  \item High bargaining power of buyers
  \item Existence of economies of scale and learning effects
  \item (Slide links these as adequacy of cost leadership advantage)
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Cost leadership is adequate when buyers are \hlpink{price sensitive} and products are \hlpink{commodity/standardized}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 8/18: 5.2 Cost Leadership Strategy (Risks)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item Constant monitoring costs
  \item Innovation can annulled experience effect
  \item Cost Inflation
  \item Changes in demand
  \item Excessive reduction product quality
  \item Competitor’s imitation / segmented competitors
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Risks: don’t let cost-cutting create \hlpink{quality loss}, and expect \hlpink{innovation/imitations}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 9/18: 5.3 Product Differentiation Strategy (Price Premium)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item PRODUCT DIFFERENTIATION ADVANTAGE
  \item When the firm offers product/service with certain attributes perceived by customers as \hlpink{``unique''} $\rightarrow$ \hlpink{``Price Premium''}
  \item Competitive Advantage is represented against cost and margin structure
  \item Source: Navas \& Guerras (2018:158)
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Differentiation is about \hlpink{perceived uniqueness} $\rightarrow$ \hlpink{price premium}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 10/18: 5.3 Product Differentiation Strategy (Drivers)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item Drivers of PRODUCT DIFFERENTIATION ADVANTAGE:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Product features: ``Uniqueness''; product features and performance; complementary services; perceived quality; location, etc.
    \item Market features: variety of customer needs and wishes; perception; intangibles (social, psychological, ...)
    \item Firm features: Ethics, Commitment to CSR-Sustainability; Signaling and Reputation; Legitimacy
    \item Other: Time (ASAP), ...
  \end{itemize}
  \item Source: Grant (2022); Navas \& Guerras (2018)
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Differentiation drivers are \hlpink{product + market perception + firm features + time}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 11/18: 5.3 Product Differentiation Strategy (Adequacy)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item Buyers give importance to \hlpink{quality}
  \item Few competitors choose the same product differentiation criterion
  \item Product features difficult to imitate
  \item Tech-based industries $\rightarrow$ Innovation key success factor
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Adequacy: quality matters, few copy you, and \hlpink{imitation is hard}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 12/18: 5.3 Product Differentiation Strategy (Risks)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item Too high ``Price Premium''
  \item Changes in demand $\rightarrow$ Cost (economic crises)
  \item Imitation by competitors $\rightarrow$ Luxury!
  \item Competitors focused on single segments
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Key differentiation risks: \hlpink{price premium too high} and \hlpink{imitation}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 13/18: 5.4 Extending Porter’s Strategies (Critical Learning)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item CRITICAL LEARNING (cost/margin structure cues)
  \item COST LEADERSHIP: Costs NOT ALWAYS $\rightarrow$ C.A.
  \item Stuck in the “middle”?
  \item There is only ONE cost leader in the horizont...
  \item Product diff $\neq$ Price Premium
  \item PRODUCT DIFFERENTIATION: Brand Products work like Luxury?
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Main reminder: \hlpink{costs are not automatically advantage}, and \hlpink{positioning matters} (avoid the middle).}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 17/18: 5.5 Life-cycle Model (Emergence/Growth/Maturity/Decline)}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 2/13: 6.1 Firm Scope and Development (Firm Development / Firm Growth)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item CHANGE FIRM’S SCOPE: DEVELOPMENT AND GROWTH
  \item FIRM DEVELOPMENT: Quantitative and qualitative changes in the firm derived of modification of its scope of activity
  \item Development Corporate Strategies: Specialization, Diversification (Related vs Unrelated), Vertical Integration, Reestructuring, Interanationalization
  \item FIRM GROWTH: Increases (decreases) in the size of firm variables such as volumen of assets, outputs, sales, profitability, etc.
  \item Method Corporate Strategies: Internal Growth, Mergers \& Acquisitions, Strategic Alliances
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Separate: \hlpink{development (scope)} vs \hlpink{growth (size)}; also list \hlpink{methods}.}

\slidebox{

  \item Set of business(es) in which the company wants to compete
  \item Addresses the Key Question: WHERE TO COMPETE?
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{The key question for corporate development: \hlpink{WHERE TO COMPETE?}}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 4/13: 6.2 Specialization \& Diversification (Specialization)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item SPECIALIZATION
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Specialization = a scope choice; it is the foundation for consolidation logic.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 5/13: 6.2 Specialization (Main Reasons)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item SPECIALIZATION: Main Reasons
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Economies of scale
    \item Try to consolidate its position in the markets and strengthen its competitive position in the industry (lower costs or more value added)
    \item Increasing sales by increasing the use of their existing products, seek out new customers, introduce certain improvements in the products, etc.
    \item Considered as low risk strategy ``always doing the same products for the same customers needs''
  \end{itemize}
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Specialization is framed as \hlpink{low risk} and driven by \hlpink{scale + consolidation}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 6/13: 6.2 Diversification (Reasons \& Risks)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item DIVERSIFICATION
  \item REASONS:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Economies of Scope (Synergies)
    \item Other reasons:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Improve the competitive position of the firm
      \item Enter attractive industries
      \item Risk reducing
      \item Obtain internal funding
      \item Exploit managerial capabilities
    \end{itemize}
    \item ``You don't put all your eggs in one basket''
  \end{itemize}
  \item RISKS:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The creation of synergies calls for great effort: coordination and commitment
    \item It may lead to greater inflexibility
    \item Diversification can have a high cost: entry barriers, high cost of internal funding, etc.
  \end{itemize}
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Diversification trades \hlpink{synergies/risk reducing} for \hlpink{coordination effort + cost/inflexibility}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 7/13: 6.3 Vertical Integration (Heading)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item Vertical (Des)Integration
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Vertical integration is the scope change inside the production process.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 8/13: 6.3 Vertical Integration (Definition, Reasons, Risks)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item A firm is vertically integrated if it undertakes several of activities related with the production process.
  \item Reasons:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Easy coordination and planning of the production process.
    \item Reduce the opportunism risk from external suppliers and clients.
    \item Increase the value added for customers: a high level of quality or services can be guaranteed.
    \item Protect information and knowledge regarding advance technology or new products.
    \item Improve firms’ capacity to control the final market.
  \end{itemize}
  \item Risks:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Augment the risk of undertaking activities for the same final market.
    \item Firms may become more inflexible.
    \item It increases costs due to administrative costs, lack of previous experience, and low production scale in each activity.
  \end{itemize}
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Vertical integration is about \hlpink{coordination + quality assurance + control}, but adds \hlpink{cost and inflexibility}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 9/13: 6.4 Reestructuring businesses (Definition \& Alternatives)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item Reestructuring
  \item The modification or dedefinition of the firm’s scope with the possible withdrawal (DIVESTMENT) from at least ONE of its businesess
  \item ALTERNATIVES:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Specialización: Focusing on Core Businesses
    \item Business Portfolio Reestructuring: Both withdrawal and entry of businesses
    \item Business Reestructuring: To re-float a poor-performance business
  \end{itemize}
  \item Source: Navas \& Guerras (2018)
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Restructuring = \hlpink{scope change} with possible \hlpink{withdrawal}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 10/13: 6.4 Reestructuring businesses (Two Basic Options)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item TWO BASIC OPTIONS
  \item REESTRUCTURING: THERE ARE POSSIBILITIES OF RE-FLOATING; MEASURES:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Change managers
    \item Redefine Competitive Strategy
    \item Sailing assets
    \item Refinancing debt
    \item Cost reduction
  \end{itemize}
  \item WITHDRAWAL: THERE IS NO SOLUTION
  \item ALTERNATIVES: SALE (best way for investment recovery), HARVEST (Max. Short-Term financial Flows), WINDING -UP (End to all operations \& remaining Assets Sales)
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{If the scope underperforms: \hlpink{re-floating options} vs \hlpink{withdrawal options}.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 11/13: 6.4 Reestructuring businesses (When expectations aren’t met)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item One (or more businesses) DOES NOT achieve FIRM EXPECTATIONS IN TERMS OF PROFITABILITY
  \item Consequences:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Poor Performance (Decreasing sales, profits)
    \item Heavy borrowing
  \end{itemize}
  \item Source: Navas \& Guerras (2018)
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{If profitability expectations fail: diagnose \hlpink{poor performance} + \hlpink{heavy borrowing} drivers.}

\subsection*{\color{eublue}Slide 12/13: 6.4 Reestructuring businesses (Causes)}
\slidebox{
\begin{itemize}
  \item Causes:

    \item New competitors
    \item Unexpected changes in demand
    \item Bad competitive strategy
    \item High internal costs
    \item Organizational intertia
    \item Etc.
  \end{itemize}
  \item Source: Navas \& Guerras (2018)
}
\end{itemize}
}
\lockin{Causes to look for: \hlpink{management}, \hlpink{competitors}, \hlpink{demand changes}, and \hlpink{strategy/internal costs}.}
