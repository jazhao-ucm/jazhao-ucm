   B. Main Driver.

   A. Example Type.

   B. Main Driver.

   A. Example Type.

   B. Main Driver.

IV. Position 4 - Differentiation.

V. Position 5 - Focused Differentiation.

VI. Position 6 - Increased Price / Standard Value (Risk Zone).
   A. Example Type.
      1. Price increase without clear value increase.
   B. Main Driver.
      1. Margin recovery attempt not supported by differentiation.

VII. Position 7 - High Price / Low Value (Non-competitive).
   A. Example Type.
      1. Expensive offer with weak quality/experience signal.
   B. Main Driver.
      1. Weak competitive position and low customer perceived value.

VIII. Position 8 - Low Value / Standard Price (Loss of Share).
   A. Example Type.
      1. Average prices but weak execution/quality perception.
   B. Main Driver.
      1. No clear cost advantage and no clear uniqueness.

## C) "Stuck in the Middle" - Contradiction Example

I. Example Chosen.
   A. Zara (class example).
      1. It is not the cheapest fashion option.
      2. It is also not pure luxury-only positioning.

II. Why It Can Look "Stuck in the Middle."
   A. Price-position tension.
      1. Mid-to-high prices vs many substitutes.
   B. Competitive pressure.
      1. Discount chains and premium specialists attack from both sides.

III. Why It Is Still Successful (Contradiction Explained).
   A. Coherent system, not random mix.
      1. "Third place" positioning + brand/community + repeatable operations.
   B. Continuous adaptation.
      1. Matches class line: competitive advantage must be renewed, not only defended.
   C. Execution depth.
      1. Product, service, digital, and store experience reinforce each other.

IV. Exam-ready Conclusion.
   A. Core answer.
      1. "Stuck in the middle" is a risk when there is no clear logic.
      2. A firm can appear in the middle on price map but still win if its value system is coherent, hard to imitate, and constantly renewed.

## D) Class Discussion Add-On (From Today Transcript)

I. Value Chain Example Mentioned In Class.
   A. Ford-style operations and logistics coordination.
      1. Operations optimization supported by automation and process speed.
      2. Inbound logistics and warehouse flow synchronized to reduce delays and waste.
      3. Real-time coordination with suppliers improves timing and inventory control.
   B. Vertical relationship logic.
      1. Tight buyer-supplier links can support quality control and continuity of production.
      2. Coordination gains must be balanced with flexibility and dependency risks.

II. Technology + Human Resources Link.
   A. Class emphasis.
      1. Technology alone is not enough; execution depends on people and routines.
      2. Training and coordination quality can become part of the advantage.

III. RBV / Sustained Advantage Reminder.
   A. Resources and capabilities can sustain advantage when they meet course criteria.
      1. Valuable.
      2. Rare.
      3. Inimitable.
      4. Non-substitutable (or organized to capture value, as discussed in class framing).
   B. Oral exam tip from class.
      1. Explicitly applying these criteria earns stronger analytical marks.

IV. Zara (Stuck-in-the-Middle Prompt) - Class-Consistent Wrap.
   A. Why it looks in the middle.
      1. Not pure low cost and not pure luxury differentiation.
   B. Why performance can still be strong.
      1. Fast design-to-store cycles and operational coordination.
      2. Coherent hybrid positioning supported by execution, not random mixing.
