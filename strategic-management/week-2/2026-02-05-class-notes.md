## Key Concepts

### I. Measuring Company Success - Two Approaches

A. Two main ways to measure economic performance

1. Accounting profitability (ROA, ROE) - Works for small/medium companies, family-owned firms
2. Market valuation / Shareholder value - Works for listed companies on stock exchanges

B. Key insight
1. "Regular companies, the economic performance... could be calculated... [in] a couple of measures... This is the only way to do that."

---

### II. Market Valuation - For Listed Companies

When it works: "For large [companies], especially in [listed] companies in stock exchange... with companies called complex... [with] thousands [of] legal owners [or] shareholders... protecting [through] shareholder value operation."

A. Calculation
1. "For [listed] companies, it's so easy, [the] calculation... because we [use] the price of a [single] share... [For example] 2025: The price of a share at the beginning of the period... [minus] the price at the end of the period... [plus] dividends... [equals] shareholder return."

B. Key point
1. Market value reflects what many investors think the company is worth, not just one manager's calculation.

---

### III. Satisfaction and Opportunity Cost

Concept: "We need to test or identify [if] the decrease or increase in the price [is enough] to satisfy shareholders... [This] is satisfaction... The [minimum] satisfaction of [shareholders] will be [the] required [return]."

Benchmark: "What [should] be the majority of [investors'] [expectation]? They keep [their] money... Who is the main [alternative]? Then you [compare]... If your [return] is higher than [that of] a competing company... I will be happy, satisfied... [This is] because of the opportunity [cost]."

A. Key insight
1. Satisfaction = compare your return to the next best alternative (opportunity cost).

---

### IV. Cost of Equity - Minimum Required Return

Definition: "The [minimum] satisfaction... will be [the] required [return]... This is [the] cost of equity."

A. Components (three elements)

1. Risk-free rate (Rf) - "The risk-free increase... usually used, for example, [for] government bonds... The [minimum] satisfaction... should be over at least... the rate of [a] risk-free investment... For example, you [invest] money for one year... If you buy a national [government] bond... the risk-free increase... This is the Rf."

2. Market risk premium - "Market [return]... [minus] the rate of [risk-free] companies [listed] in stock exchange... [For example] IBEX 35 [Spanish stock index]... [The] rising [return]... [over] the last year... [This is] the financial [market] return."

3. Beta (β) - "This number is usually about one... Values over one [require] that the company [has] more risk than the average of [companies]... For example, [a] value of 1.2... The specific rate of that [company] is 20% greater than the [market]... [A] value of 0.5... The company [risk] is 50% less than [the market]."

B. Formula
1. Cost of Equity = Rf + β × (Market Return - Rf)

C. Key point
1. Cost of equity = minimum return shareholders require to be satisfied, considering risk-free rate, market return, and company-specific risk (beta).

### V. Why Market Valuation Is Better (Four Reasons)

1. External market calculation:
2. "The price is [set by] the perfect play of the law of [supply and] demand... This is external [market] calculation... [versus] the internal [calculation]... [which] is impossible for others to [verify]."

3. Discounted future expectations:
4. "Market value is a price... [that reflects] future [expectations]... that you need to discount... But this future expectations... [are] what [many] investors [think]... [It's] better to take account the future expectations of [many] investors... [than] one [manager's] calculation."

5. Harder to manipulate:
6. "Market value is [set by many participants], not [just] one person... You with a few [basic] knowledge of finance can [verify] if the price is a fair price."

7. Forward-looking:
8. "Market price [reflects] future expectations... [It's] mathematical... [based on] what investors and financial experts [think]."

A. Key insight
1. Market valuation is external, forward-looking, harder to manipulate, and reflects consensus expectations.

### VI. Limitation - Only Works for Listed Companies

A. Reality
1. "The only disadvantage of [market] valuation... This [approach] only works... for [listed] companies... Otherwise it is almost impossible... to calculate the market value of the [company]... Because if there is no market, it is impossible."

Who can use it: "For [listed] companies... [For] small [and] medium [companies]... [they] will not [be listed]... Usually the CEO [and] owner is [the] family... They can solve [things] between them... The problem is usually with large [companies]... with many thousands or millions of [shareholders]."

B. Key point
1. Market valuation only works for publicly traded companies; most companies (80-90%) are private and must use accounting profitability.

### VII. Companies as Social Realities - Stakeholder Framework

Core idea: "The real nature of the company... All companies are... organizations... And we look at them... the only thing that we can see... [is] people... People collaborating... together... [This is the] social nature of [companies]."

Stakeholder view: "Companies should be... economic [entities]... but also [should be] created by... [and] should be [run] by all [groups] of people... [including] workers, [shareholders], customers... Of course, society."

A. Key insight
1. "This is the issue for corporate social responsibility, sustainability... [We need to] explore the [many] conflicts that arise in all companies, especially in [companies] where [shareholders] are [different from] managers."

### VIII. Stakeholder Framework - Negotiation and Power

Framework: "This is [the] stakeholder framework that we are going to [use]... [Remember], what should be the company's aim? The company's [objective] will be the result of a process of [bargaining] and negotiation between managers, [shareholders], workers, [and] society... And who [has] the power will [decide]."

Why it matters: "Because [companies are] legal fictions... They don't behave... Legal [entities] don't behave... [They are] people [who] behave... This is the reason why we need to know the social [nature] of [companies] and then talk about... beyond shareholder primacy."

A. Key point
1. Company objectives emerge from negotiation between stakeholders; power determines whose interests prevail.

### IX. Corporate Social Responsibility and Sustainability

2. Stakeholder theory - Balance interests of multiple stakeholders
3. Corporate social responsibility / Sustainability - Go beyond profit to consider social and environmental impacts

B. Key insight
1. "This is [Unit] 3 and 4... about the [variety] of the companies... [The] big [topic]... [is] corporate governance issues, and then ethics, values, and corporate social responsibility."

### X. Externalities - Positive and Negative

Definition: "Externalities... What are externalities? ... [When] you [produce]... you [create] effects... [that] you don't pay for [or] you don't receive [payment for]."

A. Negative externalities
1. Pollution - "We naturally assume that [companies] could not make any... pollution... But we don't pay for pollution... It's for free... Because we [treat] it as natural... But this is not natural... Today we have technology to measure pollution... [Companies] are responsible [for pollution]... but we don't pay for that... [We] can choose... [to] reduce pollution... Or maybe to pollute, but pay for that... [If] you [include] this pollution cost in the price [of the] product... This is one of the main market failures."

B. Positive externalities
1. Business growth - "If this [products] are sold and are [bought] by customers... customers consider that the [value] in the product is equal or higher than the price they are paying... [In] business growth... you are going to buy more [raw] materials... [You] are making money for suppliers... [You] will hire people... Employment... is a social [good]... Companies also produce... [positive effects]... that are [unpaid] for that... Positive externalities... [also named] positive impacts... [As] your revenues [grow]... you will pay more taxes... [Taxes] are [also] social [benefits]."

C. Key insight
1. Companies create effects (positive and negative) that they don't pay for or receive payment for; these are externalities.

### XI. Should Companies Consider Externalities?

[This is] ethics... in the business field."

A. Reality

[They] improve [economic results]... [But] people [also] act [in] collective [ways]... [We need to] recognize that [companies] are [social] organizations... [We] should [consider] social [and] environmental [performance]."

B. Key point
1. Companies should consider social and environmental impacts, not only economic performance.

### XII. Corporate Governance - Controlling Managers

Problem: "When managers are not the owners... you have an [agency] problem... [The] only thing [you] need [is] to put into [place]... corporate governance mechanisms... Otherwise, the company will be in [the] hands of managers... [They] only [care about] things that make [managers] happy: higher remuneration, more power, and more status... [which] many times are not [the same as] shareholder [interests]."

A. Example - Mergers & Acquisitions
1. "Mergers and acquisitions... [are] the fastest way to grow... For sure, you will appear in the news... [Your] name will be [known] in the profession... [This] implies that your power and your remuneration will be higher... Because if you [grow] the double in a week... you will have [the] double of subordinates... [and] you can manage [the] double budget."
2. "But what happened? [It's] scientifically demonstrated [that] after mergers and acquisitions, the company enters in a period [of] usually five or six years [where they] destroy [shareholder] value... Why? Because mergers and acquisitions [are] so expensive... [You] need to pay much more than the market value... [You] need to pay [a premium]... [to] convince the majority of shareholders... [Many times] the only [reason] for that merger [is] to have a new technology... [which] maybe [is worth] 5, 10, or even 25% of the total [value]... [But] you need to buy [the] whole company at that crazy price... [And] your decision [is] based [on] managers [using] money that isn't yours... [The] risk is [for] shareholders, not [for] managers."

B. Key insight
1. Managers may pursue growth (M&A) for personal benefit even when it destroys shareholder value; corporate governance is needed to control this.

### XIII. Corporate Governance Mechanisms - Carrots and Sticks

A. Two types

1. Carrots (Incentives):
2. Stock options - "Stock options... [give managers] the right to have shares... but in the future, in four or five years... [If] I have the promise to have a certain [number] of shares of my company, in five years, but at the price of today... I'm going to take a lot of decisions to increase the value of the shares... in five years... because I could have a lot of money... This is good for [all] shareholders... This is one of the best [ways] to control or to [align] managers."

3. Performance-based bonuses - "Incentives... more money based on return on assets... [or] giving shares for free to them... [so] that at least that person [has to act] as manager, but also as an owner."

4. Sticks (Control):
5. Board of directors - "A stick is [to] control management oversight... Control managers how? Through the board of directors... Who are the board of directors? People... in charge [of] shareholders... [With] the main function [of] controlling [managerial] discretion... Each decision that a manager is proposing... should be checked... and pass through the analysis of experts... Those experts are experts... in charge of [controlling] the strategic decisions by managers."

Cost: "Remember that... it's the same... to establish sticks or carrots... This is extra money that shareholders have to invest in the company to control managers."

B. Key point
1. Corporate governance uses incentives (carrots) and oversight (sticks) to align managers with shareholder interests.

### XIV. Criticisms of Corporate Social Responsibility

1. Competitive disadvantage:
2. "If you go below [legal] compliance... [CSR] implies that all corporate social responsibility [activities] are voluntary activities... assumed by the company... But if you are competing in a [competitive] environment... and you assume more CSR activities, which imply that you will have more expenses than your [competitors]... You voluntarily assume more expenses than your competitors... How [can you compete]? ... It has no sense."

A. Who decides?
1. "Who decides to carry out corporate social responsibility activities and expenses? ... Customers? No. Workers? No. Owners? No. Only [those] who manage a company are managers... In the case of [companies] where managers are not owners... [It's] quite simple as a manager... to invest a lot of money in corporate social responsibility activities, non-productive activities... Why? I say [it's] so easy to take that decision, because this money [with] no [direct] return... it's not mine. It's from my owners... This is a very good example of [an] agency problem."

2. Managerial self-interest:
3. "Why managers [especially]... love to spend a lot of money in corporate social responsibility activities? ... What is the [utility] function of managers? ... Status, remuneration, and power... Through donations to these activities... they increase their status... and their personal image... And it's for free [to them]... Many times corporate [social responsibility]... [is] so linked to marketing and public relations units... [with] the only purpose [to] increase or to improve the company [image]... [For example] if I am a very important company... I try to [show] that... [I] put money in activities... [to] quiet the local community [affected] by [company] activities... I'm going to give [to] a hospital... [or] a school... [People] are happy... [This is] against [these] specific activities."

B. Key insight
1. CSR can be used by managers for personal benefit (status, image) at shareholders' expense, and may create competitive disadvantage.

### XV. Arguments For Corporate Social Responsibility

1. Climate change and sustainability:
2. "All of us are responsible to face or to frame the big challenge of climate change... and sustainable development... Of course, citizens [have] our roles... [as] students, [as] teachers, [as] voters... [as] consumers... [When] you buy things... [as] shareholders... [when] choosing the company [that has] better behavior... [When] you invest... 3. Externalities:

[You] only have [two] choices... [Either] I'm going to reduce... my pollution... Or [I'm going to] pollute, but pay for that... [This] implies that the price mechanism... 5. World Economic Forum statement:
6. "Even the World Economic Forum... in Switzerland in 2020... [stated] that it's impossible to think about healthy businesses without taking [into] consideration social and environment... Of course, the economic nature of companies [is to] make money... But not at any price."

A. Key point
1. CSR is justified by climate change, externalities, and the need for sustainable business practices.

### XVI. Business Sustainability vs. Corporate Social Responsibility

A. Difference
1. CSR - "Corporate social responsibility... implies that [companies] take [into account] stakeholders... but also [a] set of stakeholders... [They] invest money in [other] types of activities... on [a] voluntary basis... to satisfy [stakeholders], not for making money... [to] cover social or [environmental] necessities... [on a] voluntary basis... for free."

2. Business Sustainability - "Business sustainability... [means] to change your strategy, but your DNA... Why? Because the only way to do things... in favor of social [and environmental] issues... you need to change your technology, your production process, your products... in order to minimize the negative [impacts], your pollution... or maybe to increase [positive impacts]... The only [way] is to change your technology, to change your products... and to change the way in which you understand your business... which implies that you need to change everything, everything."

B. Key insight
1. Sustainability requires fundamental change in how business operates; CSR can be superficial marketing.

## Key Takeaways

## Important Examples from Class

A. Market valuation calculation
1. Share price at end - Share price at start + Dividends = Shareholder return
2. Compare to cost of equity or competitor returns for satisfaction

B. Cost of equity components
1. Rf (risk-free rate) - e.g. government bonds
2. Market return - e.g. IBEX 35 average return
3. Beta (β) - Company risk vs. market (1.2 = 20% riskier; 0.5 = 50% less risky)

C. M&A agency problem
1. Manager wants to grow (status, power, remuneration)
2. Pays premium (e.g. 125M for company worth 100M) to acquire competitor
3. Uses shareholders' money; risk is shareholders', not manager's
4. Often destroys value for 5-6 years after acquisition
5. But manager benefits from growth

D. Ecoalf example
1. Spanish fashion company
2. Materials from plastics collected by 10,000+ Mediterranean fishermen
3. Purpose: make money AND clean the Mediterranean
4. "Emotional wealth" - take from nature, give back to nature

E. CSR agency problem
1. Manager spends company money on CSR (donations, hospitals, schools)
2. Increases manager's status and personal image
3. Uses shareholders' money; no direct return to shareholders
4. "It's for free [to the manager]"
