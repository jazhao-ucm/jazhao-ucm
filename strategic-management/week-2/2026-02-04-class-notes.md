## Key Concepts

### I. Mission, Vision, and Objectives – Why They Matter

Core idea: "It is absolutely necessary to have a [clear] chance to be successful... To have so clear what I want... Objectives and values."

A. **Strategic point of view:** Companies (and people) need to state
1. Mission – Who am I? What do I do? (current state)
2. Vision – What do I want to become? (future state, long-term)
3. Strategic objectives – Concrete steps to get from mission to vision

B. Key insight
1. Clarity on mission, vision, and objectives is the starting point for strategy.

---

### II. Mission – Who Am I? (Current State)

A. For people
1. "In the current times... we can talk about our [situation]"
2. Example (instructor): "I am [at the] university... I have two main [tasks]: research and teaching. And in that order. Because 70% of time I spend in doing research, less than 30% [teaching]."

B. For companies
1. Mission = define "in what business or businesses I compete"
2. Definition of business (two dimensions):
   a. **Set of products or services** that the company produces and commercializes
   b. **Target market(s)**
3. "The set of products and the market define... one of the main things that the CEO has to [define]... What [is] our business or [businesses] in which my company wants to [compete]?"

C. Keep it simple
1. "I produce and sell cars, regular cars."
2. "I produce mineral water."
3. Personal: "I study computer science" (maybe mention university)
4. "Many of you are not sure... what you do? Because [there is a] difference between to attend classes and to study... Is simple, but it's so clear."

D. Key point
1. Mission answers: What do you do? What business are you in? In a simple sentence.

---

### III. Vision – What Do I Want to Be? (Future State)

Time horizon: "Strategically... these objectives should be projected in the future... Long term usually in strategy is more than 10 years."

A. Vision = aspirational
1. "What will we be? What should we do in the future? In 10, 20 years?"
2. "It's your second question... If you want a small chance of [reaching] your future [vision]... you need [to define it]."

B. Characteristics
1. Success – "Should be successful, should be [worthy]"
2. Aspirational – "I want to be the best company in the market, the number one in the world"
3. Often not fully achievable – e.g. "All people on Earth can have a [Coca-Cola] in less than [two minutes]? It is impossible. But this is an ideal, [an] inspiration."

Personal example: Gap between "now you are studying... first or second year of your [degree]" and "your [vision] in 20 years" – "It's so difficult to approach in a single step."

C. Key insight
1. Vision is long-term (10–20 years), aspirational, and guides where you are heading.

---

### IV. Strategic Objectives – Steps from Mission to Vision

Role: "The gap between the [mission] and the [vision]... is a couple of... steps that you need to follow to reach the future."

A. Strategic objectives
1. Time frame: "Six months, a year, a year and a half"
2. Step by step – concrete actions to move toward the vision
3. Measurable – "Targets... Numbers, ratios, percentages"
4. Concrete – e.g. "In the next year, in the next five years... I targeted... Numbers."

Example (personal): "When will you be [done with] the [degree]? ... Today [you study] nine hours a day... [Objectives might be:] three [years], four [years]."

B. Key point
1. Strategic objectives are the measurable, time-bound steps that bridge mission (today) and vision (future).

---

### V. Each Company (and Person) Is Different

A. Mission, vision, and objectives are unique
1. "The mission of each of you is totally different, because you are you."
2. "The same for companies. Each company is going to [have] a specific [set of] products, a specific [set of] markets... to understand the business, philosophy, values... in a unique way."

Implication: "This first way of [establishing] companies' objectives... implies that each company's mission [and] business objectives are for sure different, even [between] two companies [that] are so similar... They are different."

B. Key insight
1. There is no one-size-fits-all mission or vision; they depend on identity and context.

---

### VI. Economic Nature of Companies – Making Money

First approach (economic): "Considering [the] economic [nature]... What do [companies] make? Money. Profits."

A. Why "profit" is problematic
1. "I don't like [accounting] profit... Because it's relative. It's a process. It can be easily manipulated by managers."
2. "Depending on the [accounting] rules that you apply... Even [in] the same company, if I [apply] one [set of] rules and you [apply] another... For sure the final result will be different."
3. "There are 30 [or more] issues that [you can] change... Because they are highly [manipulable]."

Better focus: "I will refer... to the concept of accounting profit... The purpose is make money... You end up with the [concept of] profitability."

B. Components of profit (reminder)
1. Revenues − expenses = gross profit
2. − taxes, − interest on debt = net income (for shareholders)

C. Key point
1. Accounting profit is necessary but manipulable; we need clearer ways to define and measure "success."

---

### VII. Why Relative Measures? Profitability in Ratios

A. Problem with absolute profit
1. Company A: net profit 1 million dollars
2. Company B: net profit 1.5 million dollars
3. "Who performed better?" → "We don't know. It depends."
4. Why: "Performance should be taken into account according to [the] resources... people, assets, investments."

B. Example
1. Company A: 1M profit, 100 workers → 10,000 per worker
2. Company B: 1.5M profit, 200 workers → 7,500 per worker
3. "According to this [ratio], based on [productivity] of [labor]... Company A performs better."

Conclusion: "That is a reason why we [use] profitability in relative terms... We need to [use] ratios... In order to make [comparisons] that are [meaningful]."

C. Key insight
1. Success and performance must be assessed in relative terms (per unit of resource), not only in absolute profit.

---

### VIII. Two Main Ratios – ROA and ROE

A. Return on Assets (ROA)
1. "Profit / total assets"
2. "From each 100 [euros] that I have invested [in assets], the company [returns] to me [X]"
3. Measures how efficiently the company uses its assets

B. Return on Equity (ROE)
1. "Return on equity... from the point of view of shareholders"
2. "From each 100 [euros] that [shareholders] have invested... the company [returns] to me [X]"
3. Directly relevant for owners

Why use ratios: "That way you can compare two companies from different countries, different sizes, different activities... We can say, Company A from industry X, ROA 10%. Company B from industry Y, ROA 8%. In that case we can say that the best performer is Company A."

C. Key point
1. Ratios allow comparison across companies, industries, and sizes; absolute profit does not.

---

### IX. Context Matters – Benchmarking

A. "10% means nothing by itself"
1. "10% is more than 9% and less than 11%, right? And this is a good performer? ... The answer is, I have no idea. I need more information."

B. Ways to interpret
1. Industry: "If the company works in industry X, and [the] industry average is 8%, you can say this company is achieving competitive advantage... 10% is 2 points higher than the industry average."
2. Competitor: "If Company A is performing 10% and Company B [main competitor] is performing 7%... Company A is [better] for the owners... This 10% is a good performer... The company has a competitive advantage over its main rival."

C. Key insight
1. "Remember, context matters... The only way [to] approach... success [is] to compare... situation, history, circumstances [against a] benchmark."

---

### X. Who Calculates Profitability? Information Asymmetry

In practice: "In the end, the worker [does the work]... The final decision for [accounts], for taxes, and for the general assembly of shareholders [rests with] the person in charge of the [company]."

A. Who has the power to state the number?
1. "There is only one person in the company with the power [over] finance" – the CFO (Chief Financial Officer).
2. "Top managers... Someone in the C-suite... The CFO."

B. Agency problem
1. "If I [the CFO] have [a] bonus [that says] at the end of the year I will have a bonus of half a million dollars [if we hit the target]... Do you think that company is going to achieve 10% or [something else]? ... Even [by] lying... Because I have the faculty for the final calculation. Who knows?"
2. "Which implies that the [numbers], the [figures]... [can be] highly [manipulable]."

Link to corporate governance: "This is the core issue... for corporate governance... [We need] different corporate governance mechanisms to control managers."

C. Key point
1. Managers (especially CFO) have information and discretion; profit figures can be influenced by their incentives (remuneration, status, power).

---

### XI. Companies as Legal Fictions vs. Social Realities

Legal view: "Companies are legal fictions... They are a very good [invention] of [law]... to avoid responsibilities."

Social view: "From [a] strategic point of view... companies, in the end, are social realities... Because companies are organizations, and organizations are [a] set of people working together, carrying out [activities]... in that case economic [and] commercial [activities]."

Implication: "We [should] understand [companies] as complex social realities of people... with individual motivation and behavior... [and] people with other people, acting in collective ways, as workers, managers, [shareholders], customers... They will have different motivations and different behaviors because their utility functions are different."

A. Key insight
1. To understand strategy and objectives, treat companies as social organizations with multiple stakeholders, not only as legal entities.

---

### XII. Stakeholder Utility Functions (Recap)

Shareholders: Maximize return on investment → profit for owners.

Managers: "Power, status, and remuneration" – not the same as profit maximization.

Workers: "Higher remuneration and better working conditions" – not the same as managers or owners.

Customers: "Higher quality [goods] and services at [the] lowest price."

A. Reality
1. "We have no resources to satisfy everyone. We have to choose... There will be a lot of [tension]... Because [the] utility function of customers is different from [that of] managers, [different] from [that of] owners, and different from workers."

B. Key point
1. Different stakeholders want different things; strategy and "success" are the result of negotiation and power, not only profit.

---

### XIII. Who Governs? Objectives Depend on Power

A. If the company is governed by owners (shareholders)
1. "The objective will be making money for [the] legal owners."
2. Focus on profitability, shareholder returns.

B. If the company is governed by managers (e.g. CEO, top team)
1. "The objective will [tend to be] growth, diversification, internationalization."
2. "Because with company growth, managers will have more power, more remuneration, and more status in the profession."

C. If the company is governed by workers/unions
1. "In Spain, France, Germany... there are a lot of companies [in the] hands of [unions]."
2. Example: "This university... is mainly governed by professors... Professors want... more remuneration, better working conditions, and more power."
3. "Is a university a profitable organization? I don't care... My purpose is to have better working conditions... for example, to minimize the number of hours I teach a week... I only have four teaching hours [a] week – good for me. And for the [rest of the] university? I don't know."
4. "If companies are [governed] by unions, by workers... profitability, efficiency, utilization of capacity [may] go [down]... The only [measure] to [save] this university would be to fire [some] people. No, it is impossible."

D. Key insight
1. "The final objective of the company is a process of negotiation and bargaining between the different stakeholders... The result [depends on] power... If the company is well governed by owners... the [main] objective will be making money for [owners]. If [governed] by managers... [objectives will be] growth, diversification, internationalization."

---

### XIV. Corporate Governance – Controlling Managers

Problem: "When the company is in the hands of managers, especially the CEO, without control... the only purpose of the company will be growth... [which does] not [always mean] profitability."

Question: "How can we assume that the only purpose of the company is make money for its legal owners, [its] shareholders?"

Answer: "Establish different corporate governance mechanisms to control managers."

A. Examples of mechanisms
1. Board of directors
2. Audits
3. Incentive design (align manager goals with owner goals)

B. Key point
1. Corporate governance exists to protect owners when managers have discretion and different incentives.

---

### XV. Corporate Social Responsibility and Sustainability

Beyond shareholder primacy: "We have to go beyond [the] gate [of] one of the key principles of financial [capitalism]."

Course approach: "This [course] is conceived also as a critique... I want to show you the economic [and] management [orthodoxy]... And [then] criticize [it]."

Topics: Corporate governance, corporate values, corporate social responsibility (CSR), and business sustainability – implying attention to multiple stakeholders, not only shareholders.

A. Key insight
1. Strategy and objectives are also discussed from a CSR/sustainability perspective, not only profit maximization.

---

### XVI. Vision and Mission – Formal Definitions

Vision: "What is the sense of our business? Where do we want to be? It is an aspiration of the person [or organization]... This is one of the main [tasks] of the CEO."

Mission: "Who am I? ... It's a philosophical [question]... Who are we? What is my basis? What do I do?"

A. Mission usually includes
1. Products/technology – "What technology [is] embedded... What are the main [characteristics] of [the] product"
2. Market – "Who are my customers? What do my customers want? What are their [needs], wishes, [and] preferences?"

B. Key point
1. "If you [understand] very well the [nature] of your business... you have a chance to be successful."

---

### XVII. Values

Definition: "The way... The meaning... For example, for [our] company, [values might be] community, respect... And in many times [we] wish to understand [complex] behavior [through] this statement."

Form: "A formal statement... A sentence or a couple of sentences, no more."

Stakeholders in the statement: "To read in the text the different types of stakeholders mentioned [and] in what order... [For example] owners, investors [might appear] first... This is the most [strategic]."

A. Key point
1. Values express how the organization behaves and which stakeholders it prioritizes.

---

### XVIII. Defining Success – Satisfaction vs. Maximization

Personal level: "What means success for you? In 15 years, 20 years... You are going to be a successful person if what?"

A. Examples from class
1. "A house... my own house" – "This fits perfectly well with [a] Spanish [view]... We need to possess a house to be successful... If you are in your 40s [without] a house, you are a loser here."
2. "A dog"
3. "Money... How much [to] be happy?" – "This is satisfaction level... We are going to work with satisfaction instead of maximizing... Satisfaction is more real."
4. "Family... two kids, one wife"
5. "Salary... six figures"
6. "Stability... so I can [support] my family"

Strategic implication: "To reach your [vision], you need to be concrete... [Then we talk about] money... profitability, revenue growth... [or] to be famous, or whatever... [Then] we [set] a target to meet."

B. Key point
1. Success is framed as satisfaction (meeting a sufficient level) rather than mathematical maximization; it varies by person and culture.

---

### XIX. Strategic Objectives – Measurable Targets

A. Requirements
1. Concrete – e.g. "Two dogs," "I don't want to have children," "A house when I retire"
2. Measurable – "Numbers, ratios, percentages"
3. Time-bound – "In the next year, in the next five years"
4. Aligned with mission and vision – "This commitment, these strategic objectives... [may] require many changes in technology, production system, [and] activities... But you need to [set] a target."

B. Key point
1. Strategic objectives translate vision into actionable, measurable targets.

---

### XX. Criticisms of Accounting Profitability

A. Manipulation
1. Managers (especially CFO) can influence accounting rules and estimates.
2. Linked to bonuses and incentives → agency problem.

B. No consideration of risk
1. "When you [invest]... What [are the] key elements you [use] to assess an investment? ... Return... but also [you] assess the risk."
2. "Even two companies A and B in the same industry... Company A [ROA] 10%, Company B 50%. Can you say that B performs better? The answer is no. I need to assess risk... This way of measuring success according to profit doesn't take into account risk."

C. Maximization is an ideal
1. "Maximization is an ideal. It's impossible. [Use] satisfaction [instead]."

D. Key point
1. Profit and accounting ratios are useful but have limits; we need to consider risk and who calculates the numbers.

---

### XXI. Alternative: Market Valuation / Shareholder Value

Idea: "Can we find another way of measuring... company success from an economic point of view? Yes."

A. Market valuation
1. "Market valuation... [is] a measure of the value of the equity... by the market... When [shares] are [traded], the market [prices] them."
2. "When the price of the share [goes] up, we can conclude that the [company] created value... If [the price] goes down, value [was] destroyed."

Limitation: "For how many companies? For [listed] companies... This [approach] is [only] for public companies... What [about] 80%, 90% of companies [that] are not [on the] stock exchange? ... For the majority of small [and] medium [companies]... [they] will not [be listed]. Usually the CEO [and] owner is [the] family... They can solve [things] between them... The problem is usually with large [companies]... with many thousands or millions of [shareholders]. They have no information, they have no [real] political rights. In those cases... managers can manipulate many things. This is [part of] the 2008 worldwide financial crisis."

B. Key insight
1. Market value works for listed firms but not for most companies; where it applies, information and governance matter.

---

### XXII. Satisfaction and Opportunity Cost

Concept: "Satisfaction... What is the satisfaction level? ... We have to compare [returns] with [alternatives]."

A. Example (Spain – Santander vs. BBVA)
1. "I invest 5,000 [euros] in Santander or BBVA shares... At the end of the year I [check] if I am satisfied."
2. If Santander return = 8% and BBVA = 7% → "I am satisfied. My investment in Santander 8%... My [alternative] was to move [to] BBVA 7%. [8%] is more [than 7%]. I'm satisfied."
3. Same 8% from Santander, but if BBVA = 12% → "I will have a very bad day... I cannot say that Santander is destroying value [in absolute terms], but [compared to my best alternative] I am unsatisfied."

General rule: "Satisfaction is the opportunity cost in the economy... In the majority of [industries] you have two, three main [competitors]... to make [comparisons]. It's quite simple if you have the information."

B. Key point
1. Success for owners is measured against the next best alternative (opportunity cost), not only absolute profit.

---

### XXIII. Cost of Equity and Minimum Required Return

Idea: There is a "minimum level of satisfaction" for shareholders – a required return (cost of equity).

Formal side: "It could be a financial formula that tomorrow I have to show you... We are in [that] [topic]."

Intuition: "When [can] I say that I am satisfied with this 8%? If I have invested in Santander and the only alternative is to put the money in BBVA... [I need to] calculate [BBVA's return] and then [compare]."

A. Key point
1. Defining "success" for shareholders requires a benchmark (e.g. cost of equity or competitor return).

---

### XXIV. Why the Instructor Prefers Market-Based Measures

A. Reasons mentioned
1. Market value is set by many participants, not only one manager.
2. "Who determines the [price]? The market... [It's] an external [calculation]... You with a few [basic] knowledge of finance can [verify] if the price is a fair price."

Contrast: Accounting profitability is calculated internally (e.g. by CFO) and can be manipulated; market price is harder for one firm to control.

B. Key point
1. For listed companies, market valuation can complement (or challenge) accounting profitability.

## Key Takeaways

1. Mission = Who am I? What do I do? (current) – one clear sentence.
2. Vision = What do I want to be? (10–20 years, aspirational).
3. Strategic objectives = Measurable, time-bound steps (e.g. 6 months, 1 year, 5 years) from mission to vision.
4. Each company/person is different – mission, vision, and objectives are not universal.
5. Profit is relative and manipulable – need ratios (ROA, ROE) and context (industry, competitors).
6. Context matters – e.g. 10% ROA is good or bad only when compared to a benchmark.
7. Who calculates matters – CFO and managers have discretion; corporate governance is needed to protect owners.
8. Companies are social realities – multiple stakeholders with different utility functions.
9. Who governs shapes objectives – owners → profit; managers → growth; workers/unions → working conditions.
10. Success = satisfaction – compare to opportunity cost (next best alternative), not only maximization.
11. Corporate governance – mechanisms (e.g. board, audits) to align managers with owners.
12. Market valuation – alternative measure for listed firms; limited for private/family firms.

## Final Thought

> "Companies are social realities: different stakeholders have different goals. The stated objective of the company is the result of negotiation and power. Success for owners is best measured in relative terms and against the next best alternative (satisfaction and opportunity cost), not only by absolute profit."

A. Remember
1. Mission (what we do) → Vision (what we want to be) → Strategic objectives (steps and targets)
2. Use ratios and benchmarks; absolute profit is not enough
3. Corporate governance exists to control managers and protect owners
4. Satisfaction and opportunity cost are central to defining "success"
