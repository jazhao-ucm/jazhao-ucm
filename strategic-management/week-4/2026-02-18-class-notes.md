# Strategic Management - Class Notes
## February 18, 2026 - Week 4

**Focus:** External analysis – PEST, Five Forces, industry attractiveness.

---

I. PEST Analysis - Macro Environment
   A. Definition
      1. Political, Economic, Social, Technological
      2. Framework to examine macro environment
   B. Components
      1. Political factors
         a. Regulations, govt policies
         b. Political stability/instability
      2. Economic factors
         a. GDP growth, inflation, unemployment
         b. Market conditions, cycles
      3. Social factors
         a. Trends, demographics
         b. Cultural factors, preferences
      4. Technological factors
         a. Innovation, new technologies
         b. Adoption rates
   C. Purpose
      1. Detect external circumstances
      2. Identify opportunities and threats

II. Industry vs. Macro Environment
   A. Two Levels
      1. Macro = external circumstances around world
      2. Industry = direct daily interaction
   B. Key Distinction
      1. Macro = broader, affects many industries
      2. Industry = more focused, immediate impact
   C. Economic Crisis Example
      1. General impact: negative for most
      2. Fast food = opportunity
      3. Regular restaurants = threat

III. Industry Definition Complexity
   A. Challenge
      1. More complex than statistical classification
      2. Stats classify by technology
   B. Strategic Analysis Requires
      1. Customer perspective, not just technology
   C. Customer Perspective
      1. Define from customer's view
      2. Consider needs, preferences, desires
   D. Luxury Car Example
      1. Not all car producers are rivals
      2. Include all luxury producers
      3. Include substitutes (luxury travel, experiences)

IV. Identifying Competitors
   A. Strategic Challenge
      1. Rules change constantly
      2. Especially for global players
   B. Complexity
      1. Rules differ across countries
      2. Most challenging aspect of analysis
   C. Once Clear
      1. Analysis of market + players
      2. Clear picture of landscape

V. Industry Attractiveness Analysis
   A. Purpose
      1. Determine attractiveness of industry
      2. Assess survival and profitability chances
   B. Attractive Industry
      1. Opportunities >> threats
      2. Higher chance of being profitable
   C. Unattractive Industry
      1. Threats >> opportunities
      2. Difficult to survive

VI. Porter's Five Forces Model
   A. Overview
      1. Most known strategic model
      2. Michael Porter, 1980
      3. Simple, works, has value
   B. Critical Perspective
      1. Must analyze from company's POV
      2. NOT from customer's perspective
   C. Problem w/ Customer Perspective
      1. Good for customers = bad for companies
      2. Common error: customer bias
   D. Focus
      1. ~80% focuses on discovering rivals
      2. Identifying competitors = hardest part

VII. Perfect Competition vs. Monopoly
   A. Perfect Competition
      1. Many customers, many suppliers
      2. Price close to marginal cost
      3. From customer view: best (low price)
      4. From company view: worst (zero profit)
   B. Monopoly
      1. Only one company, no rivals
      2. Set prices according to capacity
      3. From company view: best (max profit)
      4. From customer view: worst (high prices)
   C. Profitability Definition
      1. Profitability = Price − Marginal Cost
      2. Attractive = large gap
      3. Perfect competition = gap ≈ zero

VIII. Industry Attractiveness Ranking
   A. Best: Monopoly
      1. Only one company
      2. Maximum profitability
   B. Second: Duopoly
      1. Two companies
      2. Can coordinate, share market
   C. Third: Triopoly
      1. Three companies
      2. Manageable coordination
   D. Fourth: Oligopoly
      1. 4-6 players
      2. CR4 ≥ 50%
      3. Can coordinate despite being rivals
   E. Worst: Perfect Competition
      1. Many players
      2. Low profitability
      3. Difficult to coordinate

IX. Five Forces - Elements
   A. Force 1: Competitive Rivalry
      1. Intensity among existing competitors
      2. Higher rivalry → lower attractiveness
   B. Force 2: New Entrants
      1. Barriers to entry prevent new competitors
      2. High barriers → higher attractiveness
   C. Force 3: Supplier Power
      1. Supplier ability to set prices
      2. High power → lower attractiveness
   D. Force 4: Buyer Power
      1. Buyer ability to negotiate
      2. High power → lower attractiveness
   E. Force 5: Substitutes
      1. Different tech meeting same needs
      2. High threat → lower attractiveness
   F. Goal
      1. Determine industry attractiveness
      2. All five forces together

X. Concentration Ratio (CR4)
   A. Definition
      1. CR4 = combined share of top 4
      2. Measures concentration
   B. Oligopoly Threshold
      1. CR4 ≥ 50% = technical oligopoly
   C. Perfect Oligopoly
      1. CR4 = 100%
      2. All sales belong to top 4
   D. Implications
      1. Fewer players → easier to coordinate
      2. Natural reasons: economies of scale

XI. Industry Life Cycle
   A. Introduction Stage
      1. New industry, sales near zero
      2. Usually monopoly or few players
   B. Growth Stage
      1. Sales growing rapidly
      2. Room for all competitors
      3. Most attractive stage
   C. Maturity Stage
      1. Sales growth slows
      2. Competition intensifies
      3. Fight for market share
   D. Decline Stage
      1. Sales decrease
      2. Intense competition
      3. Some must exit

XII. Product Differentiation vs. Standardization
   A. Product Differentiation
      1. Different features, technologies
      2. Market segmented
      3. Lower rivalry
   B. Standardized Products
      1. Products basically same
      2. Competition on price
      3. Higher rivalry
   C. Car Industry Example
      1. Luxury segment: 2-4 competitors (low rivalry)
      2. Standard cars: many competitors (high rivalry)

XIII. Economies of Scale and Rivalry
   A. Definition
      1. Production ↑ → cost per unit ↓
      2. Fixed costs divided among more units
   B. Sources
      1. Marketing: campaign cost / units
      2. Purchasing power: larger qty = lower price
      3. Learning: experience improves processes
      4. Capacity utilization: efficiency
   C. Impact on Rivalry
      1. Large companies have cost advantage
      2. Incentive to produce more → more competition

XIV. Industry Concentration Examples
   A. France Electricity
      1. EDF has ~70% market share
      2. Technically monopoly, state-controlled
   B. Spain Electricity
      1. CR4 = 85% (oligopoly)
      2. Regional monopolies in some areas

XV. Barriers to Entry
   A. Natural Barriers
      1. Economies of scale (car: ~500,000 units/yr)
      2. Capital requirements
      3. Established companies have advantage
   B. Artificial Barriers
      1. Laws and regulations
      2. Technical standards
      3. Purpose: protect domestic industries

XVI. Supplier and Buyer Bargaining Power
   A. Suppliers
      1. High power → demand higher prices
      2. Decreases industry attractiveness
   B. Buyers
      1. High power → demand lower prices
      2. Decreases industry attractiveness

XVII. Substitutive Products
   A. Characteristics
      1. Different technologies
      2. Same in customers' eyes
      3. Most difficult force to analyze
   B. Electric Cars Example
      1. Same product, different technology
      2. Tesla disrupted traditional industry
   C. High-Speed Trains Example
      1. Not rivals to flights for decades
      2. Now major competitor
      3. Many flight routes disappeared

---

## Key Takeaways

1. PEST = Political, Economic, Social, Technological (macro)
2. Industry environment = more direct daily impact
3. Industry boundaries = customer perspective, not just tech
4. Five Forces = analyze from company perspective (NOT customer)
5. Perfect competition = best for customers, worst for companies
6. Monopoly = best for companies, worst for customers
7. CR4 ≥ 50% = oligopoly (attractive for companies)
8. Rivalry ↑ as industry matures; growth stage = most attractive
9. Product differentiation = lower rivalry; standardization = higher
10. Economies of scale = cost ↓ as production ↑
11. Barriers: natural (scale, capital) or artificial (regulations)
12. Substitutive products = hardest force (disruptive tech)

X. 30-Second Cold Call Version.
A. 3-Line Speaking Frame.
   1. TOPIC: STATE THE CORE QUESTION IN ONE LINE.
   2. LOGIC: GIVE 2 DRIVERS/FACTORS THAT EXPLAIN THE ANSWER.
   3. ACTION: END WITH ONE DECISION OR IMPLICATION.
B. Delivery Rule.
   1. 25-30 SECONDS MAX.
   2. NO BACKGROUND STORY; GO STRAIGHT TO CLAIM + EVIDENCE + CLOSE.

DI. Oral Answer Template (60-90 Seconds, Class Use).
A. Opening.
   1. STATE THE CORE QUESTION OR TOPIC IN ONE LINE.
   2. STATE YOUR MAIN CLAIM/THESIS IN ONE LINE.
B. Logic.
   1. GIVE 2-3 KEY FACTORS OR DRIVERS.
   2. LINK EACH FACTOR TO THE CLAIM (CAUSE -> EFFECT).
C. Action Or Implication.
   1. END WITH ONE DECISION, RECOMMENDATION, OR CONSEQUENCE.
   2. CLOSE CLEARLY WITHOUT ADDING NEW IDEAS.
D. Delivery Rule.
   1. 60-90 SECONDS.
   2. CLAIM -> EVIDENCE -> CLOSE.
