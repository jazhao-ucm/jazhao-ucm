### II. PEST Analysis - Macro Environment

**PEST** = Political, Economic, Social, Technological. Framework to examine the macro environment.

A. Definition
1. Examines external circumstances affecting the company
2. Focuses on factors that actually or potentially impact the business
3. Identifies opportunities and threats

B. Components
1. Political factors
   a. Political issues and regulations
   b. Government policies affecting business
   c. Political stability or instability

2. Economic factors
   a. Economic conditions (GDP growth, inflation, unemployment)
   b. Market conditions
   c. Economic cycles and trends

3. Social factors
   a. Social trends and changes
   b. Demographic shifts
   c. Cultural factors
   d. Consumer preferences and behaviors

4. Technological factors
   a. Technological changes and innovations
   b. New technologies affecting the industry
   c. Technology adoption rates

C. Purpose
1. Detect all external circumstances affecting the company
2. Analyze actual and potential impact
3. Identify opportunities and threats
4. Guide strategic decision-making

D. Analysis process
1. First step: Identify external circumstances
   a. What external factors exist?
   b. Which factors affect my company?
   c. Which factors are relevant to my industry?

2. Second step: Interpret and analyze impact
   a. Actual impact vs. potential impact
   b. Positive impact = opportunities
   c. Negative impact = threats

E. Key point
1. PEST analysis helps companies understand the macro environment and its implications for business strategy.

### III. Industry Environment vs. Macro Environment

Two levels of external analysis. Both answer: *Where am I?*

A. Two levels of external analysis
1. Macro environment
   a. External circumstances around the world
   b. Political, economic, social, technological factors
   c. Affects all companies broadly

2. Industry environment
   a. Direct interaction with daily operations
   b. Includes competitors, suppliers, customers
   c. More specific and immediate impact

B. Key distinction
1. Macro environment is broader and affects many industries
2. Industry environment is more focused and directly impacts daily business operations
3. In both cases, the question is: where am I?

C. Impact varies by context
1. Depends on nature, scope, region, country, sector
2. Varies by how a company operates

D. Example - Economic crisis impact
1. General impact: Negative for most industries
2. But depends on specific industry
3. Fast food: Opportunity (people need low-cost food)
4. Regular restaurants: Threat (people reduce spending)

E. Key point
1. Both levels matter, but industry environment has more direct daily impact on company operations.

### IV. Industry Definition and Complexity

Industry definition is more complex than statistical classification. Must use **customer perspective**, not just technology.

A. The challenge of defining industry boundaries
1. More complex than simple statistical classifications
2. National statistics classify industries by technology
3. Strategic analysis requires customer perspective

B. Technology-based classification (statistical approach)
1. Companies with same technology grouped together
2. Example: Banking = all companies providing banking services
3. Example: Car industry = all companies producing cars

C. Strategic analysis requires going further
1. Statistical classification is starting point only
2. Must consider customer perspective, not just technology

D. Customer perspective approach
1. Define industry from customer's point of view
2. Consider: needs, preferences, desires
3. What products could customers buy instead?

E. Example - Luxury car manufacturer
1. Not all car producers are rivals for a luxury manufacturer
2. Must include all luxury car producers
3. Must include substitutive products (e.g., luxury travel, expensive experiences)
4. Technology may differ; customer perception matters most

F. Key insight
1. Industry boundaries depend on customer perception, not just technology or statistical classification.

### V. Identifying Competitors and Rivals

A. The strategic challenge
1. Must identify rivals and understand the competitive landscape
2. Rules change constantly, especially for global players

B. Complexity of global competition
1. Rules differ across countries (e.g., US vs. China vs. Europe)
2. Multiple sets of rules create complexity for global players

C. The difficulty
1. Identifying rivals is one of the most challenging aspects of strategic analysis
2. Requires deep understanding of customer needs and competitive dynamics

D. Once industry boundaries are clear
1. Best initial step: Analysis of market plus analysis of players
2. Provides clear picture of competitive landscape

E. Key point
1. Identifying competitors requires understanding customer perspective and competitive dynamics across different markets.

### VI. Industry Attractiveness Analysis

A. Purpose
1. Determine the attractiveness of an industry
2. Assess chances of survival and profitability
3. Identify whether opportunities outweigh threats

B. Attractive industry characteristics
1. Set of opportunities much greater than set of threats
2. Higher chance of being profitable, growing, and surviving
3. Good environment for business success

C. Unattractive industry characteristics
1. Set of threats much greater than set of opportunities
2. Difficult to survive and be profitable
3. Rough and challenging competitive environment

D. Manager's task
1. Discover which industry to compete in
2. Choose attractive industries for business operations
3. Avoid or exit unattractive industries when possible

E. Key point
1. Industry attractiveness determines the fundamental potential for business success.

### VII. Porter's Five Forces Model

**Critical:** Analyze from **company perspective** (as CEO/owner inside the industry), NOT from customer perspective. What is good for customers is bad for companies, and vice versa.

A. Overview
1. Most known strategic model
2. Created by Michael Porter in 1980
3. Simple, works, continues to have value
4. Essential tool for industry analysis

B. Critical perspective requirement
1. Must analyze from company's point of view (as CEO or owner inside the industry)
2. Not from customer's perspective

C. The problem with customer perspective
1. What is good for customers is bad for companies, and vice versa
2. Must maintain company perspective throughout analysis
3. Common error: analyzing as a customer instead of as a company

D. Model name
1. Also known as "imperfect market competition theory"
2. Explains market dynamics from company perspective

E. Focus on identifying rivals
1. ~80% of the model focuses on discovering current and potential rivals
2. Identifying competitors is the most difficult aspect of the analysis

F. Key point
1. Five Forces analyzes industry attractiveness from company perspective, not customer's
2. Most analysis focuses on identifying competitors and rivals

### VIII. Market Competition Theory - Perfect Competition vs. Monopoly

**Profitability** = Price paid by customers − Marginal cost of production. The bigger the gap, the more attractive for companies.

A. Perfect market competition
1. Characteristics
   a. Many customers
   b. Many suppliers
   c. Many players in the industry
   d. Price close to marginal cost of production

2. From customer perspective
   a. Best possible products at minimum possible price
   b. Maximum utility for consumers
   c. Optimal situation for customers

3. From company perspective
   a. Profitability close to zero
   b. Worst situation for companies

B. Monopoly
1. Characteristics
   a. Only one company in the industry - no rivals
   b. No competitors
   c. Company sets prices according to customer economic capacity
   d. Only rule: price according to economic capacity of customers

2. From company perspective
   a. Best situation for companies
   b. Maximum profitability possible
   c. Chance of being successful and making a lot of money
   d. Can charge highest prices

3. From customer perspective
   a. Worst situation for customers
   b. High prices, limited choice, no alternatives

C. Profitability definition
1. Profitability = Price paid by customers - Marginal cost of production
2. Difference between price paid by customers and marginal cost
3. In attractive industry: Difference between price and cost is high
4. This is attractive for companies belonging to that industry
5. In perfect competition: Price ≈ Marginal cost, so profitability ≈ zero
6. When marginal cost equals price, difference is almost zero

D. Key insight
1. Perfect competition: best for customers, worst for companies
2. Monopoly: best for companies, worst for customers
3. Common error in Five Forces analysis: customer bias instead of company perspective

### IX. Industry Attractiveness Ranking (From Company Perspective)

Ranking from best to worst for companies: **Monopoly** → **Duopoly** → **Triopoly** → **Oligopoly** → **Perfect competition**.

A. Best situation
1. Monopoly - only one company, no rivals
2. Maximum profitability and control

B. Second best situation
1. Duopoly - two companies
2. Can coordinate and avoid intense competition
3. Can share the market effectively

C. Third best situation
1. Triopoly - three companies
2. Still manageable coordination
3. Can share market effectively

D. Fourth best situation
1. Oligopoly - four to six players
2. CR4 (concentration ratio of top 4) ≥ 50%
3. Can coordinate and share market despite being rivals
4. Attractive for companies; customers have limited choice
5. Limited ability to switch suppliers (price, quality, value similar across players)

E. Worst situation
1. Perfect competition - many players
2. Price competition intense
3. Low profitability
4. Difficult to coordinate

F. Key point
1. Fewer competitors = more attractive industry; more competitors = less attractive
2. Model also known as "imperfect competition theory"

### X. Five Forces Model - The Five Forces

~80% of the model focuses on identifying rivals (current, new entrants, substitutes). All five forces determine industry attractiveness.

A. Force 1: Degree of current competitive rivalry
1. Intensity of competition among existing competitors
2. Higher rivalry decreases industry attractiveness
3. Lower rivalry increases industry attractiveness

B. Force 2: Threat of new entrants
1. Barriers to entry prevent new competitors
2. High barriers increase industry attractiveness
3. Low barriers decrease industry attractiveness

C. Force 3: Bargaining power of suppliers
1. Supplier power to set prices and conditions
2. High supplier power decreases industry attractiveness
3. Low supplier power increases industry attractiveness
4. Established companies have advantages: best suppliers, distribution channels

D. Force 4: Bargaining power of buyers
1. Buyer power to negotiate prices and conditions
2. High buyer power decreases industry attractiveness
3. Low buyer power increases industry attractiveness
4. Buyers can be final consumers or B2B companies

E. Force 5: Threat of substitutive products
1. Products with different technologies meeting same customer needs
2. High threat decreases industry attractiveness
3. Low threat increases industry attractiveness
4. Most complex and dynamic force; often driven by disruptive technology

F. Goal of analysis
1. Determine whether the industry is attractive or unattractive for the company
2. All five forces collectively determine industry attractiveness

G. Company behavior in attractive industries
1. Companies have incentives to reduce competition
2. Strategies: raise barriers, maintain power over suppliers and buyers
3. Perfect competition = least attractive from company perspective

H. Key point
1. All five forces determine industry attractiveness
2. ~80% of the model focuses on identifying current and potential rivals

### XI. Factors Affecting Competitive Rivalry - Concentration Ratio (CR4)

**CR4** = Combined market share of top 4 companies. Key ratio for understanding industry concentration.

A. Definition
1. CR4 = Combined market share of top 4 companies
2. Measures industry concentration
3. CR4 ≥ 50% indicates technical oligopoly

B. Perfect oligopoly
1. CR4 = 100%
2. All sales belong to four main players
3. Maximum concentration possible

C. Real-world application
1. Many industries have 50-200 competitors
2. Top 4-5 often concentrate more than 50% of sales
3. Creates technical oligopoly structure

D. Implications
1. Fewer players = easier to establish agreements and coordinate
2. Easier to set standards and influence prices

E. Natural reasons for oligopoly
1. Economies of scale requirements
2. High barriers to entry
3. Industry characteristics favor large companies

F. Corporate strategy application
1. Mergers and acquisitions reduce number of competitors
2. Can move from 10-12 companies to 4-5
3. Expensive but investment recovers over 20-40 years
4. Creates oligopoly structure

G. Key point
1. CR4 ≥ 50% indicates oligopoly, which is attractive for companies but less attractive for customers.

### XII. Industry Life Cycle Stages

Rivalry increases as industry matures. **Growth stage** = most attractive (market expanding, room for all).

A. Introduction stage
1. New industry; sales near zero
2. High marketing and development expenses
3. Usually monopoly or few players
4. Leader and followers emerge; little direct competition yet

B. Growth stage
1. Sales growing rapidly; market expanding
2. Competition increasing but manageable
3. Room for all competitors; focus on meeting demand
4. Main challenge: capacity expansion, not market share

C. Maturity stage
1. Sales growth slows; market stabilizes
2. Competition intensifies; companies fight for market share
3. Excess capacity may force production cuts or price reductions

D. Decline stage
1. Sales decrease; market shrinking
2. Intense competition; some companies must exit

E. Competitive rivalry by stage
1. Introduction: Low rivalry (monopoly or few players)
2. Growth: Moderate rivalry (growing market)
3. Maturity: High rivalry (stable market, fighting for share)
4. Decline: Very high rivalry (shrinking market, survival mode)

F. Key insight
1. Competitive rivalry increases as industry matures and declines
2. Growth stage is most attractive (market expanding, room for all)

### XIII. Product Differentiation vs. Standardization

**Product differentiation** = lower rivalry (compete within segments). **Standardization** = higher rivalry (price war).

A. Product differentiation
1. Companies offer products with different features
2. Different technologies, branding, utilities
3. Fit specific needs and preferences
4. Market segmented into different groups
5. Two ways of competing: Low cost and product differentiation

B. Standardized products
1. Products basically the same; no distinct segments
2. Competition focuses on price
3. Low differentiation

C. Impact on competitive rivalry
1. Product differentiation: Lower rivalry
   a. Competition within segments, not across all competitors
   b. Each segment has fewer competitors
   c. Less direct head-to-head competition

2. Standardization: Higher rivalry
   a. All competitors compete against all competitors
   b. Price becomes main competitive weapon

D. Example - Car industry
1. Luxury segment: 2-4 competitors (low rivalry)
2. Standard cars: Many competitors (high rivalry)
3. In customers' eyes, standardized products are interchangeable

E. Important perspective
1. Analyze from inside the industry as a current competitor
2. Must maintain company perspective throughout

F. Key point
1. Product differentiation reduces rivalry by creating segments; standardization increases rivalry

---

### XIV. Economies of Scale and Competitive Rivalry

**Economies of scale** = as production increases, cost per unit decreases. Creates pressure to produce more → more competition → lower prices.

A. Definition of economies of scale
1. As production increases, marginal cost per unit decreases
2. Fixed costs divided among more units
3. Average cost per unit drops
4. Especially strong in manufacturing industries

B. Sources of economies of scale
1. Marketing expenses: Fixed campaign cost divided among more units (e.g., $1M for 1,000 vs. 1M units)

2. Purchasing power: Larger quantities = lower prices per unit from suppliers

3. Technology and learning: Learning curve reduces mistakes and time; experience improves processes

4. Capacity utilization: Using production capacity more efficiently; reducing idle capacity

C. Impact on competitive rivalry
1. Large companies have cost advantages
2. Incentive to produce more → more products in market → more competition → lower prices

D. Manufacturing industries
1. Strong economies of scale effects
2. Example: Car industry requires ~500,000 units/year to be profitable
3. In many industries, must be large to be profitable

E. Key point
1. Economies of scale create pressure to produce more, increasing rivalry and reducing prices

### XV. Examples of Industry Concentration

A. Electricity market in France
1. EDF has ~70% market share
2. Technically a monopoly; state-controlled

B. Electricity market in Germany
1. Top company has more than 30% market share
2. More competitive than France
3. Still concentrated

C. Electricity market in Spain
1. CR4 = 85% (oligopoly)
2. Regional monopolies in some areas (e.g., north of Spain)
3. Limited switching options; companies effectively share the market

D. Mobile phone market (1980-2005)
1. Oligopoly structure; dominated by few players

E. Key insight
1. CR4 ≥ 50% indicates technical oligopoly; attractive for companies, limits customer choice

### XVI. Barriers to Entry - Natural vs. Artificial

**Natural barriers:** economies of scale, capital requirements. **Artificial barriers:** laws, regulations, technical standards.

A. Natural barriers
1. Economies of scale (e.g., car industry: need ~500,000 units/year)
2. Capital requirements (large upfront investment)
3. Example: Car industry requires millions in investment, few years to reach scale
4. Few actors can meet these requirements

B. Artificial barriers
1. Laws and regulations (e.g., tariffs, restrictions on foreign companies)
2. Technical standards (favor established companies)
3. Purpose: protect domestic industries, reduce competition

C. Example - Class analogy
1. 50 students, only 10 pass: cooperation among students
2. 40 pass: intense competition
3. Natural barrier: students who attended all semester have advantage over late joiners
4. Same logic: established companies have advantage over new entrants

D. Key point
1. Natural barriers: economies of scale, capital requirements, experience
2. Artificial barriers: laws, regulations, technical standards
3. Companies try to maintain barriers to protect their position

---

### XVII. Bargaining Power of Suppliers and Buyers

A. Bargaining power of suppliers
1. Suppliers with power can demand higher prices and better conditions
2. High supplier power decreases industry attractiveness
3. Established companies have advantages: best suppliers, distribution channels

B. Bargaining power of buyers
1. Buyers with power can demand lower prices for same product/quality
2. High buyer power decreases industry attractiveness

C. Key point
1. Both reduce industry attractiveness from company perspective
2. Companies try to maintain power over suppliers and buyers

---

### XVIII. Substitutive Products - Detailed Analysis

**Substitutive products** = different technologies, same in customers' eyes. Most difficult force to analyze; often from disruptive technology.

A. Characteristics
1. Products with different technologies but same in customers' eyes
2. Come from very different industries
3. Most difficult force to analyze; often driven by disruptive technology

B. Example - Electric cars
1. Same product (car) but different technology (electric vs. gasoline)
2. Tesla and Chinese competition disrupted traditional car industry
3. Germany's profitable car industry now faces major challenges

C. Example - High-speed trains vs. flights
1. Trains were not rivals to flights for decades (slow travel time)
2. High-speed trains emerged as new competitor
3. Many flight routes (e.g., Madrid-Barcelona) largely disappeared

D. Key point
1. Most complex and dynamic force
2. Driven by disruptive technology and changing consumer behavior

---

### XIX. Summary - Factors Affecting Industry Attractiveness

A. Main factors from Five Forces
1. Degree of current competitive rivalry
2. Threat of new entrants
3. Bargaining power of suppliers
4. Bargaining power of buyers
5. Threat of substitutive products

B. Factors affecting competitive rivalry
1. Concentration ratio (CR4)
   a. CR4 ≥ 50% = oligopoly (more attractive)
   b. Lower CR4 = more competition (less attractive)

2. Industry life cycle stage
   a. Growth stage = lower rivalry (more attractive)
   b. Maturity/decline = higher rivalry (less attractive)

3. Product differentiation
   a. High differentiation = lower rivalry (more attractive)
   b. Low differentiation/standardization = higher rivalry (less attractive)

4. Economies of scale
   a. Strong economies of scale = pressure to compete (can reduce attractiveness)
   b. But also creates barriers to entry (can increase attractiveness)

C. Key point
1. Industry attractiveness depends on multiple factors; competitive rivalry is central but influenced by concentration, life cycle, differentiation, and economies of scale.
