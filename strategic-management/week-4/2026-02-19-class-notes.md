## Key Concepts

### I. Bargaining Power of Suppliers and Buyers (Five Forces - Continued)

A. Strategic vs. marketing perspective
1. Marketing department: analyzes customer behaviors, preferences, buying patterns
2. Strategic analysis: only cares about bargaining power
3. Same logic applies to both suppliers and buyers

B. What bargaining power means
1. Party can impose: prices, delivery conditions, quality, features
2. Higher bargaining power = bad for the company
3. Goal: no organizing power for either suppliers or buyers

C. Main factors affecting bargaining power (same for both)

1. Degree of concentration
   a. Buyers: few large buyers = high concentration = more power
   b. Suppliers: few suppliers vs. thousands = suppliers have more power
   c. Concentration increases bargaining power

2. Degree of product differentiation / integration
   a. Buyers: if we sell differentiated products → buyer power low
   b. Suppliers: if we buy raw materials specific to us → supplier power high (bad)
   c. Plays same way for both sides

3. Information
   a. If suppliers/buyers have lots of info about our technology, cost structure
   b. More information = more power to impose their desires
   c. More bargaining power = lower industry attractiveness

4. Vertical integration (upstream/downstream)
   a. To what extent can suppliers become future competitors or buyers?
   b. Suppliers can become competitors (backward integration)
   c. Higher integration = higher bargaining power = bad for company

D. Key point
1. Always analyze from company perspective inside the industry
2. What is good for consumers/buyers or suppliers is bad for the company

---

### II. Five Forces Model - Simplification of Reality

A. Managerial simplification
1. Strategic tools are simplifications of complex reality
2. Humans have limited rationality
3. Simplification is the only way to have a picture
4. Five Forces = simplification, often oversimplification

B. Purpose
1. Picture of industry environment
2. Detect opportunities (positive impacts) and threats (negative impacts)
3. Know where I am

C. Not all forces equally relevant
1. Depends on industry, country, time
2. May need 4, 3, or 5 forces depending on context
3. Can add additional force if needed (e.g., sixth force)

D. Three forces focus on rivals
1. Current competitors
2. New entrants
3. Substitutive products (different technologies, same in customers' eyes)
4. CR4 (concentration ratio) is key for understanding rivalry

---

### III. Sixth Force - Public Administration

A. When to add
1. Especially relevant in continental Europe
2. France, Spain, Italy - particularly important
3. Also relevant in Germany

B. Spain example
1. Public administration moves ~45% of GDP and economic activity
2. In many sectors, weight is even higher
3. Cannot understand industry dynamics without accounting for public administration

C. Key point
1. In some countries/industries, sixth force is critical
2. Omitting it means losing critical understanding

---

### IV. Industry Segmentation

A. When industry is heterogeneous
1. Cannot apply Five Forces to entire industry
2. Example: car industry - luxury vs. standard segments
3. Need to divide market into smaller, more homogeneous parts

B. Process
1. Segment the industry into smaller parts
2. Apply Five Forces to each segment separately
3. Different segments can have very different attractiveness

C. Strategic groups
1. Market segmentation in strategy = identifying strategic groups
2. Each group has specific competitive dynamics
3. Requires extra analysis: apply Five Forces to each strategic group

D. Key point
1. Same industry can have attractive and unattractive segments
2. Industry-level analysis may be impossible; segment-level required

---

### V. Country Attractiveness - Diamond Model

A. Alternative external analysis tool
1. Companies belong to a country; affected by country-specific circumstances
2. Diamond model by Michael Porter
3. Highly popular for country-level analysis

B. World Economic Forum
1. Davos, Switzerland - February meeting
2. Presents report on global competitiveness
3. All countries analyzed according to Diamond model
4. Purpose: discover which countries more attractive for certain businesses

C. Spain example
1. Competitive in three industries: construction, banking, tourism
2. ACS - fourth largest construction company globally, largest in Europe (Florentino Pérez)
3. Banking: four large banks, Santander one of the leaders
4. Tourism: second country after France in international tourists (~100 million)

D. Key point
1. Tool to identify countries with more opportunities for business

### VI. Clusters - Geographical Agglomeration

A. Concept
1. Marshall - industrial districts, end of 19th century
2. Physical concentration of companies in few square kilometers
3. Concentration provides additional advantages for companies in the cluster

B. Three ingredients to identify a cluster

1. Many companies (hundreds, thousands)
   a. Producing same or similar products
   b. Can see competitors across the street

2. Suppliers and distributors nearby
   a. Minimizes transportation costs
   b. Geographical proximity = opportunities

3. Universities and research centers
   a. Key ingredient for successful clusters
   b. Provide best training and technological knowledge

C. Additional advantages of clusters

1. Talent attraction
   a. Workers can move between companies easily
   b. Same location, same schools, same lifestyle

2. Innovation and creation
   a. Higher chance to create new products and succeed
   b. Better than companies outside cluster

3. Technology and knowledge
   a. Universities develop in alliance with companies
   b. Access to knowledge better than companies outside

4. Knowledge spillovers
   a. Companies so close - workers share (same school, friends, barbecues)
   b. Impossible to control - innovations spread faster
   c. Imitation increases, but also innovation and best practices

5. Economies of scope
   a. Shared resources, information
   b. Main mechanism promoting best practices across companies

D. Key point
1. Companies in clusters have advantages that same-size, same-technology companies outside do not have
2. Universities and research centers are critical to cluster success

### VII. "Who Am I?" - Internal Analysis

A. Third strategic question
1. What I want? (Mission, vision, objectives)
2. Where I am? (External analysis - opportunities, threats)
3. Who am I? (Internal analysis - strengths, weaknesses)

B. Purpose
1. Discover strengths (positive) and weaknesses (negative)
2. Strategies should be based on strengths, never on weaknesses
3. Face future with strengths; address weaknesses (maybe outsource, acquire)

C. Three main tools for internal analysis

1. Strategic reflection (functional analysis)
   a. Analyze different functional areas
   b. Detect main strengths and weaknesses
   c. Basic but useful

2. Value chain analysis (Michael Porter)
   a. Core competencies and capabilities
   b. Which activities do we perform best?
   c. Activities we transfer/outsource vs. activities we excel at

3. Resource-based view
   a. Most developed analysis
   b. List all resources and abilities company has/controls
   c. Apply filter: which are strategic, critical, source of sustainable advantage?

D. SWOT analysis
1. Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats
2. Matrix combining internal (S, W) and external (O, T)
3. Executive summary of all analysis

E. Application to personal development
1. Same logic for professional success
2. Analyze your resources and abilities
3. Take action based on strengths

### VIII. Company Complexity - Focused vs. Diversified

A. Different levels of complexity
1. Focused company: single industry, single business, specialized
2. Diversified company: multiple industries, 5-10 different businesses
3. Same size but different complexity

B. Implications
1. Diversified company = much higher complexity
2. Need different analysis approaches
3. Like doctor visit: basic questions (history, age, weight) give information about strengths/weaknesses

C. Example - corporate governance
1. Family-owned vs. listed company
2. Family: decisions may protect family reputation over best economic option
3. Different governance = different strategic behavior

---

### IX. Value Chain - Sources of Competitive Advantage

A. Two levels of analysis
1. Value chain: from raw materials to final product
2. Value system: company + suppliers + distributors + channels
3. Company is inserted in the value system

B. Two ways to identify sources of competitive advantage

1. **In activities** (primary or support)
   a. Identify activities where we perform better than rivals
   b. These are core competencies
   c. Must control, manage, and improve them

2. **In relationships between activities**
   a. Horizontal links: e.g. HR training improves operations; marketing + R&D coordination
   b. Synergies between functional areas
   c. Not just single activities, but how they work together

3. **Vertical links** (with suppliers and distributors)
   a. Coordination outside the company
   b. Example: Toyota production system (Just-in-Time, lean production)
   c. Car produced only when customer orders; suppliers and distributors physically close
   d. No inventories; production orders in real time
   e. Majority of car manufacturers only assemble; parts from suppliers
   f. Coordination minimizes time and cost; ~30% of car cost is inventory in traditional model

C. Primary vs. support activities
1. Primary: inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing, service
2. Support: firm infrastructure, HR, technology, procurement
3. Support activities enable primary activities

D. Key point
1. Focus on activities where we perform better than rivals
2. Outsource activities where we perform below industry average
3. UK/Europe manufacturing in 90s: outsourced production to Asia, kept design and final assembly

---

### X. Functional Analysis - Comparative Evaluation

A. Process
1. Classify factors by functional area (marketing, finance, HR, operations, etc.)
2. Compare with industry average or benchmark
3. Rate: very negative, negative, neutral, positive, very positive

B. Comparative logic
1. 7% ROE vs. industry 5% = positive (we are above average)
2. 7% ROE vs. industry 12% = negative (we are below average)
3. Same number, different meaning depending on benchmark

C. Use of results
1. Identifies main strengths (positive areas) and weaknesses (negative areas)
2. Triggers reflection: is the analysis right? Need extra review?
3. If different tools (functional, value chain, resource-based view) reach same conclusions → higher confidence

D. Key point
1. Triangulation: perform multiple analyses; if conclusions converge, closer to truth
2. Internal analysis of competitors is difficult (information is proprietary)

## Key Takeaways

1. Bargaining power factors: concentration, differentiation, information, vertical integration
2. Five Forces is simplification; not all forces equally relevant
3. Sixth force (public administration) critical in Europe, especially Spain
4. Industry segmentation: apply Five Forces to each segment when industry is heterogeneous
5. Diamond model: country attractiveness analysis
6. Clusters: geographical agglomeration with universities, suppliers, many companies
7. Internal analysis: strengths/weaknesses via value chain, resource-based view, SWOT
8. Company complexity varies: focused vs. diversified
9. Value chain: competitive advantage from activities, activity relationships, or vertical links (suppliers/distributors)
10. Toyota/Just-in-Time: coordination with suppliers, no inventories, real-time production
11. Functional analysis: compare with industry benchmark; triangulate with multiple tools
