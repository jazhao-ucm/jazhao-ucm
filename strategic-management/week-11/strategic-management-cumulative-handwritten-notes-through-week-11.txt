## Unit 0 and Unit 1, what strategy is

I. Core idea.
   A. Strategy means long-term choices.
   B. You cannot do everything, so you choose and sacrifice.
   C. Good strategy is not random actions.

II. Why strategy is hard.
   A. Future is uncertain.
   B. Competitors react.
   C. Internal people disagree.

III. Decision levels.
   A. Corporate strategy, where to play.
      1. Which industry.
      2. Which country.
   B. Business strategy, how to win.
      1. Low cost.
      2. Differentiation.
   C. Functional strategy, how to execute.
      1. HR.
      2. Marketing.
      3. Finance.
      4. Operations.

IV. ELI5 sentence.
   A. Strategy is like planning your life path, not just your next hour.

V. Exam sentence.
   A. A strategy is a coherent long-term set of choices under constraints, linked to execution and tradeoffs.

## Unit 2, mission, vision, objectives, governance

I. Mission versus vision.
   A. Mission, who we are now.
   B. Vision, where we want to be later.

II. Objectives.
   A. Objectives connect mission to vision.
   B. Good objective has metric and time.
   C. Example, increase share from 10 percent to 14 percent in 18 months.

III. Shareholders versus managers.
   A. Shareholders want value and return.
   B. Managers may want power, status, growth.
   C. This conflict is agency problem.

IV. Governance tools.
   A. Board control.
   B. Incentives.
   C. Monitoring and reporting.

V. Profit trap.
   A. Big profit number alone can mislead.
   B. Use relative measures like ROA and ROE.
   C. Compare with opportunity cost.

VI. ELI5 sentence.
   A. Mission is present identity, vision is future dream, objectives are the bridge steps.

VII. Exam sentence.
   A. Strategy quality improves when mission, vision, measurable objectives, and governance incentives are aligned.

## Unit 4, internal analysis, value chain, RBV, VRIO

I. Value chain.
   A. Primary activities.
      1. Inbound logistics.
      2. Operations.
      3. Outbound logistics.
      4. Marketing and sales.
      5. Service.
   B. Support activities.
      1. Firm infrastructure.
      2. HR.
      3. Technology.
      4. Procurement.

II. Why value chain matters.
   A. Shows where cost comes from.
   B. Shows where differentiation comes from.
   C. Shows weak points in execution.

III. RBV basics.
   A. Firms perform differently because resources differ.
   B. Some resources are strategic.
   C. Capabilities combine resources into results.

IV. Resource types.
   A. Tangible.
   B. Intangible.
   C. Human.
   D. Organizational.

V. VRIO test.
   A. Valuable. Means it helps you make money or avoid losing money.
   B. Rare. Only a few competitors have something similar.
   C. Inimitable. Hard for others to copy quickly (it takes time or know-how).
   D. Organized. Your company is set up to use it and capture the value.

VI. Sustainability.
   A. Hard to copy.
   B. Hard to substitute.
   C. Embedded in routines and culture.
   D. Talent is retained.

VII. ELI5 sentence.
   A. Internal analysis asks, what do we have that others cannot easily copy.

VIII. Exam sentence.
   A. Internal advantage is durable only when resources pass VRIO and are converted into value by coordinated activities.

## Full exam answer template (copy and practice)

I. First sentence.
   A. The company is following [label], based on [two facts].

II. Framework paragraph.
   A. Externally, [PESTEL/Five Forces points].
   B. Internally, [resources/capabilities/value chain points].

III. Driver paragraph.
   A. Main economic driver is [x].
   B. Main strategic driver is [y].

IV. Risk paragraph.
   A. Main risk 1 is [x], because [mechanism].
   B. Main risk 2 is [y], because [mechanism].

V. Recommendation paragraph.
   A. Recommend [strategy move].
   B. Why this move fits context.
   C. One condition for success.

VI. Final line.
   A. Therefore, this strategy is viable only if execution and risk controls match the stated assumptions.

## Deep-dive expansion (long form for handwriting)

Use this section when you want fuller detail, not short bullets.
Each subsection is written so you can copy in full paragraphs to paper.

I. Deep understanding of strategy.
   A. What strategy is really doing.
      1. Strategy is not only deciding a goal, it is deciding a path under limits.
      2. Every strategy creates winners and losers inside the firm because resources are limited.
      3. If management says yes to everything, there is no real strategy.
      4. A strong strategy always includes boundaries, what we will not do.
   B. Strategy and uncertainty.
      1. Managers choose today for a future they cannot fully predict.
      2. This is why strategic decisions involve risk and irreversible commitments.
      3. Good strategic thinking compares options, not certainties.
      4. The goal is not perfect prediction, it is robust decision quality.
   C. Why argument quality is central in this course.
      1. In many strategic cases, two labels can both look possible.
      2. The highest marks go to the answer that proves mechanism and fit.
      3. Mechanism means, if we choose X, then Y changes, so outcome Z becomes more likely.

II. Mission, vision, objectives, and governance in real case use.
   A. Mission and vision mistakes students make.
      1. Common error, writing a mission that sounds like a vague vision.
      2. Common error, writing a vision with no strategic ambition.
      3. Mission should describe present identity and role.
      4. Vision should describe desired long-run position.
   B. Objective design in exam answers.
      1. State variable, for example market share, margin, retention, cycle time.
      2. State magnitude, for example improve by 3 points.
      3. State deadline, for example in 18 months.
      4. State accountability, who owns execution.
   C. Governance and agency in plain logic.
      1. Owners care about risk-adjusted value creation.
      2. Managers can be tempted by size, visibility, and control.
      3. Therefore incentives and monitoring are strategic, not administrative details.
      4. In M and A cases, always test if deal logic serves owners or managers.

III. External analysis, from model to decision.
   A. How to write PESTEL deeply.
      1. Do not only list forces.
      2. For each force, explain business transmission channel.
      3. Example structure, inflation rises, customer purchasing power falls, premium demand weakens, firm may shift mix/pricing.
   B. Five Forces with stronger causality.
      1. Rivalry influences price discipline and marketing spend intensity.
      2. New entrant threat depends on capital, scale, regulation, and brand lock-in.
      3. Substitutes matter at need level, not product category label.
      4. Buyer power rises when alternatives are many and switching cost is low.
      5. Supplier power rises when input is specialized and alternatives are narrow.
   C. Attractive industry versus attractive firm.
      1. Industry structure shapes average returns.
      2. Firm capabilities explain variance around average.
      3. In answers, separate structural pressure from firm-specific response.
   D. Cluster advantage in practical terms.
      1. Cluster locations reduce search and coordination friction.
      2. They improve knowledge flow and talent quality matching.
      3. But cluster competition can also raise wage and retention pressure.

IV. Internal analysis, writing better RBV and value-chain answers.
   A. Value chain as diagnosis tool.
      1. Use it to find where advantage is created and where leakage happens.
      2. If differentiation claim exists, identify activity that creates that perceived value.
      3. If cost-leadership claim exists, identify process or scale driver behind lower cost.
   B. RBV in case logic.
      1. Not every resource is strategic.
      2. Strategic resource must connect to measurable market effect.
      3. Resource list alone is weak, resource-to-outcome mapping is strong.
   C. VRIO with explanatory depth.
      1. Valuable means contribution to opportunity capture or threat mitigation.
      2. Rare means competitors cannot easily access equivalent asset.
      3. Inimitable means copying requires time, complexity, or tacit transfer.
      4. Organized means governance/processes are ready to monetize asset.
   D. Sustainability check students forget.
      1. Talent can leave, so human-based advantage needs retention system.
      2. Technology can diffuse, so technical advantage needs update cycle.
      3. Brand can decay, so reputation advantage needs consistency and trust repair capability.

V. Competitive strategy, deeper use of positioning.
   A. Cost leadership with realism.
      1. Cost leadership is not cheap quality by default.
      2. It is system-level efficiency and discipline.
      3. Sustainable cost advantage often combines scale, process design, and procurement power.
   B. Differentiation with realism.
      1. Differentiation is valuable only if customer recognizes and pays for it.
      2. Internal belief in quality is not enough.
      3. Perceived value evidence can come from retention, conversion, premium acceptance, referral behavior.
   C. Strategy clock, better argumenting.
      1. Position number alone is weak.
      2. Add price level, perceived value signal, and segment behavior evidence.
      3. Explain transition path if you recommend movement across positions.
   D. Recession scenario logic.
      1. Premium-only strategy can lose traction when disposable income falls.
      2. Hybrid recommendation should preserve core brand meaning while improving affordability.
      3. State specific lever, bundle redesign, channel mix, promotional architecture, cost redesign.

VI. Corporate strategy and M and A deep use.
   A. Related versus unrelated diversification in practice.
      1. Test overlap in capabilities, channels, customer logic, brand architecture, technology base.
      2. If overlap is low and integration burden is high, classify closer to unrelated.
   B. Vertical integration decisions.
      1. Backward integration increases input control and quality consistency.
      2. Forward integration increases market access and data ownership.
      3. Both increase fixed commitment and reduce flexibility if demand shifts.
   C. M and A motive map.
      1. Economic, cost synergies, revenue synergies, capital efficiency.
      2. Strategic, speed to market, capability acquisition, competitive power.
      3. Managerial/private, prestige, control expansion, legacy behavior.
   D. M and A risk map.
      1. Valuation risk, overpaying.
      2. Integration risk, culture, systems, leadership mismatch.
      3. Execution risk, distraction from core operations.
      4. Regulatory risk, antitrust and cross-border review.
   E. M and A exam recommendation style.
      1. Support deal only with clear synergy mechanism and integration plan.
      2. If uncertainty is high, recommend phased integration or alliance alternative.

VII. Internationalization, from label to explanation.
   A. Labeling internationalization methods.
      1. Internal growth, building own operations abroad.
      2. Alliance, partnership without full ownership.
      3. Acquisition, buying existing local player.
   B. Why firms choose acquisition abroad.
      1. Faster entry.
      2. Local capability and distribution access.
      3. Existing relationships and regulatory familiarity.
   C. Why this can fail.
      1. Post-deal integration overload.
      2. Misread local demand heterogeneity.
      3. Governance conflict across headquarters and local leadership.
   D. Strong international recommendation format.
      1. Name entry mode.
      2. Explain fit with goal and capability.
      3. List top three local risks.
      4. Add mitigation sequence by time.

VIII. Case-answer engineering, how to score higher.
   A. Baseline structure.
      1. Label.
      2. Evidence.
      3. Mechanism.
      4. Driver.
      5. Risk.
      6. Recommendation.
   B. How to make evidence stronger.
      1. Use two or three case facts minimum per major claim.
      2. Mix quantitative and qualitative evidence when possible.
      3. Avoid unsupported words like clearly, obvious, always.
   C. How to make recommendations stronger.
      1. Include specific action.
      2. Include expected effect.
      3. Include timeline.
      4. Include risk control.
   D. Closing line quality.
      1. End with conditional validity, strategy works if assumptions hold and execution milestones are met.

IX. Extended phrase bank for long answers.
   A. Strategic classification.
      1. This case is best classified as [label] because the company commits resources to [fact 1] and [fact 2], which indicates [mechanism].
   B. External argument.
      1. The external environment creates a mixed opportunity-threat profile, where [force] increases [effect], requiring [response].
   C. Internal argument.
      1. The firm can support this move only if its resource base is not just valuable but organized for consistent capture.
   D. Competitive argument.
      1. The current position appears unsustainable because price and perceived value are misaligned for the target segment.
   E. Corporate argument.
      1. The proposed expansion is strategically coherent only if capability transfer and integration feasibility are demonstrated.
   F. Risk argument.
      1. The main downside risk is not the strategic idea itself but implementation failure at the organizational interface.
   G. Recommendation argument.
      1. Therefore, the preferred option is [x], implemented in phases, with milestones on [kpi 1], [kpi 2], and [kpi 3].

X. Deep practice routine for 3 to 4 weeks.
   A. Daily writing block.
      1. Copy one deep-dive subsection.
      2. Write one mini-case answer from memory.
      3. Self-check with rubric, label clarity, evidence quality, mechanism clarity, risk realism.
   B. Weekly integration block.
      1. Build one full case response under timed conditions.
      2. Rewrite only weak sections.
      3. Build one-page summary for each unit after deep copy.
   C. Final-week simulation.
      1. Use printed notes only.
      2. Simulate two short business cases back to back.
      3. Grade yourself on argument strength, not just terminology.
