## Formal outline (Strategic Management, Week 11)

I. Strategy workshop format and applied analysis focus.
   A. Class dynamic.
      1. Team-based paper review and oral defense.
      2. Instructor feedback on argument clarity and evidence quality.
   B. Analytical expectation.
      1. Not only final answer, also reasoning path and indicator logic.
      2. Use comparable metrics, explicit assumptions, and decision criteria.

II. Market share interpretation and limits.
   A. Basic interpretation.
      1. Higher share can signal competitive strength.
      2. Share alone does not prove long-run superiority.
   B. Required companion indicators.
      1. Profitability and cash-generation stability.
      2. Market growth trajectory and competitive volatility.
   C. Time-window caution.
      1. Short-period changes can mislead.
      2. Multi-period trend check is required before strategic conclusion.

III. Positioning tradeoffs, price versus perceived value.
   A. Core strategic question.
      1. Position by low price, premium differentiation, or focused niche.
      2. Position choice must fit customer sensitivity and category norms.
   B. Perception mechanics.
      1. Higher price can increase perceived value in selected categories.
      2. In highly competitive markets, unjustified price premium destroys demand.
   C. Strategic consistency.
      1. Positioning must align with value proposition and delivery system.
      2. Contradictory signals weaken credibility and brand meaning.

IV. Strategic groups and relative position mapping.
   A. Group logic.
      1. Firms cluster by similar strategic choices.
      2. Relative advantage depends on position against direct group rivals.
   B. Comparative diagnosis.
      1. Evaluate distance from price center and differentiation center.
      2. Detect overextension and under-differentiation risk.

V. Service-industry strategy, perception and intangibility.
   A. Service-specific challenge.
      1. Service quality is harder to evaluate ex ante than product quality.
      2. Buyer decision relies more on signals than direct inspection.
   B. Key signals.
      1. Brand, reputation, and prior customer experience.
      2. Trust cues and reference effects.
   C. Strategic implication.
      1. Service firms compete heavily through perceived reliability.
      2. Reputation is a critical strategic asset, not only communication output.

VI. Resource-based logic in service contexts.
   A. Resource focus.
      1. Brand equity and reputation as scarce strategic resources.
      2. Customer loyalty and referral dynamics as capability outcomes.
   B. Durability question.
      1. Advantage must be sustained over time, not one-cycle success.
      2. If competitors can imitate quickly, advantage erodes.
   C. Protection and appropriation.
      1. Value creation, value delivery, and value appropriation must stay linked.
      2. If appropriation fails, strategic success is not financially captured.

VII. Strategic diagnosis discipline.
   A. Distinguish data layers.
      1. Objective indicators, market share, growth, margin, cash.
      2. Subjective indicators, customer perception, preference, and trust.
   B. Integrative judgment.
      1. Strong strategy combines objective performance with favorable perception.
      2. Overreliance on one layer leads to biased decisions.

VIII. Managerial takeaways from in-class critiques.
   A. Analytical rigor.
      1. Define terms before using them in argument.
      2. Support each claim with explicit metric relevance.
   B. Strategic communication.
      1. Explain why a position is superior, not only that it is superior.
      2. Show how recommendations hold under realistic competitive response.

## Quick study summary

- Market share is necessary but insufficient; combine it with profitability, growth, and trend stability.
- Positioning choices require fit between price, perceived value, and customer sensitivity.
- Service strategy depends heavily on reputation and trust because quality is less observable before purchase.
- Brand and reputation can be strategic resources if they are difficult to imitate and linked to value capture.
- Good strategic diagnosis integrates hard metrics and perception evidence with clear assumptions.
