# Section 1: Summary (AP Level / Accessible)

*Use this section first. Plain language. Short sentences.*

## Key Words
- **Macro-environment** = PESTEL: factors beyond the firm (political, economic, social, technological, legal, ecological).
- **Industry environment** = Competitors, customers, suppliers, potential entrants, substitutes.
- **Five Forces** = Porter: rivalry, threat of entry, substitutes, buyer power, supplier power.
- **Industry attractiveness** = Likelihood of generating return/profit in an industry.
- **Clusters** = Geographical agglomeration of firms, suppliers, universities; extra opportunities.

## Main Ideas (Simple Version)

1. **Two levels of environment: macro and industry.**
   - Macro: PESTEL affects all players.
   - Industry: competitors, customers, suppliers, entrants, substitutes.

2. **PESTEL = general environment.**
   - Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Ecological.
   - Examples: Ukraine invasion, climate change.

3. **Five Forces = industry attractiveness.**
   - Rivalry among existing firms.
   - Threat of new entrants.
   - Threat of substitute products.
   - Bargaining power of buyers.
   - Bargaining power of suppliers.
   - Stronger forces → less attractive industry.

4. **Each force has factors.**
   - Rivalry: number of competitors, concentration (CR4), growth, differentiation, cost structure.
   - Entry: barriers (artificial vs natural), economies of scale, capital, retaliation.
   - Substitutes: disruptive technology, disruptive consumer behaviour.
   - Buyers/Suppliers: concentration, differentiation, information, threat of integration.

5. **Industry vs firm effect.**
   - Research shows both matter; industry effect explains part of variance.

6. **Clusters = geographical agglomeration.**
   - Talent, entrepreneurship, innovation, access to resources.
   - Firms + suppliers + universities/research centres.

# Section 2: Full Notes (from Unit 3 Slides)

## 3.1. The Business Environment

### A. Definition
- Firms operate within economic, social, cultural, political, legal systems.
- Management: discover and evaluate opportunities and threats.
- **Environment:** External characteristics the firm cannot control but that influence its activity, behaviour, and results.

### B. Two Levels

| Level | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Macro-environment** | Socio-economic factors (PESTEL) |
| **Industry environment** | Factors relevant to the industry in which the firm competes |

### C. Industry
- Market for products + market for factors of production.

---

## 3.2. Analysis of the General Environment (PESTEL)

### A. Definition
Variables and factors from different spheres (economic, political, legal, etc.) that affect all economic and social players in a society, country, or area.

### B. PESTEL Factors
- **P**olitical
- **E**conomic
- **S**ocial
- **T**echnological
- **E**cological
- **L**egal

### C. Examples
- Ukraine invasion impact.
- Climate change impact (Brown University, Kotz et al. Nature).

---

## 3.3. Analysis of the Competitive Environment (Five Forces)

### A. Source
Industrial Organization: structure of industry conditions strategic behaviour and profitability.

### B. Aim
Determine industry attractiveness → likelihood of generating return/profit.

### C. Porter’s Five Forces (1980)

| Force | Meaning | Stronger = |
|-------|---------|------------|
| **Rivalry among existing firms** | Degree of competition among current competitors | Less attractive |
| **Threat of new entrants** | Risk of new competitors entering | Less attractive |
| **Threat of substitute products** | Products meeting same need with different technology | Less attractive |
| **Bargaining power of buyers** | Customers’ power to impose conditions | Less attractive |
| **Bargaining power of suppliers** | Suppliers’ power to impose conditions | Less attractive |

### D. Rivalry Factors
- Number of competitors and concentration (CR4).
- Growth in demand, excess capacity, exit barriers.
- Degree of differentiation.
- Cost conditions (minimum effective size).

### E. Threat of Entry Factors
- **Barriers:** Artificial (government, legal, tariffs) vs Natural (economies of scale, capital, differentiation).
- **Reaction:** Retaliation by incumbents.

### F. Substitutes Sources
1. Disruptive technology.
2. Disruptive consumer behaviour.

### G. Buyer Power Factors
- Buyer concentration and number.
- Product differentiation.
- Information.
- Threat of upstream integration.

### H. Supplier Power Factors
- Supplier concentration and number.
- Product differentiation.
- Information.
- Threat of downstream integration.

### I. Criticisms of Five Forces
- Are all forces equally relevant?
- Just five? Role of complements? Public administrations?
- Industry segmentation and strategic groups.

---

## 3.4. Importance of Environment

### A. Industry vs Firm Effect
- Research (e.g. Javaloyes, 2010): both industry and firm effects explain variance in performance.
- Industry effect: part of attractiveness.
- Firm effect: internal resources and capabilities.

### B. Clusters (Industrial Districts)
- **Definition:** Geographical agglomeration of firms, suppliers, related industries, universities/research centres.
- **Advantages for firms inside:**
  1. Talent attraction.
  2. Entrepreneurship and start-ups.
  3. Technological innovation.
  4. Access to other resources.

---

## Key Takeaways
1. Environment: macro (PESTEL) + industry (Five Forces).
2. Five Forces: rivalry, entry, substitutes, buyers, suppliers.
3. Stronger forces → less attractive industry.
4. Industry vs firm effect: both matter.
5. Clusters: geographical agglomeration creates extra opportunities.

## Exam Prep: Very Short Version (from handwritten notes)

### Purpose
- Analyse external factors the firm \textbf{cannot control} but that influence behaviour and results.
- Identify \textbf{opportunities} and \textbf{threats}.
- Understand impact on profitability and competitiveness.

### Levels
- \textbf{Macro} (PESTEL): broad context.
- \textbf{Industry} (Five Forces): specific competitive arena.

### Five Forces (one line each)
1. Rivalry among existing firms.
2. Threat of new entrants (barriers to entry).
3. Threat of substitutes.
4. Bargaining power of buyers.
5. Bargaining power of suppliers.

Strong forces $\Rightarrow$ low profit potential; weak forces $\Rightarrow$ high profit potential. Performance = industry effect + firm effect.
