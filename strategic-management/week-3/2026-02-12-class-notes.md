## Key Concepts

### I. Cost of Equity - Formula and Satisfaction (Review)

A. Formula
1. Cost of Equity = Rf + β × (Market Return - Rf)

B. Components
1. Rf - Risk-free rate (e.g. government bonds)
2. β (Beta) - Company-specific risk vs. market
3. Market Return - Average return of market (e.g. IBEX 35)

C. Satisfaction
1. "When shareholders are satisfied... when [their return] is higher than [the cost of equity]... [or] maybe [compared to] the [average] for the European industry."

D. Calculation tools
1. "There is software... [that can] calculate satisfaction... [It's] simple [to use]."

E. Key point
1. Shareholders are satisfied when their return exceeds the cost of equity or industry average.

### V. Illustration 3: Five Forces Model - Substitutive Products

Focus: "Explore... substitutive products and the reasons behind [them]."

A. Definition of substitutive products
1. "Two different products... but with different technologies... [They] are not [direct] substitutes... [Substitutive products are] products with different technologies, coming from different industries, but in the eyes of customers, they meet the same wishes, [needs], and [preferences]."

B. Example - Coca-Cola
1. "What is Coca-Cola? ... Sugar... Energy... Caffeine... [The] key issue"
2. Substitutive products: "Behind Coca-Cola... we have... a soda... but especially [sugar] and [caffeine]... [which implies] energy... [So substitutive products are] all type of products... that we [consume]... [that provide] the same [energy]... [like] coffee, tea, [energy drinks]"

C. Example - Cars
1. "Two different cars... they are not substitutive... because they are similar [technology]... What are substitutive [products for cars]? ... Products with different technologies... [that meet] transportation [needs]"
2. Luxury cars vs. regular cars = not substitutive (same technology, different needs)
3. Car vs. train, plane, bike = substitutive (different technologies, same need: transportation)

D. Key insight
1. "This is the most difficult [part] in strategy... because when I go to work... I need to know who are my enemies, who is my rival... [In] the case of war... it's so complicated to discover who are my rivals... And this is the most difficult part of the analysis."

### VI. Why Identifying Rivals Is Difficult

A. Challenge
1. "Rivals are [other] companies which offer... [products]... [When] the products are similar in technology, you can [easily] identify [rivals]... [But] many competitors in the industry offering the same type of product... are not your rivals."

B. Example - Car industry
1. "If I am a car producer... All companies around the world producing cars are potentially my rivals... The answer is, not at all... Because [customers] don't make a choice [just] to say, 'I want to buy a car'... [They consider] many other things... Status, [speed], many things... That is what is so complex to discover, who are my rivals."

C. Luxury market example
1. "If I am competing in the luxury market... all type of products... could be a direct rival... [A customer] can buy... a Ferrari or a Lamborghini... [or] travel in Africa... [In] the eyes, in the mind of customers, they are [substitutive]... [To] discover who are my rivals... this [is] one of the most [challenging] [tasks]."

D. Key point
1. Rivals are not just companies with similar products; they are companies that meet the same customer needs/wishes, even with different technologies.

### VII. Barriers to Entry

Concept: "If I [am] in [an] industry... I don't like new competitors... New [entrants] are dangerous... How can companies close the door? ... There are two [types] of doors that we [call] barriers to entry... They can be natural [barriers] or economic ones... [or] artificial [barriers]."

Purpose: Prevent new competitors from entering the industry.

### VIII. Natural/Economic Barriers - Economies of Scale

A. Example - Car industry
1. "How many cars do you need to produce to be profitable in the car industry? ... [With] current technology... the minimum level of production... to have a chance to be profitable is about a half million cars per year, produced and sold... [This] implies that [to] enter the car [industry]... you need to be [a] company... [that] in four or five years... [reaches] at least a half million of cars produced... at a planetary level."

B. This is economies of scale
1. "There are companies subject to a lot of economies of scale... which implies that all actors in the industry should be very large companies... This is a natural [barrier]... [This is an] economic barrier."

C. Why few competitors
1. "Why many industries you can find only a few competitors, usually two, three, four... [It's because of] the economic nature of industry... [Economies of scale]... [companies] need to have [large] size to be profitable."

D. Examples
1. Aircraft industry: "How many [aircraft manufacturers] in the world? ... There are two [main] ones... Boeing... and Airbus... [China and India are introducing]... But in the [world]... only four... What is the reason? ... The amount of economies of scale are enormous... [So] you can find a few actors."

2. Banking: "Why in the majority of countries... there are [only] a few [banks]... [with] high market share? ... Because the market requires... [if] you want to [be profitable], you [need to] be very large... [This is because of] economies of scale."

E. Key insight
1. Industries with high economies of scale require large size to be profitable, creating natural barriers to entry and limiting the number of competitors.

### IX. What Are Economies of Scale?

Definition: "When you are a large company... you produce more... [and] you [can] reduce the cost per unit... [As] you produce more, the cost of production of the last product [produced] is lower."

A. Sources of economies of scale

1. Learning curve:
   a. "If you perform an activity... in a repetitive basis... for sure, you will have less mistakes... [The] time employed in production will be reduced... which implies that you are going to increase the productivity [and] efficiency rate"

2. Purchasing power:
   a. "In the case of Coca-Cola... to buy plastic or bottles... for 10,000 million of bottles... you will have more [bargaining] power and you can achieve lower prices for your products"

3. Advertising:
   a. "When you commercialize... advertising... you [spend] money in advertising... If the products you sell [are] a lot of them, you can divide this expense among a [large] amount of products"

4. R&D:
   a. "If you carry out research and development expenses... [If] those million dollars that you [invest] in developing new products... can be [spread] over a growing number of products... [The] cost of production, the cost of investment, because of innovation... [is reduced]"

5. Capacity utilization:
   a. "When you use 24 hours a day, seven days a week, [your] production capacity... you will reduce [your idle] capacity... [This] saves time and saves money... [As] you produce more... the cost of production of the last product [produced] is lower"

B. Key insight
1. "This is the effect of economies of scale and the advantage of being a large company... In the case of manufacturing companies... this is [a] few reality."

### X. Services vs. Manufacturing - Economies of Scale

A. Manufacturing
1. "In the case of manufacturing... for example, cars, aircraft... the learning [curve] is so high... [that] only you can compete [and] be profitable if you are very large."

B. Services
1. "In the case of services, it depends... This is the reason why many professional services, even restaurants... or [other] services... they prefer to be a small company and [serve] a specific part of customers... In the case of services it is more difficult to achieve economies of scale."

C. Key point
1. Economies of scale are stronger in manufacturing than in services; many service businesses can be profitable at smaller sizes.

### XI. Business Growth and Economies of Scale

Main reason for growth: "The first idea behind business growth is the achievement of economies of scale... because it's true... [Usually] there will be more reasons, but for sure, behind the phenomenon of business growth and development, economies of scale explains a lot."

A. Key insight
1. Companies grow (internally, through M&A, or alliances) partly to achieve economies of scale and reduce costs.

### XII. Artificial Barriers to Entry

Definition: "Artificial barriers... are [imposed]... [by] government or [other] institutions... [They] are [imposed] barriers for international competition."

A. Example - Banking in Spain
1. "If I want to... compete in Spain... [in] the bank industry... [I] have to [comply with] Spanish [regulations]... [This] is a way... to close [the] door... [to] American [or] British [or] German [banks]... [This creates] artificial barriers to entry... [which] constitute... economic barriers."

B. Key point
1. Governments can create artificial barriers (regulations, licenses, tariffs) that protect domestic industries from foreign competition.

### XIII. Clusters - Geographic Agglomeration

Definition: "Clusters imply that geography... agglomeration of companies... in a few square kilometers matters."

Advantage: "All companies belonging to Silicon Valley... have additional opportunities that make them more profitable than similar companies doing the same business, but located outside [the cluster]."

B. Key point
1. Geographic concentration (clusters) can provide competitive advantages beyond what individual companies could achieve alone.

### XIX. External Environment - Everything Outside the Company

Framework: "PESTEL analysis... Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal... [to] distinguish between opportunities and threats."

Purpose: "In general terms... [to] discover... potential... positive and negative... [factors]... [to] distinguish factors around the company... [between] the macro environment... [and] the industry environment."

### XXII. Five Forces Model - Industry Environment

Purpose: "The five forces model... the main task is to discover who are your rivals... We're going to [explore] five forces... [We'll cover] three of them [today]... [about] rivals... [and] how to [understand]... barriers to entry."

Model creator: Michael Porter (Harvard Professor)

Why it's important: "It's so simple and this is one of the advantages of this model... this is why this model is the most [well-]known model in strategy... so simple, so powerful... A. Key insight
1. Five Forces is simple, powerful, and widely used to analyze industry competitiveness.

### XXIII. Five Forces - The Five Elements

A. The five forces
1. Rivalry among existing competitors
2. Threat of new entrants (barriers to entry)
3. Bargaining power of suppliers
4. Bargaining power of buyers
5. Threat of substitutive products

Important note: "The only thing... that you [should] take into account when approaching the five forces model is... The five forces model... [analyzes]... the company, the rivals, the suppliers, and the customers... [But] you [are] a regular [competitor]... [You] are inside the industry... [You] carry out the analysis... [You are] a current competitor in the industry... [You] analyze [from that position]."

B. Key point
1. Analyze Five Forces from the perspective of a current competitor in the industry, not from outside.

---

### XXIV. Why Five Forces Focuses on Three (Rivals)

Question: "We have five forces, not just three... What happened? ... [Why focus on] three of the five forces... [which are] focused on analyzing... [and] potential... rivals?"

Answer: "Because this is the most difficult question to address... Who are my rivals? ... In politics... in work... and strategy, it is so difficult to have [a clear] picture of who are my rivals... [In] the case of war... it's so complicated to discover who are my rivals... And this is the most difficult part of the analysis."

A. Key insight
1. Three of the five forces (rivalry, new entrants, substitutes) all relate to identifying competitors/rivals, which is the hardest part of strategic analysis.

### XXV. Macro vs. Industry Environment - What Matters?

A. Key question
1. "The issue is... [which] factors are having [an] impact on my business... or my [industry]... Obviously, there are more [factors]... but I don't care... What are they? ... They are factors, but I assume that they are not going to [affect] my business."

B. Key point
1. Not all external factors matter equally; focus on factors that actually affect your business/industry.

### XXVI. PESTEL Analysis - Two Steps

Step 1 - Identify: "The first [step] is to identify... according to [the] six [dimensions]... [For example] GDP growth is a typical economic factor... [that] could affect [your business]."

Step 2 - Assess impact: "The second step is to identify [whether it's a] threat or opportunity... [For example] GDP growth... in Germany [or] Spain is close to zero or below zero... [But] the analysis is [to determine] to what extent this negative growth... is [a] negative threat or positive opportunity to my business."

A. Key point
1. PESTEL requires both identification (what factors exist) and assessment (are they threats or opportunities for your business).
