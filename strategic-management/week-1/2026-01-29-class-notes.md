# Strategic Management - Class Notes
## January 29, 2026 - Week 1

**Focus:** Historical origins of strategy, strategic management evolution.

---

I. Historical Origins of Strategy
   A. Ancient Origins
      1. Greek "strategos" = the general
      2. Political sphere: Machiavelli's The Prince
      3. Military: know your rivals
      4. Sports: competitive games
   B. Modern Business Strategy
      1. Started ~70 years ago in business schools
      2. First label: "Business Policy"
      3. Second label: "Strategic Planning"
      4. Current label: "Strategic Management"

II. Strategy Definition
   A. Core Meaning
      1. Strategy = having a plan
      2. Having a plan = course of action
   B. Components
      1. Objective = what you want
      2. Course of action = how to achieve
      3. Alignment = plan w/ business reality

III. Types of Strategies
   A. Many Types Exist
      1. Local vs. international
      2. Cultural strategies
      3. Alliances
      4. Vertical integration
      5. Diversification vs. specialization
   B. Key Insight
      1. Will classify in Units 1-2
      2. Framework for understanding

IV. Michael Porter Era
   A. Timeline
      1. Two publications: 1980 and 1985
      2. First strategy course in faculty: 1991
   B. Foundation
      1. Established strategic mgmt as distinct field
      2. American origin

V. Main Questions of Strategic Management
   A. Three Core Questions
      1. What is company success?
         a. How to make success
         b. When stakeholders satisfied
         c. "Golden issue" = Unit 2
      2. What are strategic objectives?
         a. Long-term impact decisions
         b. Key info for best decision
      3. What is current situation?
         a. Analyze current conditions
         b. No guarantee situation maintained

VI. Uncertainty and Forecasting
   A. Reality
      1. Impossible to forecast accurately
      2. Political decisions unpredictable
   B. Implications
      1. Strategic decisions last 5-50 years
      2. Must face uncertainty
   C. Examples
      1. Expanding to China/Europe
      2. Opening factories in US/Canada/Mexico
      3. Conditions change unpredictably

VII. Complexity of Strategic Decisions
   A. Personal Life Examples
      1. Choosing bachelor's degree
      2. Where to live
      3. Having children
   B. Processing Challenge
      1. Too many variables
      2. Impossible to process all info
   C. Choosing Partner Example
      1. No database review
      2. Usually choose from 1-3 people
      3. Emotional process

VIII. Companies as Legal Fictions
   A. Key Concept
      1. Companies = legal fictions
      2. Real decisions by persons (CEO)
   B. Stakeholder Acceptance
      1. Policymakers (regulators)
      2. Workers (unions)
      3. Customers
      4. Society

IX. Four Pillars of Successful Strategy
   A. What Do You Want? (Objectives)
      1. Simple, consistent, long-term
      2. Clear goals essential
   B. Where Are You? (External Analysis)
      1. Understand competitive environment
      2. Know stakeholders, rules, rivals
   C. Who Are You? (Internal Analysis)
      1. Detect strengths (positive)
      2. Detect weaknesses (negative)
      3. Base decisions on strengths, never weaknesses
   D. Effective Implementation
      1. Hard work required
      2. Extra effort to compete

X. Three Levels of Strategy
   A. Corporate Strategy (Highest)
      1. Maximum complexity
      2. CEO responsibility
      3. Key Q: "Where to compete?"
         a. Business scope: single vs. multiple
         b. Geographic scope: domestic vs. global
   B. Competitive Strategy (Business-Level)
      1. Key Q: "How to compete?"
      2. Need one for each business
      3. Product differentiation vs. cost leadership
      4. No optimal strategy; depends on context
   C. Functional Strategy (Lowest)
      1. Implementation through functional areas
      2. Marketing: 4 Ps
      3. HR: talent, motivation, development
      4. Operations: what/how/where/how much to produce

XI. Strategic Analysis Process
   A. Step 1: Analysis
      1. What I want? = mission, objectives
      2. Where I am? = external (opportunities/threats)
      3. Who I am? = internal (strengths/weaknesses)
   B. Step 2: Strategy Formulation
      1. Corporate strategy: where to compete
      2. Competitive strategy: how to compete
   C. Step 3: Implementation
      1. Functional strategies
      2. Execution and control

XII. Growth Strategies
   A. Three Main Ways
      1. Internal growth = open factories, hire, improve
      2. M&A = absorb/buy competitors (faster but expensive)
      3. Alliances = franchises, licenses, partnerships
   B. In Mature Industry
      1. Internal growth difficult
      2. M&A often only viable option
      3. Capacity stays same; grow by acquiring rivals

XIII. Rational vs. Real Decision-Making
   A. Theory (Rational Model)
      1. Analyze all info
      2. Use technical reports
      3. Find optimal solution
   B. Reality
      1. Impossible to analyze everything
      2. Decisions made by people (CEOs)
      3. Companies = legal fictions

XIV. Personal Traits and Strategic Decisions
   A. Key Question
      1. Do CEO traits influence decisions?
      2. Answer: Yes
   B. Traits That Matter
      1. Age: older = more conservative
      2. Education: lawyer vs. economist differ
      3. Risk tolerance: risk-averse vs. risk-seeker
      4. Emotional traits: affect decisions
      5. Location/background: geography, culture

XV. Stakeholder Theory vs. Shareholder Theory
   A. Shareholder Theory (Legal View)
      1. Company for shareholders
      2. Friedman: only responsibility = profit
   B. Stakeholder Theory (Reality)
      1. Real power holders vary
         a. Managers w/ real power
         b. Workers/unions (Spain, Germany)
         c. Owners (sometimes)
   C. Union Power Example
      1. Best solution = reduce staff 55%
      2. If unions powerful → never implemented
      3. Manager who proposes gets fired
      4. Company bankrupts in 1.5 years

XVI. Utility Functions of Stakeholders
   A. Shareholders
      1. Profit maximization
   B. Managers
      1. Remuneration
      2. Status
      3. Power
      4. Security
   C. Workers
      1. Remuneration
      2. Security
      3. Status
      4. Power

XVII. Managerial Incentives and Growth
   A. Manager Motivation
      1. Want: remuneration, status, power
   B. How Managers Gain These
      1. More people in charge
      2. More sales/revenues
      3. Larger company
      4. Public recognition
   C. Problem
      1. Growth ≠ profitability
      2. Managers prefer growth (benefits them)
      3. Even against profitability

XVIII. M&A - Managerial Perspective
   A. Example
      1. Tech worth 25M to develop
      2. Company price: 100M
      3. Willing to pay 125M+
   B. Agency Problem
      1. Manager plays w/ owner's money
      2. Studies show M&A destroys value (5-6 years)
      3. Decision in manager hands, not owner

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Strategy origins: Greek → military → politics → business (~70 years)
2. Strategy = objective + course of action + alignment
3. Three levels: corporate (where), competitive (how), functional (implement)
4. Four pillars: what want, where am, who am, effective implementation
5. No single best strategy; depends on objectives, resources, environment
6. Forecasting impossible; must face uncertainty
7. Companies = legal fictions; decisions by people (CEOs)
8. Stakeholder theory: real power varies (managers, unions, owners)
9. Personal traits matter: age, education, risk, emotions
10. Managers prefer growth (status, power) over profitability
11. Rational process limited; real decisions involve politics, power

X. 30-Second Cold Call Version.
A. 3-Line Speaking Frame.
   1. TOPIC: STATE THE CORE QUESTION IN ONE LINE.
   2. LOGIC: GIVE 2 DRIVERS/FACTORS THAT EXPLAIN THE ANSWER.
   3. ACTION: END WITH ONE DECISION OR IMPLICATION.
B. Delivery Rule.
   1. 25-30 SECONDS MAX.
   2. NO BACKGROUND STORY; GO STRAIGHT TO CLAIM + EVIDENCE + CLOSE.

DI. Oral Answer Template (60-90 Seconds, Class Use).
A. Opening.
   1. STATE THE CORE QUESTION OR TOPIC IN ONE LINE.
   2. STATE YOUR MAIN CLAIM/THESIS IN ONE LINE.
B. Logic.
   1. GIVE 2-3 KEY FACTORS OR DRIVERS.
   2. LINK EACH FACTOR TO THE CLAIM (CAUSE -> EFFECT).
C. Action Or Implication.
   1. END WITH ONE DECISION, RECOMMENDATION, OR CONSEQUENCE.
   2. CLOSE CLEARLY WITHOUT ADDING NEW IDEAS.
D. Delivery Rule.
   1. 60-90 SECONDS.
   2. CLAIM -> EVIDENCE -> CLOSE.
