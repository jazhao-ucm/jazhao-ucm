## Key Concepts

### I. Historical Origins of Strategy

A. Ancient Origins
1. Strategy originally appeared "many times ago" - from Greek strategos (the general)
2. Used in political sphere - e.g., Machiavelli's *The Prince* (gift to Lorenzo de' Medici)
3. Used in military - "If you want to be the prince of Italy... you need to know your rivals"
4. Used in sports - competitive games and strategy

B. Modern Business Strategy
1. Started ~70 years ago in business schools
2. First label: "Business Policy"
3. Second label: "Strategic Planning"
4. Current label: "Strategic Management"

C. Key insight
1. Strategy has evolved from military/political origins to business application.

---

### II. What Is Strategy? Definition

A. Core Definition
1. Strategy = "Having a plan"
2. Having a plan = "Having a course of action"

B. Components
1. Objective - What you want to achieve
2. Course of action - How you're going to achieve it
3. Alignment - Your plan must align with business reality

C. Key point
1. "You detect your objective... and then, for this [objective], a course of action to achieve that point... Your plan... with the business reality."

A. Three Core Questions

1. What is company success?

   c. "What means make money?"
   d. "When my [stakeholders] are satisfied?"

   b. "Especially because... decisions can affect [people] for [many] years"

### VI. Uncertainty and Forecasting

A. Reality
1. "It is impossible to forecast"
2. "What would be the main international political decisions in the next year? It is impossible to forecast."
3. "What will happen in the world when [the] president finishes his mandate? Who knows?"

B. Implications
1. Strategic decisions have long-term effects (5, 10, 50 years)
2. "The fact [that decisions last] 5, 10, 50 years... It is impossible to [forecast]... Also [we] need to face... uncertainty"

Example: Expanding activities in China or Europe, opening factories in US/Canada/Mexico/UK - these investments last many years, but political/economic conditions change unpredictably.

C. Key insight
1. "It is impossible to [forecast]... Also [we] need to face... uncertainty."

### VII. Complexity of Strategic Decisions

A. Personal Life Examples
1. Choosing a bachelor's degree
2. Where to live
3. Having children or not
4. "How many variables do you need to match in order to take [this] decision?"

B. Many Circumstances
1. Economic factors
2. Social factors
3. "Many, many [factors]"

C. Processing Challenge
1. "Even the best... information... Even [if] it's so clear what is the current situation of my business, many times, it is impossible to process all this information... To take the best decision."

D. Example - Choosing a Partner
1. "Imagine if you have to choose a partner... To live together forever... And you have children"
2. "I guess that you don't have [a] database... You don't review [their] financial [situation], personality tests... And after interviewing [a] thousand of [potential] partners, you'd choose the optimal one"
3. Reality: Usually choose between one, sometimes two, maybe three people - "no more"
4. "It is [an emotional] process"

E. Key point
1. Strategic decisions are complex and cannot be fully rationalized.

### X. Four Pillars of Successful Strategy (Framework)

A. **Model:** Four main pillars for successful strategy

B. What Do You Want? (Objectives)
1. "Do you need to be so clear what do you want"
2. Simple, consistent, long-term
3. Example: "I want to be the number one in [the] world" - simple, clear, long-term
4. Companies should define their end/goal clearly
5. Personal: "If you want to be successful in your personal life [or] professional... The first point is to have [clear goals]"

C. Where Are You? (External Analysis)
1. Understand your competitive environment
2. "You will have rivals, competitors"
3. "You need to carry out your activities... in a specific context, usually in [an] economic [system]"
4. Understand stakeholders: sources, workers, customers
5. "You need to understand... your world"
6. Questions:
   a. What is your study/educational/professional environment?
   b. Who are the gatekeepers?
   c. Who are your main rivals?
   d. What are the rules of the game?
   e. What is the game [you're playing]?

D. Who Are You? (Internal Analysis)
1. "Understand who are you"
2. Detect your strengths (positive features)
3. Detect your weaknesses (negative features)
4. "If you want to be successful... base your decisions... based on your strength, and never [on] your weaknesses"

E. Effective Implementation
1. "I want to be the number one... For sure, this [requires] that you need to work [a] lot"
2. "Extra work to [compete] with your peers"
3. Example: Study 7 days a week, 10-12 hours a day
4. "Because maybe you [need to work harder] than [others]"

F. Key insight
1. "What I want, where I am, who I am, [and] effective [implementation]."

### XII. Three Levels of Strategy

Question: "Are all [strategies] equally relevant, complex? The answer is no."

A. Three Levels

1. Corporate Strategy (Highest complexity)
   a. Maximum level of complexity
   b. "The only person in [large] companies to carry out corporate strategies is the CEO, the only one, the president"
   c. "No more people"
   d. "This is one of the main duties of CEO"
a. Key question: "Where to compete?"
   a. **Scope:** "What means the scope? [The] set of businesses in which my company [is] going to compete"
   b. Decisions:
   c. Single business vs. multiple businesses (specialization vs. diversification)
   d. Domestic vs. international vs. global
   e. Mission, vision, business portfolio
   f. Examples: Internationalization (Units 6-8), growth strategies, mergers & acquisitions

2. Competitive Strategy (Business-level)
   a. "Once we have clear... where to compete... The next [level] is... competitive strategy"
   b. If company operates in multiple businesses: need competitive strategy **for each business**
a. Key question: "How [to compete]? How do [I] compete?"
   a. "What is the way to be successful in the market over [competitors]?"
   b. "How to compete... This is the key question of competitive strategy"
   c. Focus: Product differentiation vs. cost leadership
   d. "90%... of companies compete [in] three ways: [product] differentiation, or cost leadership"
   e. "What is the best? It depends on what you want, [and] who are you"
   f. "There is no optimal [strategy]... There is no unique solution. It depends"

3. Functional Strategy (Lowest level)
   a. "Once [the] company has [decided] how to compete... The companies need to put into action that decision through the different functional areas"
   b. Examples:
   c. **Marketing:** Product, Price, Place, Promotion (4 Ps)
   d. **Human Resources:** Talent attraction, motivation, development, rotation
   e. **Operations:** What to produce, how to produce, where to produce, how much to produce
   f. "These are strategic decisions, but this type [is] third level"
   g. Usually studied in specialized courses (marketing, finance, HR, operations)
   h. "Usually this course is [taught] in the third [year]... In the fourth year is when [students] are ready to learn strategy, because they know about finance, human resources, operations, marketing"

B. Key insight
1. Strategy has hierarchy - corporate (where), competitive (how), functional (implementation).

### XIII. Corporate Strategy - Where to Compete

A. Key question
1. "Where to compete?"

B. Dimensions

1. Business Scope:
   a. Single business (specialization) vs. multiple businesses (diversification)
   b. "I'm going to produce [one type of product]... or maybe [I want] to enter in 2, 3 [different] businesses"

2. Geographic Scope:
   a. Domestic vs. international vs. global
   b. "I need to be a domestic company, or I would like to be a global company, international"

C. Complexity
1. "These key decisions are the most complex"
2. Definition of mission, vision, business portfolio
3. "Who I am? [What] sector? That's [the scope]"

D. Examples Covered in Units 6-8
1. Growth strategies (internal growth, mergers & acquisitions, alliances)
2. Internationalization (local, domestic, international, multinational)
3. Ways of internationalization (export, licensing, franchises, joint ventures)

E. Key point
1. Corporate strategy addresses the highest-level "where" questions.

### XIV. Growth Strategies

A. Three Main Ways to Grow

1. Internal Growth
   a. "Open new factories and shops, [hire] people, [improve] processes"
   b. Organic expansion
   c. "This is [internal] growth"

2. Mergers & Acquisitions
   a. "Absorbing or buying... some of my competitors"
   b. "With a lot of strategic advantages"
   c. "Which implies that the process of growth is [faster]"
   d. "But the money that you [need] to invest in this [process] is very [high]"

3. Alliances
   a. "For example, franchises, licenses"
   b. "Different ways of [growing]"
   c. Example: Strategic partnerships

B. Key question
1. "What is the best way of grow? There is no [single] option... It depends on what [you want], who are you, and [the] environment"

C. Example - Mature Industry
1. "If I am in a mature industry... Mature industry, no growth, [or] slow growth"
2. "If a single company [wants] to grow... internal growth through new [facilities], [hiring] people... [is] very [difficult]"
3. "Because you are promoting [more] price work in the market"
4. "The only way to grow... in a mature industry, the only way is through mergers & acquisitions"
5. "Because in the end, you are growing, but you are absorbing or buying [your] competitors, which implies that the capacity in the industry is the same"

D. Key insight
1. Best growth strategy depends on industry stage and company situation.

### XV. Competitive Strategy - How to Compete

A. Key question
1. "How [to compete]?"

Focus: "What is the way to be successful in the market over [competitors]?"

B. Simple Model (Michael Porter)
1. "90%... of companies compete [in] three ways: [product] differentiation, or cost leadership"
2. Most companies use one of these approaches

C. No Optimal Solution
1. "What is the best? It depends on what you want, [and] who are you"
2. "There is no optimal [strategy]... There is no unique solution. It depends"
3. Depends on:
   a. What you want (objectives)
   b. Who you are (resources, capabilities)
   c. Environment (market conditions)

D. Key point
1. Competitive strategy is about choosing how to win in your chosen markets.

### XVII. Strategic Analysis Process

A. Three-Step Process

Step 1: Analysis
1. What I want? - Mission, objectives
2. Where I am? - External analysis (opportunities and threats)
3. Who I am? - Internal analysis (strengths and weaknesses)

Step 2: Strategy Formulation
4. After analysis, formulate strategy
5. Corporate strategy (Units 6-8): Where to compete
6. Competitive strategy (Unit 5): How to compete

Step 3: Implementation
7. Put strategy into action
8. Functional strategies
9. Execution and control

B. Key insight
1. Analysis → Formulation → Implementation

### XVIII. External Analysis - Opportunities and Threats

Purpose: "To understand your business environment"

A. Three External Analyses
1. Detect opportunities - "Current or potential... positive impact in my company"
2. "To take advantage [of opportunities]"
3. Detect threats - "Negative or potential... effects on my company"
4. "Threats that we need to avoid or [minimize] if [they] happen"

B. Key point
1. External analysis identifies what you can leverage (opportunities) and what you must guard against (threats).

### XIX. Internal Analysis - Strengths and Weaknesses

Purpose: "Who I am?"

A. Focus
1. Detect strengths - "Positive features... [your] strong points"
2. Detect weaknesses - "Your negative features... Your weaknesses"

B. Strategic Principle
1. "If you want to be successful... base your decisions... based on your strength, and never [on] your weaknesses"
2. Build on what you're good at; avoid competing where you're weak

C. Key insight
1. "Base your way of [competing]... based on your strength, and never [on] your weaknesses."

### XX. Strategy Formulation - Corporate Level

After Analysis: Formulate corporate strategy

A. Key Decisions (Units 6-8)

1. Specialization vs. Diversification
   a. "I want to be [focused] on [one] moment of business" = Specialization
   b. "Maybe I [would] like to [diversify] my activities... in different industries" = Diversification

2. Reasons for Diversification:
   a. **Synergies** - "I can produce several types of products, the same factory, the same workers"
   b. "I can reduce... capacity... [and increase] efficiency"
   c. "More [profitability]"
   d. **Market scope** - "I can offer in the market more products with the same brand"
   e. "Market [expansion]"
   f. "Once I have a [good] customer base... When I [launch] new products... [I can use] distribution... [and] marketing campaigns"
   g. **Geographic expansion** - "If I am successful... in my region or in my country, maybe my business can work in other [countries]"
   h. **Economies of scale** - "It is not the same to produce 10,000 [units]... [vs.] 1 million [units]... The average cost... will be reduced... by the effect of economies of scale"
   i. **Technology access** - "I have detected... a [biotech] company... with [technology] in development... that could be so useful for my business"
   j. "Maybe [I decide] to absorb [or] buy these [tech] companies to reinforce... my business activities"
   k. **Competition reduction** - "If I buy one of my rivals... I will [reduce] competition... which implies a lot of strategic benefits"

B. Key point
1. Many strategic alternatives exist; choose based on your situation and goals.

### XXII. Rational vs. Real Decision-Making

A. Theory vs. Reality

B. Theory (Rational Model)
1. Analyze all information
2. Use technical/economic reports
3. Find optimal solution
4. "In theory, the law says that the company is in charge of shareholders"

C. Reality
1. "It is impossible... to analyze all the information, all [circumstances], all [scenarios]"
2. "It is impossible, like in mathematical programming to find the optimization"
3. Decisions are made by people (CEOs, managers), not abstract entities
4. "Companies don't exist. They are legal fictions"
5. "Who takes strategic issues are persons in [charge], usually a CEO"

D. Key point
1. Real strategic decisions are made by people with limited information and personal motivations.

### XXIII. Personal Traits and Strategic Decisions

A. Key question
1. "Do personal traits of CEOs have an influence on strategic decisions?"

Answer: Yes - "We assume... that personal traits of CEOs have an influence on strategic decisions"

B. Personal Traits That Matter

1. Age
   a. "Older [CEOs] are more conservative"
   b. "More focused on financial results and not [on] growth results"
   c. Exception: "CEOs close to retirement... over [age] 70... The effect is contrary"
   d. "They prefer... activities... [that leave a] mark"

2. Education
   a. "A lawyer or an economist, as a CEO... They are different [in] mental[ity]"
   b. Different educational backgrounds lead to different decision-making approaches

3. Risk Tolerance
   a. "What [personal] traits... [make CEOs] more inclined... to [take] risks?"
   b. Some CEOs are more risk-averse; others are risk-seekers

4. Emotional Traits
   a. "How emotional [a person is]... [affects] decisions"
   b. "If you are [very] emotional... [you make decisions differently]"
   c. Emotions can influence strategic choices

5. Location/Background
   a. "Where you're located" - geographic and cultural background
   b. "Demographics" - age, education, location all matter

C. Key insight
1. "Personal traits... [influence] strategic decisions... This is true... But in management, this [was] denied [for] 50 years... We presume that decisions are [purely] rational... This is false."

### XXIV. Stakeholder Theory vs. Shareholder Theory

A. Legal View (Shareholder Theory)
1. "In theory, the law says that the company is in charge of shareholders"
2. Milton Friedman: Only responsibility is to make money for shareholders

B. Reality (Stakeholder Theory)
1. "But this is not true in many, many times"
2. Real power holders:
   a. **Managers** - "The managers... who [have] the real power"
   b. **Workers/Unions** - "In Spain and Germany, for example... Workers [and] unions, they have a lot of power"
   c. **Owners** - Sometimes, but not always

C. Example - Union Power
1. "If after all the analyses... you state that the only way to [save] the company is to reduce the staff... 55% of people or 25% of people"
2. "If [the] company [has] unions... Never [will] that decision be implemented"
3. "For sure, the only person that will be fired, will be the person, the manager, who proposed that proposition"
4. "In [one] year and a half... The company [will go] bankrupt"
5. "And this is rational? No, this is real"

D. Key insight
1. "The decisions that are finally implemented are those... which benefit the stakeholder... that [has] the real power."

### XXVI. Utility Functions of Stakeholders

A. Key question
1. "What makes [stakeholders] happy?"

B. Shareholders
1. "What makes happy [shareholders]? Profit maximization"
2. "Make money... only [satisfies] shareholders"

C. Managers
1. "What is the utility function of managers?"
2. "Profit maximization? No, because this profit [goes] to shareholders... not [to managers]"
3. What makes managers happy:
4. Remuneration - Salary, bonuses
5. Status - "Being the best... [having] reputation"
6. Power - "If you are [in charge]... you have power"
7. Security - "If you are [a] manager... you [feel] secure"
8. Lower opportunity cost - "If you [are] fired... [you have] fewer [options]"

D. Workers
1. "What [makes] workers happy?"
2. Remuneration - Pay, benefits
3. Security - Job stability
4. Status - Respect, recognition
5. Power - Influence, voice

E. Key insight
1. Different stakeholders have different goals; strategy must balance these interests.

### XXVII. Managerial Incentives and Growth

A. Manager Motivation
1. Managers want: Remuneration, status, power
2. "How can you achieve [these]? Because [these are] the motivation"

B. How Managers Gain These
1. More people in charge - "More managers and more workers... I [am] responsible [for] more factories"
2. More sales/revenues - "More sales volumes or volumes of revenues"
3. Larger company - "When you pass from medium size to a large company"
4. More resources - "You are in charge of a large [amount] of money"
5. Public recognition - "When you appear in national... newspapers"

C. Problem
1. "Is this same to grow in sales or in [size] or in factories [as] to be more profitable? The answer is, no"
2. "Usually when you don't control your managers, they tend to grow your business"
3. "Even if that decision goes against [profitability]"
4. "This is very common... [especially] the most expensive way of growing"

D. Key insight
1. Managers often prefer growth (which benefits them) over profitability (which benefits shareholders).

### XXVIII. Mergers & Acquisitions - Managerial Perspective

A. Example
1. Company developing new technology
2. Cost to develop internally: 25 million
3. "But it's not possible to buy [the technology] in [the] factors market"
4. "I need to buy the company"
5. Market price of company: 100 million
6. "But I really want that... I [am willing to] pay 125 million"
7. "But this is not the end of the story"

B. Why Overpay
1. "When you try to absorb or to buy a company, you need to convince all the owners"
2. "The only way to convince is [to offer] impressive [premium]"
3. "The price that you [offer]... is higher than the real price"
4. "You [offer] over 100... [plus] 20%, 30%, 50%... You need to find a point"
5. "Which implies that you are investing a lot of money"

C. Agency Problem
1. "The worst thing is when you decide that as a manager, as the CEO, if you are not the owner of the company, you are playing... with the money... of owners"
2. "We [have] demonstrated... [in] many studies that... after mergers and [acquisition] processes, they lose a lot of money"
3. "But the decision is not in the hands of owners. The decision is in the hands of managers"

D. Manager Incentives
1. "Managers... [have] incentives in... business growth"
2. "Because business growth [implies] more remuneration, more status... [and] more power"
3. "This is why many... [decisions] are [made] by [managers]... In the case of CEO, [it's] the [CEO's] decision"

E. Key insight
1. Managers may pursue M&A for personal benefit (growth, status) even when it's not optimal for shareholders.

### XXX. Strategic Management as Social Science

A. Key Concept
1. Strategic management is not pure science
2. It is social science
3. "Social science implies that... [decisions involve] negotiation with other agents"
4. "Implies that you are going to [negotiate]... [and] impose... your [ideas]"
5. "Many times, [decisions are] not rational"

## Key Takeaways

1. Strategy origins - From Greek "strategos" (general) → military → politics → sports → business (~70 years ago)
2. Strategy definition - Having a plan = objective + course of action aligned with reality
3. Three levels - Corporate (where), Competitive (how), Functional (implementation)
4. Four pillars - What you want, Where you are, Who you are, Effective implementation
5. No single best strategy - Depends on objectives, resources, environment
6. Uncertainty - Forecasting is impossible; must deal with uncertainty
7. Complexity - Too many variables; impossible to process all information
8. Companies as legal fictions - Real decisions made by people (CEOs, managers)
9. Stakeholder theory - Real power holders (managers, unions) often differ from legal owners
10. Personal traits matter - Age, education, risk tolerance, emotions influence decisions
11. Managerial incentives - Managers often prefer growth (status, power) over profitability
12. Rational process is limited - Real decisions involve politics, power, negotiation
13. Social science - Strategy is not pure science; involves people and their motivations
