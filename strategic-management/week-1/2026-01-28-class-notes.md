## Key Concepts

### I. Course Introduction

Subject: Politics and economics in the context of strategic management.

A. Materials
1. Slides uploaded every week
2. Business cases and illustrations
3. Focus on classical strategic management plus contemporary issues (e.g. sustainability, change)

Instructor background: Experience at Harvard, University of California (UCLA and UC), and teaching in Spain.

---

### II. What Is Strategy?

A. Definition (etymology)
1. From Greek strategos – the general; the art of commanding the army
2. Strategy is what the general does: lead, decide, compete

B. Why “army” and “competition”?
1. We compete for scarce resources: customers, talent, capital
2. “If we assume more pursued that we have the scarcity of resources… we need to compete.”
3. In work and life there are limited “spots” – we compete for them

C. Key insight
1. Strategy in business = strategic decisions: long-term, hard to reverse, with lasting effects.

---

### III. Strategic vs. Tactical Decisions

A. Strategic
1. Long-term effects
2. Affect your life, career, family for years
3. Require serious thought and information

B. Tactical
1. Short-term
2. Operational, day-to-day

Example: Choosing university, what to study, where to live, career change, partner, having children – these are strategic. They shape your life for a long time and are hard to undo.

C. Key point
1. “Every day you take decisions… the strategic [ones]… have effect in the long term.”

---

### IV. Complexity and Information

A. Reality
1. Decisions are complex – too many factors to process fully
2. “Our brain… it is impossible [to process] whole information… to find an optimal [solution].”
3. We cannot know everything; we must simplify

B. How the course handles this
1. Use models with a limited number of dimensions (e.g. 2–3)
2. “More than three dimensions is so complicated… When you have two options, A or B. You have four… Three is something… It is.”
3. In business, even big firms often simplify to a few strategic numbers or types

C. Key insight
1. “The only way to take… the best decision is to take a sequence [of steps], trying… and assuming [we simplify].”

---

### V. Forecasting and Uncertainty

A. Forecasting doesn’t work well
1. “It is impossible to make forecasts.”
2. Conditions change: personal, economic, political, health, climate, social
3. “What do we have… in five years? We don’t know.”

B. Role of foresight
1. “Prospection, imagining the future” – usually at least two years ahead
2. More uncertainty → more need to think ahead and adapt
3. “When you [are] a politic[ian] or… manager… you have to [think ahead]. Because… maybe something happens from climate, from political [events], from… social movement.”

C. Key point
1. Even in the best situation, unexpected events can change the picture. Strategy must account for uncertainty.

---

### VI. The Art of War and Business

Teaching tool: Apply ideas from Sun Tzu / *The Art of War* (and related strategic classics) to business.

A. Analogies
1. Battlefield → market
2. Enemy → competitor (or the “obstacle” you must overcome)
3. General → leader / strategist

Approach: Use “strategic ideas by… Chinese [classics], and apply to the business sphere.”

Main lessons: Strategy is about knowing yourself, knowing the environment, and making decisions with incomplete information – in war and in business.

### VII. Course Structure: Theory and Practice

A. Rough split
1. ~50% theory – main principles, models, frameworks
2. ~50% practice – business cases, illustrations, group work

Theory: Classical strategic management plus modern tools and concepts (economics, industry analysis, competitive strategy).

B. Practice
1. Business cases: read before class, discuss in groups, present
2. Illustrations: shorter, more informal; you find extra information yourself
3. Apply theory to “specific economic, industry, and business situations”

C. Key point
1. “The way to teach strategy… we need to [combine] theory… and try to [apply]… even [when] things are different.”

---

### VIII. Learning Objectives

A. Goals
1. Learn the principles of strategic decision-making
2. Understand that there is no single “correct” answer – “It’s impossible to find the optimum”
3. Use interpretation, not just formulas – “Don’t use [only] mathematics… They are tools”
4. Apply concepts to real business and personal decisions

B. Examples of strategic life decisions
1. Where to study / what to study (shapes career and brain)
2. Where to live (job, family, cost – e.g. buying a house in Spain)
3. Career change (especially mid-life – “so difficult”)
4. Partner and children (huge impact on happiness, health, education, finances)

C. Key insight
1. “The majority of… important decisions… [are] one [shot]. No second [chance]. If you are to have [children]… you have one number… For sure.”

### IX. Know Yourself

First principle for success: Know yourself.

1. “The first thing is to love your thing. The second is to know perfectly well the [context] around you.”
2. “The only way to be happy… is to know [yourself].”
3. “The only way to be successful… is to know yourself.”

A. Why it matters
1. If your personal goals don’t fit the world (or your strengths), you will struggle
2. “If you have [unrealistic] objectives… you will fail.”
3. Knowing your strengths and weaknesses helps you choose where to compete and how

Connection to strategy: Like the “Chinese general” idea: you have a better chance of success if you know yourself and your environment well.

---

### X. Know Your External Environment

Second principle: Know your external context.

1. Opportunities – positive factors you can use
2. Threats – risks you must prepare for or avoid
3. “Try to take advantage of possibility… and [guard against]… threats.”

Then: Choose the actions that fit your goals and your situation.

In business: e.g. how to compete, how to grow (organic, franchise, export, etc.), which countries or segments to focus on.

A. Key point
1. “Once you have [a] picture of what you want [and] where you are… then you… take the actions that you need to take.”

---

### XI. First Case: Three Personal Stories

Focus: Three people’s stories of success (and how they used strategy in life/career).

A. Questions to explore
1. Why did they succeed? Why might others fail?
2. What do successful people have in common?
3. Role of luck vs. strategy (“black swan” type events)?

Pattern: Success in life, as in business, is not just about being “the best” in one dimension – it’s about goals, self-knowledge, and adapting to the environment.

Example (Malona): “Her aim was to be the best… So simple, so clear. When you are so young, what you want… you have a chance to be [successful].”

---

### XII. Long-Term Success and Stakeholders

Short term: Focus on operations, profit, “what’s going on now.”

A. **Long term:** Must consider **stakeholders**, not only shareholders
1. Workers and local community
2. Society and environment
3. Political and regulatory context

Key idea: “The only way to be successful in the long term… especially facing… change… is to [balance]… not [just] do your business and make money.”

Contrast: Milton Friedman (Nobel, University of Chicago) – “The only responsibility of business is to make money for their [shareholders].” = financial capitalism, dominant in 1980s–90s and early 2000s. The course will also criticize this view and introduce stakeholder and sustainability perspectives.

5. Discussion: link case to theory, tools, and strategic concepts

B. Expectations

2. Use course frameworks (e.g. industry analysis, competitive strategy)
3. “All the members [should be] ready to [participate].”

### XV. Illustrations vs. Business Cases

A. Business cases
1. Structured; read before class
2. Discussed in class with clear link to theory
3. Graded / part of assessment

B. Illustrations
1. Shorter; often current events or examples (e.g. a company, industry, controversy)
2. You find information by yourself
3. “I am only [introducing]… You need to [find] the information by yourself.”
4. More voluntary; used to deepen understanding and debate

Example topics: Sustainability claims (e.g. refillable products, greenwashing), industry structure, specific firms (e.g. wine, banking, retail).

---

### XVI. No Single Optimal Solution

Important idea: In strategy there is no one “correct” answer.

1. “It is impossible to find the optimal… Only with business cases and illustrations… [we] try to [address] specific situations.”
2. “The question is not… what is the best decision according to [one formula]?”
3. Different good answers are possible depending on assumptions and priorities
4. “We can find two optimal solutions or… second-best solutions that also work.”

A. **Why:** Strategy is **social science**, not pure math. It involves
1. Policy – negotiation, convincing others
2. Non-rational factors – power, emotions, politics
3. Context – who decides, what they care about (e.g. family, unions, state)

B. Key point
1. “This is not pure science… This is a social science.”

---

### XVII. Real-World Constraints: Politics and People

A. Decisions are filtered by
1. Gatekeepers in the organization
2. Unions – e.g. “We are going to reduce the staff. No, no, no.”
3. State-owned companies – “It’s impossible… to fire people. The company is not profitable? Look for another solution.”
4. Family-owned firms – “40, 50% of companies… are family [owned]. The family… is the [main] matter. Profitability maybe could be a second issue.”

Example – merger: “The best way to improve [the] company might be to merge. But the other [company] has been the enemy of my family for decades… Impossible. Because the company is mine… I don’t care [what] technical or economic reports [say].”

B. Key insight
1. The “best” strategy on paper is often not chosen because of power, culture, and politics.

### XVIII. Four Elements of Successful Stories

A. **Framework (to be used in cases):** Successful strategic stories often share elements such as
1. Clear objectives
2. Self-knowledge and fit with the environment
3. External analysis (opportunities and threats)
4. Actions aligned with goals and context

Application: Use these (and course tools) when analyzing the “three people” case and other business cases.

## Key Takeaways

1. Strategy = from Greek “strategos”; competing for scarce resources with long-term impact
2. Strategic vs. tactical – strategic decisions are long-term and hard to reverse
3. Complexity – we simplify (e.g. 2–3 dimensions); no one can process “all” information
