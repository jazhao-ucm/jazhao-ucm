## Formal outline (Strategic Management, Week 12)

I. International strategy as a tradeoff problem.
   A. Core question raised in class.
      1. Should firms standardize globally for efficiency?
      2. Or adapt locally for market fit and customer acceptance?
   B. Main tension.
      1. Pressure for global cost reduction and scale.
      2. Pressure for local responsiveness by country.
   C. Managerial implication.
      1. Strategy choice depends on where value is created and where adaptation is required.
      2. The same firm may need different balances across product lines.

II. Four international strategy types (integration-responsiveness logic).
   A. **International strategy**.
      1. Low local adaptation, limited foreign customization.
      2. Core capabilities developed at home and transferred abroad.
   B. **Global strategy**.
      1. High standardization, centralized decisions.
      2. Scale economies and consistency are prioritized.
   C. **Multidomestic strategy**.
      1. High local adaptation by country market.
      2. Local units receive more autonomy for product, pricing, and marketing decisions.
   D. **Transnational strategy**.
      1. Simultaneous pressure for efficiency and local adaptation.
      2. Hardest model to execute because coordination and flexibility are both required.

III. Why adaptation matters in practice.
   A. Demand-side differences.
      1. Consumer preferences, language, and cultural meanings vary by country.
      2. The same value proposition does not transfer automatically.
   B. Institution-side differences.
      1. Regulation, legal environment, and channel structure differ across regions.
      2. Product positioning and operating model must account for institutional constraints.
   C. Pricing and willingness-to-pay differences.
      1. Price points that work in one market can fail in another.
      2. Premium positioning requires market-specific evidence of perceived value.

IV. Why standardization still matters.
   A. Efficiency logic.
      1. Concentrated production can lower unit costs through economies of scale.
      2. Shared design and operations can reduce complexity.
   B. Brand consistency logic.
      1. Some categories rely on globally stable identity and quality cues.
      2. Standardization can support trust for international customers.
   C. Location logic.
      1. In some industries, production concentration is driven by resource, capability, or heritage constraints.
      2. Not all value-chain activities are equally movable.

V. Industry and category examples discussed.
   A. Luxury and origin-sensitive categories.
      1. Cases where production remains concentrated to preserve authenticity and signaling.
      2. Global commercialization with selective adaptation around communication and distribution.
   B. Food and beverage examples.
      1. Taste and usage contexts vary across countries.
      2. Product mix may require local adaptation even when brand identity is global.
   C. Industrial and transport examples.
      1. Global production logic can deliver major scale benefits.
      2. But downstream adaptation still appears in service, regulation, and market access.

VI. Cultural and institutional distance as strategic variables.
   A. Cultural distance.
      1. Communication style, authority expectations, and social norms affect market response.
      2. Leadership and marketing choices should reflect local context.
   B. Institutional distance.
      1. Political, legal, and economic systems shape entry risk and operating constraints.
      2. Country choice should include risk-adjusted feasibility, not only market size.
   C. Decision implication.
      1. Entry mode, adaptation level, and rollout speed should be linked to distance analysis.
      2. One-country success is not proof of automatic transfer.

VII. Strategic recommendation logic from class.
   A. Avoid extreme one-size-fits-all reasoning.
      1. Pure standardization can miss local demand and regulation.
      2. Pure localization can destroy scale and coordination benefits.
   B. Use a deliberate hybrid logic when needed.
      1. Standardize core elements that create efficiency and brand consistency.
      2. Adapt market-facing elements where local differences are decisive.
   C. Evaluate by fit, not ideology.
      1. Match strategy type to category economics and country context.
      2. Reassess as competitive and macro conditions change.

- Main lecture theme was the integration-versus-responsiveness tradeoff in international strategy.
- Four strategy types were emphasized: international, global, multidomestic, transnational.
- Local adaptation is driven by consumer, regulatory, and pricing differences.
- Global standardization is driven by scale, efficiency, and consistency.
- Best strategic choice is context-dependent and often requires selective hybrid design.
