# Section 1: Summary (AP Level / Accessible)

*Use this section first. Plain language. Short sentences.*

## Key Words
- **Competitive advantage** = Something important the firm does better than rivals; based on industry key success factors; must be substantial and sustainable.
- **Competitive strategy** = Offensive–defensive actions to create a defendable position in the industry and achieve better performance (how to compete).
- **Cost leadership** = Being able to offer similar quality at lower cost than rivals.
- **Differentiation** = Offering something customers see as unique so they are willing to pay more or stay loyal.
- **Focus** = Competing in a narrow segment (niche), with cost or differentiation.
- **Stuck in the middle** = Neither clearly low-cost nor clearly differentiated → weak position.

## Main Ideas (Simple Version)

1. **Competitive advantage = better position in the market.**
   - Comes from firm features linked to key success factors (cost, quality, service, innovation, etc.).
   - Must be large enough and last long enough to matter for performance.

2. **Competitive strategy = how to win vs competitors.**
   - Offensive/defensive moves to create and defend advantage.
   - Links resources/capabilities (RBV) to market behaviour (Five Forces).

3. **Three generic strategies (Porter).**
   - **Cost leadership:** lower cost than rivals for similar product.
   - **Differentiation:** customers perceive uniqueness and accept higher price.
   - **Focus:** serve one segment deeply (cost focus or differentiation focus).

4. **Danger of ``stuck in the middle''.**
   - Firm that is neither clear low-cost nor clearly differentiated suffers low margins and no strong customer loyalty.
   - Important risk in mature, competitive industries.

5. **Drivers of cost leadership.**
   - Economies of scale, learning, process innovation, standardised product design, input cost advantages, high capacity utilisation, residual efficiency (organisation, motivation).

6. **Strategy clock idea (from illustrations).**
   - Different combinations of price and perceived value.
   - Only some positions are viable long-term; others lead to failure (e.g. high price/low value).

# Section 2: Full Notes (from Unit 5 Slides)

## I. Competitive Advantage and Strategy (5.1)

### A. Competitive advantage
1. Firm features that distinguish it from rivals and give it a better position in the market.
2. Usually related to industry \textbf{key success factors} (e.g. cost, quality, brand, service).
3. Should be \textbf{substantial} (big enough) and \textbf{sustainable} (over time).
4. Linked to added value: customers perceive more value or firm produces at lower cost.

### B. Competitive strategy
1. Offensive–defensive actions to build a defendable position and achieve superior performance.
2. Answers: \textbf{How do we compete in this industry to overcome competitors?}
3. Connects external analysis (Five Forces) with internal resources and capabilities (RBV/VRIO).

---

## II. Cost Leadership Strategy (5.2)

### A. Definition
1. Cost leadership advantage when firm costs are lower than competitors / industry average for a product/service of similar features/quality.
2. Allows the firm to:
   - Maintain industry price and earn higher margins, or
   - Cut prices and still earn acceptable margins to gain market share.

### B. Main drivers of cost advantage (Grant, Navas \& Guerras)
1. **Economies of scale:** input–output relations, indivisibilities, specialisation.
2. **Learning economies:** accumulated experience improves skills and routines.
3. **Production techniques:** process innovation, re-engineering.
4. **Product design:** standardisation, design for manufacture.
5. **Input costs:** access to cheap raw materials, favourable location, bargaining power.
6. **Capacity utilisation:** spreading fixed costs, lean/JIT production.
7. **Residual efficiency:** organisation quality, employee motivation, X-inefficiency reduction.

---

## III. Differentiation Strategy (5.3)

### A. Definition
1. Strategy based on \textbf{uniqueness perception}: customers see the firm's offer as different and preferable.
2. Can be based on many dimensions: brand, quality, design, technology, service, image.
3. Allows firm to charge a premium price and/or protect volume against rivals.

### B. Conditions for successful differentiation
1. Attributes must matter to customers (willingness to pay).
2. Difference must be difficult to copy quickly (barriers to imitation).
3. Extra price or loyalty must compensate extra costs of differentiation.

---

## IV. Focus Strategy and ``Stuck in the Middle'' (5.4)

### A. Focus
1. Firm chooses a \textbf{narrow segment}: specific customer group, geography, or niche.
2. Can be combined with cost focus (low-cost in that niche) or differentiation focus (unique for that niche).

### B. Stuck in the middle
1. Firm does not have clear cost advantage or clear differentiation.
2. Faces low margins (cannot charge premium) and weak loyalty (customers can switch).
3. Common risk when trying to mix strategies without coherence.

---

## V. Industry Evolution and Strategy (5.5) \textit{(to complete with class discussion)}

1. Different stages of industry life cycle (emergence, growth, maturity, decline) favour different strategies.
2. Early stages: innovation, differentiation, experimentation.
3. Mature stages: cost efficiency, consolidation, focus.
4. Decline: harvesting, exit, or niche strategies.
