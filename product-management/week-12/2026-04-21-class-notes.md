## Formal outline (Product Management, Week 12)

I. Diffusion of innovation as market reality.
   A. Adoption segments discussed in class.
      1. Innovators.
      2. Early adopters.
      3. Early majority.
      4. Late majority.
      5. Laggards.
   B. Core message.
      1. Different segments buy for different reasons.
      2. Product strategy must adapt by segment rather than assume one universal launch logic.

II. Chasm problem between early adopters and early majority.
   A. Failure point emphasized.
      1. Many products gain initial traction but fail to reach mainstream adoption.
      2. This transition was framed as the critical risk zone.
   B. Segment expectation differences.
      1. Early users tolerate experimentation and product roughness.
      2. Mainstream users expect reliability, references, and lower perceived risk.
   C. Practical implication.
      1. Product teams must redesign messaging, evidence, and delivery for pragmatic buyers.

III. Bowling alley sequencing logic.
   A. Metaphor used in lecture.
      1. Treat target niches as pins.
      2. Knock one down first, then use momentum to hit adjacent pins.
   B. Entry strategy.
      1. Choose one specific segment with clear pain and attainable proof.
      2. Build a strong beachhead case before broad expansion.
   C. Scaling strategy.
      1. Expand segment by segment.
      2. Avoid trying to sell to all segments at once.

IV. Why products fail to scale adoption.
   A. Feature bias.
      1. Teams over-invest in features and under-invest in adoption design.
      2. Product novelty is mistaken for broad market readiness.
   B. Transfer assumption error.
      1. Early-adopter demand is incorrectly assumed to represent mainstream demand.
      2. Teams skip the proof mechanisms mainstream users require.
   C. Commercial mismatch.
      1. Willingness-to-pay differs across segments.
      2. Pricing and value propositions must be tuned to adoption stage.

V. Segment-specific behavior and PM decisions.
   A. Innovators and early adopters.
      1. Motivated by novelty, speed, and first access.
      2. More willing to experiment with incomplete systems.
   B. Early majority.
      1. Motivated by practical value and social validation.
      2. Adoption depends on trust, reliability, and reference evidence.
   C. Late majority and laggards.
      1. Adoption follows standardization and risk reduction.
      2. Price sensitivity and ecosystem maturity become stronger purchase drivers.

VI. Strategic PM takeaway.
   A. Product-market fit is staged, not one-time.
      1. Fit must be re-earned at each segment boundary.
      2. Roadmaps should reflect adoption stage constraints.
   B. Go-to-market and product design must stay coupled.
      1. Messaging, onboarding, and pricing are part of adoption mechanics.
      2. Adoption should be managed as a process, not a single launch event.
