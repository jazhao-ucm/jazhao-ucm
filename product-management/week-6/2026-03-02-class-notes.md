## Key Concepts

### I. Don't Ask Users What They Want

Purpose: Users often cannot articulate their needs. Asking directly leads to wrong solutions.

A. Key insight (Steve Jobs / Henry Ford)

1. "People don't know what they want until you show it to them."
2. Users will give you answers. But those answers are often wrong.
3. They describe what they think they want, not what they need.

B. Why asking fails

1. Users are not designers.
2. They respond to what they know. Not what could exist.
3. They may say what they think you want to hear.
4. Behavior reveals more than words.

C. What to do instead

1. Observe behavior. Watch what they do.
2. Ask about the past: "What happened when...?"
3. Ask about problems: "What frustrates you?"
4. Do not ask: "What would you like?" or "What would you want if we built X?"

---

### II. Design Thinking Process

Purpose: Human-centered approach to innovation. Understand before you build.

A. Stages (IDEO / Stanford d.school)

1. **Empathize**
   a. Understand the user. Their context. Their pain.
   b. Go where they are. Observe.
   c. Do not assume. Listen.

2. **Define**
   a. Synthesize what you learned.
   b. Find the real problem. Not the surface complaint.
   c. Frame the problem clearly.

3. **Ideate**
   a. Generate many ideas. No judgment.
   b. Build on others' ideas.
   c. Diverse perspectives help.

4. **Prototype**
   a. Build something tangible. Fast. Cheap.
   b. Not the final product. A test.
   c. Learn from it.

5. **Test**
   a. Put it in front of users.
   b. Observe. Iterate.
   c. Repeat.

B. Key principle

1. This is a process. Not a one-time event.
2. Iterate. Go back to empathize when you learn something new.

---

### III. Observation and Empathy

Purpose: Observation reveals what asking cannot.

A. Go to the Gemba

1. Gemba = the real place. Where the work happens.
2. Do not design from a desk.
3. Go to the store. The hospital. The home.
4. See the context.

B. Two observers rule

1. When observing users, send two people.
2. One person may have a biased interpretation.
3. Two people = different observations = richer insight.
4. Compare notes. Synthesize.

C. What to observe

1. Behavior. What do they do? In what order?
2. Environment. Where are they? What's around them?
3. Pain points. Where do they struggle? Hesitate?
4. Workarounds. What do they do to compensate?

---

### IV. Extreme Users

Purpose: People who use a product intensely reveal problems the average user doesn't.

A. IDEO shopping cart example

1. Challenge: redesign the shopping cart.
2. Research: went to stores. Observed. Found extreme users.
3. Insight: ~22,000 children hospitalized yearly from shopping cart injuries.
4. Extreme users: parents with kids, elderly, store employees.
5. Learned from them. Not from the "average" shopper.

B. Why extreme users matter

1. They push the product to its limits.
2. They have strong needs. Clear pain.
3. Solutions for them often help everyone.
4. They expose flaws the average user tolerates.

C. How to find them

1. Who uses this product the most?
2. Who has the most problems with it?
3. Who uses it in unusual ways?
4. Go to them. Observe. Interview.

---

### V. Team Diversity (IDEO Model)

Purpose: Different perspectives lead to better design.

A. IDEO team composition

1. Not just engineers. Or just designers.
2. Psychologists, linguists, biologists, engineers.
3. Different backgrounds = different questions.
4. Different questions = better solutions.

B. Example: shopping cart team

1. Peter (engineer, 35). Team lead. Not because of seniority.
2. Diverse team: linguist, psychologist, biology major.
3. Each sees different aspects of the problem.
4. Synthesis = stronger solution.

C. Key insight

1. "Experts in the process, not experts in the product."
2. IDEO designs toothbrushes, tractors, medical equipment.
3. Same process. Different domains.
4. The process transfers.

---

### VI. Corporate Culture and User Research

Purpose: Culture can block good user research.

A. The desk trap

1. Some bosses reward people at their desks.
2. "Good employees" = visible, present, in the office.
3. But the best insight comes from the field.
4. People talking to customers are often invisible to management.

B. Contrast

1. Corporate America: measure by desk time.
2. Design thinking: measure by customer contact.
3. The people who get information are "out there."
4. Talking to users. Observing. Learning.

C. Implication

1. Support and reward user research.
2. Going to the Gemba is work. Not a distraction.
3. Protect time for observation and empathy.

## Key Takeaways

1. Don't ask users what they want. Observe behavior. Ask about problems and past experience.
2. Design thinking: empathize → define → ideate → prototype → test. Iterate.
3. Go to the Gemba. Observe in real context. Use two observers to reduce bias.
4. Extreme users reveal insights the average user doesn't. Find them. Learn from them.
5. Diverse teams (psychologists, engineers, etc.) ask better questions and find better solutions.
6. IDEO: experts in the process, not the product. Process transfers across domains.
7. Corporate culture: reward customer contact, not just desk time.

## Exam Prep – Very Short Version (Design Thinking & Gemba)

1. **Don’t just ask, observe:** Users often cannot explain what they need; watch behaviour in context and ask about past experience.
2. **Design thinking loop:** Empathise → define → ideate → prototype → test; you can always map a case answer onto these five steps.
3. **Go to the Gemba:** Real insight comes from the place where the work or use happens, not from your desk.
4. **Extreme users:** Look at unusual or intense users to spot problems and opportunities that average users hide.
5. **Process expertise:** Good design teams are experts in the process, not in a single product category; mention IDEO and diverse teams in answers.
