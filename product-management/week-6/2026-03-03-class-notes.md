## Key Words
- **Brainstorming rules** = One conversation at a time. No judging. Wild ideas. Build on others.
- **Prototype** = A quick, rough version you build to test an idea.
- **Design thinking is not magic** = A good process does not fix a bad business model.
- **Interview guide** = List of questions you use when talking to customers.
- **Behavioral questions** = Ask about real past events, not dreams or “features.”

## Main Ideas (Simple Version)

1. **IDEO shows design thinking in real life.**
   - They use clear rules for brainstorming.
   - They turn many ideas into a few prototypes.
   - They test in the real world (supermarket).

2. **The office supports creativity.**
   - Flexible spaces, wheels on furniture, playful details.
   - Culture: ask forgiveness, not permission.
   - The process is serious, even if the vibe looks fun.

3. **Design thinking has limits.**
   - Great design cannot save a weak business model.
   - Example: folding bike company with a good product but bad market fit.
   - You still need channels, pricing, demand, and strategy.

4. **Good interviews focus on real stories.**
   - Ask: “Tell me about the last time…”, “What happened when…”
   - Do not ask: “What features do you want?”, “Imagine an app that…”
   - Avoid yes/no questions and leading questions.

5. **Prepare a structured interview guide.**
   - 10–15 questions is enough.
   - Start with context, then specific situations, then feelings and reflections.
   - Use the same script across the team to compare answers.

## I. Design Thinking in Practice: IDEO Shopping Cart (Continuation)

Purpose: See how the design thinking stages (empathize, ideate, prototype, test) actually play out in a real firm.

A. Brainstorming (Ideation) Rules

1. IDEO’s classic rules (posted on the wall)
   a. One conversation at a time.
   b. Defer judgment. No criticizing ideas during brainstorming.
   c. Encourage wild ideas.
   d. Build on the ideas of others (“yes, and…”).
   e. Go for quantity first, quality later.

2. Why the rules matter
   a. Separate **idea generation** from **evaluation**.
   b. Protect fragile early ideas from being killed too soon.
   c. Create psychological safety so everyone contributes.

3. Process in the video
   a. Dozens of ideas posted on the walls on sticky notes.
   b. Team clusters and filters ideas after the free‑flow phase.
   c. They converge on a smaller set of promising directions to prototype.

B. From Ideas to Prototypes

1. Time‑boxed prototyping
   a. Teams are told: this is “prototype day.”
   b. Limited time forces decisions and action.
   c. Imperfect but concrete beats a perfect idea in someone’s head.

2. Multiple teams, multiple angles
   a. Different sub‑teams explore different aspects:
      - Safety
      - Checkout
      - Navigation / steering
      - Basket and storage
   b. At the end, they combine “best elements” into one integrated cart concept.

3. Example prototype features (from the video)
   a. Removable baskets instead of one big cart.
   b. Cart frame that can move sideways more easily in aisles.
   c. Concept of scanners or tech to speed checkout (future‑oriented).

4. Testing in a real supermarket
   a. They wheel the prototype into an actual store.
   b. Store manager and shoppers react: some enthusiasm, some concerns.
   c. The purpose is learning, not proving they were right.

C. Culture and Space at IDEO

1. Playful but intentional environment
   a. Music, flexible furniture on wheels, improvised work areas.
   b. People can build or rearrange their own workstations.
   c. Even “unnecessary” things (like DC‑3 airplane sound effects) add to the creative culture.

2. “Ask forgiveness, not permission”
   a. People try things first, see if anyone complains later.
   b. Encourages experimentation over bureaucracy.
   c. Fits with rapid prototyping mindset.

3. Key point
   a. Space and culture are designed to **support** the process.
   b. But process and culture alone do not guarantee business success.

## II. The Limits of Design Thinking: Folding Bike Case

Purpose: Challenge the naive belief that “if we use design thinking, the product will succeed.”

A. Story Outline (Folding Bicycle)

1. Company designs a folding bike.
   a. Uses user research, prototyping, iteration.
   b. Technically well‑designed, clever engineering.
   c. Looks like a textbook design‑thinking success story.

2. Market reality
   a. Bicycle market already crowded with many brands.
   b. Retailers have limited space and established relationships.
   c. Price point high compared to alternatives.

3. Real‑world adoption
   a. Potential users (e.g., students, commuters) may not actually need or want a folding bike.
   b. Many students live near campus or already use public transport.
   c. Existing habits and infrastructure make behavior change unlikely.

B. Why It Failed (Even with Good Design)

1. Strategy and market problems
   a. Over‑saturated market: hard to stand out in the “red ocean.”
   b. Distribution: retailers push existing brands with better margins.
   c. Channel incentives misaligned with the new product.

2. Misreading demand
   a. Desire to solve a “cool” problem vs. a real pain point.
   b. Aspirational use cases (bike to school, fold, store) did not match actual behavior.
   c. People already had workable solutions (walk, metro, bus, standard bikes).

3. Lesson
   a. Design thinking ≠ automatic product‑market fit.
   b. You still need:
      - Attractive economics (price, costs, margins),
      - Clear target segment and strong pain point,
      - Realistic go‑to‑market (channels, partners, incentives).

4. Professor’s message
   a. Design thinking is powerful but not magic.
   b. Use it **with** strategy, not instead of strategy.

## III. Designing Good Customer Interviews

Purpose: Help students move from bad, leading questions to strong, behavioral interviews that support real discovery.

A. Interview Structure (Three Stages)

1. Stage 1 – Opening and context
   a. Introduce yourself and the purpose in simple terms.
   b. Ask general context questions:
      - “Tell me about a normal weekday at university.”
      - “How do you usually meet or stay in touch with friends?”

2. Stage 2 – Specific situations and stories
   a. Move quickly to concrete events:
      - “Tell me about the last time you felt left out of something because you heard too late.”
      - “Describe the last time you tried to make plans with a group and it got messy.”
   b. Ask follow‑ups: what happened, what they did, what they felt.

3. Stage 3 – Reflection and wrap‑up
   a. Ask what is most frustrating or most important to them.
   b. Ask what they would change **in their experience**, not in your app.
   c. Thank them, check if they want to hear about the results later.

B. Do’s and Don’ts for Interview Questions

1. Do: Behavioral, past‑tense questions
   a. “Tell me about the last time you ___.”
   b. “What happened when you tried to___?”
   c. “How did you handle ___ the last time it came up?”

2. Do: Open questions
   a. Avoid yes/no.
   b. Use “how,” “what,” “when,” “tell me about…”

3. Don’t: Ask for solutions or features
   a. “What features would you like?” – forbidden.
   b. “If you could design an app to do X, what would it do?” – forbidden.
   c. “Would you use an app that…?” – hypothetical and unreliable.

4. Don’t: Lead the witness
   a. Questions that contain the answer:
      - “Do you feel more connected when you use app X?”
      - “Don’t you think it would be useful if…?”
   b. These produce polite agreement, not insight.

5. Don’t: Overload with too many questions
   a. 10–15 focused questions is usually enough.
   b. You can always ask follow‑ups based on their stories.

C. Example: Student Project on Friends / Roommates

1. Student idea
   a. An app to help 18–25 year‑olds find friends or roommates who match their habits.
   b. They drafted an interview script for this.

2. Questions that **worked** (behavioral)
   a. “Can you briefly describe your overall experience with finding friends in [location]?”
   b. “How did you meet your current friends or roommates?”
   c. “How well do your routines and habits fit together?”
   d. “Tell me about a time you felt left out because you heard about something too late.”
   e. “Describe a recent moment when you felt really connected to others.”

3. Questions that were **problematic**
   a. “If you used a matching platform, what features would you like?” – solution‑focused.
   b. “Imagine an app that actually helps you feel less alone, what should it do?” – speculative.
   c. These belong (if at all) much later, after you deeply understand the problem.

4. Professor’s corrections
   a. Rephrase into behavioral questions.
   b. Replace “which features” with “tell me about the last time you tried to solve this.”
   c. Remove “imagine an app…” until you are in a **solution test**, not discovery.

## IV. Building the Team Interview Guide

Purpose: Ensure all team members collect comparable data.

A. One Shared Script

1. All teams will merge their drafts into **one** common script.
2. Same order and wording of core questions.
3. This allows you to compare answers across interviewees and across teams.

B. Customization and Flexibility

1. The script is a guide, not a prison.
   a. You can ask natural follow‑ups.
   b. You can skip questions that clearly don’t apply.

2. Key is consistency on the **main** questions.
   a. Everyone asks the same “tell me about the last time…” questions.
   b. Everyone avoids leading, feature‑based questions.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. IDEO shows how design thinking works in practice: rules for brainstorming, heavy use of prototypes, and real‑world testing.
2. Office design and culture can support creativity, but they are not enough on their own.
3. Design thinking is powerful but not a magic wand; a product can still fail if the market, channel, or economics are wrong.
4. Good customer interviews focus on real past behavior, not hypothetical features or imagined apps.
5. A clear, shared interview guide helps teams collect comparable, high‑quality data for product decisions.
