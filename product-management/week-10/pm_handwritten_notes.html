★ Cost vs. Price · Fixed/Variable Costs · Economies of Scale
I. Cost vs. Price
  • Cost = what you spend to produce  Price = what you charge
    • low cost ≠ low price — it's a strategic choice
    • low cost → higher margins or lower price for market share
II. Fixed vs. Variable Costs
Fixed
  • don't change w/ volume
  • building, insurance, salaries
  • pay even at zero output
Variable
  • change w/ volume
  • raw materials, packaging
  • more output = more cost
★ high fixed costs → need high volume to spread them out
III. Economies of Scale
  • bigger production = cheaper per unit
  • sources: spread fixed costs · labor specialization · bulk purchasing · learning effects
  • limits: diseconomies at very large scale — harder to coordinate, less flex
IV–V. Mass Production & Low-Cost Strategy
  • requires: high volume + standardization + efficient processes
  • benefits: lower unit cost, consistent quality
  • risks: race to bottom · thin margins · quality suffers
VI. Differentiation Strategy
  • compete on uniqueness, not price → premium pricing
  • examples: Apple · Mercedes · Starbucks
  • smaller market, higher margins, less price-sensitive customers
VII–VIII. Cost Structure & Break-Even
  • components: direct materials · direct labor · overhead · admin
  • ask: which costs are fixed vs variable? where can we cut?
Break-even units = Fixed Costs ÷ (Price − Variable Cost/unit)
  • below break-even = loss  /  above = profit
  • use for: pricing decisions, volume targets, investment calls

★ Car Industry · McDonald's Case · Speed vs. Customization
I. Car Industry Efficiency
  • many brands competing on efficiency as advantage
  • some brands = divisions of parent co. (Lexus = Toyota)
  • ★ look at parent co. performance, not just brand market share
II. Case: McDonald's Production Systems
Old System (forecast-based)
  • predict demand → pre-prepare
  • problem: waste, cold food
  • limited menu flex
New: "Made for You"
  • produce when ordered
  • customization possible
  • fresher food, less waste
III–IV. Forecast vs. Responsive Production
core trade-off: Speed vs. Flexibility  /  Cost vs. Customization
  • forecast-based → fast delivery but risk of waste / stockouts
  • make-to-order → no waste, slower delivery, customization ✓
  • which to choose depends on your target customer
V. No One-Size-Fits-All
  • McDonald's → balance speed + customization
  • Subway → high customization, slower
  • Toyota → efficiency · BMW → performance/luxury · Tesla → innovation
VI. Production Strategy Options
Make-to-Stock
  • produce in advance
  • immediate delivery
  • risk of obsolescence
Make-to-Order
  • produce when ordered
  • no finished goods inventory
  • lead time required
Assemble-to-Order = pre-make components, assemble on demand → best of both (ex: Dell)
VII. Inventory Trade-Offs
  • high inventory → fast delivery but carrying costs + waste risk
  • low inventory → less cost but longer lead times + stockout risk
  • JIT (Just-in-Time) → minimize inventory, receive materials when needed · requires reliable suppliers (Toyota Production System)
