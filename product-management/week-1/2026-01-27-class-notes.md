# Product Management Class Notes
## January 27, 2026 Week 1

**Topic:** Rational vs. irrational decision making; information and emotion; patent strategy; open innovation.

---

I. Rational vs. Irrational Decision Making
   A. Core principle
      1. Decisions based on both rationality and emotion
      2. Not purely rational process
   B. Information-emotion relationship
      1. More info → more rational decisions
      2. Less info → more emotional decisions
      3. "The less information we have, the more we rely on emotion"
   C. Circle model
      1. Black circle = limited information (~5% of what needed)
      2. Emotion fills the gap
      3. Structured info reduces emotional reliance

II. Behavioral Economics & Consumer Decisions
   A. Factors influencing decisions
      1. Information availability
      2. Risk perception
      3. Opportunity assessment
      4. Brand reputation
      5. Emotion and gut reactions
   B. Key insight
      1. People don't make perfectly rational decisions
      2. Context and framing matter

III. Brand Strategy & Information Asymmetry
   A. Strong brands
      1. Less need to share detailed info
      2. Rely on emotional connections
      3. Create trust through reputation
      4. Ex: Chanel, luxury brands
   B. Weak brands
      1. Must provide information themselves
      2. Need to help customers decide
      3. More effort required to build trust
   C. Strategic implication
      1. Brand strength determines info strategy
      2. Strong brand = emotion; Weak brand = information

IV. Case Study Chanel & Luxury Brands
   A. Why not sell on Amazon
      1. Would diminish brand prestige
      2. Loss of controlled experience
      3. Exclusivity justifies high prices (€3,000+)
   B. Strategy
      1. Rely on emotion and exclusivity
      2. No need for detailed product info
      3. Scarcity creates desire

V. Market Signaling & Asymmetric Information
   A. The "lemons market" problem
      1. Products that look good but have hidden issues
      2. Ex: used cars, AI-generated product ads
   B. Solution
      1. Brands signal quality when buyers have limited info
      2. Reputation reduces information asymmetry

VI. Patent Strategy & Technology Protection
   A. Two purposes of patents
      1. Prevent competitors from stealing technology
      2. Prevent competitors from blocking your innovation
   B. Patent portfolios
      1. Strategic tool for negotiation
      2. "Patent thickets" = barriers around technology
      3. Build portfolio even in non-core areas
   C. Process
      1. Check competitor patents
      2. Map patent landscape in your area
      3. Build portfolio for leverage

VII. Case Study Solar Panel Competition
   A. Scenario
      1. Firm A developing solar tech
      2. Firm B has existing patents
   B. Solutions
      1. Pay Firm B for license
      2. Develop own technology (more R&D)
      3. Acquire Firm B
      4. Build patent portfolio for negotiation

VIII. Open Innovation
   A. Definition
      1. Sharing knowledge/technology for free
      2. Benefits industry as a whole
   B. Why companies do this
      1. Create industry standards
      2. Generate demand for complementary products
      3. Build ecosystem around technology

IX. Case Study IBM & Open Source
   A. Strategy
      1. Made development platform open source
      2. Gave away for free
   B. Result
      1. Created industry standard
      2. Generated demand for IBM hardware/servers
      3. Real profit from infrastructure, not software
   C. Lesson
      1. Value capture through complementary assets
      2. Free product can create paid demand

X. David Teece Open Innovation Theory
   A. Background
      1. Vice president at IBM
      2. Developed open innovation framework
   B. Key insight
      1. Sharing knowledge can create more value than hoarding
      2. Ecosystem thinking > individual firm thinking

XI. Case Study Xerox PARC
   A. Innovations developed
      1. Computer mouse
      2. Graphical user interface (GUI)
      3. Ethernet
      4. Laser printing
   B. The problem
      1. Great at creating technology
      2. Terrible at commercialization
      3. Sold technologies for almost nothing
   C. Lesson
      1. Innovation alone not enough
      2. Need commercialization strategy
      3. Value creation ≠ value capture

XII. Samsung Business Model
   A. Three main businesses
      1. Consumer electronics (phones, TVs)
      2. Semiconductors (chips, components)
      3. Licensing (technology rights)
   B. Revenue breakdown
      1. 40% from licensing
      2. 60% from products
   C. Key insight
      1. Licensing as major revenue stream
      2. Diversified value capture

XIII. Value Capture Mechanisms Review
   A. Brand
      1. Reputation and trust
      2. Allows premium pricing
   B. Market access
      1. Distribution channels
      2. Customer relationships
   C. IP or secrecy
      1. Patents for legal protection
      2. Trade secrets for proprietary info
   D. Key principle
      1. Multiple mechanisms needed
      2. Combine for stronger position
