# Product Management — Class Notes
## January 26, 2026 — Week 1

**Topic:** Value creation vs. value capture; market access.

---

I. Value Creation vs. Value Capture
   A. Value creation
      1. Total value delivered to users/customers
      2. Making something useful that solves a problem
   B. Value capture
      1. Money the firm actually earns from the value created
      2. Gap between creation and capture often large
   C. Key distinction
      1. Creating value ≠ capturing value
      2. Need both for business success

II. Michael Porter — Value Chain Framework
   A. Definition
      1. Breakdown of firm into value-creating activities
      2. Shows how resources combine to deliver value
   B. Primary activities
      1. Inbound logistics
      2. Operations
      3. Outbound logistics
      4. Marketing and sales
      5. Service
   C. Support activities
      1. Firm infrastructure
      2. Human resource management
      3. Technology development
      4. Procurement

III. David Teece — Complementary Assets
   A. Core idea
      1. Market access determines value capture
      2. Great product alone not enough
   B. Types of complementary assets
      1. Distribution networks
      2. Customer relationships
      3. Brand reputation
      4. Manufacturing capability
   C. Strategic implication
      1. Build complementary assets
      2. Or acquire them through partnerships/M&A

IV. Case Study — Spanish Research & Takeda
   A. Background
      1. Spanish researchers developed technology
      2. Initial valuation: €15,000
   B. Outcome
      1. Takeda (Japanese pharma) bought it
      2. Market value: €250M/year
      3. Market size: 150,000 patients
   C. Why Takeda captured value
      1. Global distribution network
      2. Strong healthcare brand
      3. Resources to scale production
   D. Lesson
      1. Spanish company created value
      2. Takeda captured value
      3. Market access = key differentiator

V. Three Mechanisms of Value Capture
   A. Patents
      1. Legal protection for innovation
      2. Helps capture value even after sale
      3. Not sufficient alone without market access
   B. Branding
      1. Customer trust and recognition
      2. Allows premium pricing
      3. Ex: Takeda brand in healthcare
   C. Market access
      1. Distribution channels
      2. Sales force
      3. Geographic presence
      4. Customer relationships

VI. Cost-Specialized Assets
   A. Definition
      1. Assets specialized to firm's business
      2. Difficult for competitors to replicate
   B. Examples
      1. Proprietary technology
      2. Trade secrets (ex: Coca-Cola formula)
      3. Exclusive supplier relationships
      4. Specialized manufacturing equipment

VII. European Companies — Challenges
   A. Problems
      1. Smaller market reach vs. global players
      2. Limited distribution networks
      3. Less brand recognition internationally
   B. Consequences
      1. Often sell innovations to larger firms
      2. Lose value capture to US/Japan/China companies
      3. Scale and access determine who wins

VIII. Product Management Goal — CSDA
   A. Framework components
      1. Customer-focused
      2. Strategic
      3. Data-driven
      4. Action-oriented
   B. Core principle
      1. Create value for users
      2. Capture value for the firm
