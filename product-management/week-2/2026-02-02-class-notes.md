# Product Management — Class Notes
## February 2, 2026 — Week 2

**Topic:** Community needs assessment; finding connections; willingness to pay.

---

I. Community Needs Assessment
   A. Core principle
      1. "Community before product"
      2. Understand needs before building
      3. Don't build and hope they will come
   B. Process
      1. Find connections between people with similar interests
      2. Assess what community actually needs
      3. Understand willingness to pay
   C. Key insight
      1. Build community first, then product
      2. Community validates the problem exists

II. Finding User Needs
   A. The "box of space"
      1. Find gaps in current solutions
      2. What is happening vs. what they want
      3. Identify unmet needs
   B. Methods
      1. Observe behavior
      2. Ask about problems
      3. Test willingness to pay
   C. Key insight
      1. Personal problem ≠ market problem
      2. Your problem may not be everyone's problem

III. Development Cycles
   A. Iterative process
      1. First sprint — initial development
      2. Testing with users
      3. Iteration and improvement
      4. Repeat cycle
   B. Key principle
      1. Each cycle develops new feature
      2. Prioritize investment in most important aspects
      3. Testing as investing
   C. Book reference
      1. "Testing as Investing" approach
      2. Testing is part of development, not separate

IV. Case Study — Social App Failure
   A. The story
      1. App to help people meet friends
      2. AI matching based on interests
      3. Free to use initially
   B. The problem
      1. No one willing to pay
      2. Traveled all States, no sales
      3. Spent significant money
   C. Lessons learned
      1. Test willingness to pay early
      2. "Build it and they will come" = mistake
      3. Personal need ≠ market demand

V. Willingness to Pay
   A. Key question
      1. "Would you pay for this?"
      2. Must test early
   B. Who might pay
      1. People who moved to new area
      2. People looking for roommates
      3. Specific use cases
   C. Reality check
      1. If no one wants to pay, product not solving real problem
      2. Free users ≠ paying customers

VI. Pivoting the Business Model
   A. When to pivot
      1. No willingness to pay
      2. Wrong target market
      3. Wrong value proposition
   B. New strategies
      1. Partnerships with universities
      2. B2B instead of B2C
      3. Different monetization model
   C. Example
      1. Social app pivoted to university partnerships
      2. Focus on onboarding new students
      3. Solve retention problem for schools

VII. Business Model Alternatives
   A. Initial model — direct payment
      1. Users pay for service
      2. Often fails for social apps
   B. Partnership model
      1. Work with universities/corporations
      2. They pay, users get free
      3. Solve their problems (retention, engagement)
   C. Advertising model (Google)
      1. Free service for users
      2. Revenue from advertisers
      3. Value creation through free, value capture through ads

VIII. Success Metrics
   A. Possible metrics
      1. Number of matches
      2. Successful connections
      3. User engagement
      4. Paid subscriptions
   B. Key principle
      1. Define success before building
      2. Metrics align with business model
   C. Reality
      1. Paying customers = strongest signal
      2. Free users may not convert

IX. Branding & Information Strategy
   A. When to use branding
      1. Strong brand = rely on reputation
      2. Weak brand = provide information
   B. Investment decision
      1. Invest in brand vs. invest in information
      2. Depends on brand strength
   C. Key principle
      1. Information compensates for weak brand
      2. Strong brand reduces need for info

X. Service Quality & Expectations
   A. Review problem
      1. Reviews often polarized (1 or 5 stars)
      2. Middle ground rarely represented
   B. Service variability
      1. Services vary more than products
      2. Same restaurant, different experience
   C. Managing expectations
      1. Set realistic expectations
      2. Deliver consistently
