\section*{\color{eublue}1. Make to Stock vs.\ Make to Order}

\textbf{STOCK} = on the shelf \emph{before} anyone orders.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Make to Stock} = before orders. Ex.: supermarket bread, McDonald's (pre-made).
\item \textbf{Make to Order} = after order. Ex.: handmade piano, bespoke suit, McDonald's Made for You.
\item \textbf{Assemble to Order} = parts before, assemble after. Ex.: Dell, BMW.
\end{itemize}

\emph{Memory:} Stock = already there. Order = you asked, then we make it.

\section*{\color{eublue}2. Lean Validation vs.\ Design Thinking}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Design Thinking} = understand users by \textbf{observation} and \textbf{empathy}. Go where they are; watch; find the real problem. Stages: Empathize $\to$ Define $\to$ Ideate $\to$ Prototype $\to$ Test.
\item \textbf{Lean Validation} = test whether people will \textbf{pay} before building. Build–Measure–Learn; MVP; iterate or pivot.
\end{itemize}

Design Thinking = understand problem. Lean = test the money.

\section*{\color{eublue}3. Innovation: Sustaining/Disruptive, Radical/Incremental}

\textbf{Q1 — Sustaining or Disruptive?} Does something similar already exist? YES $\to$ Sustaining. NO or way cheaper / new market $\to$ Disruptive.

\textbf{Q2 — Radical or Incremental?} Did the core technology exist before? YES $\to$ Incremental. NO $\to$ Radical.

\section*{\color{eublue}4. Extreme Users vs.\ Target Personas}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Extreme users} = use product \emph{intensely} or in unusual ways; reveal hidden problems (e.g.\ IDEO cart, kids injured).
\item \textbf{Personas} = who you design \emph{for}; fictional typical customer. Define after observing.
\end{itemize}

Observe \textbf{extreme users} to find problems; define \textbf{personas} to describe who you serve.

\section*{\color{eublue}5. Innovator's Dilemma vs.\ S-Curve}

\textbf{S-Curve} = how technology performs over time: slow $\to$ rapid $\to$ plateau $\to$ decline. \textbf{Innovator's Dilemma} = why companies fail (e.g.\ Kodak, Blockbuster). Memory: S-Curve = graph; Innovator's Dilemma = company trap.

\section*{\color{eublue}6. Innovators vs.\ Early Adopters (Moore's Chasm)}

\begin{center}
\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Who} & \textbf{Accepts} \\
\midrule
Innovators & Tech enthusiasts & Unfinished; they tinker \\
Early Adopters & Visionaries & Product works; see vision \\
Early Majority & Pragmatists & Proven, references \\
Late Majority / Laggards & Skeptics & Last to adopt \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Memory: Innovators = broken, don't care. Early Adopters = works, see vision.

\section*{\color{eublue}7. Value Creation vs.\ Value Capture}

\textbf{Value creation} = making something useful. \textbf{Value capture} = money you make.

Three ways to capture value (Teece: complementary assets):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Brand} — customer trust, premium pricing, emotion over information (e.g.\ Chanel).
\item \textbf{Market access} — distribution, sales force, geographic presence, customer relationships (e.g.\ Takeda).
\item \textbf{IP/Patents} — legal protection, licensing (e.g.\ Samsung 40\% from licensing).
\end{itemize}

Great product is not enough; you need a way to \emph{capture} value (e.g.\ Takeda captured what Spanish researchers created).

\section*{\color{eublue}8. Break Even Formula}

\[ \text{Break Even (units)} = \frac{\text{Fixed Costs}}{\text{Price} - \text{Variable Cost}} \]

Bracket first, then divide. \textbf{Example:} Fixed €6,000, Price €40, Variable €20 $\Rightarrow$ 6,000 $\div$ (40$-$20) = 300 units. Each unit gives €20 above variable cost; need 300 to cover €6,000 fixed.

\section*{\color{eublue}9. Learning Effect}

The more you produce, the better and cheaper you get. Triggered by volume. Dacia: (1) Share CMF-A $\to$ massive volume $\to$ learning effect. (2) Low price $\to$ demand $\to$ volume $\to$ learning effect.

\section*{\color{eublue}10. Economies of Scale}

More units $\to$ lower cost per unit (fixed costs spread). Vs.\ Learning effect = getting better at the process through repetition.

\section*{\color{eublue}11. Competitive Strategies}

\textbf{Cost} = cheap, standardized, high volume (Dacia, Ryanair). \textbf{Differentiation} = unique, premium (Apple, Chanel, Moley). \textbf{Response} = fast, flexible (Zara, FedEx). Clues: cheap$\to$Cost; premium$\to$Diff.; fast$\to$Response.

\section*{\color{eublue}12. Business Model Canvas — Nine Boxes}

Roof: Value Proposition. Right: Customer Segments, Channels, Customer Relationships. Left: Key Resources, Key Activities, Key Partners. Floor: Revenue Streams, Cost Structure. Golden rule: specific names; all nine tell one story.

\section*{\color{eublue}13. Design Thinking — Yiami Checklist}

Correct order: Observe $\to$ Personas $\to$ Needs Analysis $\to$ Ideate $\to$ Prototype $\to$ Test with real users. Yiami's mistakes: personas before observing; no needs analysis; straight to functionality; tested with developers. Good: ``Tell me about the last time...'' Bad: ``What features do you want?''

\section*{\color{eublue}14. The Four Magic Questions}

(1) Cost leadership, differentiation or response? (2) Mass producing or customizing? (3) Creating value or capturing it? (4) Exploring or exploiting?

Craftsman = differentiation. Factory = cost leadership.

\section*{\color{eublue}16. House of Quality}

Pick 3 requirements; weight 5/3/1. Rate components 5/3/1. Score = $\sum$(weight$\times$rating). Highest = build first.

\section*{\color{eublue}17. Exploration vs.\ Exploitation}

Exploration = finding new opportunities; risky; necessary. Exploitation = doing more of what works; safe; dangerous if market changes. Need both. Lean validation = test before you invest.

\section*{\color{eublue}18. Goods vs.\ Services}

Heterogeneity (services vary). Information asymmetry (brand as signal). Customer involvement (low productivity, hard to automate).

\subsection*{Yiami Midterm (May 2024)}

\textbf{Q1. Disruptive or sustaining? Radical or incremental?}\\
Yiami is \textbf{sustaining} (friend-making apps like Tinder already exist; no new market, not way cheaper). \textbf{Incremental} (AI, geolocation, chat — existing tech).

\textbf{Q2. Design thinking correct? Mistakes?}\\
Procedure: Personas $\to$ Observing $\to$ Functionality $\to$ Tech testing $\to$ Launch. Mistakes: (1) Personas before observing. (2) No needs analysis (What/How/Why). (3) No ideation — straight to functionality. (4) Tested with Reddit devs/beta testers, not target users. \textbf{House of Quality:} Requirements: Chatting (1), Compatible friends (3), Events offline (5). Scores: Database of filters 41 (build first), Matching 31, Geolocation 31, Event mgmt 29, Search 21, ID 17, Conversation 13.

\textbf{Q3. Moore's Chasm stage? Niche?}\\
\textbf{Innovators} — app not finished, not selling commercially; target tech enthusiasts. Niche: universities (Milan, Paris, Utrecht, Munich) = beachhead; word of mouth; dominate one then next.

\textbf{Q4. Ambassador training plan?}\\
Market landscape and competitors; target personas; value prop and differentiators; product usage and demos; common objections and responses.

\subsection*{Dacia Spring Midterm (Dec 2024)}

\textbf{Q1. Competitive strategy?}\\
\textbf{Cost leadership} (task specialisation, standardisation). Evidence: CMF-A with Renault/Dongfeng/Nissan; China, specialised workers; minimal features; simple marketing.

\textbf{Q2. Skilled or specialised workers?}\\
\textbf{Specialised.} Repetitive tasks, lower wages, supports cost leadership. Skilled = whole craft = higher wages = undermines low cost.

\textbf{Q3. Learning effect?}\\
(1) Share CMF-A $\to$ huge volume on same platform $\to$ repetition $\to$ learning effect, lower cost/unit. (2) Low price $\to$ demand $\to$ volume $\to$ learning effect again.

\textbf{Q4. Economies of scale?}\\
Greater output $\to$ lower per-unit fixed cost. Dacia: low prices (volume); shared platform with Renault/Dongfeng (volume).

\textbf{Q5. Three differences goods vs.\ services?}\\
(1) \textbf{Heterogeneity} — services inconsistent (same restaurant, different experience). (2) \textbf{Information asymmetry} — seller knows quality; brand as signal. (3) \textbf{Customer involvement} — customer in process; low productivity, hard to automate.

\textbf{Q6. BMC Dacia Spring.}\\
Value Prop: affordable EV, functional, Zity. Activities: manufacturing, marketing. Resources: CMF-A. Partners: Renault, Dongfeng, Nissan, Zity. Cost: low fixed (platform), low marketing, low variable (China). Relationships: non-recurrent (showrooms), recurrent (Zity). Segments: cheap EV buyers, Zity renters, Cargo, car-sharing. Channels: showrooms, Zity. Revenue: car sales, car sharing.

\subsection*{Moley Robot Kitchen — Final Sample}

\textbf{Q1. Product strategy and sustainability?}\\
\textbf{Differentiation} — uniqueness not price. Evidence: £248k; autonomous cooking, 5,000 recipes; targets enthusiasts/early adopters; Thermomix €1k, can't cook. Sustainable: R\&D; patents; recipe ecosystem; cheaper models later.

\textbf{Q2. BMC MRK.}\\
Value Prop: first autonomous robot chef, 5,000+ recipes, self-clean, restaurant quality at home. Segments: wealthy, tech enthusiasts, chefs, hotels. Channels: Moley site, showrooms, trade shows. Relationships: premium service, updates, support. Revenue: hardware £248k, subscriptions, maintenance. Resources: robotic arms, recipe DB, AI, patents. Activities: R\&D, motion capture, software, manufacturing. Partners: chefs, suppliers, luxury distribution. Cost: high R\&D, high manufacturing, value driven.

\textbf{Q3. Budget allocation (\$1M).}\\
Intro/innovators stage. R\&D 35\%; Technical design 25\%; Market research 15\%; Production process 10\%; Marketing 10\%; Sales forecasts 5\%. Rationale: limitations (peel, dice); precision and cheaper models; early adopters; manufacturing; targeted campaigns; forecasts secondary.

\textbf{Q4. Disruptive/sustaining, radical/incremental, and budget?}\\
\textbf{Disruptive} (new market — autonomous home chef; Thermomix blends, MRK cooks). \textbf{Radical} (robotic arms replicating chef movements = new tech; Yiami = incremental). Budget: radical+disruptive in early stage $\to$ heavy R\&D and technical (60\%); if sustaining+incremental like Dacia $\to$ more marketing and production.
